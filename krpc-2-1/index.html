KRPC 2.1 / MRPC 4-2.1: Advisor

The advisor role architecture: independent professional judgment, candid counsel, and the permitted scope of non-legal considerations.

Verbatim text of KRPC 2.1 (Kansas)

In representing a client, a lawyer shall exercise independent professional judgment and render candid advice. In rendering advice, a lawyer may refer not only to law but to other considerations such as moral, economic, social and political factors, that may be relevant to the client's situation. Kan. Sup. Ct. R. 226, KRPC 2.1.

Missouri Rule 4-2.1

Missouri Rule 4-2.1 is substantively identical and tracks ABA Model Rule 2.1. The rule is structurally affirmative: it establishes what the advisor role looks like rather than enumerating what it forbids. Two operative duties, each cast in active voice, frame the rule: the lawyer shall exercise independent professional judgment, and the lawyer shall render candid advice. A third clause expands the permitted scope of advice: the lawyer may refer to non-legal considerations relevant to the client's situation.

The advisor role 2.1 establishes

Rule 2.1 distinguishes the advisor role from the advocate role and supplies the operational content of advisor practice. As an advisor, the lawyer is helping the client understand legal rights and obligations, evaluate options, and decide what to do. The two duties and the one permission together constitute the advisor-role architecture.

Independent professional judgment

The judgment offered to the client must be the lawyer's own, formed through the lawyer's professional analysis, free of distortion by the lawyer's own interests, by the interests of third parties, or by reluctance to deliver advice the client may not want to hear. The independence is structural; it is the condition that makes the advice worth obtaining.

Candid advice

The advice delivered must be candid - direct, complete, and unvarnished by the lawyer's discomfort with the conclusion. Comment [1] to the rule notes that a client may be entitled to straightforward advice expressing the lawyer's honest assessment, even when the assessment is unwelcome.

Independent professional judgment as professional infrastructure

Independence under 2.1 is more than the absence of disqualifying conflicts; it is the affirmative formation of the lawyer's own judgment as the basis for advice. The infrastructural reading requires:

  • Adequate analysis of the legal question - sufficient research, factual investigation, and consideration of alternatives to support a real professional opinion
  • Insulation from influences that would distort the judgment - third-party payers, business referral sources, and the lawyer's own interest in particular outcomes
  • Resistance to client pressure for the advice the client wants to hear; the advisor role would collapse if the lawyer simply mirrored back the client's preferred conclusion
  • Acknowledgement of uncertainty where the law or facts do not support a confident position; manufactured certainty is not professional judgment

Comment [1] frames the duty in these terms: legal advice often involves unpleasant facts and alternatives that a client may be disinclined to confront. In presenting advice, a lawyer endeavors to sustain the client's morale and may put advice in as acceptable a form as honesty permits. However, a lawyer should not be deterred from giving candid advice by the prospect that the advice will be unpalatable to the client.

The permitted scope of candid counsel - non-legal considerations

The second sentence of Rule 2.1 expands the advisor's range. In rendering advice, a lawyer may refer not only to law but to other considerations - moral, economic, social, and political factors - that may be relevant to the client's situation. The clause is permissive ("may refer"), not mandatory, and it is calibrated to what is "relevant to the client's situation."

The permission is significant because it positions the advisor as a counselor rather than a narrow legal-question-answering function. A client deciding whether to bring a lawsuit, structure a transaction, or accept a settlement is rarely choosing among options on legal grounds alone. The advisor role contemplates that the lawyer may bring the full range of considerations to bear, while respecting that the decision belongs to the client.

Moral considerations

Comment [2] addresses the moral dimension directly: advice couched in narrowly legal terms may be of little value to a client, especially where practical considerations such as cost or effects on other people are predominant. Purely technical legal advice can therefore be inadequate. The advisor is permitted to acknowledge the moral dimension where it bears on the client's actual situation.

Economic, social, and political considerations

The economic dimension is often inseparable from the legal one - a client deciding whether to litigate is also deciding how to allocate capital and attention. The social and political dimensions become relevant where the client's reputation, relationships, or public standing are affected by the legal choice. The advisor's permitted scope under 2.1 makes it appropriate to surface these dimensions as part of candid counsel.

Advisor versus advocate - the role distinction

Rule 2.1 occupies the "Counselor" section of the rules of professional conduct, sitting alongside Rules 2.3 (evaluation for use by third persons) and 2.4 (lawyer serving as third-party neutral). The placement signals that the advisor role is structurally distinct from the advocate role addressed in Article 3 of the rules.

Advisor (Article 2)

The lawyer is helping the client understand and decide. Independent judgment and candid advice are the operative qualities. The audience is the client.

Advocate (Article 3)

The lawyer is asserting the client's position before a tribunal or in negotiation with adverse parties. Diligent advocacy bounded by candor toward the tribunal under Rule 3.3 is the operative quality. The audience is external.

The same lawyer in the same matter regularly shifts between roles. The advisor-advocate distinction does not mean the lawyer cannot do both; it means the lawyer should be aware of which role is operative at a given moment, because the operative qualities differ. A lawyer who delivers advocate-style framing to the client - presenting only the favorable case - has not satisfied the advisor duty.

When to volunteer advice

Comment [5] addresses when a lawyer is expected to initiate advice that the client has not requested. In general, a lawyer is not expected to give advice until asked by the client. However, when a lawyer knows that a client proposes a course of action likely to result in substantial adverse legal consequences to the client, the lawyer's duty under Rule 1.4 may require that the lawyer offer advice if the client's course of action is related to the representation. The advisor duty under 2.1 reinforces the communication duty under Rule 1.4: candid advice volunteered at the moment it would be useful is part of the advisor role.

Counsel-to-counsel referral

Comment [4] notes that matters that go beyond strictly legal questions may also be in the domain of another profession. Where consultation with a professional in another field is itself something a competent lawyer would recommend, the lawyer should make such a recommendation. The advisor role is not the role of universal expert; it includes the judgment to identify when other professional input would serve the client and to surface that need as part of candid advice.

The architecture in summary

Rule 2.1 establishes a three-part advisor architecture: (1) independent professional judgment formed through the lawyer's own analysis; (2) candid advice delivered without distortion by the lawyer's discomfort with the conclusion; (3) permitted scope that extends to moral, economic, social, and political considerations relevant to the client's situation. The architecture is what makes the advisor relationship valuable to a client deciding what to do.

Relationship to Rule 1.4 (Communication)

Rule 2.1 is in close dialogue with Rule 1.4. Communication under 1.4 governs what the lawyer must tell the client about the status, scope, and material developments of the representation; the advisor role under 2.1 governs the quality of the substantive advice the lawyer delivers within that communication. The two rules work together: candid advice rendered with independent judgment is one of the most important things 1.4 communication conveys.

Relationship to Rule 1.2 (Scope of Representation)

Rule 1.2 allocates decision-making authority between lawyer and client. The advisor role under 2.1 supplies the substantive input that makes the client's allocated decisions meaningful. A client who is to decide the objectives of the representation under 1.2(a) needs the candid, independent advice under 2.1 to decide well.

Relationship to Rule 1.7 (Conflicts)

Independent professional judgment under 2.1 presupposes the absence of disqualifying conflicts under Rule 1.7. The conflicts framework protects independence at the structural level (by precluding the representation entirely or requiring informed consent); the 2.1 duty operates within the cleared space.

Practical impact

Rule 2.1 is the rule that makes the lawyer's advice valuable. The architecture - independence + candor + permitted breadth of considerations - is what distinguishes professional advice from agreement, from sales pitch, and from narrow legal-question answering. A practice organized around the 2.1 architecture treats every advice moment as an opportunity to deliver something the client could not otherwise obtain: the lawyer's own honest assessment of the client's actual situation across the dimensions that matter.
