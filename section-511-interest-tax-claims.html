11 U.S.C. Section 511 - Rate of Interest on Tax Claims

The BAPCPA rule fixing the interest rate on tax claims at the rate determined under applicable nonbankruptcy law - displacing the Till prime-plus-risk methodology for tax obligations.

What Is Section 511?

Section 511, added by the Bankruptcy Abuse Prevention and Consumer Protection Act of 2005 (BAPCPA), prescribes the interest rate to be paid on tax claims in any case under Title 11. It overrides the case-by-case interest analysis required by Till v. SCS Credit Corp., 541 U.S. 465 (2004), for tax claims specifically. For tax claims, courts no longer apply a prime-plus-risk-adjustment methodology; the applicable rate is whatever rate applicable nonbankruptcy law - typically the Internal Revenue Code's underpayment rate, or the analogous state-tax rate - prescribes.

Official citation: 11 U.S.C. § 511

The Statutory Text

Section 511(a) provides: "If any provision of this title requires the payment of interest on a tax claim or on an administrative expense tax, or the payment of interest to enable a creditor to receive the present value of the allowed amount of a tax claim, the rate of interest shall be the rate determined under applicable nonbankruptcy law."

Section 511(b) adds a timing rule for non-assessed taxes: "In the case of taxes paid under a confirmed plan under this title, the rate of interest shall be determined as of the calendar month in which the plan is confirmed."

The IRS Underpayment Rate

For federal tax claims, the applicable nonbankruptcy law is Internal Revenue Code § 6621, which sets the underpayment rate at the federal short-term rate plus three percentage points (plus an additional two points for large corporate underpayments above $100,000). The rate is determined quarterly by the IRS and published in revenue rulings. For state and local tax claims, the rate is set by the analogous statute of the taxing authority - many states track the federal rate, others apply fixed statutory percentages.

The practical effect: tax claimants in bankruptcy receive a non-negotiable, statutorily fixed rate, not a Till-style market rate. This is generally favorable to taxing authorities because the IRS underpayment rate frequently exceeds the prime rate in low-interest-rate environments and approximates or slightly exceeds prime in moderate-rate environments.

Claims to Which Section 511 Applies

Section 511 applies wherever Title 11 calls for interest on a tax claim, including:

  • Priority unsecured tax claims under Section 507(a)(8): When a Chapter 13 plan or a Chapter 11 plan pays the priority tax claim in installments under Section 1129(a)(9)(C) or Section 1322(a)(2), the present-value interest component is computed at the Section 511 rate;
  • Secured tax claims (tax liens): When a plan provides for cramdown of a secured tax claim under Section 1129(b)(2)(A) or Section 1325(a)(5)(B), the present-value interest is computed at the Section 511 rate, not the Till rate;
  • Administrative expense tax claims under Section 503(b)(1)(B): Post-petition taxes incurred by the estate, where Title 11 requires interest, accrue at the Section 511 rate;
  • Cures under Section 1322(b)(5) or 1123(a)(5)(G): Where a tax obligation is cured under a plan, the cure interest, if any, is determined under Section 511.

The Till Backdrop

In Till v. SCS Credit Corp., the Supreme Court adopted a "formula approach" for determining the cramdown interest rate under Section 1325(a)(5)(B)(ii) - prime rate plus a risk adjustment (typically 1-3%) tailored to the debtor's circumstances. Till applies to general secured-creditor cramdown, but Section 511 carves taxes out: for tax claims, Congress directed the use of the nonbankruptcy rate, which in many cases is materially higher than a Till rate.

The carve-out reflects a policy judgment that taxing authorities are involuntary creditors entitled to the same interest treatment they would receive outside bankruptcy, rather than a court-derived market rate. The Internal Revenue Manual and the U.S. Trustee Program's tax-claim guidance reflect this position.

Variable Rates Under Section 511(b)

Section 511(b) fixes the rate as of the calendar month of confirmation. Because the IRS underpayment rate changes quarterly, a confirmation in (for example) March 2026 locks in the Q1 2026 rate for the life of the plan, even if rates later rise or fall. Some courts and commentators have debated whether 511(b) creates a true fixed rate or whether it merely sets the initial rate, with subsequent adjustments tracking quarterly changes. The majority view treats 511(b) as fixing the rate at confirmation, providing certainty for plan feasibility analysis under Section 1129(a)(11) and Section 1325(a)(6).

Common Procedural Postures

Section 511 issues typically arise in the following contexts:

  • Chapter 13 plan confirmation where the IRS or state tax authority objects to the interest rate proposed in the plan;
  • Chapter 11 plan confirmation involving priority tax claims paid over up to five years from the petition date under Section 1129(a)(9)(C);
  • Cramdown of a secured tax lien claim (real-property tax lien, federal tax lien) where the debtor proposes to retain the encumbered property and pay the secured portion with interest;
  • Determining whether a plan is feasible when the required Section 511 interest pushes payments above the debtor's projected disposable income.
