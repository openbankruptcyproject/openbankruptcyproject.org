Patterson v. Shumate

504 U.S. 753 (1992)

ERISA pension plans excluded from bankruptcy estate

Facts

Joseph Shumate held a vested interest in his employer's ERISA-qualified pension plan. The plan contained the anti-alienation language required by 29 U.S.C. § 1056(d)(1). Shumate filed Chapter 7; the trustee, Patterson, sought to recover the pension benefits for the estate.

Procedural Posture

The Bankruptcy Court ruled for the trustee; the District Court reversed; the Fourth Circuit affirmed the district court. The Supreme Court granted certiorari to resolve a circuit split over whether the ERISA anti-alienation provision is enforceable under 11 U.S.C. § 541(c)(2).

Issue Presented

Whether the ERISA anti-alienation provision is a 'restriction on the transfer of a beneficial interest of the debtor in a trust that is enforceable under applicable nonbankruptcy law' within the meaning of 11 U.S.C. § 541(c)(2), thereby excluding the pension interest from the bankruptcy estate.

Holding

Yes. A unanimous Court held that ERISA's anti-alienation provision is enforceable under 'applicable nonbankruptcy law' within the meaning of § 541(c)(2). An ERISA-qualified pension plan interest is therefore excluded from the bankruptcy estate.

Key Reasoning

Justice Blackmun gave 'applicable nonbankruptcy law' its plain meaning — federal as well as state law qualifies. ERISA's anti-alienation provision is a federal restriction on transfer that is enforceable in its own right. The exclusion under § 541(c)(2) is not limited to interests in traditional spendthrift trusts under state law. The Court rejected the argument that 'applicable nonbankruptcy law' meant only state law, noting that the phrase appears throughout the Code and consistently encompasses both state and federal law.

Statutes Interpreted

  • 11 U.S.C. § 541(c)(2) — Exclusion of beneficial interests subject to transfer restrictions
  • 29 U.S.C. § 1056(d)(1) — ERISA anti-alienation provision

Post-Precedent Impact

Patterson is a foundational property-of-the-estate decision. Most retirement assets governed by ERISA — 401(k), pension, and similar plans — are categorically excluded from the bankruptcy estate. The decision did not address IRAs (which are not ERISA-qualified for these purposes); Congress addressed IRAs separately in the 2005 BAPCPA amendments through § 522(b)(3)(C) and § 522(n). Patterson remains the controlling authority on the scope of § 541(c)(2) and on the federal-law reach of 'applicable nonbankruptcy law.'

Statutory Text and Structure

Section 541(c)(2) provides that 'a restriction on the transfer of a beneficial interest of the debtor in a trust that is enforceable under applicable nonbankruptcy law is enforceable in a case under this title.' The provision functions as an exclusion: assets subject to an enforceable transfer restriction are excluded from the estate entirely, not merely exempted from creditors' reach. ERISA's anti-alienation provision at 29 U.S.C. § 1056(d)(1) is a transfer restriction enforceable under federal nonbankruptcy law.

Doctrinal Lineage

Patterson resolved a long-running circuit split over the scope of 'applicable nonbankruptcy law' in § 541(c)(2). Some circuits had limited the phrase to state spendthrift-trust law; others had read it to encompass federal law as well. Congress later supplemented Patterson with BAPCPA's 2005 retirement-savings amendments, which provided exemption protections for IRAs through § 522(b)(3)(C) and § 522(n) regardless of ERISA qualification.

Open Questions and Subsequent Developments

Patterson did not address how to treat ERISA-qualified plans that have been improperly administered or that have lost their qualified status. It also did not address top-hat plans, deferred-compensation arrangements, or other non-ERISA executive-compensation vehicles. Subsequent decisions have generally treated each type of arrangement on its own terms, examining whether the relevant nonbankruptcy law restricts transfer. The 2014 decision in Clark v. Rameker, 573 U.S. 122 (2014), distinguished inherited IRAs from ordinary retirement accounts, narrowing the post-Patterson protection in that specific context.

Current Practice

Counsel routinely confirm ERISA qualification of retirement plans early in the case to identify property excluded from the estate. Documentation typically includes the plan instrument, the IRS determination letter, and confirmation of the anti-alienation provision. Non-ERISA retirement assets — most IRAs, certain church plans, and certain governmental plans — are evaluated separately under § 522(b)(3)(C) and § 522(n) or state exemption schemes. The exclusion under Patterson is categorical: no claim of exemption is needed when the asset is excluded from the estate in the first instance.

Broader Significance

Patterson is foundational to the contemporary treatment of retirement assets in bankruptcy. The decision reflects the policy judgment that retirement savings deserve broad protection from creditor reach to support the social-insurance function of private retirement systems. Together with the 2005 BAPCPA amendments, Patterson creates a layered framework: ERISA-qualified plans are excluded from the estate under section 541(c)(2), most IRAs are subject to substantial exemption protection under section 522(b)(3)(C) and section 522(n), and inherited IRAs (per Clark v. Rameker) receive less protection. The decision continues to influence retirement-plan design, asset-protection planning, and the administration of bankruptcy estates with significant retirement components.

Practitioner Notes

Counsel should confirm ERISA qualification of any retirement plan early in the case. Documentation typically includes the plan instrument, the IRS determination letter, and confirmation of the anti-alienation provision. Property excluded from the estate under section 541(c)(2) need not be exempted; it is simply not part of the estate. Non-ERISA retirement assets, including most IRAs, are evaluated separately under section 522(b)(3)(C) and section 522(n). Inherited IRAs receive substantially reduced protection per Clark v. Rameker. The exclusion-vs-exemption distinction matters procedurally because exclusion is jurisdictional while exemption requires affirmative claim and timely objection.
