Grogan v. Garner

498 U.S. 279 (1991)

Preponderance of the evidence governs § 523 nondischargeability

Facts

Garner obtained a state-court judgment against Grogan for fraud and then filed Chapter 7. Garner brought an adversary proceeding to except the judgment from discharge under § 523(a)(2)(A). Grogan disputed the nondischargeability claim and argued, among other things, that nondischargeability under § 523(a) required clear-and-convincing evidence.

Procedural Posture

The Bankruptcy Court ruled for Garner; the District Court and the Eighth Circuit affirmed. The Supreme Court granted certiorari to resolve a circuit split over the burden of proof in § 523(a) actions.

Issue Presented

Whether the standard of proof for the dischargeability exceptions in 11 U.S.C. § 523(a) is preponderance of the evidence or clear and convincing evidence; and the related question of whether issue preclusion applies to state-court fraud judgments in § 523(a)(2)(A) actions.

Holding

A unanimous Court held that the standard of proof under § 523(a) is preponderance of the evidence, the default civil standard, and that issue preclusion may apply to underlying state-court fraud judgments when the state-court proceeding satisfied the elements of § 523(a)(2)(A) under a preponderance standard or higher.

Key Reasoning

Justice Stevens emphasized that Congress did not specify a heightened standard in § 523(a) and the default in civil litigation is preponderance. The Code's discharge policy favors debtors who acted honestly; debtors whose obligations arise from fraud are outside the 'honest but unfortunate' category at the heart of the discharge. Applying the default standard balances the debtor's discharge interest against the creditor's interest in collecting on a debt obtained by wrongful conduct.

Statutes Interpreted

  • 11 U.S.C. § 523(a) — Exceptions to discharge

Post-Precedent Impact

Grogan is the bedrock authority on the burden of proof in § 523(a) actions. It applies uniformly across all subsections — fraud, embezzlement, willful and malicious injury, defalcation, and others — unless a specific provision indicates otherwise. The issue-preclusion holding has been particularly influential, leading creditors to seek state-court judgments with specific fraud findings to streamline the subsequent bankruptcy litigation.

Statutory Text and Structure

Section 523(a) sets out nineteen categories of debts excepted from discharge, including fraud-related claims under § 523(a)(2), embezzlement and larceny under § 523(a)(4), willful and malicious injury under § 523(a)(6), and others. The statute does not specify the burden of proof. Grogan supplied the default rule: preponderance of the evidence governs each subsection unless a specific provision indicates otherwise. The decision's burden-of-proof reasoning controls all § 523(a) dischargeability litigation.

Doctrinal Lineage

Pre-Grogan circuit split: several circuits had applied clear-and-convincing evidence as the standard for § 523 nondischargeability claims, drawing on the discharge's status as a fundamental fresh-start protection and on general common-law fraud-pleading standards. Grogan rejected that approach in favor of the default preponderance standard articulated in Steadman v. Securities and Exchange Commission, 450 U.S. 91 (1981). The issue-preclusion ruling complements In re Brown, 442 U.S. 127 (1979), which had established the broader res judicata framework for bankruptcy litigation.

Open Questions and Subsequent Developments

Grogan did not address whether the preponderance standard applies equally to objections to discharge under § 727(a) (denial of all discharge in Chapter 7). Most courts have applied the same standard by analogy, though some have suggested a clear-and-convincing standard in light of § 727's more severe consequences. The decision's issue-preclusion holding requires careful element-by-element comparison: a state-court judgment for 'fraud' may or may not meet the specific elements of § 523(a)(2)(A) depending on how the state defines fraud and what facts the state court actually adjudicated.

Current Practice

Creditors now litigate § 523 actions under the standard civil burden. Issue preclusion from prior state-court fraud judgments is routinely invoked; creditors with potential fraud claims often pursue state-court fraud judgments before bankruptcy precisely to lock in preclusive findings. Defense counsel scrutinize prior judgments for the precise elements found and the burden of proof applied; preclusion requires that the prior tribunal made findings that meet or exceed the § 523 elements under at least a preponderance standard.

Broader Significance

Grogan is the bedrock burden-of-proof decision for section 523 dischargeability litigation. The decision reflects the Court's general preference for the default civil burden absent clear statutory direction otherwise. The decision also recognizes the legitimate role of issue preclusion in bankruptcy litigation, encouraging creditors to litigate fraud claims to judgment in non-bankruptcy forums when feasible. The decision's burden-of-proof holding applies uniformly across section 523(a) subsections and has shaped the analytical framework for nondischargeability litigation for more than three decades.

Practitioner Notes

Creditors pursuing section 523 nondischargeability actions should evaluate whether issue preclusion from a prior state-court judgment is available. Preclusion requires identification of the specific findings made by the prior tribunal, the elements adjudicated, and the burden of proof applied. State-court fraud judgments often provide preclusive findings on the section 523(a)(2)(A) elements; default judgments typically do not, because no actual litigation of the elements occurred. Where preclusion is not available, the section 523 action must independently establish each element by a preponderance of the evidence under Grogan's burden of proof.
