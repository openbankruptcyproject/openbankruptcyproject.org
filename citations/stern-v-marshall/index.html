Stern v. Marshall

564 U.S. 462 (2011)

Article III limits on bankruptcy-court final adjudication

Facts

Vickie Lynn Marshall (Anna Nicole Smith) filed Chapter 11. E. Pierce Marshall, son of her late husband, filed a non-dischargeability complaint asserting that Vickie defamed him. Vickie counterclaimed against Pierce for tortious interference with an expected inheritance. The bankruptcy court entered judgment for Vickie on the counterclaim despite that claim's status as a state common-law cause of action with no necessary resolution as part of the bankruptcy claims-allowance process.

Procedural Posture

The District Court reviewed and altered the bankruptcy court's judgment; the Ninth Circuit reversed; the Supreme Court granted certiorari to address whether the bankruptcy court's entry of a final judgment on the counterclaim was constitutional.

Issue Presented

Whether the bankruptcy court, as an Article I tribunal, had the constitutional authority under Article III of the United States Constitution to enter a final judgment on a state common-law counterclaim that did not stem from the bankruptcy itself or necessarily get resolved in the claims-allowance process.

Holding

The bankruptcy court lacked constitutional authority to enter a final judgment on the counterclaim, even though 28 U.S.C. § 157(b)(2)(C) designated counterclaims by the estate as 'core' proceedings. Adjudication of such state-law claims requires an Article III tribunal absent the parties' consent.

Key Reasoning

Chief Justice Roberts, writing for a 5-4 majority, applied Northern Pipeline Construction Co. v. Marathon Pipe Line Co., 458 U.S. 50 (1982), and Granfinanciera, S.A. v. Nordberg, 492 U.S. 33 (1989). Adjudicatory authority over private rights belongs to Article III courts. Statutory designation as 'core' cannot, by itself, vest Article I judges with constitutional adjudicatory power. The bankruptcy court may still hear the proceeding and submit proposed findings and conclusions to the district court for de novo review.

Statutes Interpreted

  • 28 U.S.C. § 157(b) — Core proceedings
  • 28 U.S.C. § 157(c) — Non-core proceedings

Post-Precedent Impact

Stern reshaped the procedural posture of countless bankruptcy adversary proceedings. Bankruptcy courts and parties now routinely confront 'Stern issues': identifying which 'core' matters under § 157(b) nevertheless fall outside the bankruptcy court's constitutional authority to enter final judgment. The decision spurred local-rule and procedural reforms across districts and was the launching pad for Executive Benefits Insurance Agency v. Arkison and Wellness International Network v. Sharif, which clarified consent and proposed-findings remedies.

Statutory Text and Structure

28 U.S.C. § 157(b)(2) lists sixteen categories of 'core' proceedings, including '(C) counterclaims by the estate against persons filing claims against the estate.' Stern held that this textual designation, while statutorily authorizing the bankruptcy court to hear the proceeding, could not constitutionally vest final judgment authority where the underlying claim was a state common-law cause of action with no necessary resolution as part of the claims-allowance process. The decision distinguishes between statutory authority to adjudicate and constitutional authority to enter final judgment.

Doctrinal Lineage

Stern revisited Article III concerns first raised in Northern Pipeline, which had invalidated the original 1978 Code's broad grant of jurisdiction to bankruptcy judges. Congress responded with the 1984 Amendments, distinguishing 'core' from 'non-core' proceedings at 28 U.S.C. § 157. Stern held that the core/non-core line drawn by Congress does not necessarily track the Article III line drawn by the Constitution. The decision builds on the Court's earlier public-rights doctrine in Granfinanciera and Crowell v. Benson, 285 U.S. 22 (1932), and on the structural-protections discussion in Commodity Futures Trading Commission v. Schor.

Open Questions and Subsequent Developments

After Stern, lower courts and commentators identified an extensive list of potentially affected 'core' categories: fraudulent-conveyance actions (including Granfinanciera-type avoidance claims when the defendant has not filed a claim), tortious-interference counterclaims, contract counterclaims for affirmative recovery, and others. The Court has not provided a comprehensive Stern taxonomy; practitioners and courts must analyze each claim under the public-rights/private-rights distinction. Wellness substantially eased the practical burden by recognizing consent, but the constitutional framework remains complex.

Current Practice

Bankruptcy courts now routinely address Stern issues at the outset of adversary proceedings — often through standing orders requiring parties to state consent to final adjudication. When the court lacks Stern authority, the bankruptcy judge submits proposed findings of fact and conclusions of law to the district court under 28 U.S.C. § 157(c)(1), which the district court reviews de novo. Wellness Int'l Network v. Sharif clarified that consent can be implied from conduct, mitigating much of the procedural disruption Stern initially created. Litigants nonetheless must analyze each claim for Stern implications before judgment.

Broader Significance

Stern reframed bankruptcy procedure around an Article III analysis that had been largely dormant since Northern Pipeline. The decision's significance extends beyond bankruptcy to the broader question of when Congress may vest adjudicatory authority in non-Article III tribunals. The decision continues to generate scholarly commentary on the public-rights and private-rights distinction, the proper scope of Congressional authority to channel disputes into specialized tribunals, and the relationship between consent and constitutional structure. In bankruptcy practice, Stern shifted the day-to-day operations of many adversary proceedings, prompted local-rule reform across districts, and reshaped the analytical framework that practitioners use when evaluating which forum can finally adjudicate a given claim.

Practitioner Notes

Litigants in bankruptcy adversary proceedings should perform a Stern analysis at the outset of each claim. The analysis typically asks: (1) is the claim a 'core' proceeding under section 157(b); (2) if so, does the claim nonetheless implicate a private right that requires Article III adjudication; (3) if Stern applies, what consent or proposed-findings procedure should be invoked. Many districts have standing orders that require the parties' Stern position in initial pleadings. Failing to address Stern can result in a void judgment or in waste of trial resources if the issue surfaces post-judgment.
