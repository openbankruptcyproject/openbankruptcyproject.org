Associates Commercial Corporation v. Rash

520 U.S. 953 (1997)

Replacement value (not foreclosure value) governs cramdown of retained collateral

Facts

Elray Rash filed Chapter 13 and proposed to retain a tractor that secured a debt to Associates Commercial Corporation. The plan paid Associates the foreclosure (wholesale) value of the tractor. Associates objected and contended that the appropriate valuation was the replacement (retail) value the debtor would have to pay to acquire comparable collateral.

Procedural Posture

The Bankruptcy Court adopted foreclosure value; the District Court and the Fifth Circuit (sitting en banc) affirmed. The Supreme Court granted certiorari to resolve a circuit split.

Issue Presented

When a Chapter 13 debtor retains and uses collateral, what is the appropriate valuation method for purposes of determining the secured creditor's allowed secured claim under 11 U.S.C. § 506(a) and the cramdown protections of § 1325(a)(5)?

Holding

When the debtor retains and uses collateral, § 506(a)'s 'use'-driven valuation standard requires replacement value — the price the debtor would pay to acquire comparable property — not foreclosure value.

Key Reasoning

Justice Ginsburg, writing for the majority, focused on § 506(a)'s instruction that valuation depend on 'the proposed disposition or use' of the property. A debtor who retains and uses the collateral is not in a foreclosure posture, and the creditor is denied the opportunity to recover by repossession and sale. Replacement value reflects the actual economic position of the parties. Congress codified this approach for Chapter 13 individual consumer debtors at § 506(a)(2) in BAPCPA, expressly adopting Rash for those cases.

Statutes Interpreted

  • 11 U.S.C. § 506(a) — Determination of secured status; valuation
  • 11 U.S.C. § 1325(a)(5) — Chapter 13 cramdown

Post-Precedent Impact

Rash governs cramdown valuation in Chapter 13 (now reinforced by § 506(a)(2) for consumer debtors), Chapter 11 (when collateral is retained), Subchapter V (by extension to § 1191(c)), and Chapter 12 family-farmer cases. Practitioners use Rash to support replacement-cost appraisals over foreclosure-auction comparables. The decision is foundational to contemporary cramdown valuation practice and complements Till on the interest-rate side of the cramdown calculation.

Statutory Text and Structure

Section 506(a)(1) provides that the value of an allowed secured claim 'shall be determined in light of the purpose of the valuation and of the proposed disposition or use of such property.' Section 506(a)(2), added by BAPCPA, specifies for consumer Chapter 13 cases that value is 'the replacement value of the property as of the date of the filing of the petition without deduction for costs of sale or marketing.' Rash interpreted § 506(a)(1)'s use-driven framework, and Congress later codified Rash's outcome for the most common consumer scenarios.

Doctrinal Lineage

Before Rash, lower courts used either foreclosure (wholesale) value, replacement (retail) value, or a midpoint figure to value retained collateral. The Fifth Circuit's foreclosure-value approach had been the focus of significant academic and practical debate. Congress codified Rash for consumer Chapter 13 cases through BAPCPA's § 506(a)(2). Together with Till on the interest-rate question, Rash forms the two-part backbone of the modern cramdown calculation.

Open Questions and Subsequent Developments

Rash addressed the valuation methodology but not many of the practical implementation questions: whether to use retail, wholesale-to-retail-spread, or appraised replacement value; how to account for collateral condition; how to address collateral that has no comparable retail market (specialty equipment, custom items). The decision also did not address Chapter 11 cramdown valuation when the collateral is retained and used by the reorganized debtor. Most courts apply Rash's reasoning to Chapter 11 retention cases by analogy.

Current Practice

Replacement value is typically established through retail-market evidence: NADA or Black Book retail values, professional appraisals, or comparable retail-sales data. For real property, replacement value generally aligns with fair market value as determined by appraisal. The valuation date is the petition date in Chapter 13 cases under § 506(a)(2) and is typically the effective date of the plan in Chapter 11 cases. Cramdown valuation disputes often dominate confirmation hearings in cases involving high-value collateral.

Broader Significance

Rash is one of the foundational valuation decisions in modern bankruptcy. The decision's use-driven valuation framework reflects the economic reality that retained collateral is not equivalent to liquidated collateral. Together with Till on the interest-rate side, Rash forms the two-part backbone of contemporary cramdown calculations. Congress's codification of Rash for consumer Chapter 13 cases through BAPCPA's section 506(a)(2) confirms the durability of the decision's framework. The decision continues to inform valuation disputes across all chapters, including Chapter 11 and Chapter 12 family-farmer cases.

Practitioner Notes

Practitioners proposing a cramdown should support the proposed value with credible retail-market evidence: NADA or Black Book retail values, professional appraisals, or comparable retail-sales data. For real property, replacement value typically aligns with fair market value as determined by appraisal. The valuation date is the petition date for consumer Chapter 13 cases under section 506(a)(2) and typically the effective date of the plan in Chapter 11 cases. Cramdown valuation disputes often dominate confirmation hearings; well-prepared appraisal evidence frequently determines the outcome. Live testimony from an expert appraiser typically carries more weight than written appraisal reports alone, particularly when valuation methodology is contested.
