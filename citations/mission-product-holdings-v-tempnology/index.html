Mission Product Holdings, Inc. v. Tempnology, LLC

587 U.S. 370 (2019)

Rejection of a trademark license does not rescind the licensee's rights

Facts

Tempnology granted Mission Product Holdings a trademark license. Tempnology filed Chapter 11 and rejected the license under § 365(a). Tempnology argued that rejection rescinded the license and terminated Mission's trademark-use rights. Mission countered that rejection is a breach — not a rescission — and that its trademark-use rights survived.

Procedural Posture

The Bankruptcy Court ruled for Tempnology; the Bankruptcy Appellate Panel reversed; the First Circuit reinstated the rescission view, joining the Fourth Circuit. The Supreme Court granted certiorari to resolve a deepening circuit split.

Issue Presented

Whether the rejection of a trademark license under 11 U.S.C. § 365 has the effect of (i) a breach of the license, leaving the licensee's rights intact and converting the debtor's nonperformance into a pre-petition damages claim, or (ii) a rescission that terminates the licensee's right to use the trademark.

Holding

Rejection of a trademark license is a breach, not a rescission. The non-debtor licensee retains its trademark-use rights after rejection. Rejection converts the debtor's continuing performance obligations into a pre-petition damages claim under § 365(g) but does not terminate rights already conferred.

Key Reasoning

Justice Kagan, writing for a 8-1 majority, applied the statutory definition of rejection in § 365(g): rejection 'constitutes a breach' of the contract. A breach does not undo contract rights already conferred; it gives the non-breaching party a damages claim for the breach. The absence of trademarks from the § 365(n) intellectual-property list — added by Congress in 1988 — does not negatively imply rescission of trademark licenses. The 'negative inference' canon must yield to § 365(g)'s direct text and to background contract-law principles.

Statutes Interpreted

  • 11 U.S.C. § 365(g) — Rejection of executory contracts; breach
  • 11 U.S.C. § 365(n) — Intellectual-property license preservation

Post-Precedent Impact

Tempnology resolved the principal post-§ 365(n) IP-licensing question and aligned trademark-license treatment with patent, copyright, and trade-secret licenses (which Congress had protected in 1988 via § 365(n)). Licensees can now invest in trademark-bearing products with the confidence that licensor bankruptcy will not terminate their rights. The decision shapes Chapter 11 strategy for both debtors and counterparties to IP-bearing executory contracts.

Statutory Text and Structure

Section 365(g) provides that 'the rejection of an executory contract or unexpired lease of the debtor constitutes a breach of such contract or lease' that is treated as if it 'occurred immediately before the date of the filing of the petition.' Section 365(n) lists intellectual property categories (patent, copyright, trade secret) for which the licensee may elect to retain rights despite the debtor-licensor's rejection. Trademarks are notably absent from the § 365(n) list. Tempnology held that this omission does not authorize a different rule for trademarks; § 365(g)'s breach-not-rescission default applies.

Doctrinal Lineage

Section 365(n) was enacted in 1988 in response to the Fourth Circuit's decision in Lubrizol Enterprises, Inc. v. Richmond Metal Finishers, Inc., 756 F.2d 1043 (4th Cir. 1985), which had treated rejection as a rescission of intellectual-property licenses. Congress protected patent, copyright, and trade-secret licenses through § 365(n) but omitted trademarks. The Tempnology question — whether trademarks should also be protected — was the principal remaining licensing issue post-§ 365(n). The decision resolves it through general contract-law principles rather than reliance on negative implication from the IP statutory list.

Open Questions and Subsequent Developments

Tempnology did not address how to handle quality-control failures by a debtor-licensor who has rejected the license. Trademarks function as source identifiers, and continued use by the licensee without quality control could compromise the trademark's validity. Some courts have suggested that the licensee bears the practical risk of post-rejection quality issues. The decision also did not address rejection of intellectual-property licenses by the licensee (rather than the licensor); most courts apply ordinary § 365 principles to licensee rejection, with the licensor having only a pre-petition damages claim.

Current Practice

Trademark licensees now enjoy substantial protection against licensor bankruptcy. The decision encourages investment in trademark-bearing products and licensing arrangements that would previously have carried significant insolvency risk. Quality-control provisions in trademark licenses remain enforceable contract obligations; the decision did not address whether a debtor-licensor's failure to police quality post-rejection could affect the licensee's continued rights. Trademark practitioners now structure agreements with explicit attention to post-rejection scenarios.

Broader Significance

Tempnology resolves a long-running question about the post-rejection treatment of trademark licenses and aligns trademark practice with the broader contract-law principles that govern executory-contract rejection. The decision's general-principles approach is significant: it treats trademark licenses like any other contract rather than crafting a special rule based on negative implication from section 365(n). The decision has practical consequences for IP-licensing practice, encouraging more secure licensing structures for trademark-bearing products. The decision also fits within a broader doctrinal arc that gives counterparties to debtor contracts substantial protection against retroactive rights termination through bankruptcy proceedings.

Practitioner Notes

Counsel for trademark licensees should be prepared to assert continued rights despite licensor rejection. The licensee should document the license terms, the rejection event, and the licensee's continued performance of any required obligations. Counsel for trademark licensors anticipating rejection should evaluate whether contract-restructuring or strategic renegotiation can produce more favorable outcomes than rejection. The decision's general-principles approach applies broadly across executory contracts; counterparties to debtor contracts should generally expect that their rights survive rejection, subject to any specific Bankruptcy Code provision that provides otherwise.
