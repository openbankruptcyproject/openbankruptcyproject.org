Till v. SCS Credit Corp.

541 U.S. 465 (2004)

Prime-plus formula for Chapter 13 cramdown interest rate

Facts

Lee and Amy Till purchased a used truck financed by SCS Credit at a 21 percent contract rate. They subsequently filed Chapter 13 and proposed a plan that retained the truck and paid SCS Credit the present value of its secured claim through plan payments. The plan proposed a 9.5 percent interest rate, computed as the national prime rate plus a 1.5 percent risk adjustment.

Procedural Posture

The Bankruptcy Court confirmed; the District Court reversed and adopted the contract rate; the Seventh Circuit affirmed the contract-rate approach. The Supreme Court granted certiorari to resolve a four-way circuit split over the appropriate methodology for setting a cramdown interest rate under 11 U.S.C. § 1325(a)(5)(B)(ii).

Issue Presented

What method should bankruptcy courts use to determine the appropriate interest rate to satisfy the requirement of 11 U.S.C. § 1325(a)(5)(B)(ii) that a Chapter 13 cramdown distribute property of a value 'not less than the allowed amount of such claim'?

Holding

A plurality of four Justices adopted a formula approach starting from the national prime rate and adjusting upward to account for the risk of nonpayment in the particular case. Justice Thomas concurred on different grounds — that the statute requires only the face amount of the claim, not a market-rate adjustment — but provided the fifth vote for reversal. The plurality's prime-plus methodology has become the operative standard in lower courts.

Key Reasoning

The plurality rejected the contract-rate, presumptive-contract-rate, and cost-of-funds approaches because each carried administrative costs and asymmetries between debtors and creditors. The prime rate is publicly available, reflects the financial market's view of risk-free lending, and can be adjusted upward modestly to account for the specific debtor's risk profile and the security's depreciation. The plurality suggested a 1-3 percent adjustment in most cases and placed the burden of justifying a higher rate on the creditor.

Statutes Interpreted

  • 11 U.S.C. § 1325(a)(5) — Confirmation of Chapter 13 plan; cramdown of secured claims

Post-Precedent Impact

Till is the dominant authority for cramdown interest rates in Chapter 13 and, by extension, in many Chapter 11 single-asset and small-business cases. Lower courts have extended the prime-plus formula to Chapter 11 in the absence of an efficient market for exit financing, while some circuits apply a market-rate inquiry when an efficient market exists (see In re Texas Grand Prairie Hotel Realty, 710 F.3d 324 (5th Cir. 2013)). Till also shapes Chapter 12 family-farmer cases and Subchapter V cramdown under § 1191(b)/(c).

Statutory Text and Structure

Section 1325(a)(5)(B)(ii) directs the court to confirm a plan if the secured creditor retains the lien and 'the value, as of the effective date of the plan, of property to be distributed under the plan' is not less than the allowed amount of the claim. The phrase 'value, as of the effective date' requires a present-value calculation, which in turn requires an interest rate. The statute does not specify which rate. Till's prime-plus formula fills that interpretive gap. Lower courts apply identical reasoning to § 1129(b)(2)(A)(i)(II) for Chapter 11 cramdowns.

Doctrinal Lineage

Before Till, lower courts used four distinct methodologies to set cramdown rates: the contract rate, the presumptive-contract-rate (a contract-rate starting point subject to adjustment), the cost-of-funds approach, and the formula approach. Each method had textual and policy adherents, producing inconsistent outcomes across districts and within the same circuit. Till resolved that variation in favor of an objective, replicable starting point. The decision built on the cramdown valuation framework of Associates Commercial Corp. v. Rash, 520 U.S. 953 (1997), which had similarly favored an objective, use-driven approach to collateral valuation.

Open Questions and Subsequent Developments

Till did not address how to compute interest when a debtor's plan defers payment over an unusually long period, when the collateral is particularly volatile, or when the secured creditor has provided post-petition financing on commercial terms. The Texas Grand Prairie efficient-market exception remains debated; some circuits limit it to Chapter 11 single-asset real estate cases, while others extend it more broadly. The interaction of Till with § 1129(b)(2)(A)(iii) (indubitable equivalent) was clarified in RadLAX for sale settings but remains contested for deferred-payment settings.

Current Practice

Bankruptcy courts apply Till's prime-plus formula across most Chapter 13 cramdowns, typically starting from the published prime rate and adjusting upward 1 to 3 percent based on the debtor's specific risk profile, the collateral type, and the projected length of plan payments. Several Chapter 11 cases — particularly single-asset real estate cases — have adopted Till as the default. The Fifth Circuit in In re Texas Grand Prairie Hotel Realty, 710 F.3d 324 (5th Cir. 2013), recognized a market-rate alternative when an efficient market for exit financing exists. The footnote-fourteen tension between the plurality and Justice Thomas continues to shape lower-court reasoning when the parties contest the appropriate methodology.

Broader Significance

Till sits at the intersection of finance theory and consumer-bankruptcy practice. The plurality's rejection of the contract-rate approach reflects a doctrinal preference for objective, market-independent inputs in cramdown calculations, while the formula approach's upward adjustment for risk accommodates the lender's legitimate interest in present-value compensation. The decision is also notable for what it does not do: it does not authorize a market-rate inquiry across the board, even when secondary markets for similar debt exist. The plurality's skepticism of market-rate proxies reflects a recognition that bankruptcy-context lending is structurally different from prime-rate lending. The footnote-fourteen discussion of an exception for Chapter 11 cases involving exit financing has generated significant downstream litigation about when, if ever, a market-rate departure from prime-plus is justified.

Practitioner Notes

Practitioners proposing a Chapter 13 cramdown rate typically start with the published Wall Street Journal prime rate and add 1 to 3 percent depending on the collateral's depreciation profile, the plan length, and the debtor's repayment history. Cramdown disputes often turn on the precise risk-adjustment figure rather than on the methodology. In Chapter 11, debtors and lenders sometimes negotiate a hybrid rate that splits the difference between prime-plus and a documented market rate. The Texas Grand Prairie efficient-market exception is invoked sparingly and typically requires concrete evidence of a functioning secondary market.
