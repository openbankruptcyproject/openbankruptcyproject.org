Bullard v. Blue Hills Bank

575 U.S. 496 (2015)

Order denying confirmation of a Chapter 13 plan is not a final, appealable order

Facts

Louis Bullard proposed a Chapter 13 plan that bifurcated Blue Hills Bank's claim. The bankruptcy court denied confirmation but gave Bullard leave to file an amended plan. Bullard sought to appeal the denial under 28 U.S.C. § 158(a) as a final order; the Bankruptcy Appellate Panel agreed to hear the appeal but ruled against Bullard on the merits. The First Circuit dismissed the appeal for lack of finality.

Procedural Posture

The First Circuit dismissed the appeal as taken from a non-final order. The Supreme Court granted certiorari to resolve a circuit split over whether plan-confirmation denials are immediately appealable.

Issue Presented

Whether an order denying confirmation of a Chapter 13 plan, but allowing the debtor to file an amended plan, is a final order subject to immediate appeal under 28 U.S.C. § 158(a)(1).

Holding

An order denying plan confirmation without dismissal of the case is not a final order under § 158(a)(1). The relevant 'proceeding' for finality analysis is the entire process of attempting to obtain plan confirmation, not the individual order on a single proposed plan.

Key Reasoning

Chief Justice Roberts, writing for a unanimous Court, applied the conventional rule that finality requires resolution of a discrete dispute. A denial that leaves the debtor free to amend simply continues the confirmation process. Allowing appeals of every denial would slow Chapter 13 administration and incentivize strategic plan-proposal behavior. Appellate review remains available after a final outcome — confirmation of an amended plan, dismissal of the case, or conversion — or through discretionary interlocutory routes under § 158(a)(3).

Statutes Interpreted

  • 28 U.S.C. § 158(a) — Appellate jurisdiction in bankruptcy

Post-Precedent Impact

Bullard cleanly resolved the finality question for plan-confirmation denials. Practitioners now know that a strategic appeal of a denied plan generally requires waiting for a final disposition or seeking interlocutory permission. The decision applies with equal force to Chapter 11 and Subchapter V plan-confirmation denials by analogy, and many courts apply Bullard's logic to denials of other interlocutory bankruptcy orders that do not finally resolve a discrete dispute.

Statutory Text and Structure

28 U.S.C. § 158(a) provides that the district courts have jurisdiction over appeals from '(1) final judgments, orders, and decrees... (2) interlocutory orders and decrees... [under specific statutory provisions], and (3) with leave of the court, from other interlocutory orders and decrees.' Bullard analyzed whether plan-confirmation denials fall within paragraph (1). The Court answered no, framing the 'proceeding' for finality purposes as the entire plan-confirmation process, not the individual plan-confirmation hearing.

Doctrinal Lineage

Bullard sits within the broader framework established by Howard Delivery Service v. Zurich American Insurance Co., 547 U.S. 651 (2006), and other decisions that read § 158(a) and analogous appellate-jurisdiction provisions to require a discrete-dispute-resolved framing of finality. The decision is also consistent with the general federal preference against piecemeal appeals articulated in cases such as Mohawk Industries, Inc. v. Carpenter, 558 U.S. 100 (2009).

Open Questions and Subsequent Developments

Bullard left open the appropriate framework for orders that fall between clearly final (confirmation or dismissal) and clearly non-final (mere denial). Some courts have continued to entertain appeals from orders denying confirmation where the order's practical effect is to terminate the case — for example, where the debtor cannot propose a plan that addresses the court's stated objection. The decision also did not address Chapter 11 plan-denial appeals, although lower courts have generally extended its logic by analogy.

Current Practice

Practitioners contemplating an appeal of a plan-related ruling now consider three paths: (1) await final disposition of the case through confirmation, dismissal, or conversion; (2) seek leave for interlocutory appeal under § 158(a)(3) (rarely granted); or (3) accept the denial and propose an amended plan, preserving the substantive issue for later appeal if it recurs. The decision has reduced appellate-court intervention in plan-confirmation matters and has encouraged debtor-creditor negotiation rather than appellate litigation.

Broader Significance

Bullard provides a clean answer to a recurring procedural question in Chapter 13 practice and reflects the Court's general preference against piecemeal appellate review. The decision encourages debtors and creditors to negotiate plan modifications at the trial-court level rather than to litigate denials in the appellate courts. The decision also clarifies the proper framing of 'proceeding' for finality analysis: the relevant unit is the entire process of attempting confirmation, not the individual order on a single proposed plan. This framing has been applied by analogy to other bankruptcy procedural contexts where finality issues arise.

Practitioner Notes

Practitioners receiving an unfavorable plan-confirmation ruling should evaluate three paths: amending the plan to address the court's stated objection, seeking discretionary interlocutory appeal under section 158(a)(3), or waiting for a final order through dismissal, conversion, or confirmation of an amended plan. The first path is most often productive; the bankruptcy court's stated objection typically identifies a specific issue that can be addressed in an amended plan. Discretionary appeal is rarely granted and typically requires demonstration that the issue presents a controlling question of law that will materially advance the case.
