Ransom v. FIA Card Services, N.A.

562 U.S. 61 (2011)

Vehicle-ownership deduction available only when debtor has a loan or lease

Facts

Jason Ransom filed Chapter 13. He owned his vehicle outright — no loan, no lease. On the means test he nevertheless took the IRS Local Standards 'Ownership Cost' deduction for one car, which substantially reduced his projected disposable income and the proposed plan payment. A credit-card creditor objected.

Procedural Posture

The Bankruptcy Court sustained the objection; the Bankruptcy Appellate Panel and the Ninth Circuit affirmed. The Supreme Court granted certiorari to resolve a circuit split.

Issue Presented

Whether a Chapter 13 debtor who owns a vehicle free and clear of any loan or lease obligation may nevertheless claim the IRS Local Standards 'Ownership Cost' deduction on the means test under 11 U.S.C. § 707(b)(2) (applied through § 1325(b)(3)).

Holding

No. An 8-1 majority held that a debtor with no loan or lease obligation on a vehicle is not entitled to the Ownership Cost deduction because no such expense is 'applicable' to the debtor.

Key Reasoning

Justice Kagan, writing for the majority, focused on the statutory text directing use of the IRS Local Standards 'applicable monthly expense amounts.' The IRS itself uses Ownership Cost to capture vehicle-loan and lease payments; a debtor who has no such payment has no 'applicable' Ownership Cost expense. Allowing the deduction would create an artificial offset divorced from any actual or expected outlay. The decision pairs with Lanning's forward-looking approach to projected disposable income to constrain means-test artifice.

Statutes Interpreted

  • 11 U.S.C. § 707(b)(2) — Means test for presumption of abuse
  • 11 U.S.C. § 1325(b)(3) — Chapter 13 disposable-income calculation

Post-Precedent Impact

Ransom is the operative authority on the means-test Ownership Cost deduction. Debtors with paid-off vehicles cannot take the deduction; debtors who have made the final loan payment during or shortly before the case generally cannot either. The decision also shapes the methodology for evaluating other means-test deductions: courts ask whether the deduction is 'applicable' to the debtor's actual circumstances. Ransom is regularly cited in Chapter 7 abuse and Chapter 13 confirmation litigation.

Statutory Text and Structure

Section 707(b)(2)(A)(ii)(I) provides that the debtor's monthly expenses 'shall be the debtor's applicable monthly expense amounts specified under the National Standards and Local Standards' issued by the IRS. The word 'applicable' is the textual hook. The IRS Collection Financial Standards distinguish Operating Costs (fuel, maintenance, insurance, registration) from Ownership Costs (loan or lease payments) for vehicles. A debtor who owns a vehicle outright has 'applicable' Operating Costs but no 'applicable' Ownership Costs.

Doctrinal Lineage

BAPCPA incorporated the IRS Collection Financial Standards into the means test in 2005. The interaction between those administrative standards and the statutory directive to use 'applicable' amounts produced immediate disputes. The Ninth Circuit's approach in Ransom prevailed and was joined by the Fifth, Seventh, and Eighth Circuits before the Supreme Court resolved the question. The decision pairs with Lanning to constrain artificial inputs into the disposable-income calculation from both the income and expense sides.

Open Questions and Subsequent Developments

Ransom addressed only the Ownership Cost deduction. Subsequent cases have applied the 'applicable' framework to other deductions — secured-debt payments under § 707(b)(2)(A)(iii), priority-claim allowances under § 707(b)(2)(A)(iv), and special-circumstances deductions under § 707(b)(2)(B). The decision did not address whether a debtor about to receive a vehicle through a pending purchase or financing can claim the deduction in anticipation. Courts have generally required actual existing obligations rather than anticipated ones.

Current Practice

Means-test practitioners now distinguish carefully between the Operating Cost deduction (available whenever the debtor owns or operates a vehicle) and the Ownership Cost deduction (available only when the debtor has a loan or lease payment). Debtors who pay off a vehicle shortly before filing should expect the Ownership Cost deduction to be challenged. The Ransom 'applicable' framework also informs litigation over other means-test deductions and the broader interpretation of § 707(b)(2)(A)(ii)(I).

Broader Significance

Ransom is part of the broader post-BAPCPA framework that constrains both income and expense inputs to the means test. The decision's 'applicable' framework provides a workable test for evaluating which IRS Local Standards line items a particular debtor can claim. The decision's reasoning has been extended to a range of other means-test deduction disputes and has shaped the contemporary understanding of how to apply administrative standards within a statutory framework. The decision pairs naturally with Lanning to ensure that the means test produces results that reflect the debtor's actual economic situation rather than artificial inputs.

Practitioner Notes

Debtor's counsel evaluating the means-test Ownership Cost deduction should confirm that the debtor has an actual loan or lease payment as of the petition date. A loan paid off shortly before filing typically eliminates eligibility for the deduction. The Operating Cost deduction remains available whenever the debtor owns or operates a vehicle, regardless of whether there is a loan or lease payment. Means-test disputes often turn on small line items that have outsized effects on plan payments; careful documentation of each deduction supports successful confirmation.
