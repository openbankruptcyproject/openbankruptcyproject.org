Florida Department of Revenue v. Piccadilly Cafeterias, Inc.

554 U.S. 33 (2008)

Section 1146(a) stamp-tax exemption available only after plan confirmation

Facts

Piccadilly Cafeterias sold substantially all of its assets before plan confirmation in a Chapter 11 case and sought to invoke the stamp-tax exemption of § 1146(a) (then § 1146(c)). The Florida Department of Revenue asserted that the exemption applied only to transfers made after confirmation of a Chapter 11 plan.

Procedural Posture

The Bankruptcy Court and Eleventh Circuit ruled for Piccadilly; the Florida Department of Revenue petitioned for certiorari. The Supreme Court granted review to resolve a circuit split.

Issue Presented

Whether the stamp-tax exemption of 11 U.S.C. § 1146(a) — which exempts asset transfers made 'under a plan confirmed under section 1129' from any stamp or similar tax — applies to transfers made before plan confirmation.

Holding

Section 1146(a) applies only to transfers made under a plan that has already been confirmed. Pre-confirmation transfers — including those approved under § 363 — are not eligible for the stamp-tax exemption.

Key Reasoning

Justice Thomas, writing for a 7-2 majority, focused on the past-participle phrasing 'plan confirmed under section 1129.' The natural reading is that confirmation must precede the transfer. The Court applied the canon that tax exemptions should be construed narrowly in favor of the taxing authority. Allowing pre-confirmation transfers to qualify would dramatically expand the exemption's reach and impose significant fiscal cost on states.

Statutes Interpreted

  • 11 U.S.C. § 1146(a) — Stamp-tax exemption for transfers under a confirmed plan

Post-Precedent Impact

Piccadilly resolved a long-running circuit split that had encouraged Chapter 11 debtors to time asset sales to capture the stamp-tax exemption. The decision narrowed § 1146(a) and reduced the tax-arbitrage opportunities of going-concern sales. Practitioners structuring Chapter 11 363 sales must now factor state and local transfer taxes into pre-confirmation transactions. The decision also reflects the Court's broader textualist approach to bankruptcy tax issues.

Statutory Text and Structure

Section 1146(a) provides that 'the issuance, transfer, or exchange of a security, or the making or delivery of an instrument of transfer under a plan confirmed under section 1129 of this title, may not be taxed under any law imposing a stamp tax or similar tax.' The past-participle 'confirmed' is the textual hook. The exemption reaches stamp taxes, real-estate transfer taxes, mortgage recording taxes, and similar instruments-of-transfer taxes — but only when the transfer is made 'under' a plan that has already been confirmed.

Doctrinal Lineage

Before Piccadilly, the Third and Fourth Circuits had read § 1146(a) (then § 1146(c)) to permit pre-confirmation transfers when made in contemplation of a confirmed plan. The Eleventh Circuit had taken the broader view that ultimately prevailed in lower-court practice in Piccadilly. The Supreme Court applied the standard tax-exemption canon — narrow construction in favor of the taxing authority — that has shaped numerous federal tax-statute cases.

Open Questions and Subsequent Developments

Piccadilly did not address transfers that occur substantially contemporaneously with confirmation — for example, a closing that occurs minutes or hours after confirmation. Lower courts have generally permitted the exemption for transfers occurring after confirmation, even immediately so. The decision also did not address sales executed through a liquidating plan or a plan that exists solely to facilitate a previously completed sale; courts have varied in their willingness to treat such transactions as 'under' the plan.

Current Practice

After Piccadilly, Chapter 11 debtors routinely structure asset sales to occur either at or after plan confirmation when stamp-tax savings are economically significant. For 363 sales conducted before confirmation, parties budget for state and local transfer taxes that the § 1146(a) exemption no longer covers. In some cases, a debtor will defer closing on a pre-confirmation buyer until after confirmation to capture the exemption, with appropriate interim arrangements to bridge the gap. The decision also informs choice-of-state and venue considerations for tax-sensitive transactions.

Broader Significance

Piccadilly narrows section 1146(a) to its textual core. The decision reflects the Court's use of standard tax-exemption interpretive canons in bankruptcy contexts and aligns the federal stamp-tax exemption with state-revenue interests. The decision has practical consequences for the timing of asset sales in Chapter 11 cases: pre-confirmation 363 sales generally cannot capture the exemption, while post-confirmation transfers under a confirmed plan can. The decision also illustrates the Court's willingness to narrow bankruptcy provisions that have federal-state revenue implications when the statutory text supports the narrower reading.

Practitioner Notes

Counsel structuring asset sales in Chapter 11 cases should evaluate the stamp-tax exemption opportunity at the planning stage. Sales executed under a confirmed plan generally capture the section 1146(a) exemption; pre-confirmation 363 sales generally do not. Where stamp-tax savings are economically significant, parties sometimes structure the transaction to close after plan confirmation, with interim arrangements to bridge any gap between auction and closing. Choice-of-state and venue considerations may also affect transfer-tax exposure on pre-confirmation transactions. Real-estate-heavy reorganizations should account for state and local transfer taxes as a line item in feasibility analysis when pre-confirmation closing is anticipated.
