Bank of America Nat'l Trust & Sav. Ass'n v. 203 N. LaSalle Street Partnership

526 U.S. 434 (1999)

New-value corollary to the absolute-priority rule

Facts

An over-encumbered partnership's reorganization plan proposed that the existing equity partners would contribute new capital in exchange for the exclusive right to receive the reorganized debtor's equity. The bank held a roughly $93 million claim against the property; the partnership's plan attempted to confirm over the bank's dissent by giving partners — and only partners — the option to purchase the equity. No competing bid or market test was offered.

Procedural Posture

The Bankruptcy Court confirmed the plan; the District Court and the Seventh Circuit affirmed. The Supreme Court granted certiorari to resolve a circuit split over whether the new-value corollary to the absolute-priority rule survived enactment of the 1978 Bankruptcy Code, and if so, whether it permits old equity to retain ownership in exchange for new contributions without exposing the plan to market testing.

Issue Presented

Whether a Chapter 11 plan may be confirmed over the objection of a senior unsecured creditor when the debtor's prepetition equity holders are given an exclusive opportunity to obtain the reorganized debtor's equity in exchange for a new-value contribution.

Holding

No. The exclusive opportunity given to old equity, without any market test or competing-bidder process, violated the absolute-priority rule codified at 11 U.S.C. § 1129(b)(2)(B)(ii). The Court did not decide whether a market-tested new-value contribution can ever save a plan from the absolute-priority bar, leaving that question for another day.

Key Reasoning

Justice Souter, writing for the majority, traced the doctrinal history of the new-value corollary back to Case v. Los Angeles Lumber Products Co., 308 U.S. 106 (1939). Even assuming the corollary survived, the plan failed because old equity received something of value — the exclusive option to acquire the reorganized debtor's equity — solely by reason of their prior equity position. That exclusivity meant the property's true value to old equity could not be tested against the market. The Court emphasized that 'on account of' in § 1129(b)(2)(B)(ii) reaches benefits received by virtue of prior junior status, not only direct distributions of cash or stock.

Statutes Interpreted

  • 11 U.S.C. § 1129(b) — Confirmation of plan; cramdown

Post-Precedent Impact

LaSalle is the foundational modern decision on the absolute-priority rule in Chapter 11. Any plan that allows old equity to retain or reacquire interests over a dissenting senior unsecured class must be carefully structured to permit a genuine market test — typically a competitive bidding procedure, an auction, or open solicitation. The decision drives the structure of many single-asset real estate and small-business reorganizations and is repeatedly invoked when courts evaluate § 1129(b) cramdowns. In Subchapter V, Congress dispensed with the absolute-priority rule entirely under § 1191(b), which sidesteps the LaSalle problem for eligible small-business debtors.

Statutory Text and Structure

Section 1129(b)(2)(B) provides that the cramdown 'fair and equitable' standard requires either (i) full payment to the unsecured class, or (ii) that the holder of any junior claim or interest 'will not receive or retain under the plan on account of such junior claim or interest any property.' LaSalle's interpretive heart is the phrase 'on account of': the exclusive option to acquire the reorganized equity, even if formally separate from the prepetition equity interest, was received 'on account of' that interest because the option was offered only to old equity. The Court left open whether a properly market-tested contribution could escape the 'on account of' bar.

Doctrinal Lineage

The absolute-priority rule traces to nineteenth-century railroad equity receiverships. Northern Pacific Railway Co. v. Boyd, 228 U.S. 482 (1913), articulated the principle that junior interests cannot retain value while senior creditors remain unpaid. Case v. Los Angeles Lumber Products Co., 308 U.S. 106 (1939), recognized a narrow new-value exception. The 1978 Code's enactment of § 1129(b)(2)(B) codified the absolute-priority rule but was silent on whether the Case exception survived. LaSalle did not formally answer the survival question but constrained any plausible new-value pathway with the market-test requirement.

Open Questions and Subsequent Developments

LaSalle deliberately reserved the question of whether the new-value corollary survives the 1978 Code at all. Lower courts have continued to recognize a narrow corollary, but the contours remain unsettled: must the contribution be substantial, necessary, new, and money or money's worth, as Case suggested? Must it be reasonably equivalent to what old equity receives? How are the market-testing requirements practically satisfied — by formal auction, by competitive bidding under the plan, by termination of exclusivity under § 1121(b), or by some other procedure? Most circuits have indicated that termination of exclusivity, allowing any party to propose a competing plan, satisfies the market-test concern.

Current Practice

Chapter 11 plans that allow existing equity to retain or reacquire interests over a dissenting unsecured class now typically incorporate competitive procedures: open auction, broad solicitation, or a stalking-horse process designed to invite competing bids. Plans without such procedures face substantial confirmation risk. Several lower courts have continued to recognize the new-value corollary when contributions are genuine, market-tested, and exceed what old equity receives. The decision also drove the structural innovation behind Subchapter V's § 1191(b)/(c) approach, which expressly displaces the absolute-priority rule for eligible small-business debtors.

Broader Significance

LaSalle sits at the intersection of corporate-reorganization theory and the practical machinery of Chapter 11. The decision reflects an institutional preference for market-tested outcomes when distributional questions cannot otherwise be resolved through arms-length negotiation. Bankruptcy economists have noted that the market-test requirement creates an option-pricing dynamic: existing equity, by retaining the right to bid, retains an embedded call option whose value can be priced through competitive procedure. The decision also illustrates the Court's willingness to treat the absolute-priority rule as the structural backbone of Chapter 11 rather than as a default subject to creative workaround. Practitioners structuring single-asset real estate plans, family-business succession plans, and middle-market reorganizations all build LaSalle into their plan architecture from the outset. The decision continues to anchor academic debate over the proper role of equity holders in corporate reorganization and over the relationship between bankruptcy and corporate-finance theory more broadly.

Practitioner Notes

Counsel proposing a Chapter 11 plan in which old equity retains or reacquires an interest should incorporate, at minimum, one of: (i) termination of exclusivity under section 1121(b) before the plan goes to vote, allowing any party to propose a competing plan; (ii) an auction procedure for the equity itself with notice to all parties in interest; or (iii) a clear demonstration that the plan satisfies the absolute-priority rule independent of the new-value contribution. The single-asset-real-estate context generates the largest volume of LaSalle litigation. Bankruptcy courts evaluate the substance and adequacy of any proposed market test on a case-by-case basis.
