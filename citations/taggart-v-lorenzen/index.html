Taggart v. Lorenzen

587 U.S. 554 (2019)

Objective fair-ground-of-doubt standard for discharge-injunction contempt

Facts

Bradford Taggart received a Chapter 7 discharge. His former business partners then pursued — and obtained — a state-court attorney's-fee award against him in litigation that continued post-discharge. Taggart returned to bankruptcy court and sought civil contempt for violation of the § 524(a) discharge injunction.

Procedural Posture

The Bankruptcy Court held the creditors in contempt; the District Court vacated; the Ninth Circuit affirmed the vacatur using a subjective good-faith standard. The Supreme Court granted certiorari to resolve a circuit split over the standard for civil contempt of the discharge injunction.

Issue Presented

What is the appropriate standard for civil contempt for violation of the discharge injunction under 11 U.S.C. § 524(a) — strict liability, subjective good faith, or some objective test?

Holding

A unanimous Court adopted an objective standard: a court may hold a creditor in civil contempt for violating a discharge order if there is 'no fair ground of doubt' as to whether the order barred the creditor's conduct.

Key Reasoning

Justice Breyer drew on traditional principles of equity. Civil contempt is not a strict-liability sanction, but neither is it limited to willful or subjective bad-faith violations. The 'no fair ground of doubt' standard echoes the test articulated in California Artificial Stone Paving Co. v. Molitor, 113 U.S. 609 (1885), and balances the protection that the discharge injunction is intended to provide against the due-process concern of holding creditors liable for actions taken in good-faith uncertainty about the order's scope.

Statutes Interpreted

  • 11 U.S.C. § 524(a) — Effect of discharge; permanent injunction
  • 11 U.S.C. § 105(a) — Equitable contempt powers

Post-Precedent Impact

Taggart is now the operative standard for discharge-injunction contempt nationwide. Practitioners advising creditors must perform a careful pre-collection assessment whenever a debt may have been discharged; objective uncertainty supports a creditor's defense, but the standard does not require subjective certainty of violation. The decision shapes pre-collection due-diligence practices, particularly for institutional creditors with high collection volumes.

Statutory Text and Structure

Section 524(a)(2) provides that a discharge 'operates as an injunction against the commencement or continuation of an action, the employment of process, or an act, to collect, recover or offset any such debt as a personal liability of the debtor.' The injunction is automatic and self-executing; no separate court order is required. Section 105(a) supplies the bankruptcy court's authority to enforce the injunction through civil contempt. Taggart sets the standard for that contempt analysis.

Doctrinal Lineage

The civil-contempt standard articulated in Taggart drew on traditional equity doctrine dating to McComb v. Jacksonville Paper Co., 336 U.S. 187 (1949), and California Artificial Stone Paving Co. v. Molitor, 113 U.S. 609 (1885). The Ninth Circuit's subjective-good-faith approach had been an outlier. The decision parallels the Court's framing of automatic-stay violations under § 362(k), although the statutory text differs and the analyses are not coextensive.

Open Questions and Subsequent Developments

Taggart did not specify what monetary or non-monetary sanctions are appropriate when a violation is established. Lower courts have awarded actual damages (lost wages, emotional distress, attorney's fees) and, in some cases, punitive or coercive sanctions. The decision also did not resolve whether the Taggart standard applies to other bankruptcy injunctions — confirmation-order injunctions under § 1141, plan-implementation injunctions, or specific channeling injunctions in mass-tort cases. Most courts have applied Taggart's framework by analogy in those settings.

Current Practice

Creditors performing post-discharge collections analyze the discharge order's scope under the objective standard: would a reasonable creditor in the same position have grounds to doubt that the debt was discharged? Pre-collection compliance reviews now examine the schedules, the proof of claim, the plan, and the discharge order to determine whether any genuine doubt exists. Where doubt is genuine, motions for declaratory relief under Bankruptcy Rule 7001 are a safe alternative to collection action.

Broader Significance

Taggart establishes a workable standard for discharge-injunction enforcement that balances debtor protection against creditor due-process concerns. The decision's objective 'no fair ground of doubt' standard borrows from traditional equity doctrine and harmonizes the contempt analysis with the broader civil-contempt framework. The decision has practical implications for institutional creditors with high collection volumes who must develop systematic post-discharge collection-compliance procedures. The decision also reflects the Court's preference for objective tests over subjective good-faith inquiries in contexts where uniform application is administratively important.

Practitioner Notes

Creditors should perform a discharge-injunction compliance review before initiating any post-discharge collection activity. The review should identify the debt, confirm the date of discharge, identify any plan provisions affecting the debt, and assess whether the debt falls within any section 523(a) exception. Where the discharge analysis is uncertain, the safer course is a motion for declaratory relief or a stipulated determination of dischargeability. Continuing collection activity in the face of objective uncertainty risks contempt sanctions including actual damages, attorney's fees, and in some cases punitive sanctions. Institutional creditors should document their pre-collection analysis to support a fair-ground-of-doubt defense if the collection action is later challenged in bankruptcy court. Many courts will consider a creditor's documented good-faith compliance review favorably even when the underlying legal analysis turns out to be incorrect.
