City of Chicago v. Fulton

592 U.S. 154 (2021)

Mere retention of estate property does not violate § 362(a)(3)

Facts

The City of Chicago impounded vehicles belonging to debtors for unpaid parking fines. After each debtor filed Chapter 13, the City refused to return the vehicles absent payment or court order, asserting a possessory lien. The debtors moved for sanctions, arguing that the City's continued retention of the vehicles violated 11 U.S.C. § 362(a)(3) — which stays any act to obtain possession of or exercise control over estate property.

Procedural Posture

The Bankruptcy Court ordered turnover and sanctioned the City; the Seventh Circuit affirmed. The Supreme Court granted certiorari to resolve a circuit split over whether passive retention of property seized pre-petition violates § 362(a)(3).

Issue Presented

Whether the mere retention of estate property after a bankruptcy petition is filed — without any affirmative act — violates the automatic stay imposed by 11 U.S.C. § 362(a)(3).

Holding

Mere retention of estate property does not, by itself, violate § 362(a)(3). The provision stays affirmative acts to obtain possession of or exercise control over estate property, not the maintenance of a status quo possession that existed at the petition date. A debtor seeking the return of property must invoke the turnover provisions of 11 U.S.C. § 542.

Key Reasoning

Justice Alito, writing for a unanimous Court (Sotomayor, J., concurring), parsed the statute's language: 'any act to obtain possession of property of the estate or of property from the estate or to exercise control over property of the estate.' The word 'act' implies an affirmative step. Reading § 362(a)(3) to require active turnover would create surplusage with § 542(a). The decision did not address whether retention might violate other subsections of § 362(a) or implicate other Code provisions. Justice Sotomayor's concurrence emphasized that creditor inaction can impose substantial hardship and urged Congress and the Rules Committee to address the resulting gaps.

Statutes Interpreted

  • 11 U.S.C. § 362(a)(3) — Automatic stay; acts to obtain possession or exercise control
  • 11 U.S.C. § 542(a) — Turnover of property of the estate

Post-Precedent Impact

Fulton substantially reshaped post-petition recovery of estate property. Debtors must now affirmatively pursue turnover under § 542 rather than relying on stay-violation sanctions to force return. Many courts have developed expedited turnover procedures in response. The decision particularly affects auto-impound cases, towed-vehicle disputes, and seized-business-asset cases. Some debtors have shifted toward § 362(a)(6) (act to collect a pre-petition claim) theories where applicable.

Statutory Text and Structure

Section 362(a)(3) stays 'any act to obtain possession of property of the estate or of property from the estate or to exercise control over property of the estate.' The grammatical structure is verb-driven: 'act to obtain' or 'act to exercise control.' Section 542(a) separately requires that an entity in possession of estate property 'shall deliver to the trustee, and account for, such property or the value of such property.' Fulton held that § 362(a)(3) addresses affirmative steps to acquire or assert control, while § 542 addresses the post-petition duty to return property.

Doctrinal Lineage

Before Fulton, the majority of circuits — including the Second, Seventh, Eighth, Ninth, and Eleventh — held that retention violated § 362(a)(3). The Tenth and D.C. Circuits had taken the contrary view. Fulton adopted the minority position, drawing on the textual distinction between 'act' in § 362(a)(3) and the affirmative turnover duty of § 542. The decision continues the Court's trend toward narrow textual readings of bankruptcy stay provisions, as seen in Citizens Bank of Maryland v. Strumpf, 516 U.S. 16 (1995).

Open Questions and Subsequent Developments

Fulton expressly reserved questions about other stay subsections. Section 362(a)(4) (lien creation), § 362(a)(5) (lien enforcement against the debtor or estate), and § 362(a)(6) (collection of pre-petition claims) may apply to retained property in particular circumstances. The decision also did not address penalties for failing to comply with a turnover order under § 542, which can be substantial. Lower courts have developed varied approaches to expedited turnover, sometimes incorporating Section 362(a)(6) theories when the retention has the practical effect of compelling payment of a pre-petition debt.

Current Practice

Debtors seeking return of seized property must now affirmatively pursue turnover under § 542. Several courts have developed expedited turnover procedures — informal teleconferences, emergency orders, and standing orders requiring response within days. Practitioners pair turnover motions with stay-extension or § 362(a)(6) arguments where applicable. The decision has had particular impact on auto-impound, towed-vehicle, and seized-business-asset disputes, where time-to-recovery has real economic consequences for the debtor's reorganization or fresh start.

Broader Significance

Fulton reflects the Court's textualist approach to bankruptcy stay provisions. The decision reads section 362(a)(3) narrowly and channels post-petition recovery disputes into the turnover machinery of section 542. The decision has practical consequences for time-sensitive debtor needs — auto-impound cases, business-asset seizures, and similar scenarios where prompt recovery is essential to the debtor's reorganization or fresh start. Lower courts have responded by developing expedited turnover procedures that can produce relief in days rather than weeks. The decision also illustrates the institutional preference for clear textual boundaries between related statutory provisions.

Practitioner Notes

Debtor's counsel should be prepared to file an expedited turnover motion under section 542 immediately upon learning of any creditor's possession of estate property. The motion should include evidence of the debtor's interest in the property, the creditor's possession, and any time-sensitive economic hardship the continued retention imposes. Many courts will schedule emergency hearings on adequately supported turnover motions. Pairing the turnover motion with section 362(a)(6) arguments (where the retention has the practical effect of coercing payment of a pre-petition debt) may strengthen the request and broaden the available relief.
