RadLAX Gateway Hotel, LLC v. Amalgamated Bank

566 U.S. 639 (2012)

Secured-creditor right to credit bid in sales under a plan

Facts

RadLAX Gateway Hotel proposed a Chapter 11 plan that sold its hotel free and clear of liens under § 1123(a)(5), but explicitly barred the secured lender from credit bidding at the auction. The debtor sought confirmation by arguing that the indubitable-equivalent prong of § 1129(b)(2)(A)(iii) authorized the no-credit-bid sale without satisfying the credit-bid protections of § 1129(b)(2)(A)(ii).

Procedural Posture

The Bankruptcy Court denied confirmation; the Seventh Circuit affirmed. The Supreme Court granted certiorari to resolve a circuit split: the Third and Fifth Circuits had previously permitted sales over a credit-bid objection by relying on the indubitable-equivalent prong.

Issue Presented

Whether a Chapter 11 debtor may confirm a plan that sells the secured creditor's collateral free and clear of liens without permitting the secured creditor to credit bid, by invoking the indubitable-equivalent prong of 11 U.S.C. § 1129(b)(2)(A)(iii).

Holding

A unanimous Court held that a sale of encumbered property under a Chapter 11 plan must comply with § 1129(b)(2)(A)(ii) — which incorporates the credit-bid right of § 363(k) — and cannot proceed under the indubitable-equivalent prong of § 1129(b)(2)(A)(iii).

Key Reasoning

Justice Scalia applied the canon that the specific governs the general. Section 1129(b)(2)(A)(ii) addresses precisely the situation of a sale of collateral under a plan, and Congress incorporated the § 363(k) credit-bid right into that prong. Allowing debtors to bypass clause (ii) by recasting a sale under clause (iii) would render the credit-bid protection a nullity. The indubitable-equivalent clause governs alternatives to retention or sale — not sales themselves.

Statutes Interpreted

  • 11 U.S.C. § 1129(b)(2)(A) — Fair and equitable test for secured claims
  • 11 U.S.C. § 363(k) — Right to credit bid at sale of collateral

Post-Precedent Impact

RadLAX is the principal modern authority on the secured-creditor credit-bid right in Chapter 11. It applies equally to sales of personal property and real property and extends to Subchapter V cramdown under § 1191(c) by incorporation of § 1129(b)(2)(A). Practitioners structuring 363 sales and plan sales reference RadLAX to confirm that a secured creditor's credit-bid right is preserved absent cause to limit it under § 363(k).

Statutory Text and Structure

Section 1129(b)(2)(A) sets out three alternative ways a plan may treat a secured class to satisfy the fair-and-equitable standard: (i) deferred cash payments equal to the present value of the collateral with retention of the lien; (ii) sale of the collateral free and clear with the lien attaching to the proceeds and the secured creditor's right to credit bid under § 363(k) preserved; or (iii) realization by the secured creditor of the indubitable equivalent of its claim. RadLAX held that a sale must proceed under clause (ii), with its credit-bid protection, rather than under clause (iii).

Doctrinal Lineage

RadLAX resolved a circuit split that began with In re Philadelphia Newspapers, LLC, 599 F.3d 298 (3d Cir. 2010), which had permitted no-credit-bid plan sales. The Fifth Circuit reached a similar result in In re Pacific Lumber Co., 584 F.3d 229 (5th Cir. 2009). The Seventh Circuit's decision in In re River Road Hotel Partners, 651 F.3d 642 (7th Cir. 2011), created the conflict that RadLAX resolved. The Court relied on the specific/general statutory canon, a tool used in many contemporary Code-interpretation cases.

Open Questions and Subsequent Developments

RadLAX left open what showing of 'cause' under § 363(k) might justify limiting the credit-bid right at a plan-sale auction. Lower courts have rarely found cause, although some have done so in cases involving alleged inequitable conduct by the secured creditor, disputes about lien validity, or proof that the credit-bid right would chill bidding. The decision also did not address how to handle plan sales where multiple secured creditors hold liens of different priority on the same collateral; cross-collateralization issues remain a frequent subject of credit-bid litigation.

Current Practice

After RadLAX, a Chapter 11 plan that sells encumbered property must allow the secured creditor to credit bid unless cause exists to limit the right under § 363(k). Cause is rarely found; the secured creditor's structural protection of credit bidding is now treated as nearly inviolate. Practitioners structuring Chapter 11 going-concern sales accordingly design the auction format to accommodate a credit bid. The doctrine extends to Subchapter V cramdown sales by incorporation of § 1129(b)(2)(A) through § 1191(c).

Broader Significance

RadLAX entrenches the credit-bid right as a structural feature of Chapter 11 cramdown sales. The decision reflects a broader doctrinal commitment to protecting secured-creditor expectations in reorganization, consistent with the Court's decisions in BFP v. Resolution Trust Corp., 511 U.S. 531 (1994), and other secured-creditor protection cases. The decision also illustrates the Court's use of the specific-governs-general canon as an interpretive tool in bankruptcy contexts. Subsequent decisions in the Court and lower courts have continued to refine the credit-bid framework, particularly with respect to the rare 'cause' showings that might justify limiting the right under section 363(k).

Practitioner Notes

Counsel structuring a Chapter 11 plan sale should incorporate a clear credit-bid procedure consistent with section 363(k). The bid procedures should permit credit bidding up to the full amount of the secured claim, address treatment of disputed lien claims, and provide a clear mechanism for resolving credit-bid eligibility. Cases involving alleged inequitable conduct by the secured creditor — overreaching, conflicts of interest, or pre-petition misconduct — may justify limiting the credit-bid right, but the threshold is high. Documented evidence of misconduct, not mere allegations, is typically required.
