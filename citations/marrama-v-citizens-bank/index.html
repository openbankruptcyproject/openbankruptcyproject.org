Marrama v. Citizens Bank of Massachusetts

549 U.S. 365 (2007)

Bad-faith bar to Chapter 13 conversion

Facts

Robert Marrama filed Chapter 7 and made several material misrepresentations and omissions in his schedules concerning a Maine property and a revocable trust. After the trustee uncovered the concealment and signaled an intention to recover the property for the estate, Marrama moved to convert to Chapter 13 under 11 U.S.C. § 706(a). The trustee and Citizens Bank opposed conversion on the ground that bad faith had been established and conversion would frustrate the estate's recovery.

Procedural Posture

The Bankruptcy Court denied conversion; the Bankruptcy Appellate Panel affirmed; the First Circuit affirmed. The Supreme Court granted certiorari to resolve a circuit split over whether § 706(a)'s right to convert is absolute or qualified by good faith.

Issue Presented

Whether 11 U.S.C. § 706(a)'s provision that a Chapter 7 debtor 'may convert' to another chapter creates an absolute right to convert, or whether bankruptcy courts may deny conversion on a finding of bad-faith conduct.

Holding

In a 5-4 decision, the Court held that the right to convert under § 706(a) is qualified, not absolute. Bankruptcy courts may deny conversion where the debtor has engaged in bad-faith conduct that would justify dismissal or reconversion under § 1307(c).

Key Reasoning

Justice Stevens reasoned that any Chapter 13 case can be converted or dismissed for cause under § 1307(c), and 'cause' includes bad faith. Requiring the bankruptcy court to grant conversion only to immediately reconvert or dismiss would elevate form over substance. The bankruptcy court has the authority under § 105(a) and the implicit limitations on § 706(a) to deny the conversion in the first instance. The dissent (Alito, J., joined by Roberts, Scalia, and Thomas) read § 706(a) as unambiguous and the bad-faith carve-out as an unwarranted judicial gloss.

Statutes Interpreted

  • 11 U.S.C. § 706(a) — Conversion of Chapter 7 to another chapter
  • 11 U.S.C. § 1307(c) — Dismissal or conversion of Chapter 13
  • 11 U.S.C. § 105(a) — General equitable powers of the bankruptcy court

Post-Precedent Impact

Marrama is the cornerstone bad-faith doctrine in modern bankruptcy practice. Trustees and creditors regularly cite Marrama when opposing conversion or seeking dismissal in cases involving asset concealment, serial filings, or schedule misrepresentations. The decision also informs Chapter 7 dismissal jurisprudence under § 707(b) and § 707(a) and provides a doctrinal foundation for the contemporary judicial use of § 105(a) to police debtor conduct.

Statutory Text and Structure

Section 706(a) provides that 'the debtor may convert a case under this chapter to a case under chapter 11, 12, or 13 of this title at any time, if the case has not been converted under section 1112, 1208, or 1307 of this title.' The text contains no explicit good-faith qualifier. The Marrama Court read the limitation in as a structural inference from § 1307(c), which permits dismissal or conversion of a Chapter 13 case 'for cause' — cause that includes bad-faith conduct. The Court framed the Bankruptcy Code as a unified set of provisions whose individual sections must be read in light of the whole.

Doctrinal Lineage

Before Marrama, circuit courts disagreed on whether § 706(a)'s 'may convert' language created an absolute right. The Sixth and Eighth Circuits had read the right as absolute; the First, Fifth, and Ninth Circuits had imposed a good-faith qualification. The decision drew on the broader bad-faith jurisprudence developed under § 1325(a)(3) (good-faith plan proposal) and § 1307(c) (dismissal for cause). It also harmonized § 706(a) with the established discretion of bankruptcy courts under § 105(a) to police debtor conduct.

Open Questions and Subsequent Developments

Marrama did not provide a definitive list of conduct that constitutes bad faith for purposes of § 706(a) conversion denial. Lower courts have applied multi-factor tests drawn from § 1307(c) jurisprudence and from In re Lilley, 91 F.3d 491 (3d Cir. 1996). The decision also did not specify whether the burden of proof is on the trustee/creditor (asserting bad faith) or the debtor (defending conversion); the prevailing view places the initial burden on the objecting party but requires the debtor to rebut a prima facie case of bad faith with credible evidence.

Current Practice

Marrama is invoked routinely when a Chapter 7 debtor seeks to convert in response to trustee or creditor pressure. Common bad-faith fact patterns include serial filings, asset concealment, schedule misrepresentations, post-petition transfers, and conversions designed to delay or hinder collection. Courts often resolve Marrama motions on an evidentiary record developed at the § 341 meeting and through Rule 2004 examinations. The doctrine extends by analogy to Chapter 11 to Chapter 7 reconversions and to dismissal motions for cause under § 707(a) and § 1112(b).

Broader Significance

Marrama embodies the institutional view that bankruptcy relief depends on debtor good faith. The decision is part of a broader doctrinal arc — including the discharge-denial provisions of section 727(a), the dismissal-for-cause provisions of section 707(b)(3) and section 1112(b), and the good-faith plan-proposal requirement of section 1325(a)(3) — that conditions bankruptcy relief on debtor candor and fair dealing. The 5-4 division illustrates the textualist and purposivist split that runs through modern Bankruptcy Code interpretation. The majority's purposivist approach has prevailed in subsequent cases involving bad-faith refilings, asset concealment, and conversion strategies, while the dissent's textualism has informed subsequent textualist decisions in other Code contexts.

Practitioner Notes

Trustees opposing conversion typically build the bad-faith record through the section 341 meeting, Rule 2004 examinations, and review of the debtor's pre-petition financial activity. The evidentiary record need not establish criminal intent; a pattern of material omissions, post-petition transfers, or schedule misrepresentations often suffices. Debtors facing a Marrama challenge should be prepared to present evidence that the conversion is bona fide — typically through documentation of changed circumstances, settled disputes with creditors, and a credible Chapter 13 plan proposal. Conversion denials are appealable but rarely overturned absent clear error in the bankruptcy court's fact-finding.
