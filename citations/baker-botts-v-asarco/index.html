Baker Botts L.L.P. v. ASARCO LLC

576 U.S. 121 (2015)

No fee-defense fees under § 330(a)(1)

Facts

Baker Botts represented ASARCO as debtor's counsel in a Chapter 11 case that yielded a substantial recovery for the estate. The firm filed a fee application; ASARCO (after emergence) objected to the application. The bankruptcy court awarded the firm its underlying fees and also fees it incurred defending the fee application against ASARCO's objections. The estate appealed the fee-defense award.

Procedural Posture

The Bankruptcy Court awarded fee-defense fees; the District Court affirmed; the Fifth Circuit reversed. The Supreme Court granted certiorari.

Issue Presented

Whether 11 U.S.C. § 330(a)(1) authorizes a bankruptcy court to award attorney's fees to a professional employed under § 327 for time spent defending the professional's fee application.

Holding

Section 330(a)(1) does not authorize fee-defense fees. The statute permits compensation for services rendered to the estate, and fee-defense work is rendered to the professional, not the estate.

Key Reasoning

Justice Thomas, writing for a 6-3 majority, applied the American Rule: parties bear their own attorney's fees absent a clear statutory exception. Section 330(a)(1) compensates 'reasonable compensation for actual, necessary services rendered.' Fee-defense work benefits the professional, not the estate, and falls outside the statutory grant. The Court rejected the argument that § 330(a)(6) authorizes fee-defense fees because that subsection concerns 'preparation' of a fee application — a distinct activity. The dissent (Breyer, J., joined by Ginsburg and Kagan) argued the practical effect would be to reduce qualified counsel's willingness to represent estates against difficult parties.

Statutes Interpreted

  • 11 U.S.C. § 330(a)(1) — Compensation of professionals

Post-Precedent Impact

ASARCO has substantially changed the economics of fee disputes in Chapter 11. Professionals must absorb the costs of defending against objections — whether well-founded or not. Some jurisdictions have responded with engagement-letter provisions purporting to require the estate or debtor to pay fee-defense costs, but the enforceability of those provisions remains contested. The decision applies to fees of debtor's counsel, committee counsel, trustees' counsel, and other professionals retained under § 327, and informs Subchapter V trustee fee practice under § 330(a)(3) and § 1183(b).

Statutory Text and Structure

Section 330(a)(1)(A) authorizes the court to award 'reasonable compensation for actual, necessary services rendered by' a professional employed under § 327. The phrase 'services rendered' is the textual anchor for ASARCO's holding: defending the fee application is a service rendered to the professional, not to the estate. Section 330(a)(6) permits compensation for 'preparation' of fee applications. The Court distinguished preparation (compensable) from defense (not compensable).

Doctrinal Lineage

The American Rule against fee-shifting traces to Alyeska Pipeline Service Co. v. Wilderness Society, 421 U.S. 240 (1975). ASARCO applies that default to the bankruptcy fee-application context. The decision implicitly disapproved earlier circuit-level decisions awarding fee-defense fees on equitable or implicit-statutory grounds. The result aligns with the Court's broader textualist approach to bankruptcy-fee provisions seen in Lamie v. United States Trustee, 540 U.S. 526 (2004).

Open Questions and Subsequent Developments

ASARCO did not address the enforceability of contractual fee-defense provisions in retention agreements approved under § 328(a). Some courts have held that § 328(a) permits such provisions if specifically approved at retention; others have read ASARCO to broadly preempt them. The decision also did not address fee shifting under separate statutory bases — § 506(b) (oversecured-creditor fees), Rule 9011 sanctions, or inherent court power to sanction frivolous objections. These avenues remain available in appropriate cases but require independent statutory or rule-based justification.

Current Practice

Some retention agreements include indemnification or fee-defense provisions, the enforceability of which under § 328(a) is contested. Practitioners advising estate-side professionals typically counsel pre-petition engagement-letter review, careful time-keeping to demonstrate that fee-defense entries are not improperly included in primary-application fees, and consideration of whether a specific class of objector might justify a sanctions-based fee-shifting motion under Rule 9011 or the court's inherent authority. The decision continues to shape the economics of large Chapter 11 cases.

Broader Significance

ASARCO embodies the Court's modern textualist approach to bankruptcy-fee provisions. The decision rejects equitable arguments for fee-defense fees and aligns bankruptcy fee practice with the broader American Rule. The decision's practical consequences have shaped the economics of large Chapter 11 representations, with some professionals adjusting fee structures to compensate for the unrecoverable cost of fee-application defense. The decision also illustrates the limits of section 330(a) as a fee-authorization vehicle: the statute authorizes compensation for services rendered to the estate, not for the professional's own institutional interests. The decision continues to inform engagement-letter design and the structure of fee-application processes in complex cases.

Practitioner Notes

Estate-side professionals should structure time-keeping to clearly separate fee-application preparation (compensable under section 330(a)(6)) from fee-application defense (not compensable under ASARCO). Engagement letters should address indemnification and fee-defense in light of contested enforceability under section 328(a). Some firms include retention-letter provisions that the debtor or estate will pay fee-defense costs; whether such provisions are enforceable depends on the specific approval procedure and the local court's view. Documented compliance with fee-application standards from the outset of the case minimizes the risk of fee-defense litigation.
