Wellness International Network, Ltd. v. Sharif

575 U.S. 665 (2015)

Litigant consent cures Stern problems

Facts

Wellness International obtained a judgment against Sharif in district court and sought to enforce it in his subsequent Chapter 7. Wellness commenced an adversary proceeding to declare a trust Sharif controlled to be his alter ego (and thus property of the estate). The bankruptcy court ruled against Sharif. After Stern v. Marshall was decided, Sharif argued for the first time on appeal that the alter-ego claim was a Stern claim and the bankruptcy court therefore lacked constitutional authority to enter final judgment.

Procedural Posture

The Seventh Circuit held that the alter-ego claim was a Stern claim and that Sharif had not forfeited his Article III objection by failing to raise it earlier. The Supreme Court granted certiorari to consider whether the parties may consent to a bankruptcy court's entry of final judgment on a Stern claim.

Issue Presented

Whether Article III of the United States Constitution permits the parties to consent — knowingly and voluntarily — to final adjudication of a Stern claim by a bankruptcy court, and whether consent may be implied from conduct.

Holding

Article III permits a bankruptcy court to enter final judgment on a Stern claim with the parties' knowing and voluntary consent. Consent need not be express; it may be inferred from the parties' conduct.

Key Reasoning

Justice Sotomayor, writing for a 6-3 majority, distinguished structural Article III protections from the individual right to an Article III adjudicator. The latter can be waived. Bankruptcy courts operate under the supervision of district courts and the courts of appeals, mitigating any institutional concerns. The decision drew on Commodity Futures Trading Commission v. Schor, 478 U.S. 833 (1986). The Court remanded for the Seventh Circuit to decide whether Sharif had in fact consented.

Statutes Interpreted

  • 28 U.S.C. § 157(c)(2) — Consent to bankruptcy-court final judgment

Post-Precedent Impact

Wellness substantially narrowed the practical disruption of Stern by allowing parties to consent — including by failing to timely object — to bankruptcy-court final judgment. Districts have implemented local rules requiring parties to state their consent or refusal at the outset of adversary proceedings. The decision restored much of the bankruptcy court's day-to-day adjudicatory efficiency that Stern had threatened.

Statutory Text and Structure

28 U.S.C. § 157(c)(2) provides that 'with the consent of all the parties to the proceeding, the district court may refer a proceeding related to a case under title 11 to a bankruptcy judge to hear and determine and to enter appropriate orders and judgments, subject to review under section 158 of this title.' Wellness held that this consent mechanism — and analogous implicit consent in core-but-Stern proceedings — is constitutionally sufficient under Article III. Consent does not require formal magic words; it can arise from conduct that knowingly and voluntarily submits the matter to bankruptcy-court adjudication.

Doctrinal Lineage

Wellness completed the doctrinal arc that began with Northern Pipeline and continued through Stern. The Court had previously addressed the proposed-findings remedy in Executive Benefits Insurance Agency v. Arkison, 573 U.S. 25 (2014), but declined to reach the consent question on that occasion. Wellness built on consent precedents in the magistrate-judge context — Roell v. Withrow, 538 U.S. 580 (2003) — and on the broader recognition that personal Article III rights are waivable.

Open Questions and Subsequent Developments

Wellness did not specify the precise quality of conduct required to support implied consent. Most courts require knowing and voluntary submission — appearing, answering, and participating in proceedings without raising the Article III objection generally suffices. The decision also did not address whether consent can be retroactively withdrawn or whether a court may decline to exercise its consensual final-judgment authority. Forum-shopping concerns occasionally arise when parties strategically reserve or assert consent objections; courts have generally enforced timely-asserted objections strictly.

Current Practice

Federal district courts have adopted local-rule mechanisms that prompt parties to state consent at the pleading stage. The Bankruptcy Rules require an adversary complaint and answer to allege whether the party consents to the bankruptcy court's entry of final judgment under Bankruptcy Rule 7008 and 7012. Silence in some districts will support implied consent; in others, express consent is required. Counsel must therefore know the local rules and tailor consent statements to the specific forum.

Broader Significance

Wellness restored substantial day-to-day adjudicatory efficiency to bankruptcy courts after Stern's structural disruption. The decision reflects a broader institutional view that personal Article III protections can be waived in the same way that other procedural protections are waived. The decision's implied-consent framework has practical importance for the speed and economy of bankruptcy administration: most adversary proceedings can be efficiently litigated and resolved in the bankruptcy court without the additional layer of proposed-findings review when parties consent expressly or impliedly. The decision also harmonizes bankruptcy practice with magistrate-judge practice in the broader federal system.

Practitioner Notes

Adversary-proceeding pleadings under Bankruptcy Rules 7008 and 7012 should affirmatively address consent to bankruptcy-court final adjudication. The required statement varies by district: some districts require express consent in the complaint and answer; others permit implied consent from continued participation. Counsel should consult local rules and chambers practices before filing. Strategic withholding of consent has consequences: the case proceeds to proposed findings and conclusions reviewed de novo by the district court, lengthening the timeline and increasing cost.
