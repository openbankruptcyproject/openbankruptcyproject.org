Hamilton v. Lanning

560 U.S. 505 (2010)

Forward-looking approach to projected disposable income

Facts

Stephanie Lanning received a one-time buyout from her former employer in the six months before filing Chapter 13. The buyout caused her current monthly income, calculated mechanically from the six-month look-back of 11 U.S.C. § 101(10A), to materially exceed her actual ongoing income. The Chapter 13 trustee objected to her plan on the ground that projected disposable income should be calculated by multiplying the inflated six-month look-back figure by 60 months.

Procedural Posture

The Bankruptcy Court overruled the trustee's objection and confirmed the plan using actual expected income. The Bankruptcy Appellate Panel affirmed; the Tenth Circuit affirmed. The Supreme Court granted certiorari to resolve a circuit split between the mechanical and forward-looking approaches to calculating projected disposable income.

Issue Presented

Whether 'projected disposable income' under 11 U.S.C. § 1325(b)(1)(B) must be calculated using a mechanical multiplication of current monthly income, or whether bankruptcy courts may account for known or virtually certain changes in the debtor's income or expenses going forward.

Holding

Bankruptcy courts may use the forward-looking approach: when a debtor's current monthly income figure is materially different from anticipated future income, the court may take that change into account in computing projected disposable income for purposes of plan confirmation.

Key Reasoning

Justice Alito, writing for an 8-1 majority, emphasized that 'projected' implies an inquiry into the future rather than a backward-looking arithmetic exercise. Pre-BAPCPA case law uniformly used a forward-looking approach and Congress is presumed not to have altered settled practice without explicit indication. The mechanical approach would produce arbitrary results in cases like Lanning's, where a one-time event distorts the look-back figure.

Statutes Interpreted

  • 11 U.S.C. § 1325(b) — Confirmation of Chapter 13 plan; disposable-income test

Post-Precedent Impact

Lanning resolved a significant point of post-BAPCPA uncertainty. Trustees, debtors, and creditors now know that projected disposable income calculations can be adjusted for known or virtually certain changes — bonuses, employment changes, health-driven expense increases, or pension commencements. The decision pairs with Ransom v. FIA Card Services in shaping the contemporary approach to Form 122 means-test calculations and the § 1325(b) disposable-income inquiry.

Statutory Text and Structure

Section 1325(b)(1)(B) directs that, when the trustee or an unsecured creditor objects, the plan must provide that 'all of the debtor's projected disposable income to be received in the applicable commitment period... will be applied to make payments to unsecured creditors under the plan.' The qualifier 'projected' is the textual hook for Lanning's forward-looking reading. The 'applicable commitment period' is itself defined mechanically (three or five years depending on income), but the income amount committed remains a projection.

Doctrinal Lineage

Pre-BAPCPA practice computed projected disposable income on a forward-looking basis: trustees and courts asked what the debtor would actually earn during the commitment period. BAPCPA introduced the means-test concept of 'current monthly income' (defined by reference to a six-month look-back at § 101(10A)). The interaction between that mechanical input and the forward-looking 'projected disposable income' phrase produced a circuit split. Lanning resolved the tension in favor of preserving the traditional forward-looking inquiry.

Open Questions and Subsequent Developments

Lanning's 'known or virtually certain' standard leaves room for case-by-case judgment about what evidence supports a forward-looking adjustment. Courts have generally required credible, concrete evidence of the change — a job offer letter, a benefit-commencement notice, a documented health-driven expense increase. Speculative or aspirational changes do not suffice. Lanning also did not address whether trustees can compel post-confirmation plan modifications under § 1329 when income increases materially; most courts have answered yes, but the procedural framework varies.

Current Practice

Trustees and debtors now build projected-disposable-income calculations using the means-test figure as a starting presumption that yields to known or virtually certain changes — bonuses, employment changes, pension commencements, or material expense increases. The forward-looking approach also informs reconsideration motions when post-confirmation events materially alter the debtor's economic position. Lanning operates in tandem with Ransom v. FIA Card Services, which constrains the means-test expense side of the calculation.

Broader Significance

Lanning sits within a broader institutional debate about whether bankruptcy should rely on mechanical formulas or forward-looking projections. BAPCPA's means test embodied a mechanical philosophy; Lanning re-introduced a forward-looking discretionary element at the disposable-income stage. The decision's 8-1 majority suggests broad institutional consensus that mechanical input figures should not produce arbitrary results when reliable evidence of future circumstances is available. The decision also illustrates the Court's textual approach to statutory phrases that carry historical meaning: 'projected disposable income' had a settled pre-BAPCPA meaning, and Congress is presumed to have preserved that meaning when re-using the phrase without explicit modification. The decision continues to inform trustee and debtor practice in the calibration of plan payments, particularly when income or expense circumstances are in flux at the petition date.

Practitioner Notes

Debtor's counsel should document any material variance between the means-test current-monthly-income figure and the debtor's actual expected income. Common documentation includes pay stubs covering the post-look-back period, employer letters confirming changes in compensation, benefit-commencement notices, and medical-expense records. The forward-looking adjustment must be supported by credible evidence; speculation will not suffice. Trustees often request supplemental documentation at the section 341 meeting and may pursue Rule 2004 examinations in cases of significant variance. Post-confirmation plan modifications under section 1329 can address material changes that arise after confirmation.
