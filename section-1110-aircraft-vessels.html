11 U.S.C. Section 1110 - Aircraft Equipment and Vessels

The 60-day post-petition window in which an air carrier or water carrier debtor must agree to perform aircraft and vessel obligations, or surrender the equipment to financiers free of the automatic stay.

What Is Section 1110?

Section 1110 is the principal Chapter 11 carve-out from the automatic stay, executory-contract, and adequate-protection regimes for two specific classes of capital equipment: (1) aircraft, aircraft engines, propellers, appliances, and spare parts (as defined in 49 U.S.C. § 40102) financed for use by an air carrier; and (2) documented vessels of the United States financed for use by a water carrier (49 U.S.C. § 13102). It applies in Chapter 11 cases where the debtor is an air carrier or a water carrier (terms defined by the Federal Aviation Act and the Interstate Commerce Act respectively).

The provision gives aircraft and vessel financiers - lenders, lessors, conditional sellers - a unique, statutorily guaranteed remedy: if the debtor does not, within 60 days after the petition date, agree to perform all obligations under the financing arrangement and cure all pre-petition defaults, the financier may take possession of the equipment without seeking stay relief and may exercise any of its rights under the financing documents and applicable non-bankruptcy law.

Official citation: 11 U.S.C. § 1110

The Statutory Mechanism: Section 1110(a)

Section 1110(a)(1) preserves the financier's rights: "The right of a secured party with a security interest in equipment described in paragraph (3), or of a lessor or conditional vendor of such equipment, to take possession of such equipment in compliance with a security agreement, lease, or conditional sale contract, and to enforce any of its other rights or remedies, under such security agreement, lease, or conditional sale contract, to sell, lease, or otherwise retain or dispose of such equipment, is not limited or otherwise affected by any other provision of this title or by any power of the court."

The override is broken only if the trustee, within 60 days after the petition: (A) agrees to perform all obligations of the debtor under such security agreement, lease, or conditional sale contract; and (B) cures any default, other than a default of a kind specified in Section 365(b)(2), under such security agreement, lease, or conditional sale contract that occurred before the date of the order for relief.

The 60-Day Deadline and Section 1110(a)(2)

The 60-day deadline is mandatory and self-executing. The trustee may not seek extensions absent the consent of the financier under Section 1110(b). Pre-petition defaults must be cured by the deadline; certain post-petition payment defaults (those becoming due in the 60-day window) must also be brought current within the deadline.

Following the initial agreement, Section 1110(a)(2) imposes continuing obligations: any default (other than 365(b)(2) defaults) that occurs after the 60-day period must be cured within 30 days, and any payment default must be cured before performance is due. Failure to maintain performance restores the financier's repossession rights as if the 60-day window had never been agreed to in the first place.

The 1110(b) Stipulation Mechanism

Section 1110(b) provides a critical safety valve: "The trustee and the secured party, lessor, or conditional vendor whose right to take possession is protected under subsection (a) may agree, subject to the approval of the court, to extend the 60-day period specified in subsection (a)(1)(A)." This stipulated extension is the mechanism by which large airline reorganizations operate. The debtor and individual aircraft financiers negotiate aircraft-by-aircraft accommodations (often involving rate reductions, deferrals, or fleet rationalization), and the court approves the stipulations.

The leverage in 1110(b) negotiations is asymmetric: the debtor that wants to retain a particular aircraft has no choice but to negotiate or to cure and assume. The financier has the option to refuse and reclaim. In a depressed aviation market the financier may prefer to renegotiate; in a strong market it may prefer reclamation and re-lease to a healthy carrier.

Equipment Covered: Section 1110(a)(3)

Section 1110(a)(3) defines the covered equipment: "Equipment described in this paragraph - (A) means - (i) an aircraft, aircraft engine, propeller, appliance, or spare part (as defined in section 40102 of title 49) that is subject to a security interest granted by, leased to, or conditionally sold to a debtor that, at the time such transaction is entered into, holds an air carrier operating certificate issued pursuant to chapter 447 of title 49 for aircraft capable of carrying 10 or more individuals or 6,000 pounds or more of cargo; or (ii) a documented vessel (as defined in section 30101(1) of title 46) that is subject to a security interest granted by, leased to, or conditionally sold to a debtor that is a water carrier that, at the time such transaction is entered into, holds a certificate of public convenience and necessity or permit issued by the Department of Transportation."

The coverage is therefore industry-specific: it protects financiers extending capital to scheduled airlines and freight water carriers, not general aviation operators or pleasure vessels. The aircraft must be capable of carrying 10+ individuals or 6,000+ pounds of cargo, which excludes small piston aircraft but captures essentially all commercial jet and large turboprop equipment.

Interplay with Section 365

Section 1110 effectively overrides several of the protections an executory-contract lessee would normally enjoy under Section 365. Most importantly, the lessee cannot defer the assumption/rejection decision for the full 365(d)(2) period (which would be plan-confirmation in most Chapter 11 cases); the decision is compressed to 60 days. Section 1110(c) clarifies the interplay: with respect to equipment first placed in service after October 22, 1994, the security agreement, lease, or conditional sale contract is enforceable in accordance with its terms regardless of treatment under 365. The Chapter 11 debtor cannot use 365(b) lease-default cure mechanisms to override 1110's strict cure-and-perform requirement.

Common Procedural Postures

  • "1110 elections": The debtor files notices of election to perform under 1110(a) for each aircraft or vessel it intends to retain;
  • 1110(b) stipulations: Negotiated aircraft-by-aircraft amendments approved by motion within the 60-day window;
  • Reclamation: The financier exercises its reclamation right and seeks an order confirming that the aircraft/vessel may be removed from the debtor's possession without further court action;
  • Cape Town Convention coordination: The Cape Town Convention on International Interests in Mobile Equipment and its Aircraft Protocol layer additional international protections; U.S. implementing legislation interacts with Section 1110 to provide effectively parallel protections, with declarations made by the United States preserving 1110(a)'s 60-day cure window.
