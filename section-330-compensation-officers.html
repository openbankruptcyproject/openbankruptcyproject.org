11 U.S.C. Section 330 - Compensation of Officers

Plain-English guide to professional compensation in bankruptcy under Section 330: trustees, examiners, and Section 327 professionals; the reasonableness standard; lodestar and fee-application practice.

What Is Section 330?

Section 330 authorizes the bankruptcy court to award "reasonable compensation for actual, necessary services" rendered by a trustee, an examiner, a Subchapter V trustee, or a professional employed under Section 327 or Section 1103. It also authorizes reimbursement of "actual, necessary expenses." Section 330 is the operational complement to Section 329 (debtor-attorney fee disclosure) and Section 503 (administrative-expense priority).

Official citation: 11 U.S.C. § 330

Who Section 330 Covers (and Does Not Cover)

Section 330(a)(1) lists the eligible recipients of an award: a trustee, a consumer privacy ombudsman, an examiner, an ombudsman appointed under Section 333, or a "professional person employed under section 327 or 1103." The Supreme Court in Lamie v. United States, 540 U.S. 526 (2004), held that this enumeration is exclusive: an attorney retained by a Chapter 7 debtor (rather than by the trustee or the estate) is not entitled to compensation from estate funds under Section 330, because such an attorney is not on the list.

That holding drove the structural separation in consumer practice between Chapter 7 debtor's counsel (paid pre-petition under Section 329) and Chapter 11 / Chapter 13 debtor's counsel (paid through the estate or the plan).

The Reasonableness Standard: Section 330(a)(3)

Section 330(a)(3) lists six factors the court "shall consider" in determining the amount of reasonable compensation:

  1. The time spent on such services;
  2. The rates charged for such services;
  3. Whether the services were necessary to the administration of, or beneficial at the time at which the service was rendered toward the completion of, a case under this title;
  4. Whether the services were performed within a reasonable amount of time commensurate with the complexity, importance, and nature of the problem, issue, or task addressed;
  5. With respect to a professional person, whether the person is board certified or otherwise has demonstrated skill and expertise in the bankruptcy field; and
  6. Whether the compensation is reasonable based on the customary compensation charged by comparably skilled practitioners in cases other than cases under this title.

In practice, fee applications are evaluated under a lodestar methodology - reasonable hours times a reasonable hourly rate - subject to adjustment for the Section 330(a)(3) factors and the lodestar enhancement standard articulated in Perdue v. Kenny A., 559 U.S. 542 (2010).

Section 330(a)(4): Mandatory Exclusions

Section 330(a)(4)(A) requires the court to deny compensation for:

  • Unnecessary duplication of services (multiple professionals attending the same matter without justification); and
  • Services not reasonably likely to benefit the debtor's estate or necessary to the administration of the case.

This is a mandatory exclusion: the court has no discretion to award compensation for services that fall within either category. Subsection (a)(4)(B) carves out an exception in individual Chapter 12 and Chapter 13 cases, where the court may award compensation for services rendered in connection with the case that benefit the debtor even if they do not benefit the estate.

Trustee Compensation: Section 330(a)(7) and Section 326

Trustee compensation operates differently from professional compensation. Section 326 sets a statutory cap based on a sliding-scale percentage of disbursements. Section 330(a)(7) provides that, in a case under Chapter 7, 12, or 13, the compensation of a trustee shall be "treated as a commission" based on Section 326. The Supreme Court in Mosser v. Darrow, 341 U.S. 267 (1951), and lower courts since, have construed the trustee role as fiduciary and the compensation as a statutory entitlement subject to the Section 326 cap.

Procedure: The Fee Application

Compensation is awarded only on application under Federal Rule of Bankruptcy Procedure 2016, after notice and a hearing. Fee applications typically include:

  • A narrative description of services by project category;
  • Detailed time entries in tenths of an hour;
  • The hourly rate of each timekeeper;
  • An itemized expense schedule;
  • Disclosure of any prior payments or retainers;
  • A certification of compliance with the U.S. Trustee's fee guidelines.

Interim compensation is available under Section 331 (every 120 days, or more often if the court permits). Final compensation is awarded at the close of the case.

U.S. Trustee Review and Objections

The U.S. Trustee Program (USTP) reviews fee applications under its published guidelines for larger Chapter 11 cases and is a frequent objector. Parties in interest, including the debtor, creditors, and committees, may also object. The court may reduce fees sua sponte under Section 105(a), paired with the substantive Section 330 standard.

Related Bankruptcy Code Sections

Section 330 operates in concert with several other provisions:

  • Section 327 - Employment of professionals
  • Section 329 - Debtor's transactions with attorneys
  • Section 503 - Administrative-expense allowance
  • Section 507 - Priority of administrative expenses
  • Section 105 - Equitable enforcement of fee orders

Understanding how these sections interact is important for debtors, creditors, trustees, professionals, and the U.S. Trustee.
