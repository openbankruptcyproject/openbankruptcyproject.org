Alternative Dispute Resolution Mediation

Court-Annexed Bankruptcy Mediation

Local rule mediation programs, mediator selection, confidentiality under Rule 8.6 of the Model Standards, and the disputes most often mediated in bankruptcy.

Statutory and rule-based authority

Bankruptcy mediation operates within a layered framework of federal authority. The Alternative Dispute Resolution Act of 1998 (28 U.S.C. Sections 651-658) directs every federal district court to authorize the use of ADR processes in civil actions, including bankruptcy adversary proceedings and contested matters. Federal Rule of Bankruptcy Procedure 9019(c) independently authorizes the bankruptcy court to require parties to participate in a settlement conference or other ADR proceeding.

The Bankruptcy Court for each district has implemented mediation by local rule or general order. The local rules typically address:

  • The categories of matters eligible for or required to undergo mediation
  • The procedure for selecting a mediator (party agreement, court appointment, or panel rotation)
  • Mediator qualifications, training requirements, and disclosure obligations
  • Confidentiality of mediation communications
  • Allocation of mediation fees and expenses
  • Reporting requirements (typically a brief report of impasse, settlement, or partial settlement, without disclosure of substantive communications)

A representative example is the Bankruptcy Court for the Southern District of New York's General Order M-452 establishing the Mediation Program. Comparable programs exist in the Bankruptcy Courts for the Districts of Delaware, the Eastern District of New York, the Central District of California, the Southern District of Texas, the Eastern District of Virginia, and most other districts with significant Chapter 11 dockets.

Mediator selection

The mediator-selection process varies by district. Common models include:

Party-agreement selection

The parties agree on a mediator from a court-maintained panel or, with court approval, from outside the panel. This model predominates in larger Chapter 11 cases where the parties are sophisticated and the disputes involve unique subject matter (mass torts, complex valuation, multi-creditor priority disputes).

Court-appointment from panel

If the parties cannot agree, the court appoints a mediator from the registered mediation panel. Panel mediators have typically satisfied training and experience requirements set by local rule (often 40 hours of mediation training plus bankruptcy or commercial litigation experience).

Mediator qualifications

The qualifications required to serve as a bankruptcy mediator vary by district. Common requirements include:

  • Active bar membership in good standing (typically for at least 10-15 years)
  • Substantial experience in bankruptcy practice, commercial litigation, or related fields
  • Completion of a recognized mediation training program (often 40 hours)
  • Continuing-education requirements (often 6-10 hours every two years)
  • Compliance with disclosure obligations regarding conflicts of interest

Some districts maintain separate panels for consumer bankruptcy mediation and complex Chapter 11 mediation, reflecting the different skill sets required.

Confidentiality - the Model Standards Rule 8.6 framework

The Model Standards of Conduct for Mediators, jointly issued by the American Bar Association, the American Arbitration Association, and the Association for Conflict Resolution, address mediator confidentiality at Standard V (Confidentiality). Rule 8.6 of the corresponding professional-conduct framework requires the mediator to maintain the confidentiality of all information obtained by the mediator in mediation, unless otherwise agreed to by the parties or required by applicable law.

The confidentiality framework typically operates on three layers:

  1. Mediator confidentiality: The mediator may not disclose communications received in the course of mediation to anyone outside the mediation, including the court, except as expressly authorized by the parties or as required by law.
  2. Party-side confidentiality: The parties typically execute a mediation agreement in which they agree not to disclose communications made during mediation in any subsequent proceeding. Many local rules incorporate this restriction directly.
  3. Evidentiary protection: Federal Rule of Evidence 408 excludes evidence of settlement negotiations to prove or disprove the validity or amount of a disputed claim. Many districts supplement Rule 408 with a local-rule provision deeming mediation communications inadmissible and not subject to discovery.

The confidentiality framework is essential to the mediation process. Parties may speak frankly to the mediator (typically in caucus) about case weaknesses, settlement parameters, and underlying business considerations only if assured that those disclosures will not later be used against them. The framework has narrow exceptions for threats of bodily harm, ongoing or future criminal conduct, child or elder abuse, and limited circumstances where the parties expressly waive confidentiality.

Mediator confidentiality is not absolute. Most frameworks recognize exceptions for threats of imminent bodily harm, ongoing or future criminal conduct, and reports of child or elder abuse. The specific exceptions are governed by the local rule and the mediation agreement; parties and mediators should review the governing framework before relying on a particular confidentiality assumption.

The mediation process

A typical bankruptcy mediation proceeds through these stages:

Pre-mediation

The parties submit confidential mediation statements to the mediator, typically 7-14 days before the session. The statements identify the parties, summarize the dispute, identify key legal and factual issues, summarize prior settlement discussions, and outline the party's settlement position. The mediator reviews the statements and may conduct pre-mediation telephone conferences to clarify issues or address procedural matters.

Joint session

The mediation typically opens with a joint session at which the mediator describes the process, the parties make brief opening statements, and the principal disputed issues are identified. Some mediations dispense with the joint session entirely and proceed directly to caucus, particularly where the parties' relationship is sufficiently strained that joint discussion would be unproductive.

Caucus

The mediator meets separately with each party (caucus) to explore the party's interests, evaluate the strengths and weaknesses of the party's position, and develop settlement options. Information disclosed in caucus is presumptively confidential as to the other parties unless the disclosing party authorizes communication.

Settlement or impasse

If the parties reach agreement, the mediator typically facilitates drafting of a term sheet or memorandum of understanding signed at the conclusion of the mediation. The detailed settlement agreement is documented afterward. If the parties reach impasse, the mediator files a brief report indicating that the mediation concluded without settlement. The report does not disclose substantive communications.

Common mediated disputes in bankruptcy

Plan confirmation disputes

Mediation of valuation, claim treatment, and feasibility disputes in Chapter 11 plan confirmation. Particularly common where the dispute is between the debtor and the creditors' committee or between competing classes of creditors. The mediator often has substantial Chapter 11 valuation experience.

Stay-relief disputes

Mediation of motions for relief from the automatic stay, particularly where the underlying dispute is fact-intensive (adequate-protection valuation, equity cushion analysis, foreclosure history) or where the parties have an ongoing relationship that would benefit from negotiated resolution.

Fee disputes

Mediation of objections to professional-fee applications under Section 330. The mediator typically has experience as a fee examiner or as a member of a creditors' committee evaluating professional fees. Disputes commonly involve hourly rates, staffing levels, time-entry detail, and reasonableness of specific tasks.

Avoidance action mediation

Mediation of preference and fraudulent-transfer actions, particularly where the estate has filed a large number of avoidance actions and seeks an efficient resolution process. Some districts implement standing orders requiring mediation of all avoidance actions above a threshold dollar amount.

Allocation of mediation costs

The local rule typically allocates the mediator's fee equally among the principal parties, subject to court order otherwise. In Chapter 11 cases, the debtor's share is typically administrative expense of the estate; the creditor's share is borne by the creditor. In adversary proceedings, the allocation tracks the parties to the adversary. Some panel mediators serve pro bono for a limited number of hours, particularly in consumer or small-business cases.

Enforcement of mediated settlements

A mediated settlement that resolves a contested matter or adversary proceeding is enforced like any other settlement of an estate claim. If the settlement requires court approval - because it disposes of estate claims falling within Rule 9019(a) - the parties present the settlement to the court by motion. The court evaluates the settlement under the TMT Trailer factors regardless of the fact that the settlement was mediated. Mediation does not displace the substantive approval standard; it provides a procedural mechanism for arriving at the settlement that the court will then evaluate.
