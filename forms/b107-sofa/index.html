Official Bankruptcy Form B107

Form B107: Statement of Financial Affairs for Individuals

The narrative companion to the schedules: pre-petition history of payments, transfers, lawsuits, income, and financial accounts.

Purpose and statutory authority

Official Form B107 (Statement of Financial Affairs, often called the SOFA) implements 11 U.S.C. Section 521(a)(1)(B)(iii)'s requirement that the debtor file a "statement of the debtor's financial affairs." Where the B106 schedules photograph the debtor's situation as of the petition date, the SOFA traces the debtor's pre-petition financial activity, transfers, payments to creditors and insiders, lawsuits, repossessions, gifts, losses, and account activity. The SOFA is the primary source document the trustee uses to evaluate preferences, fraudulent transfers, undisclosed assets, and pre-petition planning.

The form is promulgated under 28 U.S.C. Section 2075 and signed under penalty of perjury, with false-oath consequences under Section 727(a)(4) and criminal exposure under 18 U.S.C. Section 152.

Who must file

Every individual debtor in Chapter 7, 11, 12, or 13 files Form B107. Joint debtors file one SOFA covering both spouses. Non-individual debtors file Form B207 instead.

The numbered inquiries

Parts 1-2: Income and payments

Lifetime employment income for the current and two prior calendar years, and other income sources. Payments to any creditor totaling $600+ within 90 days pre-petition (or $7,575 aggregate to a single non-consumer-debt creditor under Section 547(c)(9)).

Part 3: Insider transactions

All payments and transfers to or for the benefit of insiders (relatives, business affiliates, general partners) within one year pre-petition. This window matches Section 547(b)(4)(B) preference reach-back.

Parts 4-5: Legal actions and repossessions

All lawsuits, court actions, administrative proceedings, garnishments, attachments, and repossessions within one year pre-petition.

Parts 6-7: Charitable contributions and losses

Gifts and charitable contributions exceeding $600 within two years, and losses from theft, fire, casualty, or gambling within one year.

Parts 8-9: Professionals and other transfers

Payments to debt counseling agencies, bankruptcy attorneys, or other professionals consulted about debt or bankruptcy in the year pre-petition. Other transfers of property within two years (ten years for self-settled trusts under Section 548(e)).

Parts 10-13: Financial accounts and safe deposits

Bank accounts closed in the year pre-petition, safe deposit boxes used, setoffs by financial institutions, and property held for others.

Parts 14-17: Environmental, businesses, addresses, taxes

Environmental information (only if applicable), connection to any business within four years, prior addresses within three years (relevant to venue and exemption-state determination under Section 522(b)(3)), and personally identifiable customer information held.

Signature page

Declaration under penalty of perjury by each debtor and (if applicable) by a bankruptcy petition preparer under Section 110.

Filing context and deadlines

Form B107 is due with the petition or within 14 days after the petition is filed, per Fed. R. Bankr. P. 1007(c). Amendments are permitted under Fed. R. Bankr. P. 1009(a) at any time before the case is closed. The SOFA is reviewed line-by-line at the meeting of creditors under Section 341(a), where the trustee asks the debtor to confirm the answers under oath.

Common pitfalls

Part 3 insider payments are the most-litigated omission. Payments to a parent, sibling, or business affiliate within the one-year window must be disclosed even if the payment was a small loan repayment or rent. Trustees frequently pursue Section 547(b) and Section 548 actions seeded by Part 3 entries; omission can produce a Section 727(a)(4) false-oath denial of discharge.

  • Underreporting Part 1 income. Year-to-date income must be reconciled to W-2s, 1099s, and any K-1 distributions. Unreported business income from a side gig is a frequent meeting-of-creditors flag.
  • Part 4 garnishments not listed. A wage garnishment within 90 days that captured more than $600 is both a Part 4 entry and a potential Section 547(b) preference recovery for the trustee.
  • Part 9 attorney payment misalignment. The amount disclosed on the SOFA must match the disclosure on Form B2030 (Disclosure of Compensation of Attorney) and the actual fee paid; discrepancies are reviewed under Section 329.
  • Part 8 prior addresses incomplete. The two-year "domicile" lookback governs which state's exemptions apply under Section 522(b)(3)(A); missing addresses can foreclose the intended exemption election.
  • Part 18 connection to business omitted. Any sole proprietorship, partnership interest, or 5%+ interest in any corporation within four years pre-petition must be disclosed.

Where to obtain the official current version

The current SOFA is at the U.S. Courts forms library. See uscourts.gov/forms-rules/forms/bankruptcy-forms under the individual-debtor category. The form is revised periodically and dollar thresholds in Part 2 and Part 6 are adjusted on the three-year cycle of 11 U.S.C. Section 104.
