Official Bankruptcy Form B106 Series

Form B106: Individual Bankruptcy Schedules A/B through J

The complete inventory of an individual debtor's assets, liabilities, executory contracts, income, and expenses, signed under penalty of perjury.

Purpose and statutory authority

The B106 series implements the individual debtor's statutory duty under 11 U.S.C. Section 521(a)(1)(B) to file a list of creditors and a schedule of assets and liabilities, a schedule of current income and current expenditures, a statement of the debtor's financial affairs, and copies of any payment advices. The schedules are the principal documentary record of what the bankruptcy estate contains, what the debtor owes, and what the debtor claims as exempt from administration.

The forms are promulgated under 28 U.S.C. Section 2075. Schedules are signed under penalty of perjury, which gives false-oath consequences under Section 727(a)(4) for Chapter 7 cases and under 18 U.S.C. Section 152 as a federal criminal matter.

Who must file

Every individual debtor (whether under Chapter 7, 11, 12, or 13) must file the B106 series. Joint debtors file one set of schedules covering both spouses' property and obligations, with column markings to indicate which spouse holds each item. Non-individual debtors use the B206 series instead.

Schedule-by-schedule walkthrough

B106Summary

Cover summary aggregating totals from each underlying schedule. Includes statistical summary of certain liabilities required for individual cases under Section 159 reporting.

B106A/B - Property

All real property (Part 1) and personal property (Parts 2-7), valued at fair market replacement value. Includes vehicles, household goods, electronics, collectibles, cash and bank accounts, financial assets, business interests, claims and causes of action, and any other interest of value.

B106C - Exemptions

Property the debtor claims as exempt from the estate. Debtor elects either the federal exemptions of 11 U.S.C. Section 522(d) or the applicable state exemptions, subject to opt-out states. Each item must reference the specific statutory subsection and the dollar amount claimed.

B106D - Secured creditors

All creditors holding claims secured by property, with description of collateral, amount of claim, value of collateral, and unsecured deficiency if any.

B106E/F - Unsecured creditors

Part 1 lists priority unsecured claims under Section 507(a) (domestic support, tax claims, wage claims, etc.). Part 2 lists general (non-priority) unsecured claims, with check boxes for contingent, unliquidated, and disputed status.

B106G - Executory contracts and unexpired leases

All ongoing contracts and leases (residential lease, vehicle lease, service contracts, etc.) that may be assumed or rejected under Section 365.

B106H - Codebtors

Individuals or entities also liable on any debts listed, including non-filing spouses, business partners, and guarantors. Important for codebtor stay analysis under Section 1301 in Chapter 13.

B106I and B106J - Income and expenses

Schedule I reports current monthly income from all sources for each spouse, and Schedule J reports current monthly expenses. The difference is the monthly net income that funds Chapter 13 plan payments or is examined in Chapter 7 abuse analysis under Section 707(b)(3).

Declaration

Form B106Dec (Declaration About an Individual Debtor's Schedules) is signed under penalty of perjury by each debtor and acknowledges that the information in the schedules is true and correct to the best of the debtor's knowledge.

Filing context and deadlines

Schedules must be filed with the petition or within 14 days thereafter, per Fed. R. Bankr. P. 1007(c). The court may extend the deadline on motion for cause. Amendments to schedules are permitted as of right before the case is closed, per Fed. R. Bankr. P. 1009(a). An amendment must be served on the trustee and any affected creditor, and a notice that the schedules have been amended is sent to all creditors when the amendment adds a creditor.

Common pitfalls

Undisclosed causes of action are a primary false-oath risk. Pending or potential lawsuits, employment-discrimination claims, personal-injury claims, and any other contingent claim the debtor holds against a third party are property of the estate under Section 541. Omission from Schedule A/B can produce judicial-estoppel preclusion in the later litigation in addition to false-oath exposure.

  • Undervaluing personal property. Replacement value, not garage-sale value, is the standard. Aggregate "household goods $500" with no itemization invites a Rule 2004 examination.
  • Missing the codebtor on a joint loan. A non-filing spouse on a vehicle loan must appear on Schedule H. Omission can produce automatic-stay issues for the codebtor in Chapter 13 cases.
  • Citing the wrong exemption authority. States that opt out of the federal scheme require state citations only; mixing federal and state citations on Schedule C is reversible on objection.
  • Schedule J expenses that do not match Form B122 means-test deductions. Discrepancies invite trustee scrutiny on both means-test compliance and plan-feasibility analysis.
  • Failure to amend after the meeting of creditors. Information that surfaces at the Section 341 meeting (an additional bank account, a missed creditor, a non-disclosed transfer) creates an obligation to amend promptly.
