Official Bankruptcy Form B122A-1

Form B122A-1: Chapter 7 Statement of Your Current Monthly Income

The means-test entry point: compute current monthly income, annualize, and compare to the state median to determine whether Form B122A-2 must also be filed.

Purpose and statutory authority

Official Form B122A-1 implements the income-side calculation of the Chapter 7 means test under 11 U.S.C. Section 707(b)(2) and the definition of "current monthly income" in Section 101(10A). The form computes the debtor's average monthly income from all sources during the six full calendar months preceding the filing month, annualizes the figure, and compares it to the median family income for the debtor's household size in the debtor's state of residence (as published by the United States Trustee Program).

Where annualized current monthly income is at or below the state median, the means-test inquiry is satisfied at the income step. Where it exceeds the state median, the debtor must also file Form B122A-2 (Means Test Calculation) to compute allowable expense deductions and the resulting disposable-income figure that drives the presumption of abuse under Section 707(b)(2)(A).

Who must file

Every individual Chapter 7 debtor with primarily consumer debts. A debtor whose debts are primarily non-consumer (e.g., business debts exceed consumer debts in dollar amount) is exempt from the means test under Section 707(b)(1) but still files B122A-1 with the non-consumer-debt exclusion box checked. Disabled veterans who incurred their indebtedness primarily during active duty also have a statutory exclusion at Section 707(b)(2)(D).

How current monthly income is computed

  • Average gross income from all sources received in the six full calendar months immediately preceding the calendar month in which the petition is filed
  • Includes wages, salary, business income, rental income, interest, dividends, pension and retirement income, unemployment compensation (with a Section 101(10A)(B) carve-out for benefits received under the Social Security Act)
  • Includes regular contributions from any source for household expenses, including from a non-filing spouse or other household member
  • Excludes Social Security Act benefits, victims-of-war-crimes payments, victims-of-terrorism payments, and similar statutory exclusions

Marital adjustment

For a married debtor not filing jointly, the non-filing spouse's income is reported as part of household current monthly income, then adjusted on Line 13 to back out amounts not regularly contributed to household expenses of the debtor or the debtor's dependents.

Median-income comparison

The form references the United States Trustee Program's means-testing data tables, which publish state median family income by household size and update twice yearly (typically May 1 and November 1). The applicable table is determined by the petition date.

Filing context and deadlines

Form B122A-1 is filed with the petition or within 14 days after the petition date, per Fed. R. Bankr. P. 1007(b)(4) and (c). If the income computation produces an above-median result, Form B122A-2 is due on the same schedule. The United States Trustee files a Statement of Presumed Abuse under Section 704(b)(1) within 10 days of the meeting of creditors when warranted.

Common pitfalls

The six-month CMI period is fixed by statute; it does not end on the petition date. It ends on the last day of the calendar month preceding the petition month. A petition filed June 10 uses December through May income; a petition filed June 30 uses the same period. Filers sometimes shift the petition date by a few days to capture a more favorable six-month average.

  • Including or excluding bonus and overtime incorrectly. Bonus and overtime received during the CMI window are CMI even if irregular; the proper place to argue that they will not recur is Form B122A-2's "additional expense or income adjustments" or in a Section 707(b)(2)(B) special-circumstances showing.
  • Household size errors. The household size for median comparison is not always the "number of people living in the home." Courts use different approaches (income-tax dependent test, IRS dependency test, economic-unit test). The chosen method should be consistent with Schedule J and the local approach.
  • Misclassifying non-consumer debt. A bright-line dollar test compares scheduled consumer debts to scheduled non-consumer debts; misclassification of debt category on the schedules ripples into the B122A-1 non-consumer exclusion box.
  • Using stale median figures. The applicable median table is the one in effect on the petition date. A petition filed close to a USTP table update should be checked against the date-of-filing table.
