Chapter 12 Plan Confirmation: Family Farmer and Family Fisherman

A complete walk-through of the Chapter 12 plan: family-farmer and family-fisherman eligibility, plan contents under Section 1222, confirmation requirements under Section 1225, and the distinctive Chapter 12 cramdown standards for secured agricultural debt.

Empirical research on Chapter 12

Chapter 12 filings are small in number relative to the consumer chapters but concentrate in agricultural districts where seasonal income, land collateral, and federal farm-loan programs interact in distinctive ways. Quick Answer

Chapter 12 is the bankruptcy chapter purpose-built for the family farmer and the family fisherman. It combines the rehabilitative structure of Chapter 13 (a wage-earner-style three-to-five-year plan administered by a standing trustee) with the more permissive cramdown rules of Chapter 11, all calibrated to the realities of agricultural cash flow. The plan must satisfy the content requirements of Section 1222 and the confirmation requirements of Section 1225. Chapter 12 also includes a distinctive feature: secured creditors can be crammed down on present-value terms without the impaired-class-acceptance requirement that constrains Chapter 11.

Governing provisions: 11 U.S.C. §§ 1201–1232.

Who Can File: Family Farmer and Family Fisherman

Eligibility is the gateway question. The Code defines "family farmer" and "family fisherman" in Section 101(18) and Section 101(19A), respectively. Both definitions impose four interrelated tests on individuals (and on closely held corporations or partnerships in parallel form):

  • Engagement in farming or commercial fishing — the debtor must be actually engaged in the relevant operation.
  • Aggregate debt cap — total debt cannot exceed the inflation-adjusted statutory ceiling (the cap is materially higher for farmers than for fishermen and has been adjusted multiple times since 1986).
  • Debt-composition test — a specified percentage of aggregate non-contingent, liquidated debt (excluding debt secured by the principal residence, with carve-outs for residences used in farming) must arise from the farming or fishing operation.
  • Income-composition test — for individuals, more than a specified percentage of gross income for the relevant tax year (or, alternatively, in each of the second and third preceding tax years) must come from farming or fishing.

These tests filter out hobby farms, gentleman estates, and integrated agribusinesses whose scale is more suited to Chapter 11. The chapter is intentionally limited to the operator whose livelihood is in the land or on the water.

Contents of the Plan: Section 1222

Section 1222(a) sets out the mandatory plan contents. Every Chapter 12 plan must:

  • Provide for submission of all or such portion of the debtor's future earnings or other future income to the trustee's supervision and control as is necessary to execute the plan.
  • Provide for the full payment, in deferred cash payments, of all claims entitled to priority under Section 507, unless the holder agrees otherwise (with a special carve-out, discussed below, for certain tax claims arising from disposition of farm assets).
  • Provide the same treatment for each claim within a class.

Section 1222(b) lists permitted provisions. Among the most operationally important:

  • Modification of the rights of secured creditors (with no exception for residential-mortgage holders — a striking contrast to Chapter 13's anti-modification rule for principal residences).
  • Cure of defaults within a reasonable time.
  • Payments on long-term secured debt extending beyond the plan period (the "long-term obligation" provision, parallel to Section 1322(b)(5)).
  • Sale of property of the estate or distribution of property to satisfy claims.
  • Designation of certain tax claims arising from sale of farm assets used in the farming operation as unsecured claims that are not entitled to priority — an important loophole opened by the 2005 amendments and interpreted by the Supreme Court in Hall v. United States, 566 U.S. 506 (2012), which held that post-petition capital-gains taxes do not qualify for that treatment in an individual Chapter 12 case.

Section 1222(c) sets the plan period: the plan may not exceed three years unless the court, for cause, approves a longer period; the period may not in any case exceed five years.

Confirmation Requirements: Section 1225

Section 1225(a) sets the confirmation standard. The court "shall confirm" a plan if six conditions are met:

  1. The plan complies with the provisions of Chapter 12 and other applicable provisions of the Code.
  2. The required filing fees have been paid.
  3. The plan has been proposed in good faith and not by any means forbidden by law.
  4. The plan satisfies the best-interests-of-creditors test — each unsecured claim holder receives at least the Chapter 7 liquidation value.
  5. With respect to each allowed secured claim provided for by the plan, one of three things is true: (a) the secured creditor has accepted the plan; (b) the plan provides retention of the lien and distribution of property of a value, as of the effective date, not less than the allowed amount of the secured claim; or (c) the plan surrenders the collateral.
  6. The debtor will be able to make all payments and otherwise comply with the plan (feasibility).

The structural advantage of Chapter 12 is what is not on the list: there is no requirement of class acceptance and no absolute-priority rule. A secured creditor's rejection cannot block confirmation if the plan satisfies Section 1225(a)(5)(B) by retaining the lien and paying present value.

The Section 1225(b) Disposable-Income Test

If the trustee or an unsecured creditor objects to confirmation, Section 1225(b) requires that the plan either pay unsecured claims in full or commit all of the debtor's "projected disposable income" for the duration of the plan to payments under the plan. Disposable income under Section 1225(b)(2) is income received by the debtor that is not reasonably necessary for the maintenance or support of the debtor or a dependent, or for payment of expenses necessary for the continuation, preservation, and operation of the debtor's farming or fishing business.

The business-expense carve-out mirrors the language later imported into Subchapter V. This is the practical mechanism by which Chapter 12 preserves an operating margin for the next planting season, the next vessel-maintenance cycle, or the next equipment-replacement event.

Cramdown of Secured Agricultural Debt

The Chapter 12 cramdown standard under Section 1225(a)(5)(B) requires the plan to provide the secured creditor with deferred cash payments of a value, as of the effective date, "not less than the allowed amount of such claim." Computing that present value requires the application of an appropriate discount rate. The Supreme Court's reasoning in Till v. SCS Credit Corp., 541 U.S. 465 (2004), arose under Chapter 13 but has been widely applied to Chapter 12 cramdown as well: courts begin with the national prime rate and adjust upward to reflect the risk of nonpayment. The size and basis of the upward adjustment is the most common evidentiary battleground.

Collateral valuation follows the principles articulated in Associates Commercial Corp. v. Rash, 520 U.S. 953 (1997): when the debtor proposes to retain and use the collateral, valuation looks to replacement value rather than foreclosure value. For specialized agricultural equipment, that distinction can be the difference between feasibility and surrender.

A distinctive Chapter 12 feature, recognized in early appellate decisions interpreting the chapter's secured-creditor cramdown, is that the long-term-obligation provision of Section 1222(b)(5) permits secured payments to extend beyond the three-to-five-year plan period. A real-estate mortgage with a thirty-year amortization may be reinstated and cured on its original terms, with deferred-cash present-value treatment confined to any deficiency or modified principal balance. This combination of long-amortization preservation with present-value cramdown of the modified portion is what allows operating farms and fishing operations to right-size secured debt without forcing surrender of the underlying asset.

The Chapter 12 Trustee and Plan Administration

Chapter 12 cases are administered by a standing trustee appointed under Section 1202, modeled in significant respects on the Chapter 13 standing-trustee system but with adjustments reflecting the chapter's operating-business orientation. The trustee receives plan payments, distributes funds to creditors, monitors compliance with the plan, and serves as a check on the debtor's continued performance.

Unlike the Chapter 11 estate, the Chapter 12 debtor remains in possession of property of the estate under Section 1203 and continues to operate the farm or fishing business. The trustee is not a debtor-in-possession's adversary in the day-to-day operation but functions as a distribution agent and a fiduciary check at moments of plan modification, default, and conversion. The standing-trustee model spreads administrative cost across the chapter's caseload, an essential feature given Chapter 12's relatively low filing volume in most districts.

Plan modification under Section 1229

Section 1229 permits the debtor, the trustee, or an unsecured creditor to seek post-confirmation modification of the plan at any time before completion. Modifications commonly respond to harvest variability, market price moves, weather and disease events affecting livestock or crops, and gains or losses from the sale of equipment or land. The flexibility of the modification mechanism is one of the chapter's most important practical features: a rigid plan that cannot survive an off-year is no rehabilitation plan at all.

Effect of Confirmation and Discharge

Under Section 1227, confirmation binds the debtor and each creditor, secured or unsecured, whether or not the creditor objected to or accepted the plan. Property of the estate vests in the debtor on confirmation, free and clear of any claim or interest of any creditor provided for by the plan. The Chapter 12 discharge under Section 1228 is entered after the debtor completes all payments under the plan, with the broader hardship-discharge alternative in Section 1228(b) available where circumstances beyond the debtor's control (drought, flood, market collapse, vessel loss) make completion impracticable.
