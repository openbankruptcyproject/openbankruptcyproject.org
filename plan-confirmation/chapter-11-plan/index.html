Chapter 11 Plan Confirmation: Disclosure, Voting, and Cramdown

A complete walk-through of how a Chapter 11 plan moves from filing to confirmation: disclosure statement under Section 1125, solicitation and voting under Section 1126, the sixteen confirmation requirements of Section 1129(a), nonconsensual cramdown under Section 1129(b), and the absolute priority rule.

Quick Answer

A Chapter 11 plan is confirmed in a sequence of statutorily defined steps. The debtor (or another plan proponent) files a written plan together with a court-approved disclosure statement under Section 1125. Creditors and interest holders then vote by class under Section 1126. At the confirmation hearing the court applies the sixteen requirements of Section 1129(a), including the best-interests test, feasibility, and good faith. If every impaired class accepts, the plan is confirmed consensually. If at least one impaired class rejects, the proponent may still obtain confirmation by cramdown under Section 1129(b), but only if the plan is "fair and equitable" and "does not discriminate unfairly," and at least one impaired class of non-insiders has accepted.

Governing provisions: 11 U.S.C. §§ 1121–1129.

The Disclosure Statement: Section 1125

Before a plan proponent may solicit votes on a Chapter 11 plan, the court must approve a written disclosure statement containing "adequate information" sufficient to enable a hypothetical reasonable investor in a particular class to make an informed judgment about the plan. 11 U.S.C. § 1125(a)(1). The disclosure statement is the bridge between the plan as a legal instrument and the creditors and equity holders who must evaluate it as an economic proposition.

Adequate information is a fact-specific standard. Courts evaluate the size and complexity of the case, the nature of the debtor's business, the sophistication of the creditor body, and the cost and feasibility of producing additional disclosure. A small-trade-creditor case will warrant a different level of detail than a publicly held debtor whose creditors include institutional bondholders.

Typical disclosure-statement contents include:

  • A description of the debtor's pre-petition business and the circumstances that led to bankruptcy.
  • Historical financial statements and projected post-confirmation operating results.
  • A liquidation analysis comparing creditor recoveries under the plan with what those creditors would receive in a hypothetical Chapter 7 liquidation.
  • A description of the classification and treatment of claims and interests under the plan.
  • The identity and compensation of officers and directors expected to serve after confirmation.
  • Identification of insiders, related-party transactions, and known litigation.
  • A risk-factor discussion analogous to securities-law disclosure.

The disclosure statement is itself approved at a noticed hearing. Objections at that stage typically focus on factual completeness, not on the legal merits of the plan itself. A common refrain in chambers is that disclosure-statement objections that are really plan-confirmation objections should be reserved for the confirmation hearing.

Solicitation and Voting: Section 1126

Once the disclosure statement is approved, the plan proponent transmits the plan, the disclosure statement, and ballots to all holders of claims and interests in impaired classes. Under Section 1126(c), a class of claims accepts the plan if creditors holding at least two-thirds in dollar amount and more than one-half in number of allowed claims actually voting in the class vote to accept. A class of interests accepts under Section 1126(d) if holders of at least two-thirds in amount of allowed interests actually voting accept.

An unimpaired class is "conclusively presumed" to have accepted the plan and does not vote. 11 U.S.C. § 1126(f). Conversely, a class that receives or retains nothing under the plan is "deemed not to have accepted." 11 U.S.C. § 1126(g). The deemed-rejection rule is significant: a wipe-out class need not be solicited at all, but its deemed rejection is what forces the proponent into cramdown.

Section 1126(e) permits the court to designate (disqualify) a vote that was not solicited or procured in good faith or in accordance with the Code. Designation is an extraordinary remedy and requires a showing that the voter acted with an ulterior motive untethered from a legitimate creditor interest, such as voting strategically to obtain a blocking position for a competitor.

The Sixteen Requirements of Section 1129(a)

Section 1129(a) lists sixteen separate confirmation requirements. The court must find that each applicable requirement is satisfied; the proponent bears the burden of proof by a preponderance of the evidence. The most contested provisions are described below.

1129(a)(3) — Good faith

The plan must be "proposed in good faith and not by any means forbidden by law." Good faith is measured against the totality of the circumstances and the fundamental purposes of Chapter 11 (rehabilitation and fair distribution to creditors).

1129(a)(7) — Best-interests test

Each holder of an impaired claim or interest that has not accepted the plan must receive property of a value, as of the effective date, that is "not less than" what the holder would receive in a Chapter 7 liquidation. This makes the liquidation analysis indispensable to the confirmation record.

1129(a)(8) — Class acceptance

Each class of claims or interests must either accept the plan or be unimpaired. Failure of this requirement is what makes cramdown necessary.

1129(a)(10) — At least one accepting impaired class

If any class is impaired, at least one impaired class (determined without including the acceptance of any insider) must accept the plan. This is the so-called "impaired-acceptance" requirement and operates as a structural gating mechanism for cramdown.

1129(a)(11) — Feasibility

Confirmation must not be likely to be followed by liquidation or further reorganization unless such liquidation or reorganization is itself proposed in the plan. Feasibility is proven through cash-flow projections, capital-structure analysis, and (in larger cases) expert testimony.

Cramdown: Section 1129(b)

When at least one impaired class has accepted, the court may still confirm a plan over the rejection of one or more dissenting impaired classes. Section 1129(b) requires that the plan "does not discriminate unfairly" and is "fair and equitable" with respect to each dissenting class.

Fair-and-equitable for secured claims

For a dissenting class of secured claims, the plan must (i) retain the lien and provide deferred cash payments equal to the present value of the collateral, (ii) sell the collateral free and clear with the lien attaching to proceeds, or (iii) provide the "indubitable equivalent." The Supreme Court's decision in Till v. SCS Credit Corp., 541 U.S. 465 (2004), provides the prevailing framework for computing the appropriate interest rate to discount deferred payments (the formula approach), and Associates Commercial Corp. v. Rash, 520 U.S. 953 (1997), establishes that collateral is valued at replacement value when the debtor proposes to retain it.

Fair-and-equitable for unsecured claims — the absolute priority rule

For a dissenting class of unsecured claims, the plan must either pay the class in full or ensure that no holder of a junior claim or interest receives or retains property "on account of" the junior claim or interest. This is the absolute priority rule, codified at Section 1129(b)(2)(B)(ii).

The Supreme Court in Bank of America National Trust & Savings Ass'n v. 203 North LaSalle Street Partnership, 526 U.S. 434 (1999), held that the absolute priority rule prohibits a plan that gives existing equity an exclusive right to acquire new equity for a contribution of new value, without market exposure. Earlier, in Norwest Bank Worthington v. Ahlers, 485 U.S. 197 (1988), the Court held that promises of future "sweat equity" do not satisfy the new-value exception. The boundaries of any new-value exception remain narrow and litigation-intensive.

Classification and the One-Class-One-Vote Architecture

The plan's classification scheme under Section 1122 sits upstream of the entire confirmation analysis. Section 1122(a) permits classification of claims or interests in a particular class only if the claims or interests are "substantially similar." Section 1122(b) authorizes a separate class for unsecured claims under a stated dollar amount for administrative convenience. The proponent's classification decisions shape which classes are impaired, how votes are tallied, whether the at-least-one-accepting-impaired-class requirement of Section 1129(a)(10) is met, and the contours of any cramdown.

Classification gerrymandering — placing similar creditors into different classes to manufacture an accepting class — is one of the most frequently litigated confirmation issues. Courts apply a "business reason" test that focuses on whether the separate treatment is supported by a legitimate, non-strategic justification. Common approved bases for separate classification include differences in legal rights, in collateral position, in subordination posture, and in pre-petition contractual terms. Separating identical unsecured trade claims from identical unsecured tort claims solely to obtain a vote is generally rejected.

Impairment under Section 1124

A class of claims or interests is impaired unless the plan "leaves unaltered the legal, equitable, and contractual rights" to which the holder is entitled. Even a plan that pays a creditor in full in cash on the effective date can leave the class impaired if it alters the legal terms of the underlying obligation. Unimpaired classes are conclusively presumed to accept under Section 1126(f) and do not vote.

Effect of Confirmation and Appellate Posture

Confirmation discharges the debtor (with the exceptions in Section 1141(d)) and vests property of the estate in the reorganized debtor free and clear of claims and interests except as provided in the plan. The Supreme Court in Bullard v. Blue Hills Bank, 575 U.S. 496 (2015), held in the Chapter 13 context that an order denying plan confirmation is not a final, appealable order; courts have applied analogous reasoning to Chapter 11 denials, though confirmation orders themselves are routinely treated as final and reviewable.

Once confirmed, a plan binds all creditors and equity holders whether or not they voted to accept and whether or not their claims are impaired, subject only to the limited grounds for revocation under Section 1144 (fraud, brought within 180 days) and the narrow due-process exceptions recognized in United Student Aid Funds, Inc. v. Espinosa, 559 U.S. 260 (2010).
