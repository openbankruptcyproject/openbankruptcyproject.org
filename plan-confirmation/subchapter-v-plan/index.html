Subchapter V Plan: Disposable Income, 1191(b) Cramdown, and Plan Modification

A practitioner-level deep dive into the Subchapter V plan: how projected disposable income is calculated under Section 1191(d), how nonconsensual confirmation under Section 1191(b) modifies the traditional fair-and-equitable test, and how Sections 1193 and 1329 govern pre- and post-confirmation modification.

Quick Answer

Subchapter V is the Small Business Reorganization Act regime, codified at 11 U.S.C. §§ 1181–1195. The plan, filed by the debtor under Section 1189 within ninety days of the order for relief, is confirmed either consensually under Section 1191(a) (the standard Section 1129(a) requirements apply, except for paragraph 15) or nonconsensually by cramdown under Section 1191(b). The cramdown path dispenses with the absolute priority rule and the impaired-acceptance requirement and substitutes a modified "fair and equitable" standard whose centerpiece is the commitment of projected disposable income for a three-to-five-year period. Section 1193 governs modification; Section 1329 (as cross-applied) governs certain post-confirmation modifications in the deferred-discharge regime.

Governing provisions: 11 U.S.C. §§ 1181–1195; cross-applied to selected Chapter 11 sections by Section 1181(a).

Mechanics of the Disposable-Income Calculation

The most operationally significant innovation of the Subchapter V cramdown regime is the disposable-income definition in Section 1191(d). Disposable income is income received by the debtor that is not reasonably necessary for:

  • The maintenance or support of the debtor or a dependent of the debtor;
  • A domestic-support obligation that first becomes payable after the date of the filing of the petition; or
  • The payment of expenditures necessary for the continuation, preservation, or operation of the business of the debtor.

The third prong — the business-expense deduction — is the structural feature that distinguishes Subchapter V from consumer Chapter 13. A Subchapter V debtor running an operating business can deduct payroll, rent, inventory, equipment leases, insurance, utilities, professional fees, and tax obligations before computing the income that must be committed to unsecured creditors. Capital reserves necessary to preserve operations are generally treated as deductible; aggressive owner compensation, by contrast, draws scrutiny on the "reasonably necessary for support" prong.

Projection mechanics

The statute speaks of projected disposable income, which courts have generally interpreted by reference to the forward-looking framework of Hamilton v. Lanning, 560 U.S. 505 (2010). Although Lanning arose in Chapter 13, its instruction that courts may depart from a mechanical historical calculation in light of known or virtually certain changes in income or expenses is widely applied in Subchapter V. The result is that the debtor's projection model becomes a contested issue at confirmation:

  • Revenue projections must be supported by a reasonable forecasting methodology (typically a baseline tied to historical performance, with documented adjustments).
  • Expense projections should distinguish fixed and variable costs and capture seasonal patterns.
  • The debtor should explain capital-expenditure assumptions, equipment-replacement cycles, and any planned changes in the scale of operations.
  • An owner-compensation policy should be articulated and tied to comparable-business benchmarks where available.

A useful sanity check is whether the projected disposable-income line is consistent with the cash-flow line in the same plan's feasibility analysis. Inconsistencies between these two numbers are a common confirmation-objection target.

Nonconsensual Confirmation: Section 1191(b)

If all consensual confirmation requirements are met except for Section 1129(a)(8) (class acceptance), Section 1129(a)(10) (the impaired-acceptance requirement), and Section 1129(b) (the standard cramdown / absolute-priority regime), Section 1191(b) provides that the court "shall… confirm the plan notwithstanding the requirements of such paragraphs" if the plan does not discriminate unfairly and is fair and equitable.

Three structural consequences follow from this provision:

  • No impaired-acceptance gating requirement. Unlike standard Chapter 11, a Subchapter V plan can be confirmed without at least one consenting impaired class of non-insiders.
  • No absolute priority rule. Existing equity may retain its interest even if unsecured creditors are not paid in full, provided the disposable-income commitment is satisfied. This eliminates the LaSalle (Bank of America National Trust & Savings Ass'n v. 203 North LaSalle Street Partnership, 526 U.S. 434 (1999)) and Ahlers (Norwest Bank Worthington v. Ahlers, 485 U.S. 197 (1988)) obstacles that have historically constrained small-business Chapter 11 cases.
  • Deferred discharge. Under Section 1192, when confirmation is nonconsensual, the discharge is not entered until the debtor completes all payments due within the first three to five years of the plan. This creates a strong incentive to negotiate toward consensual confirmation under 1191(a), where discharge is immediate.

The Modified Fair-and-Equitable Test: Section 1191(c)

Section 1191(c) modifies the "fair and equitable" standard for Subchapter V cramdown. Three components matter at confirmation:

Secured claims

Subsection 1191(c)(1) incorporates Section 1129(b)(2)(A) for secured claims. The plan must provide retention of the lien with deferred payments equal to the present value of the collateral (the Till v. SCS Credit Corp., 541 U.S. 465 (2004), framework controls the rate calculation), sale subject to credit-bidding, or the indubitable equivalent. Collateral is valued at replacement value under Associates Commercial Corp. v. Rash, 520 U.S. 953 (1997), when the debtor proposes to retain it.

Unsecured claims — disposable-income commitment

For unsecured claims, in lieu of the absolute priority rule, Section 1191(c)(2) requires either:

  • 1191(c)(2)(A): the plan provides that all of the debtor's projected disposable income to be received in the three-to-five-year period beginning on the date of the first payment under the plan will be applied to make payments under the plan; or
  • 1191(c)(2)(B): the plan distributes property of a value, as of the effective date, equal to the projected disposable income of the debtor for that period.

The (A)/(B) choice is consequential. Under (A), the debtor commits a stream of future income subject to performance risk; under (B), the debtor commits a present-value lump sum, often funded by a third-party contribution or sale proceeds, with no performance risk in the income stream.

Reasonable likelihood and protective remedies

Subsection 1191(c)(3) imposes two further requirements: a reasonable likelihood that the debtor will be able to make all payments, and appropriate remedies (which may include the liquidation of nonexempt assets) to protect creditors if the payments are not made. These are bridges between feasibility and enforcement; a thoughtful plan addresses both with reserves, milestone reporting, and default triggers.

Pre-Confirmation Modification: Section 1193(a)

Section 1193(a) permits the debtor to modify the plan at any time before confirmation, subject only to the underlying plan-content requirements of Sections 1122 and 1123. Practical use cases include refining the disposable-income calculation in response to trustee or creditor objections, adjusting the treatment of a particular class to capture a swing vote, or substituting (A) for (B) under Section 1191(c)(2) after the financial picture changes.

Post-Confirmation Modification: Section 1193(b)–(d) and the Section 1329 Interface

Section 1193(b) governs post-confirmation modification of a plan confirmed consensually under 1191(a). The debtor (and only the debtor) may modify the plan at any time after confirmation but before substantial consummation. The modified plan must continue to meet Section 1122 and 1123 requirements; the court may confirm the modification if the circumstances warrant.

Section 1193(c) governs post-confirmation modification of a plan confirmed nonconsensually under 1191(b). Here the debtor may modify the plan at any time within the three-to-five-year period and on the same circumstances-warranted standard. The longer modification window in the cramdown regime reflects the longer payment period and the deferred-discharge structure.

Section 1193(d) provides for binding effect of confirmed modifications on all creditors, again subject to the underlying confirmation standards. The Subchapter V trustee, where one remains in office, plays a continuing role in evaluating and recommending action on modifications.

The Section 1329 interface

Section 1329 of the Code governs Chapter 13 plan modification and is not, strictly, applied within Subchapter V; Section 1181(a) does not list 1329 among the Chapter 11 provisions made inapplicable, but the statutory home for Subchapter V modification is 1193 itself. The relevant cross-reference for practitioners is conceptual: courts comparing the deferred-discharge Subchapter V regime to Chapter 13 frequently borrow Section 1329 reasoning on issues such as substantial change in circumstances and the appropriate standard for trustee-initiated modification. The Supreme Court's interpretation of the analogous Chapter 13 framework in Hamilton v. Lanning, supra, and the appellate finality teaching of Bullard v. Blue Hills Bank, 575 U.S. 496 (2015), inform how lower courts evaluate the timing and reviewability of modification orders in Subchapter V as well.

Discharge Finality and the Espinosa Lesson

Once a Subchapter V plan is confirmed, the binding effect of confirmation on creditors is broad. The Supreme Court's holding in United Student Aid Funds, Inc. v. Espinosa, 559 U.S. 260 (2010), that even a confirmation order that improperly granted relief was not "void" but only "voidable" applies with full force in the Subchapter V context. Creditors who fail to object to a plan provision on a known basis — including disposable-income mathematics, a defective remedies clause, or an aggressive owner-compensation policy — will generally find the issue foreclosed once confirmation becomes final and non-appealable.
