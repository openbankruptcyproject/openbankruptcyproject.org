Chapter 13 Plan Confirmation: Plan Contents, Confirmation, and the Disposable-Income Test

A complete walk-through of how a Chapter 13 plan is confirmed: mandatory and permissive plan contents under Section 1322, the confirmation requirements of Section 1325, the applicable commitment period, projected disposable income after Hamilton v. Lanning, and the doctrine of lien stripping in Chapter 13.

Quick Answer

A Chapter 13 plan is the centerpiece of an individual debtor's reorganization. The debtor files the plan within fourteen days of the petition (Federal Rule of Bankruptcy Procedure 3015(b)), and the contents of the plan are governed by Section 1322. The court applies the confirmation requirements of Section 1325, including the best-interests test, the good-faith test, the secured-creditor cramdown standard, and (for above-median-income debtors) the projected-disposable-income test. The plan must run for the applicable commitment period — three or five years — determined by the debtor's income relative to the state median.

Governing provisions: 11 U.S.C. §§ 1321–1330.

Contents of the Plan: Section 1322

Section 1322(a) lists four mandatory provisions. The plan must:

  • Provide for the submission of all or such portion of future earnings or other future income as is necessary for execution of the plan.
  • Provide for full payment, in deferred cash payments, of all priority claims under Section 507 (subject to limited exceptions for domestic-support obligations assigned to a governmental unit and certain tax priorities).
  • Provide the same treatment for each claim within a particular class.
  • Provide for the payment of post-petition interest on nondischargeable unsecured claims (added by BAPCPA in 2005, to the extent the debtor has projected disposable income to fund it).

Section 1322(b) lists twelve permissive provisions. Several are case-defining in practice:

  • 1322(b)(2): The plan may modify the rights of holders of secured claims, other than a claim secured only by a security interest in real property that is the debtor's principal residence. The anti-modification clause for primary residences is the source of decades of "lien-stripping" litigation discussed below.
  • 1322(b)(3): The plan may cure or waive any default.
  • 1322(b)(5): Notwithstanding 1322(b)(2), the plan may provide for cure of any default within a reasonable time and maintenance of payments while the case is pending on any claim — including a home mortgage — on which the last payment is due after the plan period. This is the principal mechanism for saving a home from foreclosure in Chapter 13.

Section 1322(d) caps the plan period at five years, and at three years for below-median-income debtors absent cause to extend.

Confirmation Requirements: Section 1325(a)

Section 1325(a) lists nine confirmation requirements that always apply. The court "shall confirm" the plan if:

  1. The plan complies with Chapter 13 and other applicable provisions of the Code.
  2. Filing fees have been paid.
  3. The plan has been proposed in good faith and not by any means forbidden by law.
  4. Best-interests test: the value, as of the effective date, of property distributed to each unsecured claim holder is not less than what the holder would receive in Chapter 7.
  5. For each secured claim provided for by the plan: (i) the secured creditor has accepted; (ii) the plan retains the lien and provides distribution of present value not less than the allowed secured amount; or (iii) the debtor surrenders the collateral.
  6. The debtor will be able to make all payments and otherwise comply with the plan (feasibility).
  7. The petition was filed in good faith.
  8. The debtor has paid post-petition domestic-support obligations as they came due.
  9. The debtor has filed all required tax returns under Section 1308.

The Supreme Court's decision in United Student Aid Funds, Inc. v. Espinosa, 559 U.S. 260 (2010), held that even a confirmation order that improperly discharged a student loan without an adversary proceeding was not "void" under Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 60(b)(4), but only "voidable," and could not be collaterally attacked years later. The opinion is a strong endorsement of confirmation finality and a cautionary lesson for creditors who fail to object at the right time.

Cramdown of Secured Claims and the Till Rate

Section 1325(a)(5)(B) permits cramdown of a non-residential secured claim by retaining the lien and distributing present value equal to the allowed secured amount. In Till v. SCS Credit Corp., 541 U.S. 465 (2004), a plurality of the Supreme Court adopted the "formula" or "prime-plus" approach: start with the national prime rate and adjust upward to reflect the risk of nonpayment. Lower courts have largely converged on a one-to-three-percent upward adjustment in routine cases, with greater adjustments available on a fact-specific showing.

For valuation of the collateral, Associates Commercial Corp. v. Rash, 520 U.S. 953 (1997), requires replacement value — "the price a willing buyer in the debtor's trade, business, or situation would pay to obtain like property from a willing seller" — when the debtor retains and uses the collateral. The 2005 amendments added the "hanging paragraph" after Section 1325(a)(9), eliminating cramdown for purchase-money car loans on vehicles acquired for personal use within 910 days of filing.

Section 1325(b): Applicable Commitment Period and Projected Disposable Income

If the trustee or an allowed unsecured creditor objects to confirmation, Section 1325(b) requires the plan to commit either (i) the value of property distributed under the plan to be not less than the allowed amount of each unsecured claim, or (ii) all of the debtor's projected disposable income for the applicable commitment period to payments under the plan.

Applicable commitment period

Section 1325(b)(4) sets the applicable commitment period at three years for below-median-income debtors and five years for above-median-income debtors. The Supreme Court in Hamilton v. Lanning, 560 U.S. 505 (2010), endorsed a "forward-looking" approach to projected disposable income: courts begin with the historical Form 122C calculation but may adjust for known or virtually certain changes in the debtor's income or expenses.

Projected disposable income

The mechanical calculation begins with current monthly income (the six-month average) and subtracts the expense allowances permitted by Section 707(b)(2) for above-median-income debtors or reasonably necessary expenses for below-median-income debtors. The forward-looking adjustments approved in Lanning — loss of a job, end of a temporary side income, retirement of a major debt — can move the calculation substantially in either direction.

Lien Stripping in Chapter 13

The Supreme Court in Nobelman v. American Savings Bank, 508 U.S. 324 (1993), held that Section 1322(b)(2)'s anti-modification clause prevents stripping down a partially secured residential mortgage to the value of the collateral, even where the collateral is worth less than the claim. A junior mortgage that is wholly unsecured (the senior debt exceeds the value of the home), however, is generally treated by lower courts as not "secured" within the meaning of 1322(b)(2) and may be stripped off entirely. That distinction — partial undersecuring versus complete undersecuring — is the operational dividing line.

For non-residential secured claims, valuation under Section 506(a) permits the plan to strip the claim down to the value of the collateral. Excess unsecured deficiency is then paid on the same terms as other general unsecured claims.

Post-Confirmation Modification: Section 1329

Section 1329 permits the debtor, the trustee, or an allowed unsecured creditor to seek modification of a confirmed plan to increase or reduce payments, extend or reduce the time for payments, or alter the amount of distribution to a particular creditor whose claim is paid other than under the plan. The modification must continue to satisfy the Section 1325(a) confirmation requirements, but the plan period may not be extended past five years from the date the first payment was due under the original plan.

The Supreme Court in Bullard v. Blue Hills Bank, 575 U.S. 496 (2015), held that an order denying confirmation of a Chapter 13 plan is not a final, appealable order — the debtor's remedy is to file a modified plan and pursue confirmation again. The opinion narrows the appellate routes available to a debtor whose original plan is rejected and reinforces the iterative nature of Chapter 13 plan confirmation.

Effect of Confirmation and Discharge

Under Section 1327(a), the provisions of a confirmed Chapter 13 plan bind the debtor and each creditor, whether or not the creditor objected to or accepted the plan. Property of the estate vests in the debtor on confirmation under Section 1327(b), free and clear of any claim or interest of any creditor provided for by the plan. The Section 1328(a) discharge follows completion of all payments and is broader than the Chapter 7 discharge in important respects (no Section 727(a)(8) eight-year bar applies to the underlying chapter, and certain non-priority unsecured claims that would survive in Chapter 7 can be discharged in Chapter 13).

The Supreme Court's enforcement-of-finality holding in United Student Aid Funds, Inc. v. Espinosa, 559 U.S. 260 (2010), is particularly important in Chapter 13 because the long plan period invites creditor inattention. A creditor that fails to object at confirmation generally cannot collaterally attack the order years later, even where the plan provision is legally questionable. The corollary for debtors is that confirmation provisions designed to bind a particular creditor must be drafted with conspicuous clarity and supported by adequate notice.
