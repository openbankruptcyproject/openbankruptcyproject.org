Federal Rule of Bankruptcy Procedure 9014

Rule 9014: Contested Matters

Motion practice in bankruptcy, the Rule 7001 line that separates contested matters from adversary proceedings, and the Part VII rules applied under Rule 9014(c).

Verbatim text of Rule 9014(a)

In a contested matter not otherwise governed by these rules, relief shall be requested by motion, and reasonable notice and opportunity for hearing shall be afforded the party against whom relief is sought. No response is required under this rule unless the court directs otherwise. Fed. R. Bankr. P. 9014(a).

The contested-matter / adversary-proceeding distinction

The Federal Rules of Bankruptcy Procedure divide disputes that arise in a bankruptcy case into two procedural tracks. The dividing line is drawn by Rule 7001, which enumerates ten categories of disputes that must be brought as adversary proceedings - separately captioned lawsuits filed with their own docket within the main bankruptcy case, governed by the Part VII rules that closely track the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure.

Any dispute that is not within one of the ten Rule 7001 categories is a "contested matter" governed by Rule 9014. Contested matters are initiated by motion within the main bankruptcy case, without a separate complaint, summons, or adversary docket.

The ten Rule 7001 adversary-proceeding categories

Rule 7001 requires an adversary proceeding for:

  1. Actions to recover money or property (with specified exceptions)
  2. Actions to determine the validity, priority, or extent of a lien or other interest in property
  3. Actions to obtain approval of the sale of both the interest of the estate and a co-owner's interest in property
  4. Actions to object to or revoke a discharge
  5. Actions to revoke an order of confirmation of a Chapter 11, Chapter 12, or Chapter 13 plan
  6. Actions to determine the dischargeability of a debt
  7. Actions to obtain an injunction or other equitable relief (with specified exceptions)
  8. Actions to subordinate any allowed claim or interest (with specified exceptions)
  9. Actions to obtain a declaratory judgment relating to any of the foregoing
  10. Actions to determine a claim or cause of action removed under 28 U.S.C. Section 1452

Common contested-matter examples

Disputes that are not within Rule 7001 and therefore proceed by motion under Rule 9014 include:

  • Motions for relief from the automatic stay under 11 U.S.C. Section 362(d)
  • Objections to proofs of claim (unless the objection is combined with an affirmative claim against the claimant, which converts it into an adversary)
  • Motions to assume, assume and assign, or reject executory contracts under Section 365
  • Motions to sell estate property under Section 363
  • Motions to approve compromises and settlements under Rule 9019
  • Motions to convert or dismiss a case under Sections 1112, 1208, or 1307
  • Applications for compensation under Section 330
  • Motions for sanctions under Rule 9011
  • Motions to use cash collateral or obtain post-petition financing under Sections 363 and 364

Rule 9014(b) - service of the motion

Rule 9014(b) requires that the motion be served on the party against whom relief is sought in the same manner as a summons and complaint under Rule 7004 - the Part VII service rule. Service by ordinary mail to counsel is generally insufficient; service must be made on the party in the manner specified by Rule 7004 for the type of party involved (individual, corporation, partnership, governmental unit, insured depository institution, etc.). The 2017 amendment to Rule 9014(b) eliminated the prior cross-reference to Rule 7005 and clarified that subsequent papers may be served as provided in Rule 7005.

Rule 9014(c) - applicable Part VII rules

Rule 9014(c) imports several Part VII rules into contested matters as if the matter were an adversary proceeding. The rules made applicable by Rule 9014(c) include:

  • Rule 7009 (pleadings)
  • Rule 7017 (parties)
  • Rule 7021 (misjoinder and non-joinder)
  • Rule 7025 (substitution of parties)
  • Rule 7026 (discovery)
  • Rule 7028 through Rule 7037 (the discovery rules - depositions, interrogatories, production, admissions, sanctions)
  • Rule 7041 (dismissal of actions)
  • Rule 7042 (consolidation and separate trials)
  • Rule 7052 (findings by the court)
  • Rule 7054 through Rule 7056 (judgments, costs, summary judgment)
  • Rule 7064 (seizures)
  • Rule 7069 (execution)
  • Rule 7071 (process in behalf of and against persons not parties)

The court may, at any stage of a particular contested matter, direct that one or more of the other Part VII rules apply. This discretion is most commonly exercised to import the pleading or amendment rules (Rules 7008, 7012, 7015) when the complexity of the dispute warrants it.

Discovery in contested matters

Because Rule 9014(c) imports the discovery rules (Rules 7026 through 7037), discovery in contested matters proceeds substantially as it does in adversary proceedings - depositions, interrogatories, requests for production, requests for admission, and the corresponding scope, protective-order, and sanctions framework all apply. The principal practical differences are:

  • Discovery in a contested matter is typically conducted on an expedited timeline reflecting the often-urgent nature of the underlying motion (stay relief, cash collateral, cure dispute, sale objection)
  • Rule 7026(f) initial-disclosure procedure is often modified or waived by local rule or court order in routine contested matters
  • Pretrial scheduling under Rule 7016 is incorporated only at the court's direction

Evidentiary standards

Contested-matter hearings are evidentiary unless the parties and the court agree otherwise. The Federal Rules of Evidence apply (made applicable to bankruptcy proceedings by 28 U.S.C. Section 2075 and Federal Rule of Evidence 1101). The court receives testimony, admits exhibits, and makes findings of fact and conclusions of law under Rule 7052 (made applicable to contested matters by Rule 9014(c)).

The burden of proof is allocated by the underlying substantive law. For example:

  • In a motion for relief from the automatic stay under Section 362(d), the moving party bears the burden of showing cause or lack of equity; the party opposing relief bears the burden on all other issues, including adequate protection, per Section 362(g)
  • In a claim objection (a contested matter unless combined with affirmative relief), a properly filed proof of claim is prima facie evidence of validity and amount under Rule 3001(f); the objector bears the burden of producing evidence to overcome the presumption
  • In a motion to approve a sale under Section 363(b), the trustee or debtor in possession bears the burden of showing a sound business justification

Conversion of a contested matter to an adversary proceeding

When a contested matter is filed but the relief actually sought falls within Rule 7001, the court may order that the matter proceed as an adversary proceeding. The procedural posture must be corrected because Rule 7001 categories carry due-process protections - particularly summons service and a formal complaint-and-answer pleading regime - that contested-matter motion practice does not replicate. A claim objection that asks the court not only to disallow a claim but also to recover prepetition transfers from the claimant must be filed as an adversary proceeding because the affirmative recovery falls within Rule 7001(1).

Common procedural posture

Stay-relief contested matter

A secured creditor files a motion for relief from the automatic stay. Service is made on the debtor under Rule 7004. The debtor responds with a written objection. The court holds a preliminary hearing within 30 days (per Section 362(e)) and, if cause is shown, conducts an evidentiary final hearing with witnesses and exhibits. The court issues findings of fact and conclusions of law under Rule 7052.

Claim objection

The debtor or trustee files an objection to a proof of claim. The objection is a contested matter. Discovery proceeds under the Part VII rules. The court holds an evidentiary hearing. If the debtor wishes to recover money from the claimant in addition to disallowing the claim, the matter must be brought as an adversary proceeding under Rule 7001(1).

Rule 9014(d) - testimony of witnesses

Rule 9014(d) provides that, in a contested matter, the court may direct that testimony of witnesses with respect to disputed material factual issues be taken in the same manner as testimony in an adversary proceeding. In practice, this means live testimony at the hearing rather than submission on declarations or affidavits. The court's discretion under Rule 9014(d) is broad; many contested matters involving disputed facts proceed on live testimony as a matter of course.

The Rule 7001 line is jurisdictional in effect. A motion filed as a contested matter that seeks relief properly classified under Rule 7001 may be procedurally defective even if the merits are sound. Counsel should evaluate the Rule 7001 enumeration before selecting the procedural vehicle; the choice of motion versus complaint is not discretionary.
