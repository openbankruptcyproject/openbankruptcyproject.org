Federal Rule of Bankruptcy Procedure 3001

Rule 3001: Proof of Claim and Supporting Documentation

Official Form 410, Rule 3001(c) writing requirement, Rule 3001(f) prima facie validity, and the circuit split on stale collection-statute claims.

What Rule 3001 does

Federal Rule of Bankruptcy Procedure 3001 specifies the form a proof of claim must take, the supporting documentation a claimant must attach, the evidentiary effect of a properly filed proof of claim, and supplemental disclosure obligations for consumer claims and claims secured by an interest in the debtor's principal residence. A proof of claim that complies with Rule 3001 is the principal vehicle by which a creditor asserts a right to payment from the bankruptcy estate.

Official Form 410

Rule 3001(a) requires that a proof of claim "conform substantially" to the appropriate Official Form. Official Form 410 is the standard proof-of-claim form used in all chapters and is structured into discrete fields covering creditor identification, the basis of the claim, the amount claimed, secured-claim disclosures, priority-claim disclosures, and the claimant's signature under Section 152 false-claim warnings. The Form 410 series includes:

  • Form 410 - Proof of claim (the principal form)
  • Form 410A - Mortgage proof of claim attachment (required for any claim secured by the debtor's principal residence under Rule 3001(c)(2)(C))
  • Form 410S1 - Notice of mortgage payment change (required for ongoing changes in a residential mortgage under Rule 3002.1(b))
  • Form 410S2 - Notice of post-petition mortgage fees, expenses, and charges (required under Rule 3002.1(c))

Rule 3001(c) - the writing-attachment requirement

Rule 3001(c) requires that a claim "based on a writing" be supported by a copy of the writing filed with the proof of claim. The requirement applies to the underlying contract, note, security agreement, judgment, or other instrument that establishes the claim. Rule 3001(c)(2), added in 2011 and amended thereafter, imposes additional disclosure for claims by individual debtors:

  • (c)(2)(A) Itemization. If the claim includes interest, fees, expenses, or other charges incurred before the petition date, an itemized statement of the interest, fees, expenses, or charges must accompany the proof of claim.
  • (c)(2)(B) Escrow. If a security interest is claimed in the debtor's property, a statement of the amount necessary to cure any default as of the petition date must accompany the claim.
  • (c)(2)(C) Principal residence. If the claim is secured by a security interest in the debtor's principal residence, the proof of claim must include Form 410A (the mortgage proof of claim attachment), which itemizes the principal balance, accrued and unpaid interest, prepetition fees, escrow balance, and the loan-history information sufficient to demonstrate how the claim amount was calculated.
  • (c)(2)(D) Sanctions for noncompliance. If the claimant fails to provide the required information, the court may, after notice and hearing, preclude the claimant from presenting the omitted information as evidence in any contested matter or adversary proceeding and may award expenses (including attorney's fees) to the prevailing party.

Rule 3001(f) - prima facie validity

Rule 3001(f) provides:

A proof of claim executed and filed in accordance with these rules shall constitute prima facie evidence of the validity and amount of the claim. Fed. R. Bankr. P. 3001(f).

The prima facie validity rule is a procedural burden-shifting mechanism. When a proof of claim is filed in compliance with Rule 3001 (i.e., on the correct form, with required attachments, with required supplemental disclosures, and signed under penalty of perjury), the claim is presumed valid and the burden of going forward shifts to the party objecting to the claim. The objector must present evidence "equal in force to the prima facie case" to overcome the presumption; once the objector does so, the burden of persuasion remains with the claimant by a preponderance of the evidence.

A proof of claim that does not comply with Rule 3001 - for example, a claim that omits required attachments, omits required Rule 3001(c)(2) itemization, or is signed by an unauthorized representative - is not entitled to the prima facie validity presumption. Such a claim may still be allowed if the claimant proves it by a preponderance of evidence at a contested-matter hearing, but the claimant carries the initial burden of going forward.

The In re Heath line and the stale-claim circuit split

One of the most-litigated questions under Rule 3001 has been whether a creditor (typically a debt buyer) violates the Fair Debt Collection Practices Act by filing a proof of claim on a debt as to which the applicable state-law statute of limitations has expired. The leading early decision is In re Heath, 331 B.R. 424 (B.A.P. 9th Cir. 2005), which addressed (in a different posture) the documentation a claimant must attach to support a credit-card claim.

The stale-claim FDCPA controversy reached the Supreme Court in Midland Funding, LLC v. Johnson, 137 S. Ct. 1407 (2017). The Court held 5-3 that filing a proof of claim on a debt that is "obviously time-barred" by the applicable state statute of limitations is not a violation of the FDCPA, reasoning that the proof of claim is a "claim" within the Bankruptcy Code's definition (a right to payment, whether or not enforceable) and that the bankruptcy claims-allowance process provides the trustee and the debtor adequate procedural protection. Midland Funding resolved the FDCPA question but left intact the procedural rule under 11 U.S.C. Section 502(b)(1) that an objection asserting the statute of limitations is a valid ground for disallowance.

The practical effect of Midland Funding is that a debt buyer may file a proof of claim on a time-barred debt without FDCPA exposure, but the trustee or debtor may object and the bankruptcy court must disallow the claim if the applicable statute of limitations has run. Several circuits had previously held (before Midland Funding) that the FDCPA prohibited such filings; the Court's decision unified the rule in favor of permissible filing-subject-to-objection.

Filing and amendment of proofs of claim

Rule 3002(c) sets the deadline for filing a proof of claim in voluntary Chapter 7, Chapter 12, and Chapter 13 cases: 70 days after the order for relief (with longer windows for governmental units under Section 502(b)(9)). In Chapter 11 cases, the court sets the bar date by order. A proof of claim filed after the bar date is generally disallowed unless an exception applies (excusable neglect, late-filed government claim within the statutory window, etc.).

Amendment of a proof of claim is permitted as a matter of right at any time before the bar date and may be permitted after the bar date when the amendment relates back to a timely-filed claim and does not assert a wholly new ground of recovery. Courts apply a discretionary standard to post-bar-date amendments, weighing prejudice to other creditors against the equities supporting amendment.

Transfer of claim (Rule 3001(e))

Rule 3001(e) governs the transfer of a claim. When a claim is transferred after the proof of claim is filed, the transferee files a notice of transfer with the clerk; the clerk gives notice to the alleged transferor, who has 21 days to object. If no objection is timely filed, the transferee is substituted for the transferor as the holder of the claim. The transfer-of-claim procedure is the principal vehicle by which debt buyers acquire and prosecute consumer claims in active bankruptcy cases.

Common procedural posture

Credit-card claim without itemization

A debt buyer files a proof of claim on a charged-off credit-card account but does not attach an account statement or itemize the interest and fees accrued before the charge-off date. The debtor objects under 11 U.S.C. Section 502(b)(1) and asserts the Rule 3001(c)(2)(A) itemization failure. The claim is not entitled to prima facie validity; the claimant must produce the underlying account documentation to carry its burden of proof.

Mortgage claim missing Form 410A

A mortgage servicer files a proof of claim on a residential mortgage without attaching the required Form 410A. The Chapter 13 trustee objects. The court treats the noncompliant filing as failing the Rule 3001(c)(2)(C) requirement, denies the claim prima facie validity, and may, under Rule 3001(c)(2)(D), preclude the servicer from later introducing the omitted loan-history evidence in any contested matter.

Form compliance is not optional for prima facie validity. Filing a proof of claim on the correct form, with required attachments and required supplemental disclosures, is the procedural predicate for the Rule 3001(f) presumption. A claim that skips the required Rule 3001(c)(2) disclosures may still be allowed, but the claimant carries the burden of going forward and may face Rule 3001(c)(2)(D) preclusion sanctions.
