Bankruptcy Appellate Panels

A Bankruptcy Appellate Panel (BAP) is a three-judge panel of bankruptcy judges authorized by 28 U.S.C. section 158(b) to hear appeals from bankruptcy-court decisions in lieu of the district court. Five circuits currently maintain active BAPs: the First, Sixth, Eighth, Ninth, and Tenth. The remaining circuits route bankruptcy appeals directly to the district court. These guides survey each active BAP's composition, opt-out rules, jurisdiction, and procedural quirks.

About the BAP system

Congress authorized BAPs in the Bankruptcy Reform Act of 1978 and refined the framework in the 1994 Bankruptcy Reform Act. A BAP exists only by judicial-council order and only if the council determines that there are insufficient judicial resources in the district courts to handle bankruptcy appeals. In circuits without a BAP, bankruptcy-court appeals proceed under 28 U.S.C. section 158(a) to the district court.

Where a BAP exists, every appeal from a bankruptcy-court final order is automatically routed to the BAP unless either party elects to have the appeal heard by the district court under 28 U.S.C. section 158(c)(1). The election must be timely - generally within 30 days of service of the notice of appeal - and is irrevocable once made. Strategic considerations for the election include the perceived expertise of the BAP (its judges hear bankruptcy appeals every day), the binding effect of its opinions within the circuit, and the procedural calendar.

BAP decisions bind the panel itself and (in most circuits) the bankruptcy courts within the circuit, but they are not binding on the district courts or on the court of appeals. Further review proceeds by appeal as of right to the court of appeals under 28 U.S.C. section 158(d)(1), or, in narrow circumstances, by direct certification to the court of appeals under section 158(d)(2).

Circuit-by-circuit guides

  • First Circuit BAPComposition, opt-out rules, citation conventions, and the role of the BAP in the courts of Maine, Massachusetts, New Hampshire, Puerto Rico, and Rhode Island.
  • Sixth Circuit BAPThree-judge panels drawn from Kentucky, Michigan, Ohio, and Tennessee bankruptcy courts; opt-out timing and the relationship to district-court review.
  • Eighth Circuit BAPBAP composition across Arkansas, Iowa, Minnesota, Missouri, Nebraska, and North and South Dakota; binding effect within the circuit.
  • Ninth Circuit BAPThe oldest and most-cited BAP; opt-out election timing under Ninth Circuit Rule 8005-1, and the use of memorandum dispositions.
  • Tenth Circuit BAPComposition across Colorado, Kansas, New Mexico, Oklahoma, Utah, and Wyoming; opt-out mechanics and citation rules.
Election timing: The right to opt out of the BAP and proceed to the district court is governed by Bankruptcy Rule 8005 and 28 U.S.C. section 158(c)(1). Missing the deadline is irrevocable. Counsel evaluating appellate strategy should make the election decision in the first week after entry of the appealable order.
