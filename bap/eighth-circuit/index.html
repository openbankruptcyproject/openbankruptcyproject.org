Eighth Circuit Bankruptcy Appellate Panel - Practitioner Guide 2026

First published 2026-05-22/Last modified 2026-05-22/CC-BY-4.0

Abstract

The Bankruptcy Appellate Panel for the Eighth Circuit (8th Cir. BAP) is an Article I appellate forum established by the Judicial Council of the Eighth Circuit under 28 U.S.C. § 158(b). Three-judge panels of sitting bankruptcy judges drawn from the bankruptcy courts within the circuit hear appeals from final orders of those courts when all parties consent under the election procedure of § 158(c)(1). This guide summarizes panel composition, the election and district-opt-out mechanics, the panel's appellate jurisdiction, briefing under Part VIII of the Federal Rules of Bankruptcy Procedure as supplemented by Eighth Circuit BAP local rules, and the strategic factors bearing on the choice of forum between the panel and the United States District Court.

Authorization and Geographic Coverage

The Eighth Circuit BAP was established by order of the Judicial Council of the Eighth Circuit pursuant to 28 U.S.C. § 158(b)(1). The panel hears appeals arising from the bankruptcy courts of the seven states comprising the Eighth Circuit, which together include ten federal judicial districts:

  • Eastern District of Arkansas (E.D. Ark. Bankr.)
  • Western District of Arkansas (W.D. Ark. Bankr.)
  • Northern District of Iowa (N.D. Iowa Bankr.)
  • Southern District of Iowa (S.D. Iowa Bankr.)
  • District of Minnesota (D. Minn. Bankr.)
  • Eastern District of Missouri (E.D. Mo. Bankr.)
  • Western District of Missouri (W.D. Mo. Bankr.)
  • District of Nebraska (D. Neb. Bankr.)
  • District of North Dakota (D.N.D. Bankr.)
  • District of South Dakota (D.S.D. Bankr.)

The panel's clerk's office is administratively colocated with the office of the Clerk of the United States Court of Appeals for the Eighth Circuit in St. Louis. Counsel should verify the current authorization status of each constituent district through the BAP clerk's office, because districts may withdraw or modify authorization for BAP service under § 158(b)(6).

Panel Composition

Under 28 U.S.C. § 158(b)(1)(A), bankruptcy appellate panels are composed of bankruptcy judges from districts within the circuit. The Eighth Circuit BAP draws its members from the bankruptcy benches of the constituent districts listed above. A sitting member of the panel cannot hear an appeal arising from his or her own home district under § 158(b)(5), which preserves the appearance of independent appellate review and prevents same-district peer review.

Each appeal is decided by a panel of three judges. Panels are constituted ad hoc for each appeal from the available pool of designated judges, subject to the same-district disqualification rule. The presiding judge of the BAP is designated by the Judicial Council and rotates on a defined cycle. Opinions issue per curiam or under a single authoring judge; concurrences and dissents are reported alongside the lead opinion.

The Eighth Circuit BAP maintains a substantial published body of bankruptcy appellate opinion, with decisions reported in West's Bankruptcy Reporter and available through commercial and free legal-research platforms. Several Eighth Circuit BAP opinions have been adopted by the Eighth Circuit Court of Appeals on further appeal.

Election Procedure and District Opt-Out

Two consent layers govern whether an appeal proceeds to the BAP rather than to the district court. The first is district-level: under 28 U.S.C. § 158(b)(6), each judicial district within a BAP circuit may, by majority vote of its district judges, decline to authorize BAP service for appeals arising from that district. Practitioners should verify current authorization status by district through the panel's clerk's office, because authorizations can be modified by subsequent district order.

The second layer is party-level: under 28 U.S.C. § 158(c)(1), even where the district has authorized BAP service, an appeal is heard by the panel only if all parties to the appeal consent. The mechanics work as a default-and-opt-out: the appellant's filing of a notice of appeal under Federal Rule of Bankruptcy Procedure 8003 routes the appeal to the BAP unless a party files a timely statement of election to have the appeal heard by the district court instead.

Federal Rule of Bankruptcy Procedure 8005 governs the election. The statement of election must be filed with the clerk of the bankruptcy court within thirty (30) days after service of the notice of appeal. If the electing party is the appellant, the election must accompany the notice of appeal. Failure to file a timely election by any party is treated as consent to BAP review.

Election is a unilateral procedural right. The statement need not state grounds; no court approval is required; and the panel cannot retain an appeal over a timely election. Counsel weighing the choice of forum should accordingly assume that a single objecting party can defeat BAP routing. Counsel for prospective appellees in cases where BAP review is strategically preferred should canvas opposing counsel informally on the election question before incurring drafting costs predicated on either forum.

Appellate Jurisdiction

The panel's subject-matter jurisdiction under 28 U.S.C. § 158(a) extends to:

  • Final judgments, orders, and decrees of the bankruptcy courts under § 158(a)(1);
  • Interlocutory orders increasing or reducing the time fixed under 11 U.S.C. § 1121(d) for filing a plan, under § 158(a)(2); and
  • Other interlocutory orders, with leave of the panel, under § 158(a)(3).

Finality in bankruptcy is interpreted more pragmatically than in ordinary civil litigation. Orders resolving discrete disputes within a larger case may be final and immediately appealable even where the underlying case continues. The panel applies the Eighth Circuit's prevailing finality framework when determining whether a challenged order meets the § 158(a)(1) threshold.

Interlocutory appeals under § 158(a)(3) require a motion for leave. The panel applies a standard derived from 28 U.S.C. § 1292(b): controlling question of law, substantial ground for difference of opinion, and material advancement of the ultimate termination of the litigation. Leave is discretionary and is sparingly granted.

Briefing and Calendar

Briefing in the BAP is governed by Part VIII of the Federal Rules of Bankruptcy Procedure as supplemented by the Eighth Circuit BAP local rules. The default schedule under FRBP 8018 runs from the filing of the appeal record:

  • Appellant's brief: 30 days after the docketing of the appeal in the BAP
  • Appellee's brief: 30 days after service of the appellant's brief
  • Appellant's reply brief: 21 days after service of the appellee's brief (and at least 7 days before argument)

Word and page limits track FRBP 8015. The appellant's principal brief and the appellee's principal brief are each limited to 13,000 words; reply briefs are limited to 6,500 words. Certificates of compliance, jurisdictional statements, and required disclosure statements track Part VIII practice.

Oral argument is set by the panel after briefing closes. The panel may decide an appeal without argument under FRBP 8019 if the legal arguments are adequately presented in the briefs and oral argument would not significantly aid the decisional process. The Eighth Circuit BAP hears argument by video conference and at sittings convened in the circuit's principal cities (including St. Louis, Minneapolis, and Kansas City) on the panel's calendar.

Standards of Review

The panel applies the same standards of review applicable to appeals taken to the district court under 28 U.S.C. § 158(a). Findings of fact are reviewed for clear error; conclusions of law are reviewed de novo; mixed questions are reviewed under the standard appropriate to the question, with the legal component receiving de novo review. Discretionary determinations (such as approvals under 11 U.S.C. § 363, sanctions, plan confirmation in the discretionary fairness aspects of 11 U.S.C. § 1129, and most case-management rulings) are reviewed for abuse of discretion.

The panel's decisions are reviewable on further appeal to the United States Court of Appeals for the Eighth Circuit under 28 U.S.C. § 158(d)(1). The court of appeals applies the same standards to bankruptcy court rulings that the BAP applied; the BAP's intermediate decision receives no formal deference on questions of law in the further appeal.

Contrast with District-Court Appeals

An appeal to the district court under 28 U.S.C. § 158(a) is heard by a single district judge sitting as an appellate court. An appeal to the BAP is heard by a three-judge panel of sitting bankruptcy judges. Several practical differences follow:

Bench expertise. BAP judges are full-time bankruptcy judges with concentrated subject-matter exposure. District judges hear bankruptcy appeals as one part of a general civil and criminal docket. For appeals turning on contested or developing bankruptcy doctrine, the panel's specialization is often the principal substantive consideration. The Eighth Circuit covers two of the largest farm-and-agricultural-creditor markets in the country, and the BAP's bench has developed particular fluency in Chapter 12 and agricultural-secured-creditor disputes.

Panel deliberation versus single judge. Three judges reviewing the same record introduce a deliberative element that a single-judge appeal lacks. Concurrences and dissents in BAP opinions can preserve issues and shape framing for further appeal to the court of appeals.

Precedential weight. BAP decisions bind the parties to the appeal but do not bind bankruptcy courts in the circuit as a matter of formal stare decisis under the prevailing view. District-court decisions on bankruptcy appeals likewise do not formally bind other district judges or other bankruptcy judges, though both forums' decisions carry substantial persuasive weight within the circuit. Court of appeals decisions bind both forums.

Calendar and disposition time. BAP calendars tend to move on a more predictable timeline than the bankruptcy appeal queue of a busy district court, where appeals share the calendar with the district's primary civil and criminal workload. The Eighth Circuit BAP has historically maintained median disposition times that compare favorably with the bankruptcy-appeal dispositions of the circuit's larger district benches.

When the BAP Is Preferable

The following considerations tend to favor BAP review when the choice is open:

  • Pure or principally legal questions in core bankruptcy doctrine (avoidance, claim classification, plan confirmation standards, automatic stay scope, discharge, Chapter 13 plan modification, Chapter 12 agricultural confirmation issues) where a specialist bench is most likely to engage the issue at depth.
  • Appeals from non-routine procedural rulings where the panel's familiarity with bankruptcy court calendaring and motion practice produces a more textured review than a generalist district judge can ordinarily provide.
  • Cases where the appellate record is dense with bankruptcy-specific factual context (plan economics, claim hierarchies, fee structures) that benefits from a bench fluent in the underlying vocabulary.
  • Cases where panel consensus will strengthen persuasive value in any further appeal, particularly where the issue is one of first impression in the circuit.

The following considerations tend to favor district-court review:

  • Appeals turning principally on non-bankruptcy substantive law (state-law contract interpretation, federal tax law, securities law, ERISA, employment-discrimination cross-claims) where the district judge's broader subject-matter range is closer to the dispositive question.
  • Appeals with significant constitutional, Stern v. Marshall, or Article III concerns where the district court's Article III status itself is part of the analytical framing.
  • Strategic considerations specific to known docket conditions at the district level or to known dispositions of district judges on bankruptcy-adjacent legal questions.

Practical Filing Notes

A notice of appeal is filed in the bankruptcy court under FRBP 8003. The appeal is transmitted to the BAP by the bankruptcy clerk unless a party files a timely election to have the appeal heard by the district court instead under FRBP 8005. The thirty-day election window runs from service of the notice of appeal; counsel for appellees should calendar this date promptly because election is the single procedural decision that controls forum.

The BAP's filing fee mirrors the district-court fee schedule. Electronic filing is mandatory; the BAP uses CM/ECF infrastructure with credentials administered through the circuit clerk. Briefing extensions are routinely granted on motion for good cause, but the panel discourages serial extensions and counsel should plan for the default schedule.
