KRPC 5.3 / MRPC 4-5.3: Responsibilities Regarding Non-Lawyer Assistants

Firm-management duty, direct supervisory duty, and individual responsibility for the work of paralegals, legal secretaries, and other non-lawyer staff.

Verbatim text of KRPC 5.3 (Kansas)

With respect to a nonlawyer employed or retained by or associated with a lawyer:

(a) a partner, and a lawyer who individually or together with other lawyers possesses comparable managerial authority in a law firm shall make reasonable efforts to ensure that the firm has in effect measures giving reasonable assurance that the person's conduct is compatible with the professional obligations of the lawyer;

(b) a lawyer having direct supervisory authority over the nonlawyer shall make reasonable efforts to ensure that the person's conduct is compatible with the professional obligations of the lawyer; and

(c) a lawyer shall be responsible for conduct of such a person that would be a violation of the Rules of Professional Conduct if engaged in by a lawyer if:
(1) the lawyer orders or, with the knowledge of the specific conduct, ratifies the conduct involved; or
(2) the lawyer is a partner or has comparable managerial authority in the law firm in which the person is employed, or has direct supervisory authority over the person, and knows of the conduct at a time when its consequences can be avoided or mitigated but fails to take reasonable remedial action. Kan. Sup. Ct. R. 226, KRPC 5.3.

Missouri Rule 4-5.3

Missouri Rule 4-5.3 is substantively identical to KRPC 5.3 and tracks ABA Model Rule 5.3. The rule applies whether the non-lawyer is an employee of the firm (paralegal, legal secretary, investigator on staff) or is retained on a contract basis (outside investigator, freelance paralegal, contract document-review staff, third-party vendor providing services that substitute for or augment lawyer work). The three-part structure parallels Rule 5.1 for supervised lawyers: firm-management duty, direct supervisory duty, and individual responsibility for conduct.

5.3(a) - Firm-management duty

Every partner and every lawyer with comparable managerial authority in the firm must make reasonable efforts to ensure that the firm maintains measures giving reasonable assurance that the conduct of each non-lawyer in or associated with the firm is compatible with the professional obligations of the lawyers themselves. The duty is structural and firm-wide; it is not satisfied by the personal compliance of any individual lawyer.

The Comment to Model Rule 5.3 identifies categories of measures that scale with the size and practice profile of the firm:

  • Written policies governing the role and scope of work of non-lawyer assistants in each practice area
  • Training programs that orient non-lawyer staff to confidentiality, conflicts, file-management, and tribunal-related obligations of the lawyers they assist
  • Procedures for review of work product prepared by non-lawyer staff before it leaves the firm or is filed with a tribunal
  • Conflict-checking protocols that include matters worked on by non-lawyer staff
  • Supervision protocols for outside vendors and contract staff whose work product is delivered to clients or filed on the firm's behalf

The standard is "reasonable efforts." What is reasonable in a sole-practitioner office with one legal secretary differs from what is reasonable in a multi-office firm with a paralegal staff measured in dozens and an array of outside vendors. The duty scales with the complexity of the operation.

5.3(b) - Direct supervisory duty

A lawyer with direct supervisory authority over a non-lawyer assistant must make reasonable efforts to ensure that the assistant's conduct is compatible with the professional obligations of the lawyer. The duty attaches to the supervisory relationship itself - the lawyer assigning work, reviewing output, or directing the assistant's day-to-day activities holds the duty regardless of partnership status.

The reasonable-efforts standard in 5.3(b) is calibrated to the specific tasks delegated. A lawyer who delegates routine document preparation to a paralegal owes a different intensity of supervision than a lawyer who delegates client communication, fact-gathering interviews, or post-filing follow-up. The greater the consequence of the delegated task for the client's interests or for compliance with the rules, the more searching the supervision the lawyer must provide.

5.3(c) - Individual responsibility for non-lawyer conduct

Subdivision (c) imposes personal responsibility on a lawyer when a non-lawyer engages in conduct that would itself violate the Rules of Professional Conduct if engaged in by a lawyer. The responsibility attaches in two circumstances:

5.3(c)(1) - Order or ratification

The lawyer orders the conduct, or with knowledge of the specific conduct ratifies it. Ratification reaches both express approval after the fact and implicit acceptance of the conduct's results. The knowledge standard is "specific conduct," not generalized awareness; a lawyer's general knowledge that staff occasionally take certain actions does not, without more, ratify any particular action.

5.3(c)(2) - Knowledge in time to avoid or mitigate

The lawyer holds managerial authority in the firm or direct supervisory authority over the non-lawyer, learns of the conduct at a time when its consequences can be avoided or mitigated, and fails to take reasonable remedial action. This is the "knew and did nothing" branch of the rule. It requires both actual knowledge of the conduct and a temporal window in which remedial action could have made a difference. Knowledge acquired after the consequences have crystallized does not, by itself, trigger 5.3(c)(2); it may, however, trigger other duties (file-correction obligations, disclosure to affected parties, or reporting obligations under separate rules).

Ratification by silence applies to non-lawyer conduct as well as lawyer conduct. A managing partner or direct supervisor who learns of conduct by a non-lawyer assistant that would be a rule violation if engaged in by a lawyer, while there is still time to correct or mitigate the conduct, is personally responsible under 5.3(c)(2) if no remedial action is taken. The supervisor's silence operates as a discrete violation independent of the underlying conduct.

Practical implications for paralegals, legal secretaries, and contract staff

Several categories of non-lawyer conduct recur in published ethics opinions and disciplinary decisions as supervision-sensitive:

Client communication

Paralegals and legal secretaries frequently transmit communications to clients and to opposing parties. Communications attributable to the firm carry the lawyer's professional obligations. The supervising lawyer is responsible for ensuring that communications transmitted by staff (a) accurately reflect the matter's status, (b) do not include legal advice the assistant is not authorized to give, and (c) preserve confidentiality as required by Rule 1.6.

File management and document production

File assembly, scanning, indexing, and production are routinely delegated to non-lawyer staff. The supervising lawyer is responsible for ensuring that the production satisfies the lawyer's obligations under Rule 1.16(d) (surrender of papers to which the client is entitled) and that documents produced or filed do not contain information that should have been redacted under Fed. R. Bankr. P. 9037 or analogous privacy rules.

Marketing and solicitation

Communications soliciting prospective clients, posted online or transmitted directly, are governed by Rules 7.1 through 7.3 regardless of whether the communication is composed by the lawyer or by non-lawyer staff. The supervising lawyer is responsible for ensuring that staff-generated marketing complies with those rules.

Court-related interactions

Filings, calendaring, and procedural follow-up are commonly delegated. The supervising lawyer remains personally responsible under Rule 3.3 for the accuracy of representations made to a tribunal, regardless of which individual at the firm typed or filed the document.

Scope of permitted non-lawyer activity

The Rules of Professional Conduct do not themselves define the boundary between work that may be performed by non-lawyer staff and work that may not. The scope of permitted non-lawyer activity is defined by state regulation outside the Rules of Professional Conduct - principally state statutes and Supreme Court rules governing the practice of law and any regulatory regimes for legal-services providers. Readers should consult published state regulations and authoritative state-bar guidance for the operative boundaries in any given jurisdiction.

Rule 5.3 operates within whatever boundary the state has drawn. Where state law permits a non-lawyer to perform a particular task under lawyer supervision, the supervising lawyer must perform that supervision in conformity with 5.3. Where state law does not permit a non-lawyer to perform a task at all, the rule provides an additional layer of supervisory accountability if the conduct nonetheless occurs.

Common procedural posture

Paralegal-assembled production package

A paralegal assembles a file-production package in response to a client's request for the client's file under Rule 1.16(d). The supervising lawyer's 5.3(b) duty includes ensuring that the package includes the materials the rule requires and that the paralegal has not, in good faith but in error, omitted material categories. Where the lawyer reviews the package before transmission, deficiencies are identified and corrected. Where the lawyer does not review the package and learns later that material was omitted, the 5.3(c)(2) question is whether the lawyer learned in time to supplement and whether reasonable remedial action followed.

Contract document-review vendor

A firm retains a contract vendor to perform first-pass document review on a large production. The 5.3(a) duty requires the firm to maintain conflict-checking and confidentiality protocols that cover the vendor. The 5.3(b) duty requires the lawyer overseeing the engagement to provide adequate instruction on responsiveness criteria, privilege screening, and chain-of-custody protocols. Vendor conduct that would violate Rule 1.6 (confidentiality) if engaged in by a lawyer is the firm's responsibility under 5.3(c) to the extent the conditions of subdivision (c)(1) or (c)(2) are met.

Relationship to Rule 5.1

Rule 5.1 governs supervision of lawyers; Rule 5.3 governs supervision of non-lawyer assistants. The two rules are structurally parallel: both impose firm-management duties on partners and managerial authorities, supervisory duties on direct supervisors, and individual responsibility for violations committed with knowledge or under direction. In a firm with both lawyer and non-lawyer staff, a complete analysis of supervisory responsibility typically requires application of both rules in tandem. See KRPC 5.1.

Relationship to Rule 8.4(a)

Rule 8.4(a) makes it professional misconduct for a lawyer to violate the rules through the acts of another. The interaction with Rule 5.3 is direct: where a non-lawyer acts at the lawyer's direction in a way that, if performed by the lawyer, would violate a rule, the lawyer is responsible under 8.4(a) as well as under 5.3(c)(1). The two rules operate concurrently rather than as alternatives.

Continuing nature of the duty

Subdivisions (a) and (b) impose continuing duties. A staffing structure adopted at firm formation and never revisited does not satisfy 5.3(a) as the firm grows, takes on new practice areas, or adds new categories of vendor relationships. A supervisory relationship establishes a 5.3(b) duty that runs for the duration of the relationship and adjusts in intensity as the scope of delegation changes.

Practical impact

Rule 5.3 carries two recurring practical consequences:

  • Delegation does not delegate the rule. A lawyer who delegates a task to a non-lawyer assistant retains the professional obligations the rules attach to that task. Subdivision (a) requires firm-level measures, subdivision (b) requires individual supervision, and subdivision (c) attaches individual responsibility where the supervision fails.
  • Awareness without action is a discrete violation. Subdivision (c)(2) treats supervisor silence in the face of known conduct as itself violative when the supervisor learns in time to avoid or mitigate consequences. The duty to act runs independently of any duty to report under other rules.
