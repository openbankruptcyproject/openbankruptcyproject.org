KRPC 1.2 / MRPC 4-1.2: Scope of Representation and Allocation of Authority Between Client and Lawyer

The decision allocation Rule 1.2 structures, the limited-scope framework it authorizes, and the counseling boundary it draws.

What Rule 1.2 affirmatively does

Rule 1.2 is the architecture of decision-making in the lawyer-client relationship. It allocates authority between the two participants so that each knows which decisions belong to whom, it expressly authorizes representation of a limited scope so that clients who cannot afford full representation can still obtain meaningful legal help, and it gives the lawyer broad latitude to counsel a client on the legal consequences of any contemplated course of conduct, including conduct of doubtful legality. The rule is structural and enabling rather than restrictive: it tells the lawyer what the lawyer is empowered to do at the client's direction and within the scope agreed upon.

Verbatim text of KRPC 1.2 (Kansas)

(a) A lawyer shall abide by a client's decisions concerning the objectives of representation and, as required by Rule 1.4, shall consult with the client as to the means by which they are to be pursued. A lawyer may take such action on behalf of the client as is impliedly authorized to carry out the representation. A lawyer shall abide by a client's decision whether to settle a matter. In a criminal case, the lawyer shall abide by the client's decision, after consultation with the lawyer, as to a plea to be entered, whether to waive jury trial and whether the client will testify.

(b) A lawyer's representation of a client, including representation by appointment, does not constitute an endorsement of the client's political, economic, social or moral views or activities.

(c) A lawyer may limit the scope of the representation if the limitation is reasonable under the circumstances and the client gives informed consent.

(d) A lawyer shall not counsel a client to engage, or assist a client, in conduct that the lawyer knows is criminal or fraudulent, but a lawyer may discuss the legal consequences of any proposed course of conduct with a client and may counsel or assist a client to make a good faith effort to determine the validity, scope, meaning or application of the law. Kan. Sup. Ct. R. 226, KRPC 1.2.

Missouri Rule 4-1.2

Missouri Rule 4-1.2 is substantively parallel to KRPC 1.2 and tracks ABA Model Rule 1.2. Both rules structure four affirmative architectures: (1) client authority over objectives, (2) lawyer authority over means in consultation with the client, (3) a limited-scope option available with informed consent, and (4) a broad counseling latitude that includes good-faith challenges to the law.

1.2(a) - The decision-allocation architecture

Subdivision (a) divides decisions into three working categories. The first category - decisions the client controls - includes the objectives of the representation, the decision whether to settle, and in a criminal matter the plea, the jury trial waiver, and whether the client will testify. These are decisions that allocate the client's substantive rights and personal exposures, and the rule reserves them to the person whose rights and exposures they are.

The second category - the means by which objectives are pursued - is shared. The lawyer brings professional judgment about strategy, tactics, and the choice among lawful methods; the client brings substantive interests and the contextual knowledge of what the client can accept. Rule 1.4 requires the lawyer to consult with the client on means. The shared architecture recognizes that strategic choices implicate both professional craft and client interest.

The third category - actions impliedly authorized to carry out the representation - lets the lawyer act efficiently on the client's behalf without separate authorization for every step. Filing a routine motion, sending a standard discovery request, agreeing to a customary scheduling extension - these proceed under implied authority once the representation is established.

1.2(b) - The non-endorsement principle

Subdivision (b) provides that representation of a client does not constitute the lawyer's endorsement of the client's views or activities. This is a load-bearing structural feature: it enables lawyers to take on clients whose views differ from the lawyer's own, and it lets appointed counsel serve clients in matters where the lawyer would otherwise face attribution of the client's positions. The principle protects the availability of counsel to controversial clients and to clients whose causes are unpopular.

1.2(c) - The limited-scope framework

Subdivision (c) authorizes limited-scope representation: a lawyer may agree to represent a client for a defined slice of a matter rather than the whole. The two conditions are (1) the limitation must be reasonable under the circumstances, and (2) the client must give informed consent.

Limited-scope (or "unbundled") representation expands access. The framework lets clients who cannot afford full representation purchase the legal services that matter most: drafting a single brief, appearing at a single hearing, ghostwriting pleadings for a pro se litigant, providing strategic consultation without entering an appearance. Many courts have adopted local rules that recognize the limited-scope appearance.

The reasonableness condition

A limitation is reasonable when the slice the lawyer agrees to handle can meaningfully be handled separately and when the lawyer can perform that slice competently. A limitation is not reasonable if it would deprive the client of effective representation - for example, agreeing to draft a complaint without preserving any pre-suit defenses the lawyer should have investigated.

The informed-consent condition

Informed consent means the client understands what is included, what is excluded, what risks attend the exclusions, and what alternatives exist. The lawyer must communicate the basis of the limitation clearly enough that a reasonable client can decide whether to accept it. Written confirmation is best practice in Kansas and is required in some Missouri contexts where fee structure intersects with scope.

1.2(d) - The counseling boundary and what it preserves

Subdivision (d) draws a single boundary - the lawyer may not counsel or assist a client in conduct the lawyer knows to be criminal or fraudulent - and then expressly preserves the lawyer's broad latitude on the affirmative side of that boundary.

What 1.2(d) preserves is substantial:

  • Discussion of legal consequences. The lawyer may discuss the legal consequences of any proposed course of conduct with the client. The client may be considering an action of doubtful legality; the lawyer is permitted - indeed, often obligated under Rule 1.4 - to lay out what the law says about it.
  • Good-faith challenges to the law. The lawyer may counsel or assist a client in a good faith effort to determine the validity, scope, meaning, or application of the law. This includes test-case litigation, declaratory-judgment actions, and structured noncompliance designed to obtain judicial review.
  • Counseling on lawful workarounds. If a client's first-instinct course of conduct would be unlawful, the lawyer may counsel the client on lawful alternatives that achieve similar objectives.

The shape of an effective engagement letter

Objectives section

Names the decisions reserved to the client under 1.2(a): the scope of relief sought, the disposition decision, and (in criminal matters) plea, jury waiver, and testimony. Confirms that the lawyer will consult with the client on means under Rule 1.4.

Scope section

If the engagement is limited under 1.2(c), the scope section names what is included and what is excluded, identifies the risks of the exclusions, and confirms the client's informed consent. If the engagement is full-scope, the section says so and notes the milestones at which scope can be revisited.

Continuing nature of the allocation

The decisions reserved to the client under 1.2(a) remain reserved throughout the representation. A client's objectives can change; the client's decision whether to settle is the client's decision at the time the offer is on the table, not a decision delegated at the engagement's outset. The lawyer's consultation duty under Rule 1.4 runs continuously and supplies the information on which the client exercises 1.2(a) authority.

Practical impact

Rule 1.2 is the rule lawyers consult first when shaping an engagement and last when a dispute arises about what the engagement covered. Two takeaways recur:

  • Clarity at the front end is the engine of the rule. A clear allocation of decisions, a clear definition of scope, and a clear written engagement letter are the operational forms in which the rule does its work.
  • The rule expands access to counsel. The limited-scope framework in 1.2(c) is the principal tool by which the profession answers the access-to-justice question. Lawyers who use 1.2(c) effectively serve clients who would otherwise proceed pro se.
