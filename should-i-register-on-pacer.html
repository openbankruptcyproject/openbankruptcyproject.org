Should I register myself on PACER as a debtor?

Will your attorney do it for you? Almost never. Should you do it yourself? Yes — on day one, before anything else moves in your case. Here is the short version, the long version, and the five-minute registration playbook.

Published by the Open Bankruptcy Project. Updated 2026-05-21. Educational information only; not legal advice.

The Short Answer

No, your attorney will probably not register you. Yes, you should register yourself.

Most bankruptcy attorneys use PACER under their own credentials and send their clients paper or PDF copies of the major filings. That is the default. It is not malpractice; it is the way most firms have always done it. But it leaves you dependent on the firm for visibility into your own case, and that is a posture an informed debtor does not need to accept.

Registering yourself takes about five minutes. It costs nothing at signup. For the typical debtor who checks their own docket once or twice a month, it stays free indefinitely. You will see exactly what the court, the trustee, your attorney, and every opposing party sees — the complete, unfiltered, chronological record of your case.

Why Your Attorney Probably Won't

The default in most firms is that clients do not have PACER accounts

This is worth saying plainly because it is the part most debtors find surprising. When you sign a representation agreement, the firm registers itself with the court as your counsel of record. The firm gets electronic notice of filings through its own CM/ECF system. It does not, as a matter of course, set up a separate PACER account for you.

There are a few reasons this is the norm:

It saves the firm a step

Registering a client requires the client's personal information, a credit card, and a few minutes of staff time. Multiply by hundreds of clients per year and it is a workflow many firms have simply never built.

The firm has the information it needs without it

Once an attorney is counsel of record, the firm receives every electronic notice the court generates. The firm does not need the client to have a PACER account to do its job.

Some attorneys prefer the simpler relationship

An informed client asks better questions, which is good for the case but means a longer phone call. A firm that operates at high volume may default to summarizing rather than copying the client on every filing.

None of these are bad reasons. But none of them are reasons you should not register. Your account costs the firm nothing, requires nothing from them, and changes nothing about how they do their work. It only changes what you can see.

Why You Should Register Yourself

Five things you can see with PACER that you might not otherwise

The point of registering is not distrust of your attorney. It is independent visibility into a public record about you. Here is what that visibility actually buys you:

1. The complete docket in date order

Every filing in your case, by every party, in the sequence the court received it. Your attorney is likely to forward you the major ones. PACER shows you every one, including the routine notices, scheduling orders, and short procedural entries that are easy to skip in summary.

2. The claims register

Every creditor who filed a proof of claim against you, the date filed, the amount claimed, and the supporting documentation. Some claims will be ones you expected. Others may be from creditors you forgot about, were unaware of, or believe were already paid. If a claim is inflated or duplicative, you will not know to flag it for your attorney unless you see it.

3. Filings by parties who are not your attorney

The trustee files status reports and statements. Creditors file motions for relief from stay, objections to discharge, and adversary complaints. The U.S. Trustee files notices and inquiries. These are all yours to read. They show what is happening around your case, not just what your side is doing.

4. The exact timing of every entry

Filings are date-stamped to the minute. If something happens late on a Friday, you can see it Friday night. You do not have to wait for the firm to flag it on Monday. For time-sensitive responses, the timestamp matters.

5. Fee applications

If your attorney files a fee application (most Chapter 13 and Chapter 11 cases require one), it lists the time entries, hourly rates, and total billed. You should know what is being charged to your estate. The fee application is on the docket whether or not anyone forwards it to you.

None of these are exotic. They are the ordinary contents of a bankruptcy file. Independent docket monitoring is the difference between knowing what is in your own case and being told a summary.

What It Actually Costs

For a typical debtor: almost certainly nothing

PACER's fee structure looks complicated but is unusually friendly to low-volume users.

  • $0.10 per page, with a $3.00 cap per document. A 50-page motion costs $3.00, not $5.00.
  • If your total fees in a calendar quarter (Jan-Mar, Apr-Jun, Jul-Sep, Oct-Dec) stay under $30.00, the bill is waived. You owe nothing.
  • Account creation is free. The card on file is for billing only and is not charged at signup.
  • 341 meeting audio recordings are a flat $2.40 each and do not count against the $3.00 document cap.

A debtor checking their own docket once or twice a month, pulling a handful of documents over the life of the case, will almost never reach the $30 quarterly threshold. The most common bill a casual user sees is $0.00.

If you do come close to the threshold — for example, because you want to read every filing in a complex Chapter 11 — install the free RECAP browser extension. Every document you pull is automatically mirrored to the free RECAP Archive. Future readers (including you) get it for free.
Common Objections (And the Honest Answer)

The things debtors hesitate over — and why they shouldn't

"Won't my attorney be offended?"

No. Reading your own court file is not a vote of no confidence; it is the normal posture of an informed client. Good attorneys welcome it because it means fewer basic questions over the phone. A firm that actively discourages you from looking at the public record of your own case is telling you something about how they operate, and it is worth knowing.

"Will my attorney know I'm checking?"

No. PACER does not notify any party when a user views a docket. Your reads are recorded in your own billing log only.

"I don't understand most of what's on a docket."

That is normal at the start. Most debtors find that after a few visits they can recognize the major entry types: motions, orders, notices, status reports, claims. You do not have to read every page. You just have to be able to scan the docket and notice when something unexpected appears. If something looks wrong, send your attorney the entry number and the date and ask.

"I'm worried about giving my credit card."

The card is held for billing only. It is not charged at signup. If you never cross the $30 quarterly threshold, it is never charged at all. PACER is a federal court system; the card-on-file requirement is standard, and the system is not in the habit of surprise charges.

"What if I find something I don't understand or that worries me?"

Ask your attorney. That is what they are there for. Independent docket monitoring is not a substitute for legal advice. It is the visibility that lets you ask better questions.

When to Register

The right time is day one

If you have not filed yet, you can register before your case is even on the docket. There is no waiting period and no requirement that you be a party to a case. PACER is open to the public.

If you have already filed, the right time to register is now. The longer you wait, the more of the early case record (petition, schedules, statement of financial affairs, first creditor notices) you are reading second-hand.

FAQ

Frequently asked questions

Will my attorney register me on PACER?

Usually no. Most bankruptcy attorneys do not register their clients on PACER. They use the system through their own credentials, send the client paper or PDF copies of the major filings, and assume the client will rely on the firm for case visibility. This is the default in most firms, but it is not a rule. You can register yourself at any time, and most experienced debtors do.

Do I need PACER if I have a lawyer?

You do not strictly need it. Your case will function whether or not you have an account. But an independent PACER account gives you the same view of your own case that your lawyer, the trustee, the court, and any opposing party already have. Most debtors who register say it took five minutes to set up and gave them confidence they did not have before.

Is it rude to my attorney if I look at my own docket?

No. The bankruptcy docket is a public record. Reading your own case is not a vote of no confidence in your attorney; it is the normal posture of an informed client. Good attorneys welcome it because it reduces the number of basic questions they have to field by phone.

How much does it cost a debtor to monitor their own case on PACER?

Almost always nothing. PACER charges $0.10 per page with a $3.00 cap per document. If your total fees for a calendar quarter stay under $30.00, the bill is waived. A typical debtor checking their own docket once or twice a month will not come close. Many never see a bill.

What can I see on PACER about my own case that I would not otherwise see?

The complete docket: every filing in the case, in date order, with the underlying PDF. That includes things your attorney may not forward you proactively: trustee status reports, creditor proofs of claim with attached documentation, U.S. Trustee inquiries, fee applications, motions filed by other parties, and the timing of every entry. You see the case the way the court sees it, not a filtered summary.

Can I register on PACER the same day my case is filed?

Yes. There is no waiting period. Registration takes about five minutes, and the system issues credentials immediately. You can do it the same day your petition is filed, or even before.

Will my attorney know if I check my own docket?

No. PACER does not notify any party when another user views a docket. Your activity is recorded in your own account billing log only. Your attorney has no visibility into your reads.

If I find something on PACER I do not understand, what should I do?

Ask your attorney. That is exactly what they are there for. The point of independent docket monitoring is not to substitute for legal advice; it is to make sure nothing important is happening in your case without your awareness. If something looks wrong, send your attorney the entry number and the date and ask.

Can I get PACER access without giving a credit card?

Not for an individual account. PACER requires a card on file at registration, but it is not charged at signup. The card is only billed if your quarterly activity exceeds $30.00. Many users never see a charge. If the card-on-file requirement is a hard stop, the free RECAP Archive at CourtListener is the best alternative; many bankruptcy dockets are mirrored there in full and require no registration at all.

What if my attorney tells me I don't need PACER?

They are right that the case will function without it. They are wrong if they suggest you should not have it. The bankruptcy docket is your own court record; reading it is normal and recommended. If a firm pushes back on a client setting up their own PACER account, that is a question worth raising directly: why?
