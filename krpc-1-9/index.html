KRPC 1.9 / MRPC 4-1.9: Duties to Former Clients

Substantial-relationship analysis, the prohibition on adverse use of former-client confidences, and the consent gate.

Verbatim text of KRPC 1.9 (Kansas)

(a) A lawyer who has formerly represented a client in a matter shall not thereafter represent another person in the same or a substantially related matter in which that person's interests are materially adverse to the interests of the former client unless the former client gives informed consent, confirmed in writing.

(b) A lawyer shall not knowingly represent a person in the same or a substantially related matter in which a firm with which the lawyer formerly was associated had previously represented a client:
(1) whose interests are materially adverse to that person; and
(2) about whom the lawyer had acquired information protected by Rules 1.6 and 1.9(c) that is material to the matter; unless the former client gives informed consent, confirmed in writing.

(c) A lawyer who has formerly represented a client in a matter or whose present or former firm has formerly represented a client in a matter shall not thereafter:
(1) use information relating to the representation to the disadvantage of the former client except as these Rules would permit or require with respect to a client, or when the information has become generally known; or
(2) reveal information relating to the representation except as these Rules would permit or require with respect to a client. Kan. Sup. Ct. R. 226, KRPC 1.9.

Missouri Rule 4-1.9

Missouri Rule 4-1.9 is substantively identical to KRPC 1.9 and tracks ABA Model Rule 1.9. The rule operates in three subdivisions: (a) governs successive representation by the same lawyer; (b) governs migration of lawyers between firms; (c) governs the continuing duty regarding former-client information regardless of representation status.

1.9(a) - The substantial-relationship test

A lawyer who formerly represented a client may not represent a new client in the same or a substantially related matter where the new client's interests are materially adverse to the former client, absent informed consent confirmed in writing. The operative inquiries:

Was the matter the same?

Identity of matter is the simplest case. The same litigation, the same transaction, the same project - all qualify.

Was the matter substantially related?

Comment 3 to Model Rule 1.9 identifies the "substantial relationship" inquiry as turning on whether the matters involve the same transaction or legal dispute, or whether there is otherwise a substantial risk that confidential factual information that would normally have been obtained in the prior representation would materially advance the new client's position in the subsequent matter. The analysis is generally factual rather than strictly procedural; matters can be substantially related even where the legal theories or causes of action differ.

Are the interests materially adverse?

Direct opposition in litigation, transactional adversity, or adverse positioning in negotiation all qualify. Adversity in the sense of "different but not opposed" - for example, two clients with parallel but not conflicting interests - typically does not trigger Rule 1.9.

1.9(b) - Migrating-lawyer rule

When a lawyer moves between firms, the new firm cannot represent persons in matters where (1) the lawyer's prior firm represented a now-adverse client in the same or substantially related matter, and (2) the migrating lawyer acquired material confidential information at the prior firm. Both conditions must be met: representation alone at the prior firm is not enough if the migrating lawyer was not personally exposed to material confidential information.

1.9(c) - Continuing duty regarding former-client information

The duty to protect former-client information continues regardless of whether a subsequent representation is undertaken. Subdivision (c) prohibits:

  • (c)(1) Using information relating to the prior representation to the disadvantage of the former client, except as the rules would permit with respect to a current client, or when the information has become generally known
  • (c)(2) Revealing such information except as the rules would permit or require with respect to a current client

The "generally known" exception in (c)(1) is narrow. Information is not generally known merely because it is contained in publicly available court filings; the standard typically requires that the information be known in the relevant community such that further dissemination by the lawyer adds nothing material.

The duty under 1.9(c) is permanent. Unlike the substantial-relationship test in 1.9(a), which conditions disqualification on the new matter being substantially related, the confidentiality duty in 1.9(c) attaches to former-client information and continues indefinitely. A lawyer who later opposes a former client in an unrelated matter still cannot use or reveal information from the prior representation to the former client's disadvantage.

Relationship to confidentiality under Rule 1.6

Rule 1.6 imposes the confidentiality duty regarding current clients; Rule 1.9(c) extends that duty to former clients. The substantive scope of protected information is the same: "information relating to the representation" is broader than attorney-client privileged communications and includes any information the lawyer learned in the course of representing the client, regardless of source.

Consent gate

The substantial-relationship and migrating-lawyer bars in subdivisions (a) and (b) are subject to the same consent mechanism: informed consent of the former client, confirmed in writing. "Informed consent" under Rule 1.0 requires that the former client be adequately informed of the material risks and reasonable alternatives. "Confirmed in writing" requires either a writing signed by the client or a writing transmitted by the lawyer to the client memorializing the consent.

The confidentiality duty under (c), by contrast, is not subject to a consent-cures analysis in the same way; revelation or adverse use is permitted only as the rules would permit for a current client. The general permission structure of Rule 1.6 (including narrow exceptions for crime/fraud prevention or rectification, defense of the lawyer in proceedings, etc.) governs.

Imputation under Rule 1.10

A conflict held by an individual lawyer is generally imputed to the lawyer's firm under Rule 1.10. The screening exception applies in some former-client contexts, but only when its specific conditions are met. The interaction of Rules 1.9 and 1.10 is the source of most firm-level former-client disqualification analysis.

Common procedural posture

New matter against a former client

The firm is approached to represent a new client against an entity that the firm formerly represented. The Rule 1.9(a) substantial-relationship inquiry runs: was the prior matter the same as or substantially related to the new matter? If yes and the interests are materially adverse, the firm must either decline or obtain informed consent from the former client confirmed in writing.

Lateral hire from a firm that previously opposed

A firm hires a lateral lawyer from a firm that previously represented a current-firm adversary. Rule 1.9(b) runs: did the lateral lawyer personally acquire material confidential information about the former client at the prior firm? If yes and the matters are substantially related, the new firm is conflicted as to the migrating lawyer, with screening under Rule 1.10 potentially available depending on the jurisdiction's specific rule.

Relationship to other rules

  • Rule 1.6 (Confidentiality) - Governs the substantive scope of information protected; Rule 1.9(c) extends the duty to former clients.
  • Rule 1.7 (Current-Client Conflicts) - The concurrent analog; Rule 1.7 governs current clients and Rule 1.9 governs former clients.
  • Rule 1.10 (Imputation) - Imputation of individual conflicts to the firm.
  • Rule 1.16 (Termination) - Mandatory withdrawal under 1.16(a)(1) if continued representation would violate Rule 1.9.
  • Rule 1.0 (Definitions) - Definitions of "informed consent," "writing," and "confirmed in writing."

Practical impact

Rule 1.9 is the framework for disqualification motions brought by former clients. Two patterns recur:

  • Substantial relationship is a low bar. Courts and reviewing authorities generally apply the substantial-relationship test in favor of the former client. Where there is reasonable doubt about whether prior-representation information could be material in the new matter, the doubt typically runs in favor of disqualification.
  • The continuing duty under (c) is independent. A lawyer can satisfy the substantial-relationship analysis in (a) - because the matters are not substantially related - but still violate (c) by using or revealing former-client information adversely.
