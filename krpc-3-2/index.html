KRPC 3.2 / MRPC 4-3.2: Expediting Litigation

The reasonable-expediting framework - efforts to advance litigation consistent with the client's legitimate interests and with the court's interest in efficient adjudication.

Reasonable expediting framework that 3.2 establishes

Rule 3.2 establishes a framework for moving litigation forward at a reasonable pace. Counsel's duty is affirmative: take reasonable steps to expedite litigation consistent with the legitimate interests of the client. The rule recognizes that litigation serves the parties and the public best when adjudication is timely; it places counsel in the role of a steward of judicial efficiency alongside being an advocate.

The framework operates by reference to two anchor concepts. The first is reasonableness, measured by what counsel of ordinary diligence would do in similar circumstances. The second is the legitimate interest of the client, which includes the client's interest in thorough preparation, in adequate investigation, in considered strategy, and in not being rushed into decisions of substance.

Verbatim text of KRPC 3.2 (Kansas)

A lawyer shall make reasonable efforts to expedite litigation consistent with the interests of the client. Kan. Sup. Ct. R. 226, KRPC 3.2.

Missouri Rule 4-3.2

Missouri Rule 4-3.2 is substantively identical to KRPC 3.2 and tracks ABA Model Rule 3.2. The Comment to Model Rule 3.2 articulates the affirmative framing: dilatory practices bring the administration of justice into disrepute, and the question of whether a delay is justified must be evaluated by reference to legitimate purposes - not by reference to whether the delay serves the lawyer's or the client's tactical advantage.

The "reasonable efforts" standard

"Reasonable efforts" is the operative phrase. It is calibrated to context. Reasonable efforts in a complex multi-district litigation differ from reasonable efforts in a small-claim collection. Reasonable efforts during a discovery-heavy phase differ from reasonable efforts on the eve of trial. The rule does not require maximum effort at every moment; it requires effort proportionate to the matter, the procedural posture, and the resources of the parties and the court.

What counts as a reasonable effort to expedite:

  • Meeting court-ordered deadlines and scheduling-order milestones
  • Responding to discovery within the time the rules and orders permit
  • Conferring in good faith on scheduling, on extensions, and on stipulations that narrow disputes
  • Identifying issues that can be resolved on the papers and presenting them for prompt adjudication
  • Communicating with opposing counsel about anticipated motions, anticipated witnesses, and anticipated needs for the court's attention

Consistency with the client's legitimate interests

The rule's qualifier - "consistent with the interests of the client" - protects counsel's ability to take the time genuinely required to serve the client. A client has a legitimate interest in being fully prepared, in not being subjected to half-formed motions, and in having counsel pursue every theory that might bear on the outcome. The rule does not require sacrifice of any of those interests in the name of speed.

The qualifier also marks the distinction between justified time-taking and unjustified delay. Time spent on substantive investigation, on legal research, on conferring with the client about strategy, on coordinating expert witnesses, on preparing dispositive motions - all of this is time the rule contemplates and protects. The Comment to Model Rule 3.2 is explicit: "realizing financial or other benefit from otherwise improper delay in litigation is not a legitimate interest of the client." Time taken for legitimate reasons is not delay in the relevant sense.

What the rule does not require

The expediting duty is not a duty to capitulate. It does not require counsel to drop colorable defenses to move a case faster. It does not require counsel to waive procedural protections that exist to give parties time to respond. It does not require counsel to forgo motions that, if granted, would dispose of the matter entirely. The Comment to Model Rule 3.2 emphasizes that the rule's question is whether a competent lawyer acting in good faith would regard the course of action as having some substantial purpose other than delay.

Affirmative read-out. Rule 3.2 protects the lawyer who works diligently to advance a case while preserving every legitimate step in the case's natural progression. It is a rule about pace, not a rule about scope; it does not require counsel to do less for the client, only that counsel do it within a reasonable timeframe.

The judicial-efficiency dimension

The Comment to Model Rule 3.2 frames the rule as serving the administration of justice. Courts have limited capacity; dockets must move; parties and witnesses have lives that proceed alongside litigation. Counsel who makes reasonable efforts to expedite contributes to a court system that can serve all litigants. This systemic dimension is the affirmative purpose of the rule, and it is the dimension most often invoked in disciplinary decisions.

The rule operates alongside court-imposed scheduling regimes - case-management orders, scheduling orders under Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 16, status conference requirements, and similar mechanisms. The lawyer who actively engages with these mechanisms - proposing realistic schedules, honoring deadlines, requesting extensions only when genuinely needed and well in advance - operates in the affirmative zone of the rule.

Common procedural posture

Discovery-period diligence

Counsel serves discovery requests promptly after the start of the discovery period, responds to received discovery within the time the rules permit, and confers in good faith on disputes before motion practice. This is the heart of 3.2 in operation - active engagement with the procedural calendar in a way that respects both the client's interest in thorough preparation and the court's interest in moving the case.

Stipulating to undisputed facts

Counsel agrees to a written stipulation of undisputed facts, narrowing the issues that need to be tried. The stipulation reflects the affirmative spirit of 3.2: it preserves the client's position on contested matters while clearing uncontested matters from the court's attention. Counsel may do so without conceding any disputed point.

Extensions and continuances

Rule 3.2 does not bar extensions or continuances. It contemplates them. Counsel may request additional time when additional time is needed - for additional discovery, for additional briefing, for accommodating witnesses, for resolving outstanding procedural issues. The rule simply requires that the request be grounded in a legitimate purpose connected to the matter, not in tactical advantage divorced from the matter's progress.

Best practices commonly observed in bar guidance:

  • Request continuances early when the need becomes apparent, rather than at the last moment
  • State the basis for the continuance plainly in the motion
  • Confer with opposing counsel on the request before filing
  • Propose a specific new date or timeframe rather than leaving the matter open-ended

Relationship to other rules

Rule 3.2 sits within the litigation-conduct cluster (Rules 3.1 through 3.9). It interacts with several adjacent rules:

  • Rule 1.3 - Diligence; the duty of diligence to the client overlaps substantially with the expediting duty to the court
  • Rule 3.1 - Meritorious claims; counsel may not delay through frivolous motions or repeated reassertion of rejected positions
  • Rule 3.4 - Fairness to opposing party and counsel; 3.4(d) addresses obstruction in discovery, which often overlaps with 3.2 concerns
  • Rule 8.4 - Misconduct; severe and unjustified delay may also implicate 8.4(d) (conduct prejudicial to the administration of justice)

Practical impact

Rule 3.2 is most often invoked as one element of broader patterns rather than as a stand-alone violation. The lawyer who consistently meets deadlines, responds to discovery, and engages with court schedules does not encounter the rule as a barrier; the rule operates in the background as a check on the rare attorney whose conduct draws the court's attention as a pattern of unjustified delay.

  • Diligent practice satisfies the rule. Counsel who runs a careful, well-managed docket complies with 3.2 as a matter of routine.
  • Justified time-taking is protected. The rule's qualifier (consistent with client interests) protects the time needed for thorough advocacy.
  • The systemic dimension matters. Counsel's pace bears on the courts and on other parties; the affirmative duty runs to that broader interest as well as to the immediate matter.
