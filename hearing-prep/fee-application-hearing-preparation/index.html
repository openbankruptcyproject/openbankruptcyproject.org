11 U.S.C. Section 330 Rule 2016

Fee Application Hearing Preparation

The Rule 2016(a) documentation requirements, lodestar reconstruction, objection categories, and the court's independent review authority that govern compensation of bankruptcy professionals.

What the fee application process is

A fee application is the procedural vehicle by which an estate-employed professional - debtor's counsel, trustee's counsel, official committee counsel, financial advisor, accountant, appraiser, or other professional retained under 11 U.S.C. Section 327 or analogous provisions - seeks court approval of compensation to be paid from estate funds. The statutory authority is 11 U.S.C. Section 330; the procedural framework is Federal Rule of Bankruptcy Procedure 2016 and corresponding local rules; substantive guidance comes from the U.S. Trustee's Guidelines for Reviewing Applications for Compensation.

Compensation in bankruptcy is fundamentally different from compensation in nonbankruptcy practice. The court must approve every dollar paid from estate funds, including fees the professional and the client have agreed upon by engagement letter. The retainer paid pre-petition is also subject to court review under Rule 2016(b) disclosure. A professional who receives or applies estate funds without court approval is exposed to disgorgement.

Rule 2016(a) supporting documentation

Rule 2016(a) requires that a fee application contain "a detailed statement of (1) the services rendered, time expended and expenses incurred, and (2) the amounts requested." In practice, this requires:

  • Contemporaneous time records in tenths-of-an-hour increments, listing for each entry the date of service, the timekeeper, the time billed, the billing rate, the dollar amount, and a narrative description of the task sufficient to permit independent review of reasonableness and necessity. Reconstructed time records (those prepared after the fact) are heavily disfavored and, in many districts, will be disallowed entirely.
  • Timekeeper biographies and rates. The application should identify each timekeeper, the timekeeper's role (partner, associate, paralegal), the timekeeper's hourly rate, and a brief biography establishing the rate's reasonableness in the relevant market.
  • Project or matter categorization. The U.S. Trustee Guidelines require segregation of time into project categories (case administration, asset analysis, litigation, plan confirmation, fee applications, etc.) with a summary by project category. Lump time entries that fail to permit project allocation are commonly reduced.
  • Expense detail. Reimbursable expenses must be itemized. Photocopy charges above actual cost, secretarial overtime, in-office meals, and overhead items billed as expenses are routinely disallowed.
  • Narrative summary of services. A description of what was accomplished in the application period, the benefit to the estate, and the relationship of the services to the case's progress.
  • Connection statement. Disclosure of any change in connection or compensation status since the original Section 327 application or prior fee application.

The lodestar method

The dominant compensation methodology in bankruptcy is the lodestar: hours reasonably expended multiplied by a reasonable hourly rate. The Section 330(a)(3) factors the court considers include (a) the time spent, (b) the rates charged, (c) whether the services were necessary to the administration of, or beneficial at the time at which the service was rendered toward the completion of, the case, (d) whether the services were performed within a reasonable amount of time commensurate with the complexity, importance, and nature of the problem, (e) the demonstrated skill and experience of the professional, and (f) whether the compensation is reasonable based on the customary compensation charged by comparably skilled practitioners in cases other than bankruptcy.

The Supreme Court in Baker Botts L.L.P. v. ASARCO LLC, 576 U.S. 121 (2015), held that Section 330 does not authorize compensation for time spent defending a fee application against an objection. Time spent preparing the application is compensable; time spent defending it is not. The distinction is implemented in many districts through a "fee defense" disallowance at the post-application stage.

Lodestar reconstruction

Where contemporaneous records are inadequate or missing, reconstruction is sometimes attempted. Reconstruction is not a substitute for contemporaneous records; courts treat reconstructed time with skepticism. Acceptable reconstruction supplementation typically requires (a) corroborating documentary evidence (calendars, email metadata, court filings, deposition transcripts) that anchor the time entries to specific activities on specific dates, and (b) an explanation of why contemporaneous records are unavailable. A professional whose entire fee application is reconstructed from billing memory will not be paid in most courts.

Objection categories

The principal categories of fee objection - whether raised by the U.S. Trustee, a creditor, a creditors' committee, or sua sponte by the court - are:

Vagueness

Time entries that do not permit the reviewer to determine what was done. "Research" without identifying the topic; "review correspondence" without identifying the correspondence; "conference with co-counsel" without identifying the subject. The remedy is reduction or disallowance of the specific entries.

Block billing

Single time entries combining multiple discrete tasks without segregation, preventing reasonableness review of any individual component. Many local rules and U.S. Trustee guidelines impose presumptive percentage reductions on block-billed entries (commonly 10% to 30%).

Overstaffing

Multiple professionals at a single deposition, hearing, or conference where the case's complexity does not justify the duplication; multiple partners on tasks suitable for an associate; partner time on tasks suitable for a paralegal. The U.S. Trustee routinely objects to "associate-shadowed-by-partner" hearing appearances absent a specific justification.

Excessive time

Hours billed disproportionate to the task. Six hours to draft a routine motion to extend a deadline; twenty hours to research an issue on which the firm has prior memoranda; multi-day "review of the file" without identification of specific deliverables. Reviewers compare hours billed to the work product produced.

Clerical or secretarial tasks

Time spent on filing, scheduling, calendaring, copying, organizing files, and other tasks that are properly overhead rather than professional services. Such time is routinely disallowed regardless of the timekeeper's seniority.

Non-bankruptcy work

Time on matters not properly the subject of estate compensation - personal matters of the principals, prepetition collection of pre-engagement fees, non-bankruptcy litigation funded by the estate without prior court approval.

Lack of benefit

Services that did not produce a benefit to the estate. The Supreme Court in Lamie v. United States Trustee, 540 U.S. 526 (2004), confirmed that Section 330(a)(1) limits compensation in Chapter 7 cases to the trustee and to professionals employed under Section 327; debtor's counsel in Chapter 7 is compensated through the retainer, not from estate funds.

Excessive rates

Rates above the prevailing market for the relevant locality and practice area. The U.S. Trustee may submit rate-survey data; objections at this level are less common but do occur, particularly where lead counsel imports out-of-market rates without a corresponding showing of necessity.

Sua sponte review

The bankruptcy court has independent authority and obligation to review fee applications even if no objection is filed. In re Busy Beaver Building Centers, Inc., 19 F.3d 833 (3d Cir. 1994), articulates the standard adopted in most circuits: the court is the gatekeeper of estate compensation and may reduce fees on its own motion. The court may direct the applicant to provide additional documentation, may set the matter for hearing, and may enter an order reducing the requested compensation with findings supporting the reduction.

In some districts the court refers fee applications to a special examiner or to a fee committee for an initial review; the court then enters an order based on the report. The applicant typically has an opportunity to respond before the order is entered.

Hearing preparation

Contested fee applications are bench-tried on the documentary record supplemented by limited live testimony. The applicant should prepare:

  • A response brief that addresses each objection category-by-category, identifying for each contested entry either a defense or a withdrawal/reduction.
  • A revised summary of fees, presenting the requested amount net of any voluntarily reduced entries, so the court has a clear bottom-line figure.
  • A timekeeper available to testify briefly about the contested entries and to explain the necessity and benefit, if the court requests.
  • Comparable-case data (other approved fee applications in similar matters) if rates or aggregate fees are challenged.

The objecting party should prepare a chart identifying each contested entry by date, timekeeper, amount, and objection basis, with citations to the supporting authority. A general objection ("the fees are excessive") without specific entry-level analysis is commonly overruled; the court will not undertake line-by-line review without prompts.

Interim and final applications

Section 331 authorizes interim fee applications no more frequently than 120 days, although shorter intervals may be approved by court order in large or complex cases. Interim applications are interlocutory; the court retains authority to reconsider interim awards in connection with the final application. A final fee application is filed at case closure or upon completion of the engagement; the court's final order is appealable as a final order.

Fee application preparation begins at engagement, not at filing. The contemporaneous-records discipline, the timekeeper rate justification, the project-category structure, and the connection-disclosure obligations all start on day one. A fee application built from clean records billed in real time and to category survives most objections; one assembled from billing memory does not.
