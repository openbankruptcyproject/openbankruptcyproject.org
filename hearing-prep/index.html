Hearing Preparation

Bankruptcy court hearings range from the highly informal 341 meeting (no judge, no formal evidence) to multi-day evidentiary trials in adversary proceedings. Knowing the rules of engagement before the hearing is the single biggest determinant of outcome. These five hearing-preparation guides walk through what to expect, what to bring, what to wear, and how to plan testimony for the most common bankruptcy hearings.

About bankruptcy hearings generally

Bankruptcy hearings fall into three procedural categories. Section 341 meetings are conducted by the trustee, not the judge, and serve a fact-gathering function akin to a deposition under oath. Contested-matter hearings (motion practice under Bankruptcy Rule 9014) are conducted by the bankruptcy judge on the motion calendar, often without live testimony, and apply a relaxed version of the rules of evidence. Adversary-proceeding hearings (Part VII of the Bankruptcy Rules) are conducted under the full Federal Rules of Civil Procedure and Federal Rules of Evidence as modified by the Bankruptcy Rules.

Preparation differs accordingly. A 341 meeting requires the debtor to bring photo ID, Social Security verification, and original of any documents the trustee has requested; testimony is in narrative form and is rarely combative. A contested matter calendar hearing usually involves attorney argument on a fully-briefed motion; live witnesses are unusual outside a few categories such as relief-from-stay hearings where valuation is disputed. An adversary trial follows the same format as a federal civil trial including pre-trial conferences, motions in limine, exhibit lists, and witness preparation.

The deep-dives below walk through each hearing type, the typical scheduling cadence, the documents to bring, the typical questions and arguments, and the post-hearing follow-up. They are pitched at the level of a debtor or creditor counsel preparing for a first hearing in an unfamiliar matter, not at the practitioner already steeped in the local culture of a given court.

Hearing-prep guides

  • 341 Meeting of CreditorsThe trustee-conducted meeting of creditors required by section 341, the standard trustee question script under the Handbook for Chapter 7 Trustees, document requirements, and how to handle creditor questioning.
  • Confirmation HearingPreparation for the chapter 11, chapter 12, chapter 13, or Subchapter V confirmation hearing including objection consolidation, declarant preparation, and the confirmation-order presentation.
  • Relief from Stay HearingThe 30-day fast-track hearing under section 362(e), valuation evidence for the equity-cushion analysis, and the typical evidentiary posture for an unopposed versus contested motion.
  • Dischargeability HearingTrial preparation for a section 523 dischargeability adversary proceeding, including the burden of proof, common section 523(a)(2), (4), and (6) trial themes, and the use of summary-judgment motions.
  • Fee Application HearingDefending a section 330 fee application against UST or party-in-interest objections, the lodestar presentation, the documentation of unusual time entries, and the no-look-fee alternative in chapter 13.
Calendar etiquette: Most bankruptcy courts publish their tentative rulings on contested matters the day before the hearing. Counsel should check the court's website or PACER for tentatives and notify the courtroom deputy whether the matter will be submitted on the tentative or argued. Failure to appear when the matter is set for argument can result in adverse rulings.
