11 U.S.C. Section 362(d) Automatic Stay

Relief from Stay Hearing Preparation

Movant's prima facie case, debtor response, equity calculation, adequate-protection negotiation, and the 30-day and 60-day deadlines that structure the proceeding.

What a relief-from-stay motion is

A motion for relief from the automatic stay is the procedural vehicle by which a secured creditor, lessor, or other party in interest seeks an order from the bankruptcy court authorizing it to take action that would otherwise violate the automatic stay imposed by 11 U.S.C. Section 362(a). The statutory grounds are at Section 362(d). The motion is heard as a contested matter under Federal Rule of Bankruptcy Procedure 9014, with the motion itself initiating the proceeding and a hearing scheduled on notice. Section 362(e) provides that the stay automatically terminates 30 days after the request unless the court (a) orders the stay to continue or (b) sets the hearing within 30 days and rules within a final hearing concluded within 30 days thereafter; the practical effect is a fast-track schedule that constrains both sides.

The grounds for relief

Section 362(d)(1) - "for cause"

The most commonly invoked ground. "Cause" is not defined in the statute and includes, by enumeration in the legislative history and developed case law, lack of adequate protection of the movant's interest in property. The movant must show that the value of its interest is declining or at substantial risk of decline and that the debtor has not provided adequate protection. Beyond inadequate protection, "cause" has been found in: bad faith filings; failure to maintain insurance on collateral; failure to make post-petition payments; pending nonbankruptcy litigation in another forum that should be permitted to proceed (the Sonnax factors and equivalents); and the existence of independent nondebtor obligors as to whom the bankruptcy provides no real benefit.

Section 362(d)(2) - lack of equity and not necessary to reorganization

The movant must show (a) the debtor has no equity in the property and the burden then shifts to the debtor to show that (b) the property is necessary to an effective reorganization. The second prong, after United Savings Association v. Timbers of Inwood Forest Associates, 484 U.S. 365 (1988), requires the debtor to demonstrate not merely that the property would be useful in a reorganization but that there is a reasonable possibility of a successful reorganization within a reasonable time. "No equity" means the aggregate of liens against the property exceeds the property's value; the value determination is made as of the hearing date, not the petition date.

Section 362(d)(3) - single asset real estate (SARE)

In a case involving single asset real estate (defined at Section 101(51B)), the stay terminates 90 days after the order for relief unless the debtor either files a plan with a reasonable possibility of confirmation within a reasonable time or commences monthly payments to the secured creditor at the then-current nondefault contract rate on the value of the creditor's interest.

Section 362(d)(4) - in rem relief

If the court finds the bankruptcy filing was part of a scheme to delay, hinder, or defraud creditors involving multiple filings, transfers, or filings without permission of the court, the court may enter in rem relief that binds successive filings as to the property for two years.

Movant's prima facie case

The movant must establish, at minimum:

  1. Standing. The movant is the holder, servicer, or authorized representative of the underlying note and security instrument. Attach the note (with all endorsements), the security instrument, the assignment chain if applicable, and either a corporate-representative declaration or testimony that the movant possesses (or has a contractual right to enforce) the note.
  2. Perfection. The lien is perfected. Real estate: recorded deed of trust or mortgage. Vehicles: certificate of title showing the lien. UCC collateral: filed financing statement.
  3. Default. The debtor is in default. For monetary default, a payment-history exhibit showing the missed payments and the current arrearage. For non-monetary default (insurance lapse, tax delinquency), corresponding documentary evidence.
  4. Value. The value of the collateral, supported by an appraisal, broker price opinion, or competent declaration if the value is contested.
  5. Statutory ground. The specific subsection of Section 362(d) on which relief is sought, with the facts mapped to the elements.

Many districts require movants to use a local-form motion and to attach a checklist or summary of relief sought; failure to comply produces immediate denial without prejudice.

Debtor's response

The debtor's response is filed within the time set by local rule or scheduling order, commonly 14 to 21 days from service. The response should identify which grounds are contested and offer affirmative defenses.

Equity calculation

Equity is the difference between the property's value and the sum of all liens. The calculation is rarely as simple as appraisal-minus-loan-balance because (a) junior liens must be aggregated, (b) the loan balance includes accrued interest and (in many circuits) reasonable fees and costs, and (c) the value figure is contested. A debtor opposing Section 362(d)(2) relief should be prepared with a competing valuation. The valuation contest at a stay hearing is usually conducted on declarations and short testimony rather than full expert depositions; preliminary or summary appraisals are common.

Adequate protection

If the motion is grounded in inadequate protection, the debtor's principal defense is to offer adequate protection. 11 U.S.C. Section 361 enumerates three forms: periodic cash payments to the extent of any decrease in value of the movant's interest; additional or replacement liens to the same extent; and the indubitable equivalent of the movant's interest. An offer of adequate protection should be specific (amount, due dates, source of funds) and supported (proof of insurance, proof of equity cushion, projected cash flow). The equity-cushion theory of adequate protection - that a substantial equity cushion is itself adequate protection because depreciation will not erode the movant's interest within a reasonable time - is recognized in most circuits, with the cushion percentage required varying but commonly cited in the 15-20% range.

Necessary to effective reorganization

If the motion proceeds under Section 362(d)(2), the debtor must put on the Timbers showing. This requires not just that the property is integral to the business or the household budget, but that there is a reasonable prospect of successful reorganization in a reasonable time. For a Chapter 13 debtor, the showing typically involves Schedules I and J supporting the plan payment, evidence of plan compliance to date, and (where applicable) employment-stability evidence. For a Chapter 11 debtor the showing involves a feasible operating plan or a path to one.

The 30-day rule and its consequences

Section 362(e)(1) provides that the stay terminates 30 days after the motion was filed unless the court enters a final or preliminary order continuing the stay. In consumer cases, Section 362(e)(2) shortens the period to 60 days for individual debtor cases unless extended on consent or for compelling circumstances. Most courts hold a preliminary hearing within the 30-day window and either grant a preliminary continuance of the stay (with adequate-protection conditions) or proceed to final hearing. A debtor or trustee who allows the 30-day window to lapse without an order continuing the stay risks termination by operation of law - a risk that no defensive strategy can recover.

Negotiation - the typical outcome

Most contested relief-from-stay motions in consumer and small-business cases resolve before final hearing through a stipulated order (sometimes called a "stay order" or "stip-and-order"). Common terms: a cure schedule for the arrearage; a default-and-cure period before the stay automatically terminates on a future default (commonly 14 days notice and 7 days to cure); a waiver of further stay protection on the same default; a payment-application schedule. The stipulated order is presented to the court and entered without hearing. From the movant's perspective, a stipulated order produces a faster and lower-cost resolution than litigation; from the debtor's, it produces breathing room without losing the case. Both sides should review the stipulation carefully: a default-and-cure clause that runs from the date a check is "received" rather than "due" has different consequences, and a waiver of further stay protection can foreclose a critical defense later.

Relief-from-stay hearings are short and document-driven. The contested issue is almost always value, payment history, or whether adequate protection has been offered. The party with the cleaner exhibits, the specific payment proposal, and the realistic value position usually prevails. Procedural deadlines under Section 362(e) are unforgiving; calendar them on filing.
