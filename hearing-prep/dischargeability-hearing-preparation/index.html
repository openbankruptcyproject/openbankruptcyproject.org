11 U.S.C. Section 523 Adversary Proceeding

Dischargeability Hearing Preparation

Section 523 adversary proceeding mechanics, the 60-day complaint deadline, evidence preservation, summary judgment versus trial, and the differing burdens that govern each subsection of nondischargeable debt.

What a dischargeability proceeding is

A dischargeability proceeding is an adversary proceeding (a discrete civil action within the bankruptcy case) seeking a judgment that a particular debt is excepted from discharge under 11 U.S.C. Section 523(a). The proceeding is governed by the adversary-proceeding rules in Part VII of the Federal Rules of Bankruptcy Procedure, which incorporate most of the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure. It is separate from the underlying bankruptcy case in docket structure (new adversary number assigned), but proceeds before the same bankruptcy judge.

The proceeding does not relitigate the underlying debt. The amount and existence of the debt are commonly the subject of a prior judgment or established by stipulation; the dischargeability proceeding adjudicates whether that debt falls within a Section 523(a) exception. Where the underlying debt is itself unliquidated, the bankruptcy court has jurisdiction to adjudicate both liability and nondischargeability in the same proceeding.

The 60-day deadline

The complaint to determine dischargeability of a debt under Section 523(a)(2), (4), or (6) must be filed not later than 60 days after the first date set for the meeting of creditors. Federal Rule of Bankruptcy Procedure 4007(c). The deadline is jurisdictional in many circuits and is not subject to equitable tolling absent unusual circumstances. The court may extend the deadline only on motion filed before its expiration. Creditors who learn of the bankruptcy after the deadline have a separate right under Section 523(a)(3) - the unscheduled-debt exception - that does not depend on a timely complaint.

Other Section 523 categories - notably (a)(1) tax debts, (a)(5) and (a)(15) domestic obligations, (a)(7) governmental fines, (a)(8) student loans, and (a)(9) drunk-driving liability - are nondischargeable by operation of law and may be the subject of a complaint at any time during or after the bankruptcy case. The 60-day deadline applies only to the three subsections that require a complaint to render the debt nondischargeable: (a)(2) fraud, (a)(4) embezzlement / fiduciary defalcation, and (a)(6) willful and malicious injury.

The complaint - elements and pleading

The complaint must plead facts establishing each element of the asserted Section 523(a) subsection. Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 9(b)'s particularity requirement, incorporated by Bankruptcy Rule 7009, applies to fraud-based claims (Sections 523(a)(2)(A) and (B), and to the extent based on fraud, 523(a)(4)). Boilerplate fraud allegations are routinely dismissed; the complaint must identify the specific representation, when made, by whom, to whom, the falsity, the scienter, the reliance, and the damages.

The elements of the most-litigated subsections:

  • Section 523(a)(2)(A) - false pretenses, false representation, or actual fraud. A misrepresentation made by the debtor with intent to deceive, on which the creditor justifiably relied, and which caused damages. The Supreme Court's decision in Husky International Electronics, Inc. v. Ritz, 578 U.S. 355 (2016), extended "actual fraud" to include fraudulent-transfer schemes that do not involve a misrepresentation to the creditor.
  • Section 523(a)(2)(B) - false written financial statements. A written statement respecting the debtor's financial condition, materially false, made with intent to deceive, on which the creditor reasonably relied. The "respecting the debtor's financial condition" language was narrowed by Lamar, Archer & Cofrin, LLP v. Appling, 584 U.S. 709 (2018), holding that the phrase covers oral statements about a single asset, removing them from the writing requirement.
  • Section 523(a)(4) - fraud or defalcation while acting in a fiduciary capacity, embezzlement, or larceny. The fiduciary capacity must be a technical trust or express trust, not a general fiduciary duty. The Supreme Court in Bullock v. BankChampaign, N.A., 569 U.S. 267 (2013), held that "defalcation" requires a culpable state of mind involving knowledge of, or gross recklessness as to, the improper nature of the relevant fiduciary behavior.
  • Section 523(a)(6) - willful and malicious injury. Kawaauhau v. Geiger, 523 U.S. 57 (1998), held that "willful" requires a deliberate or intentional injury, not merely a deliberate act that produces injury. Recklessness or negligence does not suffice.

The plaintiff's burden on each element is preponderance of the evidence (Grogan v. Garner, 498 U.S. 279 (1991)), notwithstanding the higher clear-and-convincing standard often required for common-law fraud in nonbankruptcy courts.

Evidence preservation

The plaintiff's preservation obligations begin pre-complaint. The complaint should be drafted from a documentary base, not from allegations to be later supported. Common preservation issues:

  • Original communications. Emails, text messages, recorded calls, and contemporaneous notes establishing the alleged misrepresentation. If the underlying business or banking relationship is closed, records may be subpoena-only and subject to retention-limit destruction.
  • Loan or extension-of-credit application documents. For Section 523(a)(2)(B), the actual written statement and any contemporaneous lender notes documenting reliance and the materiality of the false items.
  • Transaction records. For Section 523(a)(4) defalcation or embezzlement claims, the trust or fiduciary records, ledgers, and bank statements covering the period of alleged misconduct.
  • Prior litigation record. If a state-court judgment exists, the trial transcript, exhibits, and findings of fact may have issue-preclusive effect under collateral-estoppel doctrine, depending on the issues actually litigated and necessary to the judgment.

Defendants face parallel preservation issues. Spoliation sanctions in adversary proceedings track Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 37(e); destruction of relevant evidence after the duty to preserve attached can support adverse-inference instructions or, in extreme cases, default judgment.

Summary judgment

Section 523 adversary proceedings are summary-judgment-eligible under Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 56, incorporated by Bankruptcy Rule 7056. Summary judgment is commonly sought where:

  • The plaintiff invokes collateral estoppel from a prior state-court judgment that necessarily decided the elements of the Section 523(a) exception. The classic example: a state-court jury verdict for fraud, where the elements decided substantially overlap with Section 523(a)(2)(A). The bankruptcy court applies the issue-preclusion law of the state that rendered the judgment.
  • The defendant moves on a limitations or threshold defense (untimely complaint under Rule 4007(c), failure to plead with Rule 9(b) particularity, failure to allege a required element).
  • The dispositive issue is documentary and not credibility-dependent (the written representation was true, no reasonable jury could find scienter on the documentary record, the alleged trust was not a technical trust).

Where summary judgment is denied because credibility is at issue (intent, reliance, knowledge), the matter proceeds to bench trial. Section 523 proceedings are tried to the bankruptcy judge without a jury; jury-trial rights do not generally attach to dischargeability determinations.

Trial preparation

Trial of a Section 523 proceeding is typically one to three days. The plaintiff's case-in-chief usually comprises (a) the underlying transaction documents; (b) the plaintiff's testimony or representative testimony on reliance and damages; (c) the debtor as an adverse witness on intent, knowledge, and motive; and (d) any third-party witnesses to the misrepresentation or to the misconduct. The defendant's case typically comprises (a) explanatory testimony on intent and knowledge; (b) any third-party witnesses corroborating the debtor's account; and (c) rebuttal evidence on damages or on the asserted preclusion. Each side's cross-examination chiefly attacks scienter and reliance, where the evidence is most often documentary-light and credibility-driven.

Pretrial submissions in adversary proceedings commonly include: a joint statement of stipulated facts; exhibit lists with objections; witness lists; deposition designations; and trial briefs identifying the contested legal issues. Local rules vary widely on form and timing; consult the case scheduling order and the judge's chambers procedures.

Discovery

Discovery in adversary proceedings is governed by Bankruptcy Rules 7026 through 7037, which incorporate the federal civil discovery rules with adaptations. Initial disclosures under Rule 26(a) are required unless local practice or the scheduling order modifies. Discovery in dischargeability proceedings often focuses on (a) the debtor's contemporaneous records (financial statements, accounting records, communications) at the time of the alleged misrepresentation or misconduct; (b) the plaintiff's reliance documentation (underwriting files, decision memoranda); and (c) third-party records bearing on solvency, intent, or the transaction. Subpoenas to former employers, banks, accountants, and counterparties are routine.

Dischargeability trials reward early preparation. The discovery window is short (often four to six months in standard scheduling orders), the documentary record must usually be assembled before the complaint is filed to ensure compliance with Rule 9(b), and the 60-day complaint deadline is unforgiving. Creditors who wait until after the 341 meeting to evaluate a Section 523 claim frequently miss the window.
