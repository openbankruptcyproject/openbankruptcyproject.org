11 U.S.C. Section 341 Hearing Preparation

341 Meeting of Creditors Preparation

What the trustee asks, what to bring, what to wear, and the common pitfalls that turn a routine examination into a continued meeting or a Rule 2004 expansion.

What the 341 meeting is

The meeting of creditors is the proceeding mandated by 11 U.S.C. Section 341 at which the debtor appears under oath and is examined about the petition, schedules, statement of financial affairs, and the debtor's financial condition generally. The examiner is the case trustee in Chapter 7, Chapter 12, and Chapter 13 cases; in Chapter 11 cases the United States Trustee or a designee conducts the meeting. The bankruptcy judge does not attend and does not preside; the statute expressly forbids judicial participation in the meeting of creditors. The meeting is not a hearing in the courtroom sense - it is a recorded examination, typically held at a United States Trustee meeting room, a federal building hearing room, or (increasingly) by video or telephone conference under standing orders that survived the temporary remote-meeting authority of the pandemic period.

The proceeding is governed procedurally by Federal Rule of Bankruptcy Procedure 2003, which sets the timing window (no fewer than 21 nor more than 40 days after the petition date in Chapter 7 and 13; 21 to 50 days in Chapter 11; modified for small business and Subchapter V cases) and requires that the meeting be recorded.

Identification - the threshold requirement

The debtor must present, before the meeting begins, original (not photocopied) government-issued photo identification and an original document evidencing the Social Security number. The acceptable forms are specified by the United States Trustee Program in its handbook. Photo identification commonly accepted: a current driver's license, a state identification card, a U.S. passport, a permanent resident card, a current military identification, or a current passport from another country. Social Security number evidence commonly accepted: an original Social Security card, a current W-2, a recent payroll stub showing the full SSN, an IRS Form 1099, or an IRS transcript.

A debtor who appears without the required identification will not be examined. The meeting will be continued and the debtor must reappear. Repeated continuance for identification failure can prompt dismissal of the case. This is the most common preventable failure at the meeting; it is also the most embarrassing one.

Documents to bring

Beyond identification, prepare the following:

  • A copy of the petition, schedules, and statement of financial affairs as filed
  • The two most recent pay statements from each employment source (or self-employment income summary)
  • The most recent federal income tax return (and, in many districts, the two most recent)
  • The most recent statements for every deposit account, retirement account, and brokerage account listed on the schedules
  • Mortgage statements and vehicle loan statements showing principal balance, monthly payment, and any arrearage
  • Domestic support order, divorce decree, or property settlement agreement if any support obligations exist
  • Evidence of insurance on real property and vehicles
  • For self-employed debtors, profit-and-loss statements for the months between the most recent tax return and the petition date

Many trustees also send a pre-meeting letter listing district-specific documents. Read the letter; bring everything it asks for. A trustee who has to repeat document requests at the meeting will often continue the meeting rather than proceed with gaps, which delays the discharge.

The standard examination

The United States Trustee Program publishes required questions that every trustee must ask. The list is short and predictable:

  1. State your full name and address for the record.
  2. Did you sign the petition, schedules, statements, and related documents, and is the signature your own?
  3. Did you read the petition, schedules, statements, and related documents before signing?
  4. Are you personally familiar with the information contained in the petition, schedules, and statements?
  5. To the best of your knowledge, is the information contained in the petition, schedules, and statements true and correct?
  6. Are there any errors or omissions to bring to the trustee's attention?
  7. Are all of your assets identified on the schedules?
  8. Are all of your creditors listed on the schedules?
  9. Have you previously filed bankruptcy? (If yes, identify the case number, court, and chapter.)
  10. What is the address of your current employer?
  11. Is the copy of your most recent tax return that you provided to the trustee a true copy?
  12. Do you have a domestic support obligation? (If yes, the trustee will ask further questions about the obligee and the amount.)
  13. Have you made any payments to creditors (other than ordinary course) or to insiders within the look-back period?
  14. Have you transferred any property (other than in the ordinary course) within the look-back period?

Beyond these, the trustee may ask anything relevant to the administration of the estate. Common follow-ups: the source and use of unusual deposits in bank statements; the basis of vehicle valuations; the existence of personal-injury claims, inheritance expectancies, or tax refunds; the relationship of any insider transferees; and the disposition of recently-closed accounts.

Common pitfalls

Volunteering information

Answer the question asked. Do not narrate; do not explain motivation; do not editorialize. A long answer creates additional examination paths and additional opportunities to contradict the schedules. "Yes," "No," "I don't know," and "I don't remember" are complete answers when accurate.

Guessing

The debtor is under oath. A guess that turns out to be wrong is a false oath. If the answer is not known with confidence, say so. The trustee will accept "I don't know" and move on; the trustee will not accept a confident answer that later proves false.

Inconsistency with the schedules

If the meeting answer differs from what the schedules say, the trustee will ask which is correct. The correct response is rarely to disavow the schedules on the spot; the schedules are the sworn document. If an error exists, the trustee will direct the debtor to file amended schedules. Do not contradict the schedules without first identifying the contradiction as an error that will be amended.

Missing or untimely tax returns

11 U.S.C. Section 521(e)(2) requires the debtor to deliver to the trustee a copy of the most recent federal tax return at least 7 days before the meeting. Failure can cause dismissal under Section 521(e)(2)(B). The deadline is not waivable by the trustee informally; if missed, file a motion for extension promptly.

Cash on hand and unusual deposits

Recent large cash withdrawals or deposits are visible on bank statements and will be questioned. Prepare a one-line explanation for each transaction over the trustee's customary threshold (typically $1,000 - $2,500). "I cashed my paycheck" is acceptable when documented; "I don't recall" is not, when the amount is significant.

Conduct expectations

Dress neatly and conservatively. Business casual is appropriate; courtroom attire is not required but signals seriousness. Arrive 30 minutes early. Silence the phone. Address the trustee as "Trustee [last name]" or "Ms./Mr. [last name]." Do not interrupt; let the question finish before answering. If a creditor attends and asks a question, the trustee will allow it within the scope of the meeting; the same answer-the-question-asked discipline applies.

The meeting is recorded. Anything said becomes part of the record and can be used in subsequent adversary proceedings, in motions to dismiss for cause, in objections to discharge under Section 727, and in dischargeability complaints under Section 523. Treat the answers accordingly.

After the meeting

The trustee will state on the record whether the meeting is concluded or continued. A concluded meeting starts the clock for objections: 60 days from the conclusion for nondischargeability complaints under Section 523(a)(2), (4), and (6), and for objections to discharge under Section 727; 30 days for objections to exemptions under Rule 4003(b). A continued meeting does not start those clocks until the meeting is actually concluded. If the trustee announced an amendment requirement or a document request, deliver the items before the continuance date; failure to do so commonly results in a motion to dismiss for unreasonable delay.

The 341 meeting is short and procedural in most cases. The median Chapter 7 consumer meeting runs 4 to 8 minutes. Preparation is what produces a short meeting; the debtor who knows the schedules, brings the documents, and answers narrowly gets through the examination quickly. The debtor who does not is the one who comes back.
