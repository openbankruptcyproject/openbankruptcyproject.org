Plan Confirmation Hearing Preparation

The evidentiary record, feasibility testimony, valuation evidence, and objection handling that determine whether a plan is confirmed under Chapters 11, 12, or 13.

What confirmation is

Plan confirmation is the court's adjudication that a proposed reorganization or repayment plan satisfies the statutory requirements for that chapter and may bind creditors. The applicable provisions are 11 U.S.C. Section 1129 for Chapter 11, Section 1225 for Chapter 12, and Section 1325 for Chapter 13. Subchapter V of Chapter 11 imports the Section 1129 framework with substantial modifications under Section 1191. The confirmation hearing is an evidentiary proceeding. The proponent of the plan bears the burden of proving each confirmation requirement; objecting parties bear the burden on affirmative defenses (such as the absolute priority rule challenge or a good-faith objection grounded in non-financial facts).

The structure of the hearing

The hearing typically proceeds in three phases:

  1. Procedural rulings. The court takes up any pending motions in limine, evidentiary objections, and procedural disputes (disclosure statement adequacy in Chapter 11 cases where deferred, ballot tabulation disputes, late-filed objections). The court may make preliminary findings about the contested or uncontested status of each requirement.
  2. Proponent's case-in-chief. The plan proponent presents testimony and documentary evidence on the contested confirmation requirements. In most consumer Chapter 13 cases the proponent's case is brief and largely documentary; in Chapter 11 cases it commonly involves multiple witnesses including a financial advisor, a valuation expert, and an officer of the debtor.
  3. Objectors' case and rebuttal. Objecting creditors present their evidence on the contested issues. The proponent may put on rebuttal. Closing argument follows; the court may rule from the bench or take the matter under advisement.

The core confirmation requirements

Best interests of creditors test

Each impaired class must receive at least as much under the plan as it would receive in a hypothetical Chapter 7 liquidation as of the effective date. Section 1129(a)(7), Section 1225(a)(4), Section 1325(a)(4). The test is performed creditor by creditor for each impaired class; it requires a liquidation analysis. The proponent's liquidation analysis must value each asset at its forced-sale value (not going-concern value), subtract administrative expenses of a hypothetical Chapter 7, distribute through the priority waterfall, and identify the recovery percentage to each class. The chief vulnerabilities of liquidation analyses: overstatement of administrative expenses, understatement of unencumbered asset values, omission of preference and fraudulent-transfer recoveries that would inure to a Chapter 7 trustee.

Feasibility

The plan must be feasible: not likely to be followed by liquidation or further financial reorganization, except as proposed in the plan itself. Section 1129(a)(11), Section 1225(a)(6), Section 1325(a)(6). Feasibility is the requirement most frequently litigated in contested cases. The proponent must offer evidence (commonly through a projection witness) of sufficient cash flow to make the plan payments. The projections must be supported, not asserted. Standard impeachment of feasibility projections: comparison to historical performance, reasonableness of growth assumptions, treatment of expense items historically present but omitted prospectively, sensitivity to identified risks (commodity prices, customer concentration, regulatory exposures), and the existence of a reasonable downside scenario.

Good faith

The plan must be proposed in good faith and not by any means forbidden by law. Section 1129(a)(3), Section 1225(a)(3), Section 1325(a)(3). Good-faith objections typically involve allegations that the plan is principally a litigation-defeat device, that classification of claims was gerrymandered to assemble an accepting impaired class, that the debtor's pre-petition conduct (concealment, transfers, dischargeability-evading behavior) infects the plan, or - in Chapter 13 - that the proposed payment percentage is unreasonably low relative to ability.

Acceptance / cramdown

Each impaired class must accept the plan, or the plan must satisfy cramdown requirements. Section 1129(a)(8) and (b); Section 1325(a)(5) and (b). Acceptance of a class of claims requires acceptances from creditors holding at least two-thirds in amount and more than one-half in number of allowed claims voting. Cramdown over a non-accepting class requires fair-and-equitable treatment, which in turn requires either payment-in-full of the secured claim with present-value interest (the indubitable-equivalent or replacement-lien test) or compliance with the absolute priority rule for unsecured classes.

Disposable income (Chapter 13)

An above-median Chapter 13 debtor must commit projected disposable income for five years; a below-median debtor for three years (extendable to five for cause). Section 1325(b). The disposable-income calculation is mechanical for above-median debtors (Form 122C calculation) and discretionary for below-median debtors (Schedules I and J). Objecting trustees commonly contest the household-size determination, the validity of claimed expenses, and the categorization of secured-debt deductions.

Evidentiary record - what to put on

Anticipate the requirements at issue and assemble the record accordingly. In a Chapter 13 contested confirmation, the typical record includes: the debtor's testimony in support of Schedules I/J and the disposable-income calculation; documentation of post-petition income (pay statements or business records); documentation of post-petition expenses where contested; appraisals or valuation testimony for any contested secured-claim valuation; and (where 910-day or 1-year claim valuation is at issue) documentation of vehicle or collateral condition.

In Chapter 11 contested confirmation, the typical record adds: a financial-advisor witness for the projections and feasibility; a valuation witness for the going-concern or liquidation analyses; an officer of the debtor for the business narrative and for any management-continuity testimony required for absolute-priority compliance; counsel testimony or stipulation on solicitation compliance; and the ballot tabulation report.

Objection handling

Objections to confirmation must be filed before the hearing per the court's confirmation scheduling order. Late objections are commonly disallowed; an objecting party should preserve the objection by filing even an unverified placeholder before the deadline if the substance is still developing. At the hearing, address each filed objection systematically. The proponent's preparation should include a written objection-response chart (every objection, the proponent's position, the evidence supporting the position, the witness through whom the evidence is offered). Objecting parties should be prepared to identify (a) the specific statutory requirement allegedly violated, (b) the factual basis, and (c) the evidence to be offered.

Valuation evidence

Valuation disputes drive many contested confirmations - secured-claim bifurcation under Section 506(a), best-interests asset valuation, going-concern enterprise valuation. The court will weigh competing expert testimony. The recurring impeachment categories: methodology mismatch (comparable-sales versus income-approach versus cost-approach for the asset class), comparable inappropriateness, discount-rate selection, treatment of one-time items in normalized cash flow, and the appraiser's prior testimony in similar matters. A valuation that survives cross-examination is one that selects the right methodology for the asset, applies the methodology consistently, and discloses assumptions.

Subchapter V differences

Subchapter V (small business debtors electing under Section 1182) confirms under Section 1191. If all impaired classes accept, confirmation is consensual under Section 1191(a). If an impaired class rejects, the debtor may seek cramdown under Section 1191(b), which dispenses with the absolute priority rule and substitutes a projected-disposable-income test similar to Chapter 13: the plan must commit projected disposable income for three to five years to unsecured creditors. The Subchapter V trustee plays a facilitative role, attends the hearing, and may take a position on confirmation.

Confirmation hearings reward preparation that the plan, the disclosure statement, and the ballots cannot themselves accomplish. The contested issues at confirmation are not legal interpretations of the statute - they are factual disputes about projections, valuations, and good faith. The party that arrives with the better-supported factual record usually prevails.
