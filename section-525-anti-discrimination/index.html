11 U.S.C. Section 525 - Anti-Discrimination Protections

A deep-dive guide to the protections that bar governmental units, private employers, and certain student-loan lenders from discriminating against bankruptcy debtors.

What Is Section 525?

Section 525 is the bankruptcy anti-discrimination statute. It supplements the Section 524 discharge injunction by prohibiting certain types of post-bankruptcy retaliation that fall outside the literal collection conduct that Section 524 covers. Where Section 524 stops creditors from collecting discharged debts, Section 525 stops governmental units, employers, and student-loan creditors from punishing the debtor for having filed bankruptcy or for having discharged a debt.

Section 525 was enacted in response to Perez v. Campbell, 402 U.S. 637 (1971), in which the Supreme Court struck down an Arizona statute that suspended driver's licenses of discharged debtors who had not paid pre-bankruptcy judgment debts. Congress codified Perez and extended its rule to a broader set of governmental and employment contexts.

Official citation: 11 U.S.C. § 525

Section 525(a): Governmental Unit Discrimination

Section 525(a) prohibits a governmental unit from denying, revoking, suspending, or refusing to renew a license, permit, charter, franchise, or "other similar grant" to, condition such a grant to, discriminate with respect to such a grant against, or deny employment to, terminate the employment of, or discriminate with respect to employment against, a person that is or has been a debtor under this title, solely because the debtor is or has been a debtor under this title, has been insolvent before commencement of the case or during the case but before discharge, or has not paid a debt that is dischargeable or has been discharged.

The "solely because" qualifier matters. A governmental licensor can refuse a license for any reason unrelated to the bankruptcy filing or the discharged debt, but it cannot impose conditions specifically tied to those facts.

In re Stoltz, 315 F.3d 80 (2d Cir. 2002), held that a public housing authority's eviction of a Chapter 7 debtor for nonpayment of discharged rent violated Section 525(a). The court treated continued occupancy in public housing as a "similar grant" within the statute's reach, extending Section 525(a) beyond conventional licenses and permits.

Section 525(b): Private Employer Discrimination

Section 525(b) extends a narrower set of anti-discrimination protections to private employment. A private employer may not terminate the employment of, or discriminate with respect to employment against, an individual who is or has been a debtor under this title, has been insolvent before commencement of the case or during the case but before discharge, or has not paid a debt that is dischargeable or has been discharged.

The key textual difference from Section 525(a) is the absence of any protection against initial hiring discrimination. The leading case, In re Burnett, 635 F.3d 169 (5th Cir. 2011), and several other circuits have held that Section 525(b) does not prohibit a private employer from refusing to hire a person because of a prior bankruptcy filing. The protection runs only to existing employees, not applicants.

Section 525(b)'s "solely because" trigger applies identically, requiring causation tracing to the bankruptcy filing or the unpaid discharged debt.

Section 525(c): Student Loan Discrimination

Section 525(c), added in 1994, prohibits a governmental unit that operates a student grant or loan program, and a person engaged in a business that includes the making of loans guaranteed or insured under a student loan program, from denying a grant, loan, loan guarantee, or loan insurance to a person that is or has been a debtor under this title or a bankrupt or debtor under the Bankruptcy Act, or another person with whom the debtor or bankrupt has been associated, because the debtor or bankrupt is or has been a debtor under this title or a bankrupt or debtor under the Bankruptcy Act, has been insolvent before the commencement of a case under this title or during the pendency of the case but before the debtor is granted or denied a discharge, or has not paid a debt that is dischargeable in the case under this title or that was discharged under the Bankruptcy Act.

Section 525(c) does not prohibit denial based on standard creditworthiness factors that are independent of the bankruptcy event. It does prohibit denial that is causally traceable to the bankruptcy filing or to the underlying discharged debt.

Interplay with the Section 524 Discharge Injunction

Section 524 and Section 525 work in tandem. Section 524(a)(2) operates as a permanent injunction against any act to collect, recover, or offset any discharged debt as a personal liability of the debtor. Section 525 picks up where Section 524 leaves off, prohibiting collateral retaliation that does not technically constitute collection conduct.

The two provisions can be violated by a single act. A governmental agency that refuses to renew a contractor's license unless the contractor pays a discharged pre-petition tax debt is simultaneously attempting to coerce payment of a discharged debt (Section 524(a)(2)) and conditioning a license on payment of a discharged debt (Section 525(a)). Remedies overlap but are not identical, and litigants typically plead both provisions.

Remedies and Enforcement

Section 525 does not contain its own express damages provision. Courts have generally relied on Section 105(a), which authorizes the bankruptcy court to "issue any order, process, or judgment that is necessary or appropriate to carry out the provisions of this title," as the basis for compensatory damages, attorney fees, and injunctive relief for Section 525 violations.

In re Bracewell, 454 F.3d 1234 (11th Cir. 2006), illustrates the procedural framework. The court treated a creditor's improper offset against farm program payments as both a Section 525 and a Section 524 violation and confirmed that Section 105 supports a money remedy for noncompliance.

Punitive damages remain controversial. Some courts require willful and egregious conduct; others have declined to award them absent an express Section 525 punitive provision. Sovereign immunity issues under Section 106 must be navigated when the defendant is a governmental unit.

In re Doser, 412 F.3d 1056 (9th Cir. 2005), addresses ancillary remedy questions in the discharge-related context. The general rule is that Section 525 supports actual damages, attorney fees, and equitable relief, with punitive damages available in narrower circumstances depending on circuit.
