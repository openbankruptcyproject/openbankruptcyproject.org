KRPC 1.18 / MRPC 4-1.18: Duties to Prospective Client

The consultation duty, the limited-information protection, and the screening + consent safe harbors that preserve continued representation.

What Rule 1.18 affirmatively does

Rule 1.18 is the rule that structures the threshold lawyer-client encounter - the consultation that may or may not become an engagement. The rule recognizes that prospective clients share information during these consultations, and it both protects that information and provides three pathways by which a firm can continue to represent existing or new matters notwithstanding a prospective-client consultation. The rule is enabling for prospective clients (they can speak candidly) and for firms (they have a structured pathway to remain available for adverse work where appropriate).

Verbatim text of KRPC 1.18 (Kansas)

(a) A person who consults with a lawyer about the possibility of forming a client-lawyer relationship with respect to a matter is a prospective client.

(b) Even when no client-lawyer relationship ensues, a lawyer who has learned information from a prospective client shall not use or reveal that information, except as Rule 1.9 would permit with respect to information of a former client.

(c) A lawyer subject to paragraph (b) shall not represent a client with interests materially adverse to those of a prospective client in the same or a substantially related matter if the lawyer received information from the prospective client that could be significantly harmful to that person in the matter, except as provided in paragraph (d). If a lawyer is disqualified from representation under this paragraph, no lawyer in a firm with which that lawyer is associated may knowingly undertake or continue representation in such a matter, except as provided in paragraph (d).

(d) When the lawyer has received disqualifying information as defined in paragraph (c), representation is permissible if:
(1) both the affected client and the prospective client have given informed consent, confirmed in writing; or
(2) the lawyer who received the information took reasonable measures to avoid exposure to more disqualifying information than was reasonably necessary to determine whether to represent the prospective client; and
(i) the disqualified lawyer is timely screened from any participation in the matter and is apportioned no part of the fee therefrom; and
(ii) written notice is promptly given to the prospective client. Kan. Sup. Ct. R. 226, KRPC 1.18.

Missouri Rule 4-1.18

Missouri Rule 4-1.18 tracks ABA Model Rule 1.18 and adopts the same four-paragraph architecture: definition of prospective client, the information-use restriction, the conflict rule with the "significantly harmful" threshold, and the two safe-harbor pathways.

1.18(a) - Who is a prospective client

A prospective client is a person who consults with a lawyer about the possibility of forming a client-lawyer relationship with respect to a matter. The definition contemplates a deliberate, bidirectional inquiry: the prospective client is exploring engagement, and the lawyer is providing information that bears on whether engagement is feasible. Unsolicited communications from members of the public who broadcast their situation without consultation - for example, mass emails sent to multiple lawyers without invitation - generally do not create a prospective-client relationship.

The Comment to Model Rule 1.18 notes that a person who provides information to a lawyer in response to advertising that merely describes the lawyer's services may be a prospective client; a person who provides information unilaterally to a lawyer who has not invited it generally is not.

1.18(b) - The information-use restriction

Even when no client-lawyer relationship ensues, the lawyer who learned information from a prospective client shall not use or reveal that information except as Rule 1.9 would permit with respect to former-client information. This is the structural protection that lets the prospective client speak candidly during the consultation. The information may be used or revealed only in the limited ways Rule 1.9 permits for former-client information - principally when the information has become generally known.

1.18(c) - The conflict and the "significantly harmful" threshold

Subdivision (c) supplies a narrower disqualification standard than Rule 1.9. A lawyer who received information from a prospective client may not represent a client with interests materially adverse to the prospective client in the same or a substantially related matter if the information could be significantly harmful to the prospective client in the matter.

Three elements all must be present for disqualification under 1.18(c): (1) material adversity between the prospective client's interests and the proposed new client's interests, (2) substantial relationship between the matter discussed and the proposed new matter, and (3) information received that could be significantly harmful in the new matter.

The "significantly harmful" threshold matters. Information that is publicly available, or that is general background about a matter, or that does not bear on the strategy or vulnerabilities of the prospective client in the specific matter, generally is not "significantly harmful." The rule does not categorically foreclose representation based on any contact; it foreclosures based on contact that conveyed strategically meaningful information.

1.18(d) - The two safe-harbor pathways for continued representation

When 1.18(c) would otherwise disqualify, subdivision (d) supplies two affirmative pathways by which the firm can continue to take the adverse matter.

Pathway 1: Informed consent in writing from both

Both the affected client and the prospective client give informed consent, confirmed in writing. This is the bilateral-consent pathway. Where the prospective client retains the disqualifying-information protection but is willing to release it - perhaps because the matter the firm wishes to take is sufficiently distant from the prospective consultation - the consent pathway opens the matter.

Pathway 2: Limited information + screening + notice

This is the operational pathway most firms rely on. It has three integrated components:

Limited-information measure

The lawyer who received the information took reasonable measures to avoid exposure to more disqualifying information than was reasonably necessary to determine whether to represent the prospective client. The lawyer's intake protocol should ask enough to evaluate the engagement and stop before extracting strategic detail.

Timely screen + no fee

The lawyer who received the information is timely screened from any participation in the matter and apportioned no part of the fee. The screen elements mirror the lateral-screening elements under Rule 1.10.

Prompt written notice

Written notice is promptly given to the prospective client. The notice identifies the screened lawyer, the screen's existence, and the matter the firm is undertaking.

Documentation

The screen is documented in writing, the limited-information measures are recorded in the intake file, and the notice to the prospective client is preserved.

The shape of an effective intake protocol

A 1.18-aware intake protocol typically includes:

  • A scripted opening that identifies what information the lawyer needs to determine whether to take the matter
  • Conflict-check questions asked before substantive consultation begins
  • A signal to stop the inquiry when conflict-check returns a hit until the conflict is resolved
  • A written consultation memorandum that records what was discussed in summary terms, kept separately from substantive engagement files
  • A "thanks but no" letter for prospective clients the firm declines, confirming that the consultation did not become an engagement

Common procedural posture

Initial consultation that does not become an engagement

A prospective client meets with a lawyer to discuss a possible commercial dispute. The firm declines the engagement (for any reason - fee, scope, capacity). Later, the prospective client's adversary asks the firm to represent it in the same matter. The firm runs a 1.18 analysis: was significantly harmful information conveyed in the consultation? If yes, the firm must satisfy one of the two 1.18(d) pathways before taking the adverse matter. If no, the firm may take the matter without further analysis.

Consultation that surfaces a conflict before substantive discussion

The intake intake protocol catches a conflict in the first five minutes of the consultation. The lawyer pauses the substantive discussion, explains the conflict, and offers to refer the prospective client to other counsel. Because limited-information measures were observed, the firm preserves the option to continue representing the existing client; if the prospective client subsequently becomes an adversary's client, the screening + notice pathway under 1.18(d)(2) is available.

Relationship to other conflict rules

  • Rule 1.7 - Current-client conflicts; analytically distinct because 1.18 applies before an engagement exists
  • Rule 1.9 - Former-client conflicts; the rule whose treatment of "information" 1.18(b) incorporates
  • Rule 1.10 - Imputation; 1.18(c) explicitly extends imputation to firm members, but 1.18(d) provides the screening exception that 1.10 does not always provide for current/former conflicts

Continuing nature of the duty

The information-use restriction under 1.18(b) continues indefinitely - the prospective client whose consultation did not become an engagement nevertheless retains the protection of the information shared in the consultation. The duty to evaluate adverse matters under 1.18(c) attaches whenever the firm contemplates new work that touches the matter discussed in the prior consultation.

Practical impact

Rule 1.18 is the rule firms operationalize through intake protocols. Three takeaways recur:

  • Limited-information intake is the engine. The 1.18(d)(2) safe harbor is available only when the intake protocol limits the information received to what was reasonably necessary.
  • Documentation preserves the safe harbor. The screening, the fee apportionment, the written notice, and the consultation memo together demonstrate compliance.
  • Consent reopens the matter. Where the screening safe harbor is unavailable or insufficient, the 1.18(d)(1) bilateral-consent pathway remains.
