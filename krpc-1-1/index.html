KRPC 1.1 / MRPC 4-1.1: Competence

Legal knowledge, skill, thoroughness, and preparation reasonably necessary for the representation.

Verbatim text of KRPC 1.1 (Kansas)

A lawyer shall provide competent representation to a client. Competent representation requires the legal knowledge, skill, thoroughness and preparation reasonably necessary for the representation. Kan. Sup. Ct. R. 226, KRPC 1.1.

Missouri Rule 4-1.1

Missouri Rule 4-1.1 is substantively identical to KRPC 1.1 and tracks ABA Model Rule 1.1. Both states adopt the four-element formulation: knowledge, skill, thoroughness, and preparation. The Comment to Model Rule 1.1, which Kansas and Missouri have largely adopted, fills in the operative content of each element.

The four elements of competence

Legal knowledge

The lawyer must know the law applicable to the representation. The Comment recognizes that a lawyer need not have special training or prior experience to handle a matter; competence may be acquired through study or association with a lawyer of established competence. But the lawyer must actually have, or actually acquire, the knowledge before undertaking the action. A lawyer who files pleadings in a substantive area without the required underlying knowledge satisfies neither the knowledge nor the preparation requirement.

Skill

Skill is the practical ability to apply knowledge to client matters. The relevant skills vary by practice area. In transactional work, skill includes the ability to draft, negotiate, and structure agreements. In litigation, skill includes pleading, motion practice, discovery, examination of witnesses, and argument. A lawyer holding out for matters that require litigation skill must actually have, or acquire, that skill.

Thoroughness

Thoroughness is the discipline of complete attention to the matter. It encompasses identifying all relevant facts, all applicable law, and all reasonable courses of action. A lawyer who treats a matter as routine when the facts demonstrate non-routine elements has not provided thorough representation.

Preparation

Preparation is the work performed in advance of the lawyer's action - the legal research, factual investigation, document review, and procedural compliance that must precede competent execution. The duty of preparation scales with the complexity and stakes of the matter; it is calibrated to "reasonably necessary," not to any fixed standard.

Comment 8 - Maintaining competence (technological competence)

Comment 8 to Model Rule 1.1, adopted in substance by Kansas and Missouri, states that to maintain the requisite knowledge and skill a lawyer should keep abreast of changes in the law and its practice, including the benefits and risks associated with relevant technology. This is the source of the now-widely-recognized "duty of technological competence." A lawyer practicing in a field where electronic case management, electronic filing, electronic service, or electronic discovery is standard must be reasonably competent in the relevant technology.

Duty of technological competence. A majority of jurisdictions now expressly recognize a duty of technological competence flowing from Comment 8 to Rule 1.1. A lawyer who does not understand the relevant electronic systems - including electronic filing, electronic service, electronic discovery, and increasingly the use and limits of artificial intelligence tools - cannot rely on technological unfamiliarity to defeat competence claims.

Calibration to the matter

The rule does not require that every lawyer be competent in every field. It requires competence in the matters the lawyer undertakes. A lawyer with general practice experience may undertake a specialized matter, but must achieve competence before action - whether by study, by association with a competent lawyer, or by declining the matter. A lawyer who undertakes a matter beyond the lawyer's competence, without taking the steps competence requires, violates the rule regardless of the outcome.

Emergency exception

The Comment recognizes a narrow exception for emergencies. In situations in which referral or consultation is impractical and immediate action is required, a lawyer may give advice or assistance in a matter in which the lawyer does not have the skill ordinarily required where doing so is in the client's interest. The emergency exception does not extend beyond the emergency and does not justify continued representation in a matter beyond the lawyer's competence.

Common procedural posture

Substantive area outside ordinary practice

A lawyer is asked to handle a matter in a substantive area outside the lawyer's ordinary practice. The lawyer must either (a) acquire the necessary knowledge through study or consultation before undertaking the matter, (b) associate with a lawyer of established competence in the area, or (c) decline the representation. Taking the matter without doing one of these things is a competence violation independent of outcome.

Insufficient preparation for hearing or filing

A lawyer attends a hearing or files a paper without the preparation reasonably necessary for the matter - inadequate factual investigation, incomplete legal research, or failure to identify applicable procedural requirements. This is a thoroughness-and-preparation violation under Rule 1.1, often paired with diligence concerns under Rule 1.3 and candor concerns under Rule 3.3 where the filing contains factual or legal errors.

Relationship to other rules

Rule 1.1 sets the floor for the substantive quality of representation. Other rules build on it:

  • Rule 1.3 (Diligence) - Even competent representation can fall short if it is not pursued with reasonable diligence; the two rules are typically analyzed together.
  • Rule 1.4 (Communication) - Competent representation includes communicating with the client at the level required by Rule 1.4.
  • Rule 1.5 (Fees) - The reasonableness of fees under Rule 1.5 is partly a function of whether the work performed was competent.
  • Rule 3.1 (Meritorious Claims) - A lawyer who has not done the work to determine whether a claim has a basis in law and fact has not provided competent representation and is also at risk under Rule 3.1.

Practical impact

Competence is the foundational professional duty. Two consequences recur in disciplinary practice:

  • Unfamiliarity is not a defense. A lawyer who took on a matter without the requisite competence is responsible for the consequences. The rule does not protect lawyers who hold out for work beyond their actual competence.
  • Competence is matter-specific. The fact that a lawyer is competent in some matters does not establish competence in any particular new matter. Each undertaking requires the knowledge, skill, thoroughness, and preparation reasonably necessary for it.
