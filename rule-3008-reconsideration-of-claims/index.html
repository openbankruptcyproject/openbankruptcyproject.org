Federal Rule of Bankruptcy Procedure 3008

Rule 3008: Reconsideration of Allowed and Disallowed Claims

The for-cause reconsideration standard, the equitable-factors framework, and interplay with Rule 9024 / FRCP 60 for time-bar relief.

Verbatim text of Rule 3008

A party in interest may move for reconsideration of an order allowing or disallowing a claim against the estate. The court after a hearing on notice shall enter an appropriate order. Fed. R. Bankr. P. 3008.

The two-sentence rule and what it does not say

Rule 3008 is one of the shortest rules in the Federal Rules of Bankruptcy Procedure, and what the rule does not say is more important than what it does. The rule does not specify a deadline. It does not specify a standard. It does not specify who counts as a "party in interest" for reconsideration purposes. It does not address whether reconsideration is available after the case is closed, after a plan is confirmed, or after distribution has begun.

The case law has filled each of these gaps. The standard - drawn from the Advisory Committee Note to the 1983 rule and from the parallel statute, 11 U.S.C. Section 502(j) - is "cause." Reconsideration is committed to the discretion of the bankruptcy court, exercised in light of equitable considerations. The exact contours of "cause" depend on whether the reconsideration motion is filed within the time fixed for an appeal (in which case the standard is essentially that of FRCP 59) or after that time has expired (in which case the standard is essentially that of FRCP 60, imported through Rule 9024).

The Section 502(j) statutory hook

Section 502(j) provides that "a claim that has been allowed or disallowed may be reconsidered for cause." The statute and Rule 3008 are coextensive; together they create a regime that distinguishes claim-allowance orders from other orders in the case. Most orders are final when entered and subject to amendment only under the limited grounds of FRCP 59 and 60 (incorporated through Rule 9023 and Rule 9024). Claim-allowance orders are different: they remain reconsiderable "for cause" without the temporal and substantive constraints of the civil-procedure rules.

The structural justification for the distinction is the ongoing nature of the bankruptcy claim-administration process. A claim allowance entered early in a case may turn out to be erroneous as later evidence emerges; new information may surface during the section 341 meeting, during plan negotiations, or during distribution. The reconsideration regime exists to allow the claim allowance to track the actual entitlement rather than to lock in an early error.

The "for cause" standard

"Cause" under Rule 3008 has been articulated through a multi-factor analysis in most circuits. Typical factors include:

  • Whether new evidence has come to light that was not reasonably available at the time of the original allowance
  • Whether the original allowance was based on a mistake of fact or law
  • Whether the movant exercised diligence in seeking reconsideration after the basis for reconsideration became known
  • The extent to which other parties or the estate would be prejudiced by reconsideration
  • Whether distributions have already been made on the basis of the original allowance
  • The amount in controversy relative to the cost of further litigation

The factors are weighed equitably; no single factor is dispositive. The closer the motion is filed to the original allowance, and the less reliance has accrued on that allowance, the more readily reconsideration is granted. The further the motion is from the original allowance, and the more substantial the reliance, the more demanding the showing.

Interaction with Rule 9024 and FRCP 60

Rule 9024 imports FRCP 60 into bankruptcy proceedings, but with a critical exception: "a motion to reopen a case under the Code or for the reconsideration of an order allowing or disallowing a claim against the estate entered without a contest is not subject to the one year limitation prescribed in Rule 60(c)." The carve-out is structural: Rule 3008 reconsideration is not subject to the one-year deadline that ordinarily limits Rule 60(b) motions based on mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or newly discovered evidence.

The interaction matters most where the reconsideration motion is filed after the appeal period has expired. Within the appeal period (14 days under Rule 8002), the motion functions essentially as a motion to alter or amend under FRCP 59, suspending the appeal clock under Rule 8002(b). After the appeal period, the motion must satisfy the Rule 3008 "cause" standard - and where the original allowance was contested, must also satisfy the substantive grounds of FRCP 60(b) (subject to the equitable considerations the case law overlays on the rule).

The Supreme Court's decision in Pioneer Investment Services Co. v. Brunswick Associates Limited Partnership, 507 U.S. 380 (1993), supplies the equitable framework that bankruptcy courts apply to "excusable neglect" determinations under Rule 9006(b) and, by extension, to the equitable analysis under Rule 3008. The Pioneer factors - the danger of prejudice, the length of the delay and its potential impact on judicial proceedings, the reason for the delay (including whether it was within the reasonable control of the movant), and whether the movant acted in good faith - are the standard analytic framework for late-filed reconsideration motions.

Contested versus uncontested original allowance

The distinction between contested and uncontested original allowances drives much of the practical operation of Rule 3008. An allowance entered by default - because no objection was filed within the time fixed under Rule 3007 - is reviewed under a more lenient standard on reconsideration than an allowance entered after a contested hearing.

The justification is the absence of a prior adjudication. An uncontested allowance reflects the default rule of Section 502(a) (claims are deemed allowed unless objected to); it does not reflect a judicial determination of the merits. Reconsideration of an uncontested allowance is therefore less disturbing to the integrity of prior adjudication and to the reliance interests of the parties.

A contested allowance, by contrast, reflects an actual judicial determination. The reconsidering court is being asked to undo not a default but a decision. The showing required is correspondingly more demanding. The case law characterizes this distinction as one between "reconsideration" properly so-called (of contested allowances, requiring something like the Rule 60(b) standard) and "review" (of uncontested allowances, requiring only the Rule 3008 "cause" standard).

Reopening and post-confirmation reconsideration

Rule 3008 reconsideration is not limited to the pendency of the case. Where new information surfaces after the case has been closed, the path is typically (a) a motion to reopen under 11 U.S.C. Section 350(b) and Rule 5010, followed by (b) a Rule 3008 motion in the reopened case. The reopening motion must satisfy its own "cause" standard - typically requiring a showing that reconsideration would affect distributions or alter the rights of parties in a way that justifies the administrative cost of reopening.

Post-confirmation reconsideration in Chapter 11, 12, and 13 is procedurally more difficult. Confirmation orders are subject to the finality protection of Bullard v. Blue Hills Bank, 575 U.S. 496 (2015), which holds that a confirmation order is a final, appealable order; the reciprocal proposition is that confirmation triggers reliance interests that weigh heavily against later disturbance. Where a claim allowance is bound up with confirmation - because the allowance was a predicate to the plan's treatment of the claim - reconsideration may face the additional hurdle of demonstrating that the requested change does not collide with the binding effect of the confirmed plan.

Common procedural posture

Default allowance, newly discovered defense

A claim is deemed allowed because no objection was filed within the Rule 3007 window. The debtor or trustee subsequently discovers that the claim is barred by a statute of limitations or an exemption. A Rule 3008 motion is filed; because the allowance was uncontested, the standard is the more lenient "cause" standard. The court grants reconsideration if the equitable factors favor disturbing the allowance.

Contested allowance, intervening authority

A contested claim is allowed after a hearing. The appeal period expires without an appeal. An intervening decision of the controlling circuit overturns the rule of law on which the allowance was based. A party in interest files a Rule 3008 motion; because the allowance was contested, the court applies the equivalent of Rule 60(b)(6) - exceptional circumstances - subject to the Rule 3008 equitable analysis.

Practical impact

Rule 3008 is the procedural escape hatch that prevents claim-allowance error from becoming permanent simply because the appeal clock has expired. The rule's brevity is deceptive: the entire weight of doctrine - the contested/uncontested distinction, the equitable-factors framework, the Pioneer analysis, the Rule 9024 / FRCP 60 interaction, the Rule 5010 reopening interplay - sits on two sentences. Practitioners moving for or against reconsideration should expect that the court will not apply the rule mechanically; the court will apply equitable judgment to the totality of circumstances.

Reconsideration is discretionary, not corrective. Rule 3008 does not give the court a mandate to correct every claim-allowance error; it gives the court discretion to do so when equity supports it. A movant who treats Rule 3008 as a routine appeal-substitute will find the discretion exercised against the motion. A movant who frames the motion around concrete equitable factors - reliance, prejudice, diligence, new evidence - will find the discretion more receptive.
