28 U.S.C. Section 158(a): District Court Bankruptcy Appeals

District court appellate jurisdiction over bankruptcy court orders, final-versus-interlocutory analysis, leave to appeal, and the briefing rules of Part VIII of the Federal Rules of Bankruptcy Procedure.

Verbatim text of 28 U.S.C. Section 158(a)

The district courts of the United States shall have jurisdiction to hear appeals (1) from final judgments, orders, and decrees; (2) from interlocutory orders and decrees issued under section 1121(d) of title 11 increasing or reducing the time periods referred to in section 1121 of such title; and (3) with leave of the court, from other interlocutory orders and decrees; and, with leave of the court, from interlocutory orders and decrees of bankruptcy judges entered in cases and proceedings referred to the bankruptcy judges under section 157 of this title. 28 U.S.C. Section 158(a).

The three appellate categories under Section 158(a)

The statute distributes appellate jurisdiction across three numbered subsections, each with its own procedural footprint. The first is the workhorse; the second is a narrow specialty; the third is the gateway for everything else.

(1) Final judgments, orders, and decrees

Section 158(a)(1) confers appeal as of right from final orders of the bankruptcy court. Finality in bankruptcy is construed more flexibly than under 28 U.S.C. Section 1291 because bankruptcy cases are aggregations of discrete proceedings rather than single unitary actions. The Supreme Court confirmed in Bullard v. Blue Hills Bank, 575 U.S. 496 (2015), that finality in bankruptcy attaches at the level of the discrete proceeding, not the case as a whole - but only when the order definitively resolves a discrete dispute. An order denying confirmation of a Chapter 13 plan, without dismissing the case, is not final because the debtor may propose another plan.

(2) Section 1121(d) extensions and reductions

Section 158(a)(2) is a narrow grant covering interlocutory orders that extend or reduce the exclusivity periods under 11 U.S.C. Section 1121 for a Chapter 11 debtor to propose and obtain acceptance of a plan. These orders are appealable as of right despite their interlocutory character because they materially affect the leverage balance in plan negotiations.

(3) Discretionary interlocutory appeals

Section 158(a)(3) is the catch-all for interlocutory orders not falling within Section 158(a)(2). The district court has discretion to grant or deny leave to appeal. Most district courts apply the standards drawn by analogy from 28 U.S.C. Section 1292(b): the order must (a) involve a controlling question of law, (b) as to which there is substantial ground for difference of opinion, and (c) the immediate appeal must materially advance the ultimate termination of the litigation. The procedure is governed by Fed. R. Bankr. P. 8004.

The 14-day notice of appeal deadline

A notice of appeal under Fed. R. Bankr. P. 8002(a) must be filed with the bankruptcy clerk within 14 days after entry of the order being appealed. The deadline is jurisdictional. The 14-day period may be tolled by a timely motion under Rule 8002(b) - including motions to amend findings under Rule 7052, motions to alter or amend a judgment under Rule 9023, or motions for new trial under Rule 9023.

Rule 8002(d) authorizes the bankruptcy court to extend the time for appeal by up to 21 additional days for excusable neglect upon a motion filed before the original 14-day period expires, or within 21 days thereafter on a showing of excusable neglect. The interaction of these timing provisions is unforgiving; an appeal filed on day 15 without a timely tolling motion is dismissed for lack of jurisdiction.

Briefing under FRBP Part VIII

Part VIII of the Federal Rules of Bankruptcy Procedure - Rules 8001 through 8028 - was substantially rewritten in 2014 to align with the Federal Rules of Appellate Procedure. The principal pieces of the briefing schedule:

  • Rule 8009 - Designation of the record on appeal and statement of issues, due within 14 days after the notice of appeal
  • Rule 8010 - Completion and transmission of the record
  • Rule 8014 - Content of the briefs (parallels FRAP Rule 28)
  • Rule 8015 - Form and length of the briefs (13,000-word limit for the principal brief)
  • Rule 8018 - Briefing schedule: appellant's brief within 30 days of docketing in the district court, appellee's brief within 30 days of service of appellant's brief, reply within 14 days of service of appellee's brief

Standards of review

The district court sits in an appellate posture over the bankruptcy court and applies the same standards a circuit court applies to a district court. The standards are layered by issue type:

Findings of fact

Clearly erroneous review under Fed. R. Bankr. P. 8013. A finding is clearly erroneous when, although there is evidence to support it, the reviewing court on the entire evidence is left with the definite and firm conviction that a mistake has been committed. Credibility determinations by the bankruptcy judge receive particular deference.

Conclusions of law

De novo review. The district court applies its independent judgment to questions of statutory interpretation, contract construction, and the application of legal standards to the historical facts.

Mixed questions of law and fact

Reviewed under a sliding scale: if the historical facts dominate the inquiry, the determination is reviewed for clear error; if the analysis is dominated by the legal standard, review is de novo. See U.S. Bank N.A. v. Village at Lakeridge, LLC, 583 U.S. 387 (2018).

Discretionary rulings

Abuse of discretion review for evidentiary rulings, case-management orders, fee awards under 11 U.S.C. Section 330, and equitable subordination determinations. The reviewing court reverses only where the bankruptcy court applied an incorrect legal standard, made clearly erroneous factual findings, or rendered a decision that no reasonable court would have reached.

Final-versus-interlocutory analysis

The line between final and interlocutory orders is the most-litigated threshold issue in bankruptcy appellate practice. The general rule is that an order is final if it definitively resolves a discrete proceeding within the larger bankruptcy case. Examples drawn from the case law:

Final (appeal as of right)

  • Orders confirming a Chapter 11 or Chapter 13 plan
  • Orders granting or denying discharge under 11 U.S.C. Section 727 or Section 1328
  • Orders allowing or disallowing a proof of claim
  • Orders granting relief from the automatic stay under 11 U.S.C. Section 362(d)
  • Orders approving a sale under 11 U.S.C. Section 363(b)
  • Orders dismissing or converting a case under Section 1112 or Section 1307
  • Orders disposing of a discrete adversary proceeding

Interlocutory (leave required under 158(a)(3))

  • Orders denying confirmation of a plan without dismissal (per Bullard)
  • Orders denying motions for summary judgment in an adversary proceeding
  • Discovery orders
  • Orders denying motions to dismiss for lack of jurisdiction or for failure to state a claim
  • Most case-administrative orders that do not definitively resolve a contested matter

Stays pending appeal

An appeal does not automatically stay the bankruptcy court's order. A party seeking to suspend the operation of a sale order, a confirmation order, or any other appealable order must move for a stay pending appeal under Fed. R. Bankr. P. 8007. Rule 8007(a) requires the motion to be made first in the bankruptcy court ordinarily; only if that motion is denied, or if seeking relief in the bankruptcy court is impracticable, may the motion be made directly to the district court. The factors are the traditional four: likelihood of success on the merits, irreparable harm, balance of equities, and public interest.

Sale order finality - the equitable-mootness trap. A sale order under 11 U.S.C. Section 363(b) becomes substantially unreviewable once consummated with a good-faith purchaser, by force of Section 363(m). A party objecting to a sale must obtain a stay pending appeal under Rule 8007 to preserve appellate review. Filing the notice of appeal without a stay is insufficient.

Direct appeal to the court of appeals - Section 158(d)(2)

The 2005 BAPCPA amendments added 28 U.S.C. Section 158(d)(2), which permits the parties to bypass the district court and seek certification of a direct appeal to the court of appeals on three alternative grounds: (i) the appeal involves a question of law as to which there is no controlling decision of the court of appeals or the Supreme Court, or involves a matter of public importance; (ii) the appeal involves a question of law requiring resolution of conflicting decisions; or (iii) an immediate appeal may materially advance the progress of the case or proceeding. Certification is made by the bankruptcy court, the district court, or all the parties; the court of appeals retains discretion to accept or deny the direct appeal. Procedure is governed by Fed. R. Bankr. P. 8006.

Election between district court and the BAP

In circuits that have established a Bankruptcy Appellate Panel under 28 U.S.C. Section 158(b), the appellant must elect between district court review and BAP review under Section 158(c)(1). The election is governed by Fed. R. Bankr. P. 8005; absent a timely election, the appeal proceeds to the BAP by default.
