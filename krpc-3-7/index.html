KRPC 3.7 / MRPC 4-3.7: Lawyer as Witness

The advocate-witness exceptions - uncontested matter, value of legal services rendered, and substantial hardship - and the firm-imputation analysis under 3.7(b).

Advocate-witness exceptions that 3.7 permits

Rule 3.7 governs the relatively narrow situation in which a lawyer is likely to be a necessary witness at a trial in which the lawyer also serves as advocate. The rule is best read as a description of when the dual role is affirmatively permitted. Three express exceptions allow the same lawyer to continue as trial advocate, and a separate firm-imputation provision in subdivision (b) preserves the firm's ability to serve as advocate even when one of its lawyers will testify.

The combined structure preserves the client's freedom to retain (and keep) chosen counsel in a wide range of cases. The default rule that an advocate-witness should not try the case is more limited than is commonly understood; the three exceptions and the firm-imputation framework reach a substantial share of the cases in which the question arises.

Verbatim text of KRPC 3.7 (Kansas)

(a) A lawyer shall not act as advocate at a trial in which the lawyer is likely to be a necessary witness unless:
(1) the testimony relates to an uncontested issue;
(2) the testimony relates to the nature and value of legal services rendered in the case; or
(3) disqualification of the lawyer would work substantial hardship on the client.

(b) A lawyer may act as advocate in a trial in which another lawyer in the lawyer's firm is likely to be called as a witness unless precluded from doing so by Rule 1.7 or Rule 1.9. Kan. Sup. Ct. R. 226, KRPC 3.7.

Missouri Rule 4-3.7

Missouri Rule 4-3.7 is substantively identical to KRPC 3.7 and tracks ABA Model Rule 3.7. The structure is the same: subdivision (a)'s three exceptions, and subdivision (b)'s firm-imputation rule keyed only to the conflict-of-interest provisions.

The "necessary witness" threshold

Subdivision (a) applies only when the lawyer is "likely to be a necessary witness." Both elements matter:

  • Likely. A theoretical possibility that the lawyer might be called is not enough. The lawyer's testimony must be reasonably likely.
  • Necessary. The testimony must be necessary - meaning it cannot reasonably be obtained from another source. If another witness can provide the same testimony, the lawyer is not a necessary witness.

The "necessary witness" threshold often resolves the question without reaching the three exceptions. If documents, third-party witnesses, or other lawyers can establish the same point, the lawyer is not a necessary witness and the rule does not bar continued advocacy.

Exception 1: Uncontested issue

Subdivision (a)(1) permits the advocate-witness role when the testimony relates to an uncontested issue. Uncontested testimony - foundational background, authentication of routine documents, undisputed events on the procedural record - does not raise the concerns that motivate the general rule, because the jury is not asked to weigh the lawyer's credibility against contrary testimony. The exception is broad enough to cover most procedural and foundational testimony lawyers commonly give.

Exception 2: Value of legal services

Subdivision (a)(2) preserves the lawyer's ability to testify on the nature and value of legal services rendered in the case. This exception is especially important in fee-shifting contexts (attorney-fee awards under statute, fee applications in bankruptcy, sanction proceedings, contract-based fee provisions), where the lawyer is uniquely positioned to describe the work performed and its reasonable value. The Comment to Model Rule 3.7 specifically identifies this exception as a recognition that the lawyer is often the most qualified witness on the lawyer's own work.

Exception 3: Substantial hardship on the client

Subdivision (a)(3) permits the dual role when disqualification of the lawyer would work substantial hardship on the client. The Comment identifies factors relevant to substantial hardship:

  • The lawyer's distinctive familiarity with the case (developed expertise that another lawyer could not readily replicate)
  • The cost of bringing replacement counsel up to speed
  • The complexity of the matter and the stage at which the witness issue is identified
  • The availability of qualified replacement counsel and the time available to retain them
  • The client's choice and the disruption to the attorney-client relationship

Hardship is evaluated from the client's perspective, with weight given to the client's autonomy in selecting counsel. The exception is not narrow - it covers a substantial set of cases where late-emerging witness issues, distinctive lawyer expertise, or client choice would make disqualification disproportionate.

Affirmative read-out. Rule 3.7 permits the advocate-witness role in three substantial categories: (1) testimony on uncontested issues; (2) testimony on the value of the lawyer's own services; and (3) cases where disqualification would substantially harm the client. Combined with the "necessary witness" threshold, the rule reserves disqualification for the narrow class of cases in which a contested factual issue genuinely requires the lawyer's own testimony with no substantial hardship to the client.

Firm-imputation analysis under 3.7(b)

Subdivision (b) is the most important affirmative provision of the rule for firm practice. It provides that a lawyer may act as advocate in a trial in which another lawyer in the same firm is likely to be called as a witness, unless precluded by Rule 1.7 (concurrent conflicts) or Rule 1.9 (former-client conflicts). The default position is permission, not disqualification.

This is a deliberate narrowing of the older "imputation" rule that would have disqualified the entire firm. The current rule reflects the conclusion that the concerns driving 3.7(a) - jury confusion about whether the lawyer is testifying or arguing, conflict between the advocate's role and the witness's oath, possible adverse effect on the firm's representation - are substantially mitigated when different lawyers fill the two roles. The firm-imputation framework asks only the conflict-of-interest question (does the witness lawyer's testimony create a 1.7 or 1.9 conflict?), not the advocate-witness question.

Common procedural posture

Fee application after representation

Counsel files a fee application in a bankruptcy proceeding and testifies at a fee hearing about the nature and value of services rendered. The testimony falls squarely within subdivision (a)(2). The lawyer may continue as advocate without restriction.

Late-emerging witness issue in complex matter

Three weeks before trial in a complex commercial case, a fact emerges making the lead lawyer a potential witness on a contested issue. Counsel has been intimately involved in two years of case development; retaining and educating replacement trial counsel would cost the client substantial time and expense. Subdivision (a)(3)'s substantial-hardship exception is available.

The role of the tribunal

The Comment to Model Rule 3.7 notes that the tribunal has a strong interest in fair proceedings and may, in appropriate circumstances, raise the advocate-witness question on its own. The rule does not require the lawyer to wait for a motion to disqualify; counsel may evaluate the question proactively at the time the witness issue is identified, and may seek tribunal guidance where appropriate. The court's evaluation generally tracks the same factors the rule identifies.

Pretrial vs. trial advocacy

The rule's prohibition is keyed to acting as advocate "at a trial." Pretrial advocacy - discovery, motion practice, settlement, depositions - is not within the rule's scope, even when the lawyer is likely to be a necessary witness at trial. This is a substantial preservation of the lawyer's role: counsel who will need to step aside for trial may still conduct the case through every pretrial phase.

Relationship to other rules

  • Rule 1.7 - Conflict of interest with current clients; the gating question for firm-imputation under 3.7(b)
  • Rule 1.9 - Conflict of interest with former clients; the other gating question for 3.7(b)
  • Rule 3.4(e) - Trial reference rule; restricts personal-opinion vouching outside testimony
  • Rule 3.3 - Candor toward the tribunal; applies to the lawyer's testimony as it does to any witness

Practical impact

Rule 3.7 should be read with attention to the breadth of its affirmative content. The combined effect of the "necessary witness" threshold, the three exceptions, the firm-imputation rule, and the pretrial-only scope is that disqualification is the outcome only in a narrow class of cases: trials in which the lawyer is likely to be a necessary witness on a contested issue, where another firm lawyer could not serve as advocate (because of a 1.7 or 1.9 conflict), where the testimony is not within the value-of-services exception, and where substantial hardship to the client cannot be shown. That intersection is small.
