11 U.S.C. Section 510 - Subordination

How and why a claim can be moved down the priority ladder: contractual subordination agreements, the mandatory subordination of securities-rescission claims, equitable subordination under the Mobile Steel three-prong test, and the doctrinal cousin of recharacterizing debt as equity.

What Section 510 Does

Section 510 governs the subordination of claims and interests in bankruptcy. Subordination moves a claim down the distribution waterfall: instead of sharing pro rata with claims of the same nominal priority, a subordinated claim is paid only after the senior claims to which it is subordinated have been paid in full. The provision recognizes three distinct subordination doctrines: contractual (510(a)), mandatory by statute (510(b)), and equitable (510(c)).

Official citation: 11 U.S.C. § 510

Section 510(a): Contractual Subordination

Section 510(a) provides that a subordination agreement is "enforceable in a case under this title to the same extent that such agreement is enforceable under applicable nonbankruptcy law." The provision is a one-line statutory codification of the common-law principle that parties may contract for relative priority among themselves and that the bankruptcy court will honor those contracts.

The most common application is in syndicated and tiered debt structures. A senior credit facility will require junior debt to execute an intercreditor agreement under which the junior lender agrees that, until the senior debt is paid in full, it will turn over to the senior lender any distribution it receives on account of its junior claim. In bankruptcy, Section 510(a) tells the court to enforce that turnover obligation according to its terms.

Contractual subordination is generally enforced even when it produces outcomes that look harsh, because Section 510(a) does not give the bankruptcy court authority to rewrite the agreement on equity grounds. The bankruptcy court can refuse to enforce a subordination agreement only on the same grounds that would defeat it outside bankruptcy: lack of consideration, fraud in the inducement, unconscionability under applicable state contract law, and so on.

X-clause litigation: intercreditor agreements often contain "X-clauses" that carve out certain payments (most commonly distributions of reorganized-debtor equity in a plan of reorganization) from the turnover obligation. Whether a specific plan distribution falls inside or outside the X-clause has become a recurrent confirmation-stage fight in large Chapter 11 cases.

Section 510(b): Mandatory Subordination of Securities-Rescission Claims

Section 510(b) is a statutorily mandated subordination rule that applies regardless of contract. It provides that "a claim arising from rescission of a purchase or sale of a security of the debtor or of an affiliate of the debtor, for damages arising from the purchase or sale of such a security, or for reimbursement or contribution allowed under section 502 on account of such a claim, shall be subordinated to all claims or interests that are senior to or equal the claim or interest represented by such security."

The provision implements the policy judgment that investors who bought equity (or debt that has been recharacterized as equity) accepted the risk that the company would fail; allowing them to recover ahead of trade creditors on a fraud-on-the-market or rescission theory would shift that investment risk onto the trade creditors who never priced for it. The Third Circuit's In re Telegroup, Inc., 281 F.3d 133 (3d Cir. 2002), and the Ninth Circuit's In re Betacom of Phoenix, Inc., 240 F.3d 823 (9th Cir. 2001), are leading constructions of the provision's scope.

Section 510(b) is broad. A federal securities fraud claim against an issuer based on misrepresentations in a registration statement is subordinated. A contract claim for breach of a stock purchase agreement is subordinated. A claim for contribution from a third party whose primary liability arose from a securities-fraud claim is subordinated. The investor's claim is treated, for distribution purposes, as if it occupied the same level as the security it relates to: equity subordinated to all debt, debt securities subordinated to claims senior to or equal to that debt class.

Section 510(c): Equitable Subordination

Section 510(c) codifies the bankruptcy court's equitable subordination power. It provides that the court may "under principles of equitable subordination, subordinate for purposes of distribution all or part of an allowed claim to all or part of another allowed claim" or "order that any lien securing such a subordinated claim be transferred to the estate."

The provision codifies an equitable doctrine developed by the federal courts under prior bankruptcy regimes. The seminal articulation comes from the Fifth Circuit's In re Mobile Steel Co., 563 F.2d 692 (5th Cir. 1977), which established the three-prong test still applied across the circuits.

The Mobile Steel Three-Prong Test

  1. The claimant must have engaged in some type of inequitable conduct;
  2. The misconduct must have resulted in injury to the creditors of the bankrupt or conferred an unfair advantage on the claimant; and
  3. Equitable subordination of the claim must not be inconsistent with the provisions of the Bankruptcy Code.

The Supreme Court endorsed the Mobile Steel framework in United States v. Noland, 517 U.S. 535 (1996), and clarified that equitable subordination requires individualized misconduct, not category-wide subordination of an entire class of claims. The Court reaffirmed in United States v. Reorganized CF&I Fabricators, 518 U.S. 213 (1996), that a bankruptcy court cannot use Section 510(c) to recategorize a statutory tax priority on policy grounds alone.

The misconduct showing is more demanding when the claimant is an arms-length lender or trade creditor than when the claimant is an insider. The leading articulation of the insider-versus-outsider distinction is the Third Circuit's In re Citicorp Venture Capital, Ltd. v. Committee of Creditors Holding Unsecured Claims, 160 F.3d 982 (3d Cir. 1998): for insiders, "egregious conduct" is not required, and "breach of fiduciary duty" or "unfair advantage" suffices; for non-insiders, the conduct must be "very substantial" and approach actual fraud or moral turpitude.

Remedy is calibrated: equitable subordination is meant to undo the injury, not to punish. A court that finds inequitable conduct typically subordinates the claim only to the extent necessary to neutralize the injury or unfair advantage; full subordination of an otherwise-valid secured claim is reserved for the most serious cases.

Recharacterization Versus Equitable Subordination

Recharacterization is a doctrinally distinct cousin of equitable subordination. Where Section 510(c) subordinates a valid claim because of misconduct, recharacterization holds that the asserted claim was never debt at all but rather a disguised equity contribution, which is subordinated by Section 510(b) and Section 726(a) to all true debt claims.

The Sixth Circuit's In re AutoStyle Plastics, Inc., 269 F.3d 726 (6th Cir. 2001), is the leading treatment. AutoStyle adopted a thirteen-factor test originally developed in the Eleventh Circuit's In re Roth American, Inc. (and traceable to the Tax Court's Estate of Mixon), grouped into three core inquiries:

  1. Form of the instrument: Is there a written promissory note? Fixed maturity date? Fixed interest rate? Right to enforce repayment? Status as a creditor in event of liquidation?
  2. Intent of the parties: Did the parties intend the transaction as debt or equity? Was the loan secured? Was there a sinking fund? Did the corporation make payments on schedule? Was the lender treated as a creditor in the corporation's books and tax filings?
  3. Reasonableness of the transaction: Was the corporation adequately capitalized? Was the loan made when no disinterested third party would have lent? Was the contribution proportional to the lender's equity ownership? Was repayment dependent on the success of the business?

The test is fact-intensive and contextual. No single factor is dispositive; courts weigh the totality. The Fourth Circuit's In re Dornier Aviation (North America), Inc., 453 F.3d 225 (4th Cir. 2006), and the Tenth Circuit's In re Hedged-Investments Associates, 380 F.3d 1292 (10th Cir. 2004), follow comparable approaches.

Doctrinal Significance

The distinction matters because the elements are different. To recharacterize, the proponent must show the instrument was always equity in substance, regardless of how it was labeled. No misconduct is required. To equitably subordinate, the proponent must show conduct meeting the Mobile Steel test; even an obviously real debt can be subordinated if the lender behaved badly. A claim that survives recharacterization (it is debt) can still be subordinated under Section 510(c) (the lender's conduct was inequitable). A claim that is recharacterized never reaches the Section 510(c) inquiry, because it has been moved into the equity priority tier outright.

Subordination in Plan Distributions

Subordination determinations made under Section 510 carry through to plan-distribution allocation under Sections 1129 and 1191. A claim that has been equitably subordinated to general unsecured claims is paid only after general unsecureds are paid in full. A claim subordinated under Section 510(b) is treated as occupying the priority tier of the underlying security, which in most cases means it is paid only after all true debt has been satisfied. Subordination is a powerful tool because it reorders the waterfall without changing the allowed amount of the claim; the claim still exists, it just has to wait.
