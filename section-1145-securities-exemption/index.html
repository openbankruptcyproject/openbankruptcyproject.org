11 U.S.C. Section 1145 - Securities Act Exemption for Plan-Issued Securities

Plain-English guide to the Bankruptcy Code's exemption from Securities Act Section 5 registration for securities issued under a confirmed Chapter 11 plan, the Section 1145(b) underwriter restrictions, and integration with private-placement doctrine.

What Is Section 1145?

Section 1145 of the Bankruptcy Code exempts certain securities issued under a confirmed Chapter 11 plan from the registration requirements of Section 5 of the Securities Act of 1933 and from state blue-sky laws. The exemption is a deliberate accommodation between two federal statutory schemes: the securities laws would otherwise require an issuer to register or qualify for a registration exemption before distributing securities to creditors and equity holders, and the bankruptcy laws require plans to redistribute claims and interests in ways that often involve issuing new securities to large and diverse groups of recipients.

Without Section 1145, every Chapter 11 plan that distributed new common stock, preferred stock, debt instruments, warrants, or other securities to creditors would have to either complete a registered offering on Form S-1 or fit each distribution into a private-placement or other Section 4 exemption from registration. The transaction cost would be prohibitive and the timing - bankruptcy plans typically require certainty at confirmation - incompatible with the SEC's registration cycle.

Official citation: 11 U.S.C. § 1145

Section 1145(a)(1): The Primary Exemption

Section 1145(a)(1) provides that Section 5 of the Securities Act and any state or local law requiring registration for offer or sale of a security or registration or licensing of an issuer of, underwriter of, or broker or dealer in, a security do not apply to the offer or sale under a plan of a security of the debtor, of an affiliate participating in a joint plan with the debtor, or of a successor to the debtor under the plan, if the security is offered or sold "in exchange for a claim against, an interest in, or a claim for an administrative expense in the case concerning, the debtor or such affiliate" or "principally in such exchange and partly for cash or property."

The "principally in such exchange" qualifier is essential. A plan distribution that is genuinely in exchange for claims qualifies even if a small cash or property component is added. A distribution that is principally for cash with only a token claim component does not qualify and must find another exemption from Section 5.

Section 1145(a)(2): Warrants, Options, Conversion Rights

Section 1145(a)(2) extends the exemption to the offer of a security through any warrant, option, right to subscribe, or conversion privilege that was sold in the manner specified in 1145(a)(1), and to the sale of a security upon the exercise of such warrant, option, right, or privilege. This is important for plans that distribute warrants or convertible instruments to creditors: the original distribution is exempt under 1145(a)(1), and the subsequent issuance of underlying securities on exercise is exempt under 1145(a)(2). The recipient does not need to find a separate exemption at exercise.

Section 1145(b): The Underwriter Restriction

Section 1145(b) defines "underwriter" for purposes of the exemption and is the most consequential limit on the safe harbor. An entity is an underwriter if it purchases a claim against, an interest in, or a claim for an administrative expense against the debtor with a view to distribution of any security received in exchange for such claim or interest, or offers to sell securities offered under the plan for the holders of such securities, or offers to buy securities offered under the plan from the holders of such securities if the offer is made with a view to distribution, or is an issuer of the security, as defined in Section 2(a)(11) of the Securities Act, with respect to such security.

The practical bite is that securities received by underwriters are not freely tradable post-emergence. An underwriter's resale must satisfy either Section 4(a)(1) of the Securities Act (with the issuer-and-underwriter exclusions navigated carefully) or another exemption such as Rule 144. This is why distressed-debt investors who purchase large blocks of claims with the intention of receiving and reselling plan securities take particular care to structure their position so as not to be treated as underwriters.

Affiliate complication: Section 1145(b) treats affiliates of the issuer as underwriters with respect to plan-issued securities. Reorganized debtors with insiders who receive plan distributions in their capacity as creditors should expect resale restrictions on those insiders' securities, which can complicate post-emergence liquidity strategies for management equity.

Section 1145(c): Public Offering Status

Section 1145(c) provides that an offer or sale of securities of the kind and in the manner specified under 1145(a)(1) is, for purposes of the Securities Act, deemed to be a public offering. This characterization has two effects. First, recipients other than underwriters are not "issuers" for resale purposes and may resell their securities without registration, subject to general anti-fraud rules. Second, the deemed-public-offering status forecloses any argument that the original plan distribution was a private placement that would impose private-placement resale restrictions on all recipients.

Integration With No-Action Letter Framework

The SEC's staff has issued a body of no-action letters addressing how Section 1145 distributions interact with concurrent or near-in-time non-plan offerings. The general framework asks whether the plan distribution and the separate offering should be integrated into a single offering for Securities Act analysis. Integration would risk losing the 1145 exemption for the plan distribution (because the integrated whole might not satisfy 1145's "in exchange for claims" test) and would risk losing any private-placement exemption for the separate offering (because the integrated whole might exceed the limits of the relevant Section 4 exemption).

Plan drafters typically address integration risk by structuring concurrent transactions to fit cleanly within either 1145(a)(1) (claim-exchange distribution) or a clearly distinct registration exemption (such as a registered rights offering or a Rule 506(c) accredited-investor placement) and by separating the two transactions in time and economic substance.

State Blue-Sky Preemption

Section 1145(a) preempts state and local registration and licensing requirements for plan-issued securities. State blue-sky laws would otherwise impose qualification requirements that vary state by state and that could not realistically be satisfied in the timeframe of plan confirmation. The federal preemption ensures that the 1145 exemption operates uniformly across the country.

Anti-fraud provisions of state law are not preempted. State attorneys general and state securities regulators retain authority to pursue anti-fraud claims arising from material misstatements in disclosure statements or plan-related communications.

Practical Disclosure Considerations

Although 1145 exempts plan-issued securities from registration, it does not exempt the disclosure obligations underlying a plan. Section 1125 requires that solicitation of plan acceptances be preceded by court-approved adequate-information disclosure. The disclosure statement is the de facto offering document for plan-issued securities and is the document that recipients use to evaluate their distribution. Material misstatements or omissions in the disclosure statement create civil and potentially criminal exposure independent of 1145.

Reorganized issuers should also consider post-emergence ongoing reporting. If the reorganized entity has a class of securities registered under Section 12 of the Securities Exchange Act of 1934, or has more than the Section 15(d) holders-of-record threshold, periodic reporting obligations attach independent of the 1145 distribution itself.

Related Bankruptcy Code Sections

Section 1145 operates in concert with several other provisions of the Bankruptcy Code:

  • Section 1125 - Disclosure statement requirements
  • Section 1129 - Plan confirmation
  • Section 1123 - Contents of a plan
  • Section 1141 - Effect of confirmation
  • Section 1146 - Transfer tax exemption

Understanding how these sections interact is important for debtors, creditors, trustees, and counsel structuring or evaluating a Chapter 11 plan that contemplates the issuance of securities.
