Section 01What “chambers procedures” means

“Chambers procedures” are the generally-applicable conventions a judge follows in managing the courtroom and docket. They supplement — but do not displace — the Federal Rules of Bankruptcy Procedure and the district's Local Bankruptcy Rules. Where published, they address hearing days, motion practice, emergency relief, status conferences, remote appearances, scheduling orders, and courtesy-copy expectations. This page collects the public-record framework applicable to matters before Guy R. Humphrey in the United States Bankruptcy Court for the Southern District of Ohio and links to the chamber's own materials.

Neutral procedural orientation, not legal advice and not a characterization of any ruling or scheduling preference. For a specific matter consult the Local Rules, the judge's official court page, and licensed counsel admitted in the district.

Section 02Hearing days & standard motion calendars

Most bankruptcy chambers maintain recurring hearing days by chapter (commonly a Chapter 13 confirmation calendar, a Chapter 7 relief-from-stay calendar, a Chapter 11 omnibus calendar, and a contested-matters / adversary calendar). The schedule is published on the court's website; the calendaring clerk assigns times. Parties notice matters consistent with that calendar and Rule 9006(d) notice periods unless an emergency standard is met.

Section 03Emergency motion protocols

Federal Rule of Bankruptcy Procedure 9006(c) authorizes the court to shorten notice “for cause shown.” Chambers practice typically requires:

  • A separate motion to shorten notice, filed with the underlying motion, identifying the specific harm if relief is delayed.
  • A proposed order shortening notice that sets the hearing date and objection deadline.
  • Service of motion, motion to shorten, and any order entered on all affected parties in the manner the order directs.
  • Advance contact with the courtroom deputy in most chambers to identify an available slot.

“Cause” under Rule 9006(c) is fact-bound; generic urgency is not enough.

Section 04Discovery-dispute procedures

Discovery in contested matters and adversaries follows Federal Rules of Civil Procedure 26-37 as incorporated by Federal Rules of Bankruptcy Procedure 7026-7037. Most chambers require a documented Rule 37(a)(1) meet-and-confer before any discovery motion. Common conventions:

  • Short-form discovery-dispute letters or joint statements may substitute for a formal motion under a standing chambers order.
  • Telephonic status conferences are routinely used to resolve disputes before motion practice.
  • Privilege-log requirements and protective-order templates are typically posted by the court or available from the courtroom deputy.

Section 05Status conferences

Status conferences are a routine case-management tool, particularly in Chapter 11 (11 U.S.C. § 105(d)) and in adversaries under Rule 7016. Most chambers set an initial conference within 30-60 days after filing in Chapter 11, and at or shortly after issue-joinder in an adversary. Counsel should arrive familiar with the entire docket and prepared to address scheduling, mediation referral, and any pending motion.

Section 06Telephonic and remote appearances

Most courts maintain a standing order on telephonic and video appearances. The typical post-pandemic default:

  • Routine, non-evidentiary calendars are available by phone or video.
  • Contested evidentiary hearings and trials are presumptively in-person absent leave of court.
  • Remote witness testimony requires advance leave under Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 43(a) as incorporated by Rule 9017.

The court's published standing order controls; practice varies between districts and between chambers within a district.

Section 07Pretrial scheduling orders

Federal Rule of Bankruptcy Procedure 7016 incorporates Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 16. The chamber typically enters a scheduling order after the initial status conference setting deadlines for amendments, fact discovery, expert disclosures, dispositive motions, and trial. The order may require a joint Rule 26(f) report, complex-case designation, or a mediation referral. Modifications require good cause under Rule 16(b)(4).

Section 08Courtesy copies

Many chambers require a paper or electronic courtesy copy of any filing exceeding a stated page-count, or of any submission with voluminous exhibits. The threshold is typically published in a chambers standing order. Courtesy copies aid chambers; they do not substitute for electronic filing and service under the Local Rules.

Section 10Appellate context — 28 U.S.C. § 158(a)

Final judgments, orders, and decrees of a bankruptcy judge may be appealed under 28 U.S.C. § 158(a) to the district court (or, where constituted, the bankruptcy appellate panel of the circuit). Notice of appeal is governed by Federal Rule of Bankruptcy Procedure 8002, default deadline 14 days. Some interlocutory orders are appealable by leave under Rule 8004.
