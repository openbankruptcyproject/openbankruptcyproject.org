Federal Rule of Bankruptcy Procedure 2017

Rule 2017: Court Review of Debtor Counsel Transactions

Pre-petition fee review under Rule 2017(a), post-petition transactions under 2017(b), the section 329 statutory framework, and the disgorgement remedy.

Verbatim text of Rule 2017

(a) Payment or transfer to attorney before order for relief. On motion by any party in interest or on the court's own initiative, the court after notice and a hearing may determine whether any payment of money or any transfer of property by the debtor, made directly or indirectly and in contemplation of the filing of a petition under the Code by or against the debtor or before entry of the order for relief in an involuntary case, to an attorney for services rendered or to be rendered is excessive.
(b) Payment or transfer to attorney after order for relief. On motion by the debtor, the United States trustee, or on the court's own initiative, the court after notice and a hearing may determine whether any payment of money or any transfer of property, or any agreement therefor, by the debtor to an attorney after entry of an order for relief in a case under the Code is excessive, whether the payment or transfer is made or is to be made directly or indirectly, if the payment, transfer, or agreement therefor is for services in any way related to the case. Fed. R. Bankr. P. 2017.

The statutory anchor: 11 U.S.C. Section 329

Rule 2017 is the procedural mechanism for the substantive authority granted by 11 U.S.C. Section 329. Section 329(a) requires debtor counsel to file a statement of the compensation paid or agreed to be paid for services rendered or to be rendered in contemplation of or in connection with the case. Section 329(b) authorizes the court to cancel any agreement or order the return of any payment "to the extent excessive."

Section 329 is one of the few statutory provisions in the Bankruptcy Code that grants the court roving authority over transactions in which the court itself is not directly a party. The premise is that debtor counsel occupies a position of trust relative to a debtor whose financial distress impairs ordinary arm's-length bargaining. The court is the disinterested check on that bargain.

The structure of Rule 2017

Rule 2017 has two operative subdivisions corresponding to the two temporal phases of the attorney-debtor relationship:

  • Rule 2017(a) governs pre-petition transactions - any payment or transfer made "in contemplation of" the filing of a petition or before the order for relief in an involuntary case. The standard is whether the payment is excessive.
  • Rule 2017(b) governs post-petition transactions - any payment, transfer, or agreement made after the order for relief that is "in any way related to the case." The standard is again whether the payment is excessive.

The two subdivisions differ in the parties authorized to initiate review. Rule 2017(a) permits initiation by "any party in interest" or by the court. Rule 2017(b) limits party initiation to the debtor and the United States trustee, but preserves the court's sua sponte authority. The distinction reflects the different constituency interests implicated: pre-petition fees affect the size of the estate available to all creditors, while post-petition fees come from the estate itself and are subject to other oversight regimes.

The "excessive" standard

Neither Section 329 nor Rule 2017 defines "excessive." Courts have developed a multi-factor analysis substantially overlapping the lodestar framework applied to professional fee applications under Section 330. Typical factors include:

  • The nature and complexity of the services rendered
  • The time reasonably required for the services
  • The customary fee charged in the local market for comparable services
  • The experience and skill of the attorney
  • The results achieved
  • Whether the fee was the product of arm's-length negotiation under conditions permitting informed consent

The "excessive" standard is not coextensive with the Section 330 "reasonable" standard, however. Section 329 review reaches transactions that may not be reviewable under Section 330 at all - notably, pre-petition retainer agreements with consumer debtors who were not yet debtors when the agreement was made.

Sua sponte review

One of the structural features of Rule 2017 that distinguishes it from most procedural rules is the express grant of sua sponte authority to the court. The court need not wait for a motion; it may set fees for review on its own initiative after notice and a hearing.

The practical implication is significant. A debtor who has paid fees to counsel pre-petition often has no incentive to challenge those fees - the debtor has already paid and may continue to depend on counsel. A creditor with standing to challenge under Rule 2017(a) may lack the information to identify a problem. The court's sua sponte authority closes the gap. A judge who observes an unusual fee disclosure, a sudden lawsuit by counsel against a former client, or an unexplained absence of post-petition representation may set the fee for review without any motion from the parties.

Disgorgement remedy

The remedy under Section 329(b) is structured in the alternative: the court may "cancel" the agreement (preventing future enforcement) or order the return of any payment "to the extent excessive." The two remedies are not mutually exclusive. A court may cancel a contingent or installment-fee agreement going forward and order disgorgement of payments already received that exceed the value of services actually rendered.

Disgorgement under Section 329(b) is mandatory once the court finds excessiveness; the statute provides that the court "may" cancel and order return, but the consistent case law treats the discretion as one over the scope of disgorgement rather than over whether to disgorge at all. A finding that a fee is excessive without a corresponding disgorgement order is rare.

The destination of disgorged funds depends on the circumstances. Where the fee was paid from estate property, return is to the estate (with redistribution to creditors through ordinary distribution mechanisms). Where the fee was paid from non-estate property (a relative's contribution, for example), return is typically to the source of the payment.

Interaction with the Rule 2016 disclosure

Rule 2017 review is operationally dependent on the disclosure required by Section 329(a) and Rule 2016(b). Section 329(a) requires debtor counsel to file a statement of compensation within 14 days after the order for relief. The Rule 2016(b) disclosure - filed on Official Form 2030 - identifies the source of compensation, the amount, the services covered, and any agreement for further payment.

An incomplete or inaccurate Section 329(a) / Rule 2016(b) disclosure has two consequences. First, it deprives the court and parties in interest of the information needed to evaluate whether to seek Rule 2017 review. Second, it is itself an independently sanctionable defect: a knowing failure to disclose is a basis for denial of all compensation under the "fiduciary disclosure" doctrine that pre-dates the Bankruptcy Code and has been consistently applied under it.

Common procedural posture

Pre-petition retainer review

A consumer debtor pays a flat-fee retainer to counsel before filing a Chapter 7 petition. A creditor or the U.S. trustee believes the fee was excessive in light of the simplicity of the case. The challenger files a Rule 2017(a) motion. The court reviews the fee against the local-market customary fee, the work actually performed, and any unusual circumstances justifying departure. If the court finds the fee excessive, it orders disgorgement of the excess.

Post-petition installment-fee review

Debtor counsel enters into a post-petition installment-payment agreement for services rendered in the bankruptcy. The U.S. trustee believes the agreement was not disclosed under Rule 2016(b) and that the installment structure operates as an undisclosed contingent fee. The U.S. trustee files a Rule 2017(b) motion. The court may cancel the agreement going forward and order disgorgement of installments already received.

Practical impact

Rule 2017 is the procedural rule that most directly enforces the substantive principle that debtor counsel does not have unconstrained pricing authority over a financially distressed client. The rule operates at three time horizons: pre-petition (subdivision (a)), post-petition (subdivision (b)), and post-confirmation - because Section 329 review remains available, by the consistent case law, even after the case is closed, when an excessive fee was concealed at the time of disclosure.

The court's sua sponte authority is the load-bearing feature. A debtor who has paid counsel and remains dependent on counsel for representation has little incentive to challenge the fee; a creditor without access to the underlying engagement information cannot identify a problem. Sua sponte review under Rule 2017 closes the gap that party-driven review would otherwise leave open.
