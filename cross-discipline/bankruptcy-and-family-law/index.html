Bankruptcy and Family Law — Interaction Primer 2026

First published 2026-05-22/Version 0.1.0/CC-BY-4.0/status: draft

Abstract

Family law obligations occupy a privileged position in bankruptcy. Domestic support obligations — alimony, maintenance, and child support — are first-priority unsecured claims under 11 U.S.C. § 507(a)(1) and are never dischargeable under any chapter. Non-support divorce-decree obligations (typically property settlements and hold-harmless agreements) are non-dischargeable in Chapter 7 and Chapter 11 cases of individual debtors under § 523(a)(15), though historically dischargeable in Chapter 13. The automatic stay yields to specified family proceedings under § 362(b)(2). State equitable-distribution rights interact with the bankruptcy estate in subtle and consequential ways.

This primer summarizes the principal interaction points between the Bankruptcy Code and state family law. It is general information and does not constitute legal advice in any jurisdiction.

The Domestic Support Obligation

The 2005 BAPCPA amendments introduced the unified concept of the "domestic support obligation" (DSO) and gave it elevated status throughout the Code. Section 101(14A) defines a DSO as a debt that (i) is owed to or recoverable by a spouse, former spouse, child of the debtor, that child's parent, legal guardian, or responsible relative, or a governmental unit; (ii) is "in the nature of alimony, maintenance, or support" without regard to whether the underlying instrument labels it as such; (iii) is established by a separation agreement, divorce decree, property settlement, court order, or determination of a governmental unit; and (iv) has not been assigned to a non-governmental entity (except for collection purposes).

The "in the nature of" language is operative. The bankruptcy court is not bound by the label affixed by the state divorce court; it conducts a federal-law inquiry into the actual function of the obligation. Standard factors examined by the courts include the language of the decree, the parties' relative financial positions at the time of the decree, whether the obligation terminates on death or remarriage, whether it varies with the recipient's needs, and whether it provides for present support or for a long-term division of marital property.

Non-Dischargeability of DSO Under Section 523(a)(5)

Section 523(a)(5) excepts from discharge any debt "for a domestic support obligation." The exception applies in every chapter and is one of the few exceptions that applies without an adversary proceeding: a DSO survives discharge automatically, and the creditor need not file a § 523 complaint to preserve the claim. Sections 727(b), 1141(d)(2), 1192(2), 1228(a)(2), and 1328(a)(2) each cross-reference § 523(a)(5) and confirm the across-the-board treatment.

The non-dischargeability of DSO is doctrinally tied to public policy: support obligations exist to protect dependents whose welfare cannot be left to the vagaries of bankruptcy distribution. The Supreme Court's pre-Code decision in Wetmore v. Markoe, 196 U.S. 68 (1904), held that alimony obligations were not provable debts under the prior bankruptcy statute precisely because they served a continuing protective function. The principle persists, recodified.

Priority of DSO Claims Under Section 507(a)(1)

Section 507(a)(1) confers first-priority status on allowed unsecured DSO claims, ahead of administrative expenses. Subsection (a)(1)(A) treats claims owed directly to a spouse, former spouse, or child as first priority; subsection (a)(1)(B) treats claims that have been assigned to a governmental unit or that are owed directly to a governmental unit as a closely-following first-priority sub-class, but still ahead of administrative expenses.

The practical consequence in a Chapter 7 case is that DSO arrears are paid first from any liquidation proceeds available for unsecured creditors. In Chapter 11 and Chapter 13, full payment of priority DSO claims through the plan is required as a precondition to confirmation under §§ 1129(a)(9)(B), 1322(a)(2), and 1322(a)(4) (special treatment for assigned support claims).

Section 1325(a)(8) further requires, as a precondition to Chapter 13 plan confirmation, that the debtor be current on post-petition DSO obligations that became due after filing. Section 1328(a) conditions Chapter 13 discharge on certification by the debtor that all DSO obligations — both pre-petition arrears handled through the plan and post-petition payments — have been paid.

Section 523(a)(15) and Non-Support Divorce-Decree Obligations

Not every obligation arising from a divorce is a DSO. Property-equalization payments, debt-allocation provisions, hold-harmless agreements, and attorney's-fee awards that are not "in the nature of" support fall outside § 523(a)(5). Before 2005, such non-support obligations were dischargeable in Chapter 7 unless the creditor brought a § 523(a)(15) action and proved one of two statutory factors (inability to pay or balance-of-equities).

BAPCPA simplified the framework. Section 523(a)(15) now excepts from discharge any debt "to a spouse, former spouse, or child of the debtor and not of the kind described in paragraph (5) that is incurred by the debtor in the course of a divorce or separation or in connection with a separation agreement, divorce decree or other order of a court of record, or a determination made in accordance with State or territorial law by a governmental unit." The pre-BAPCPA defenses are gone in Chapter 7 and Chapter 11 cases.

Section 523(a)(15) is not cross-referenced in § 1328(a)(2). The omission means that § 523(a)(15) obligations remain dischargeable in a Chapter 13 case that proceeds to full-payment discharge under § 1328(a). Chapter 13 thus retains its historical role as a relief mechanism for debtors burdened by non-support divorce-decree obligations, although DSO arrears remain non-dischargeable in Chapter 13 under § 523(a)(5).

The distinction between § 523(a)(5) and § 523(a)(15) accordingly remains consequential in Chapter 13 even after BAPCPA. The same factual inquiry into the function of the obligation determines the outcome.

The Automatic Stay and Family Court Proceedings

Section 362(a) imposes the automatic stay on most actions to collect pre-petition claims against the debtor. Section 362(b)(2) carves out specific family-court activities. As amended by BAPCPA, the stay does not bar:

  • The commencement or continuation of a civil action or proceeding "for the establishment of paternity" (§ 362(b)(2)(A)(i));
  • The establishment or modification of an order for domestic support obligations (§ 362(b)(2)(A)(ii));
  • Proceedings concerning child custody or visitation (§ 362(b)(2)(A)(iii));
  • Proceedings for the dissolution of a marriage, except to the extent that the proceeding seeks to determine the division of property that is property of the estate (§ 362(b)(2)(A)(iv));
  • Proceedings regarding domestic violence (§ 362(b)(2)(A)(v));
  • The collection of a DSO from property that is not property of the estate (§ 362(b)(2)(B));
  • The withholding of income that is property of the estate or of the debtor for payment of a DSO (§ 362(b)(2)(C)); and
  • The reporting of overdue support to a credit reporting agency (§ 362(b)(2)(E)).

The carve-outs reflect a policy judgment that ongoing family-law functions should not be disrupted by the debtor's bankruptcy. The principal stay-protected zone within a pending dissolution proceeding is the division of estate property: the family court may issue the divorce decree and may resolve custody and support, but it may not finally partition assets that, as of the petition date, are property of the bankruptcy estate.

Equitable Distribution and the Bankruptcy Estate

In a marital-property regime — whether common-law equitable distribution or community property — the non-debtor spouse holds some form of pre-existing interest in marital assets at the moment of the petition. The exact nature of that interest is a question of state law, and its bankruptcy treatment depends on whether it rises to the level of a "legal or equitable interest" recognizable under § 541(a)(1) or whether it is merely an inchoate claim that ripens only on the entry of a divorce decree.

In community-property jurisdictions, § 541(a)(2) brings into the estate all interests of the debtor and the debtor's spouse in community property that is (a) under the sole, equal, or joint management and control of the debtor, or (b) liable for an allowable claim against the debtor. The non-debtor spouse's community interest is thus drawn into the federal proceeding.

In equitable-distribution jurisdictions, the analysis turns on whether state law treats the non-debtor spouse's pre-decree interest as a present property right or as a contingent right that crystallizes only with the decree. The circuits have divided on the question, with most decisions favoring estate inclusion where state law confers a present marital-property interest.

The timing interplay matters. If the dissolution decree is entered pre-petition, the property division is generally a fait accompli, subject to attack under § 547 (preference) or § 548 (fraudulent transfer) only in narrow circumstances. If the petition precedes the decree, the bankruptcy court generally controls disposition of estate property, and the family court must proceed within the boundaries that § 362(b)(2)(A)(iv) draws.

Exemptions and the Non-Debtor Spouse

Several federal exemption provisions under § 522(d) and many state exemption schemes contain doubling provisions or joint-filing enhancements that allow married debtors filing jointly under § 302 to claim larger exemptions than either spouse could claim individually. The homestead exemption is the most frequently doubled item, and it is governed by § 522(d)(1) under federal exemptions or by the state-law analog under § 522(b)(3) where the state has opted out of the federal scheme.

Joint filing under § 302 is permitted only for spouses; it creates a single jointly-administered estate and does not require consolidation, though courts may order consolidation in appropriate cases. A jointly-filed petition triggers an automatic stay applicable to both spouses and to property in which either holds an interest.
