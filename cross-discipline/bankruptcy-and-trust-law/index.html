Bankruptcy and Trust Law — Interaction Primer 2026

First published 2026-05-22/Version 0.1.0/CC-BY-4.0/status: draft

Abstract

The intersection of bankruptcy and trust law is governed by a single doctrinal lever: 11 U.S.C. § 541(c)(2). That provision incorporates by reference state-law transfer restrictions on beneficial interests in trusts, making spendthrift protection enforceable in bankruptcy where it is enforceable outside bankruptcy. A traditional third-party-settled spendthrift trust shields the beneficiary-debtor's interest from the estate. A self-settled trust generally does not: most states' trust law denies spendthrift effect against the settlor's own creditors, and most jurisdictions' fraudulent-transfer statutes provide additional avenues of attack. The Bankruptcy Code's principal weapon against asset-protection trusts is § 548(e), enacted by BAPCPA, which extends the fraudulent-transfer reach to a ten-year lookback for self-settled trusts and similar devices.

This primer summarizes the principal interaction points. It is general information and is not legal or estate-planning advice.

Spendthrift Trusts and Section 541(c)(2)

The traditional spendthrift trust is a third-party-settled trust in which the settlor (a parent, grandparent, or other donor) provides for distributions to one or more beneficiaries, subject to an express transfer restriction that prevents the beneficiary from voluntarily assigning, and creditors from involuntarily reaching, the beneficial interest. The restriction is enforceable under the law of nearly every state, codified in the Uniform Trust Code at § 502 and in the Restatement (Third) of Trusts at § 58.

Section 541(c)(2) of the Bankruptcy Code provides that "a restriction on the transfer of a beneficial interest of the debtor in a trust that is enforceable under applicable nonbankruptcy law is enforceable in a case under this title." The text incorporates state spendthrift law by reference. If a state would recognize the transfer restriction outside bankruptcy, the bankruptcy court will recognize it within bankruptcy. The beneficiary-debtor's interest in the trust does not enter the estate; the trustee in bankruptcy takes nothing with respect to the trust.

The Supreme Court in Patterson v. Shumate, 504 U.S. 753 (1992), addressed § 541(c)(2) in the context of ERISA-qualified pension plans and held that "applicable nonbankruptcy law" includes federal law as well as state law. The reasoning extends across the trust-law landscape: any enforceable transfer restriction — whether grounded in a state spendthrift statute, a federal pension provision, or another body of nonbankruptcy law — will shield the beneficial interest from the estate.

The protection is limited to the beneficial interest itself, not to distributions actually made. Once a distribution is paid to the beneficiary, the funds lose their trust character and become the beneficiary's general property, subject to ordinary creditor remedies and (post-petition) to ordinary estate-property analysis. A pre-petition distribution that the beneficiary holds at the petition date is property of the estate; a post-petition distribution in a Chapter 7 case is generally not property of the estate, though it may be reachable in Chapter 13 under § 1306.

Self-Settled Trusts: The Traditional Rule

The traditional rule, embodied in the Restatement (Second) of Trusts § 156 and the Restatement (Third) of Trusts § 58(2), is that a settlor cannot place property in trust for the settlor's own benefit and shield it from the settlor's creditors. The rationale is straightforward: the settlor cannot, by self-declaration, secure for itself the use of property while denying creditors access to it. The doctrine is sometimes summarized as "what the settlor gives, the settlor's creditors can reach."

The Uniform Trust Code § 505(a) preserves the traditional rule in default form: "during the lifetime of the settlor, the property of a revocable trust is subject to claims of the settlor's creditors," and "with respect to an irrevocable trust, a creditor or assignee of the settlor may reach the maximum amount that can be distributed to or for the settlor's benefit."

In bankruptcy, the application of state self-settled-trust law through § 541(c)(2) generally results in the inclusion of self-settled trust property in the estate to the extent the settlor-debtor is a beneficiary. The state-law transfer restriction is unenforceable against the settlor's own creditors, so it does not qualify under § 541(c)(2) as an "enforceable" restriction.

Domestic Asset-Protection Trusts

A minority of states — including, among others, Alaska, Delaware, Nevada, South Dakota, and Wyoming — have enacted domestic asset-protection trust (DAPT) statutes that purport to validate self-settled spendthrift trusts and to shield trust property from the settlor's creditors after specified statutory lookback periods. The statutes vary in their specifics: the lookback period for creditor reach typically ranges from two to four years, the categories of exempt creditors (alimony, child support, pre-existing torts) vary, and the procedural requirements for trust formation differ.

The bankruptcy analysis of DAPTs has been complicated by two distinct issues. First, conflict-of-laws: a settlor domiciled outside the DAPT state who creates a DAPT under the favorable state's law may face the argument that the settlor's domiciliary state's traditional rule governs, particularly where the settlor's creditors are also outside the DAPT state. Some bankruptcy courts have applied the settlor's domiciliary state's law and denied protection.

Second, § 548(e). Enacted by BAPCPA, this provision extends the fraudulent-transfer lookback to ten years for transfers to a "self-settled trust or similar device" if the debtor is a beneficiary, the transfer was made with actual intent to hinder, delay, or defraud a creditor, and the debtor is the settlor of the trust. The ten-year window is substantially longer than the ordinary two-year § 548(a) period and longer than any DAPT statute's lookback. The provision is structured as a federal overlay that operates independently of state validation of the trust form.

Irrevocable Life Insurance Trusts and Other Third-Party Irrevocable Trusts

An irrevocable life insurance trust (ILIT) is a third-party irrevocable trust commonly used in estate planning to hold a life insurance policy outside the insured's gross estate for federal estate-tax purposes. The insured contributes annual premium payments to the trust, the trustee uses those contributions to pay the policy premium, and the trust holds the death benefit on the insured's death for distribution to designated beneficiaries.

From a bankruptcy perspective, the ILIT raises two distinct questions. First, if the insured-settlor files bankruptcy during life: the insured is not a beneficiary of the ILIT (the structure is designed to keep the insured outside the beneficial class for estate-tax reasons), so the ILIT corpus is not property of the insured's estate under § 541. The insured retains no interest that the trustee in bankruptcy can reach. Annual premium contributions to the trust may, however, be attacked as fraudulent transfers under § 548 or under state-law equivalents through § 544(b), depending on the contribution dates and the settlor's solvency at the time of each contribution.

Second, if a beneficiary files bankruptcy: the spendthrift analysis of § 541(c)(2) governs. A well-drafted ILIT will contain transfer restrictions that satisfy the applicable state spendthrift statute; the beneficiary-debtor's interest will then be excluded from the estate. Distributions actually received pre-petition and held at the petition date enter the estate by ordinary operation; post-petition distributions are governed by the chapter-specific analysis.

Other third-party irrevocable trusts — testamentary trusts created by a parent's will, inter vivos trusts created by a grandparent, dynasty trusts — receive the same treatment. The beneficiary's interest is sheltered by § 541(c)(2) so long as the trust contains an enforceable transfer restriction; distributions are addressed under ordinary estate-property rules.

Fraudulent Transfer Recovery Under Section 548

Section 548 is the principal federal fraudulent-transfer provision and the principal weapon against asset-protection strategies that depend on pre-petition transfers from the debtor to trusts or other holding vehicles. Two branches of § 548 are operative:

Section 548(a)(1)(A) — actual fraud. The trustee may avoid any transfer made within two years before the petition if the transfer was made "with actual intent to hinder, delay, or defraud" any creditor. The intent standard is satisfied by direct or circumstantial evidence and is frequently proven through the "badges of fraud" doctrine: insider status of the transferee, retention of possession by the transferor, secrecy of the transfer, pending or threatened litigation against the transferor at the time of the transfer, transfer of substantially all the transferor's assets, absconding by the transferor, removal or concealment of assets, lack of reasonably equivalent value, and insolvency at the time of or shortly after the transfer.

Section 548(a)(1)(B) — constructive fraud. The trustee may avoid any transfer made within two years before the petition if the debtor received less than reasonably equivalent value in exchange and was insolvent on the date of the transfer or became insolvent as a result, was engaged in business with unreasonably small capital, or intended to incur debts beyond the ability to pay as they matured. No intent element is required; the constructive-fraud branch operates on objective criteria.

Section 548(e) — ten-year self-settled trust reach. This subsection, enacted by BAPCPA, provides a separate ten-year lookback for transfers made to self-settled trusts or similar devices where (i) the transfer was made within ten years before the petition, (ii) the transfer was made to a self-settled trust or similar device, (iii) the transfer was by the debtor, (iv) the debtor is a beneficiary, and (v) the debtor made the transfer with actual intent to hinder, delay, or defraud any entity to which the debtor was or became indebted on or after the date of the transfer.

Section 544(b) supplements § 548 by allowing the trustee to invoke any applicable state fraudulent-transfer statute (typically the Uniform Voidable Transactions Act, the successor to the Uniform Fraudulent Transfer Act) through any actual unsecured creditor in existence at the petition date. Many state UVTA enactments provide a four-year lookback, longer than § 548's general two-year window, with extensions for actual-fraud claims discovered late.

Strong-Arm Powers and Trust Distributions

Section 544(a), the strong-arm clause, gives the trustee the rights and powers of a hypothetical judicial lien creditor, a hypothetical execution creditor, and (with respect to real property) a hypothetical bona fide purchaser, all as of the petition date. The strong-arm powers can interact with trust law in two principal contexts.

First, a beneficiary's interest in a trust that is recorded or otherwise of record may be reachable by the strong-arm trustee to the extent that an actual judicial lien creditor of the beneficiary could reach it under state law. This is the analytical step that defeats the strong-arm clause where the trust contains an enforceable spendthrift provision: an actual judicial lien creditor could not reach the beneficial interest, so the hypothetical-lien trustee cannot reach it either.

Second, mandatory distributions due to the beneficiary as of the petition date may be reachable as accrued but unpaid distributions, even where the trust as a whole is spendthrift-protected. The right to a fixed and currently-due distribution is a present property right that survives spendthrift protection in most jurisdictions; the executor of that right enters the estate.
