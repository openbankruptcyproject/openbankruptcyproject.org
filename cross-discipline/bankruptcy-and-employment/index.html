Bankruptcy and Employment — Interaction Primer 2026

First published 2026-05-22/Version 0.1.0/CC-BY-4.0/status: draft

Abstract

Bankruptcy law sits at the boundary of employment law in three principal registers. First, the filing of a petition halts wage garnishments and other collection activity directed at the debtor's earnings under the automatic stay of 11 U.S.C. § 362(a)(2). Second, the treatment of post-petition earnings differs sharply between Chapter 7 (where they are excluded from the estate) and Chapter 13 (where they are property of the estate). Third, employer-sponsored retirement assets receive substantial protection: ERISA-qualified pension benefits are excluded from the estate under § 541(c)(2) as construed in Patterson v. Shumate, 504 U.S. 753 (1992), while non-ERISA individual retirement accounts and Roth IRAs are exempt under § 522(d)(12) and § 522(n), subject to a statutory cap.

This primer summarizes the principal interaction points between the Bankruptcy Code, the Employee Retirement Income Security Act, the Internal Revenue Code's retirement-account provisions, and federal and state wage-protection law.

Wage Garnishment and the Automatic Stay

Section 362(a)(2) prohibits "the enforcement, against the debtor or against property of the estate, of a judgment obtained before the commencement of the case under this title." Wage-garnishment writs are post-judgment enforcement mechanisms, and they fall squarely within the prohibition. Section 362(a)(6) independently bars "any act to collect, assess, or recover a claim against the debtor that arose before the commencement of the case." A garnishment-in-progress must terminate upon notice of the petition; the employer must cease withholding under the garnishment writ, and any funds withheld but not yet remitted should be returned to the debtor or otherwise dealt with as the bankruptcy court directs.

Garnishments for domestic support obligations are an exception. Section 362(b)(2)(C) permits "the withholding of income that is property of the estate or property of the debtor for payment of a domestic support obligation under a judicial or administrative order or a statute." Child-support and alimony withholdings continue uninterrupted.

An employer's failure to halt a non-exempt garnishment after notice of the petition can expose the employer to liability for violation of the stay under § 362(k), which authorizes "actual damages, including costs and attorneys' fees, and, in appropriate circumstances, punitive damages" for willful stay violations. The case-law has developed a practical-knowledge standard: notice received by the payroll department or by an officer authorized to act on garnishment matters is generally sufficient.

Post-Petition Earnings: Chapter 7 vs. Chapter 13

The treatment of post-petition earnings is one of the sharpest doctrinal divides in consumer bankruptcy.

Chapter 7

Section 541(a)(6) excludes from property of the estate "earnings from services performed by an individual debtor after the commencement of the case." The exclusion is foundational to the fresh-start policy of Chapter 7: the debtor's post-petition labor income belongs to the debtor and is not available to creditors. The Chapter 7 trustee's reach is limited to property that existed as of the petition date (including pre-petition accrued wages not yet paid) and to certain proceeds and after-acquired property captured by other § 541 subsections.

Chapter 13

Section 1306(a)(2) sweeps in "earnings from services performed by the debtor after the commencement of the case but before the case is closed, dismissed, or converted to a case under chapter 7." Chapter 13 thus extends the estate to include three to five years of post-petition wages, which are used to fund the plan payments to creditors. The interaction with § 541(a)(6) is governed by § 1306, which expressly supersedes the Chapter 7 exclusion for the duration of the Chapter 13 case.

The Supreme Court addressed the disposition of post-petition earnings upon conversion of a Chapter 13 case to Chapter 7 in Harris v. Viegelahn, 575 U.S. 510 (2015), holding that undistributed Chapter 13 plan payments revert to the debtor on conversion. Funds in the hands of the Chapter 13 trustee at the moment of conversion are not transferred to the Chapter 7 estate; they are returned to the debtor for the debtor's use.

Severance and Bonus Payments

The line between excluded post-petition earnings and included pre-petition property can be subtle. Severance pay attributable to pre-petition employment is generally property of the estate even if paid after filing; severance attributable to post-petition continuation of employment is not. Performance bonuses that are earned through a mix of pre- and post-petition services typically require pro-ration. The standard inquiry is into the consideration that gave rise to the payment, not into the date of the check.

ERISA-Qualified Retirement Plans and Patterson v. Shumate

Section 541(c)(2) of the Bankruptcy Code provides that "a restriction on the transfer of a beneficial interest of the debtor in a trust that is enforceable under applicable nonbankruptcy law is enforceable in a case under this title." The Supreme Court in Patterson v. Shumate, 504 U.S. 753 (1992), unanimously held that the anti-alienation provision of the Employee Retirement Income Security Act, codified at 29 U.S.C. § 1056(d)(1) and 26 U.S.C. § 401(a)(13), constitutes "applicable nonbankruptcy law" within the meaning of § 541(c)(2).

The practical result is that the debtor's beneficial interest in an ERISA-qualified pension plan, 401(k) plan, profit-sharing plan, or defined-benefit plan is excluded from the estate altogether. It does not enter the estate, and the trustee has no claim on it. The exclusion is not subject to a dollar cap. A debtor with a multi-million-dollar 401(k) balance keeps the entire account through Chapter 7, provided the plan is ERISA-qualified and the anti-alienation provision is enforceable.

The qualification matters. Plans that fail ERISA's qualification requirements — for example, top-hat plans for executives, certain church plans that have opted out, certain governmental plans — do not enjoy the § 541(c)(2) shelter. They may nonetheless be exempt under § 522(d)(12) or under analogous state-law exemptions, but they enter the estate first and must be claimed as exempt to be retained.

Non-ERISA IRAs and the Section 522(n) Cap

Individual retirement accounts under IRC §§ 408 and 408A (traditional IRAs and Roth IRAs) are not ERISA-qualified. They are not excluded from the estate under § 541(c)(2). Instead, they are exempt under § 522(d)(12), subject to the dollar cap in § 522(n).

Section 522(n) was added by BAPCPA in 2005 and caps the exemption of "amounts attributable to individual retirement accounts described in section 408 or 408A of the Internal Revenue Code" at a statutory amount that adjusts triennially under § 104. The cap does not apply to rollovers from ERISA-qualified plans into IRAs; those rolled-over amounts remain fully exempt. The cap also does not apply to SEP-IRAs and SIMPLE IRAs to the extent they constitute employer-sponsored plans subject to ERISA-like treatment.

The interplay creates a planning consideration. A debtor contemplating bankruptcy who has accumulated IRA assets approaching the cap should consider whether to consolidate ERISA-rollover IRAs separately from contributory IRAs, since the rollover portion retains its fully-exempt character. Documentation of rollover origin matters for the post-petition exemption defense.

The Supreme Court addressed a separate IRA-related question in Clark v. Rameker, 573 U.S. 122 (2014), holding that inherited IRAs are not "retirement funds" within the meaning of the § 522(d)(12) exemption. An IRA inherited from a non-spouse decedent is not a retirement vehicle for the inheritor; it is a transferred asset subject to mandatory distribution rules under IRC § 401(a)(9). The inherited IRA enters the estate and is not exempt under federal law.

Wage Priority Under Section 507(a)(4)

When the debtor is an employer rather than an employee, employment-law considerations enter the bankruptcy estate from the other side. Section 507(a)(4) confers fourth-priority status on unsecured claims for "wages, salaries, or commissions, including vacation, severance, and sick leave pay" earned by an individual within 180 days before the petition or before cessation of the debtor's business, whichever is earlier, subject to a statutory cap per individual employee that adjusts under § 104.

Section 507(a)(5) confers fifth-priority status on unsecured contributions to employee benefit plans arising from services rendered within the same 180-day window, again subject to a statutory cap.

In Chapter 11, the WARN Act (29 U.S.C. §§ 2101–2109) interacts with priority treatment. WARN claims for the 60-day notice period typically receive priority treatment for the post-petition portion (as administrative expense) and fourth-priority treatment for the pre-petition portion that falls within the § 507(a)(4) window. The case-law has split on the precise characterization, particularly for closures effected immediately upon filing.

First-Day Wage Orders in Chapter 11

In Chapter 11 cases of operating businesses, a typical first-day motion is a "wage motion" requesting authority to pay pre-petition wages, salaries, commissions, and benefits to employees in the ordinary course. The motion is supported by the "necessity of payment" doctrine and by the § 507(a)(4) priority status of the underlying claims. Courts routinely grant the motion to preserve workforce stability and to comply with state wage-payment laws that may impose personal liability on corporate officers for non-payment of accrued wages.

The order typically caps the amount payable per employee at the § 507(a)(4) priority cap, on the rationale that any amount above the cap is a general unsecured claim and should await the plan process. Some districts have local guidelines or standing orders that prescribe the form of relief.
