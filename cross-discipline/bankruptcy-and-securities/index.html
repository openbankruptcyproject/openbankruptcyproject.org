Bankruptcy and Securities — Interaction Primer 2026

First published 2026-05-22/Version 0.1.0/CC-BY-4.0/status: draft

Abstract

The intersection of bankruptcy law and federal securities law is dominated by a handful of recurring questions. When a Chapter 11 plan distributes new securities of the reorganized debtor to claim and interest holders, 11 U.S.C. § 1145 supplies a federal registration exemption that displaces the Securities Act of 1933 registration requirement and preempts most state blue sky registration. The Securities and Exchange Commission has statutory standing to appear and be heard in Chapter 11 cases under § 1109(a). Securities-fraud claims against the debtor are non-dischargeable for individual debtors under § 523(a)(19), added by Sarbanes-Oxley. Registered investment companies and stockbrokers are barred from filing Chapter 11 and instead receive specialized liquidation regimes under Subchapter III and the Securities Investor Protection Act.

This primer summarizes those interactions. It is general information and does not constitute legal advice.

The Section 1145 Plan-Securities Exemption

Section 1145(a)(1) of the Bankruptcy Code provides that the registration requirements of Section 5 of the Securities Act of 1933 do not apply to "the offer or sale under a plan of a security of the debtor, of an affiliate participating in a joint plan with the debtor, or of a successor to the debtor under the plan" if the offer or sale is "in exchange for a claim against, an interest in, or a claim for an administrative expense in the case concerning, the debtor or such affiliate" or is principally in such exchange and partly for cash or property.

The exemption is automatic and self-executing. No application to the Securities and Exchange Commission, no filing of a Form S-1 or other registration statement, and no quiet period are required. The reorganization court's confirmation order is treated as the functional equivalent of registration for securities-law purposes. Section 1145(a)(2) extends the exemption to subsequent transfers by entities other than "underwriters" as defined in § 1145(b).

The underwriter definition in § 1145(b) is narrower than the Securities Act definition. It encompasses (i) those who purchase claims against the debtor or interests with a view to distribution, (ii) those who offer to sell securities offered under the plan for holders, (iii) those who offer to buy securities offered under the plan from holders if the offer to buy is with a view to distribution, and (iv) issuers and any "person directly or indirectly controlling or controlled by, or under direct or indirect common control with," the issuer. Resales by non-underwriters are exempt from registration under § 1145(a)(2); resales by § 1145(b) underwriters are not, and instead require registration or compliance with another exemption (most commonly Rule 144).

Limits of the Exemption

The § 1145 exemption is limited in three principal respects. First, it covers only securities of the debtor, an affiliate participating in a joint plan, or a successor to the debtor; securities of unaffiliated third parties (for example, securities of a sponsor or financial partner that are bundled into a plan distribution) are not exempt and require independent registration or another exemption such as Section 4(a)(2) or Rule 506.

Second, the exemption applies only when the securities are issued in exchange for claims or interests; cash sales to new investors for additional capital are not within § 1145(a)(1) and typically rely on the § 1145(a)(3) provision for "portfolio sales" by the reorganized debtor of unused authorized securities, or on a private placement under Section 4(a)(2) of the Securities Act.

Third, the antifraud provisions of the federal securities laws — principally Section 10(b) of the Securities Exchange Act of 1934 and Rule 10b-5 thereunder — apply with full force to plan-issued securities. The § 1145 exemption is from registration, not from disclosure-based liability. The disclosure statement approved under § 1125 is the principal vehicle for satisfying the antifraud regime in the plan-confirmation context.

Blue Sky Law Interplay

Section 1145(a)(2) provides that "the offer of a security through any warrant, option, right to subscribe, or conversion privilege that was sold in the manner specified in paragraph (1) of this subsection, or the sale of a security upon the exercise of such a warrant, option, right, or privilege" is exempt. Section 1145(d) preempts state blue sky registration: a security issued under a Chapter 11 plan that qualifies for the § 1145 federal exemption is also exempt from state securities-act registration requirements.

The state-law preemption operates by virtue of the Supremacy Clause and the Code's express federal-exemption framework. State antifraud provisions are generally not preempted; only registration and qualification requirements are displaced. State securities administrators retain enforcement authority over fraudulent conduct in connection with plan-issued securities.

The interaction with the National Securities Markets Improvement Act of 1996 (NSMIA) is layered. NSMIA generally preempts state blue sky registration for "covered securities," a class that includes securities listed on national exchanges and certain other categories. Plan-issued securities that would not qualify as NSMIA-covered securities still receive state-law exemption through § 1145(d) directly.

SEC Participation in Chapter 11 Cases

Section 1109(a) provides that "the Securities and Exchange Commission may raise and may appear and be heard on any issue in a case under this chapter, but the Securities and Exchange Commission may not appeal from any judgment, order, or decree entered in the case." The provision recognizes the SEC's institutional interest in the protection of public investors and gives it a standing right to participate in Chapter 11 cases without proving party-in-interest status under § 1109(b).

The SEC's principal interventions in Chapter 11 practice address (i) the adequacy of disclosure statements under § 1125 where the debtor's securities are or will be publicly traded, (ii) the propriety of plan provisions that release non-debtor third parties from securities-fraud liability, (iii) plan provisions that exculpate or release officers and directors with potential securities-law exposure, and (iv) the appointment of an equity committee where existing equity may have a meaningful interest.

The disclosure-statement role overlaps but does not coincide with the antifraud regime. Section 1125 imposes a federal "adequate information" standard; the SEC may comment on whether the disclosure statement satisfies that standard with reference to the investor-protection considerations that animate the federal securities laws generally.

Non-Dischargeability of Securities-Fraud Claims Under Section 523(a)(19)

Section 523(a)(19) was added by the Sarbanes-Oxley Act of 2002 in response to corporate-fraud scandals of the early 2000s. The subsection excepts from individual discharge any debt that is:

"(A) for the violation of any of the Federal securities laws (as that term is defined in section 3(a)(47) of the Securities Exchange Act of 1934), any of the State securities laws, or any regulation or order issued under such Federal or State securities laws; or (B) for common law fraud, deceit, or manipulation in connection with the purchase or sale of any security; and (C) that results, before, on, or after the date on which the petition was filed, from (i) any judgment, order, consent order, or decree entered in any Federal or State judicial or administrative proceeding; (ii) any settlement agreement entered into by the debtor; or (iii) any court or administrative order for any damages, fine, penalty, citation, restitutionary payment, disgorgement payment, attorney fee, cost, or other payment owed by the debtor."

Two features of § 523(a)(19) distinguish it from other fraud-based non-dischargeability provisions. First, the predicate adjudication may occur "before, on, or after the date on which the petition was filed." A securities-fraud judgment entered post-petition is captured if the underlying conduct predates filing; the temporal asymmetry of ordinary fraud-discharge provisions does not apply.

Second, the provision sweeps both federal and state securities laws, and also reaches common-law fraud "in connection with the purchase or sale of any security." The "in connection with" language tracks Rule 10b-5 jurisprudence and is broadly construed.

The interaction with Chapter 11 corporate debtors is different. Section 1141(d)(6)(A) makes § 523(a)(2)(B) and (a)(19) claims against corporate debtors non-dischargeable in Chapter 11 to the extent owed to "a domestic governmental unit, or owed to a person as the result of an action filed under subchapter III of chapter 37 of title 31 [the False Claims Act] or any similar State statute." Private securities-fraud claims against corporate debtors remain subject to discharge under § 1141(d); only the carved-out governmental and qui tam categories survive.

Stockbrokers, Commodity Brokers, and Subchapter III

Section 109 of the Bankruptcy Code disqualifies certain securities-industry entities from filing in conventional chapters and channels them into specialized regimes. Section 109(b)(2) bars a "stockbroker" from Chapter 7 commercial liquidation in favor of Subchapter III of Chapter 7 (§§ 741–753), which provides for the orderly liquidation of stockbroker estates with special protection for customer property. Section 109(d) bars stockbrokers from Chapter 11 altogether.

Commodity brokers receive parallel treatment under Subchapter IV (§§ 761–767). Both subchapters give customer claims priority over general creditor claims with respect to "customer property" as statutorily defined, and both establish trustee duties oriented toward returning customer assets rather than maximizing distributions to general creditors.

The Securities Investor Protection Act of 1970 (SIPA), codified at 15 U.S.C. §§ 78aaa et seq., establishes a parallel liquidation regime for SIPC-member broker-dealers, administered by the Securities Investor Protection Corporation. A SIPA liquidation is initiated in federal district court and then transferred to bankruptcy court; the bankruptcy court applies SIPA-modified Bankruptcy Code provisions. Customer claims up to specified statutory limits receive SIPC insurance protection, paid from the SIPC fund.

Section 109(b)(2) also bars "domestic insurance companies" and "domestic banks" from Chapter 7, channeling them respectively into state insurance-receivership regimes and federal bank-receivership under the FDIC. Registered investment companies (mutual funds, closed-end funds) under the Investment Company Act of 1940 are not categorically barred from Chapter 11, but their reorganization is constrained by the Investment Company Act's substantive requirements, and the SEC's involvement under § 1109(a) is correspondingly more intensive.

Subordination of Securities-Damage Claims

Section 510(b) subordinates "a claim arising from rescission of a purchase or sale of a security of the debtor or of an affiliate of the debtor, for damages arising from the purchase or sale of such a security, or for reimbursement or contribution allowed under section 502 on account of such a claim" to all other claims and interests in the same class as the security, except that if the security is common stock, such claims have the same priority as common stock.

The provision implements a structural priority judgment: investors who suffered securities-fraud damages in connection with the purchase of a debtor's stock or bonds take their place in the capital structure at the level of the security they purchased, not at the senior-creditor level. The doctrine traces to the recognition that securities-fraud claimants assumed the equity-like risk associated with the security and should not be elevated above other holders of similar-class instruments.

The Supreme Court has not directly addressed § 510(b)'s scope. The leading court-of-appeals interpretation is the Third Circuit's analysis in In re Telegroup, Inc., 281 F.3d 133 (3d Cir. 2002), holding that the provision reaches any claim that "arises from" the purchase or sale of a security, including claims for breach of contract related to stock issuance and claims for fraud in connection with stock-based compensation arrangements.
