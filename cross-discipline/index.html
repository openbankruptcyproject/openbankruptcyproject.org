Cross-Discipline Interaction Primers

Bankruptcy is a federal forum that intersects with nearly every other area of substantive law. A bankruptcy filing can reshape a divorce-court support order, change the treatment of a wage-and-hour claim, alter the rights of an indenture trustee, suspend tax-collection enforcement, and bring trust assets into the property-of-the-estate analysis. These primers map the most common interaction points.

Why cross-discipline interactions matter

A bankruptcy petition does not merely consolidate financial claims. It triggers an automatic stay under 11 U.S.C. § 362, creates a bankruptcy estate under § 541, and overrides much of the procedural posture established in other forums. Practitioners who work in adjacent disciplines often encounter bankruptcy as an interruption to their primary matter without understanding how the Bankruptcy Code reorders priority, claim treatment, and enforcement.

The primers below are written for non-bankruptcy practitioners and informed pro se litigants. Each surveys the principal Code sections, exclusions, exceptions, and procedural touchpoints where the bankruptcy system collides with another body of law. None substitutes for case-specific advice from counsel admitted in the relevant district.

Practice-area primers

  • Bankruptcy and EmploymentTreatment of wage claims, WARN Act liability, employee benefit plans, executory employment contracts, and section 503(b) priority for post-petition wages.
  • Bankruptcy and Family LawDomestic-support obligations under sections 101(14A) and 523(a)(5), property settlements under section 523(a)(15), and stay exceptions for family-court proceedings.
  • Bankruptcy and SecuritiesSEC standing, section 1145 exemption from registration, indenture trustee claims, securities litigation stay practice, and subordination under section 510(b).
  • Bankruptcy and TaxPriority of tax claims under section 507(a)(8), dischargeability under section 523(a)(1), automatic-stay carve-outs for tax assessment, and section 505 determination procedure.
  • Bankruptcy and Trust LawSpendthrift trust treatment under section 541(c)(2), self-settled trust attacks, beneficial-interest valuation, and trustee duties when a beneficiary files.
For practitioners outside bankruptcy: The Bankruptcy Code reorders most priority and enforcement positions established in other forums. Reading the relevant primer before responding to a notice of bankruptcy filing usually prevents the most common procedural errors.
