Bankruptcy and Tax — Interaction Primer 2026

First published 2026-05-22/Version 0.1.0/CC-BY-4.0/status: draft

Abstract

Bankruptcy law and federal tax law overlap at almost every stage of a case. Federal income tax claims occupy a privileged position in the priority scheme under 11 U.S.C. § 507(a)(8) and are non-dischargeable in many circumstances under § 523(a)(1). Confirmation of a Chapter 11 or Chapter 12 plan can trigger a federal stamp-tax exemption under § 1146(a). Cancellation of indebtedness in bankruptcy is excluded from gross income under Internal Revenue Code § 108(a)(1)(A), but at the cost of tax-attribute reduction under § 108(b). Tax refunds attributable to pre-petition periods are property of the estate under § 541(a) and may be turned over to the trustee.

This primer summarizes the principal points at which the Bankruptcy Code interacts with the Internal Revenue Code and with state taxing authorities. It is general information for a non-specialist audience and does not constitute legal or tax advice.

Section 1146 Stamp-Tax Exemption on Plan Transfers

Section 1146(a) of the Bankruptcy Code provides that "the issuance, transfer, or exchange of a security, or the making or delivery of an instrument of transfer under a plan confirmed under section 1129 of this title, may not be taxed under any law imposing a stamp tax or similar tax." The exemption applies to deed-recording taxes, mortgage-recording taxes, and analogous transfer levies that state and local jurisdictions impose on the transfer of real property and other instruments.

The Supreme Court construed the temporal reach of the exemption in Florida Department of Revenue v. Piccadilly Cafeterias, Inc., 554 U.S. 33 (2008), holding that § 1146(a) applies only to transfers that occur after the plan is confirmed, not to pre-confirmation transfers. Pre-confirmation sales under § 363(b), whether or not later folded into a plan, are not within the exemption. The exemption's scope is narrow but useful: it can save the estate significant sums in real-estate-heavy reorganizations, and the savings flow through to creditor recoveries.

Practitioners structuring a Chapter 11 plan that contemplates real-property transfers should attend to the timing question. The transfer must occur "under a plan confirmed under section 1129" and the instrument of transfer must be tied to the confirmed plan, not to an earlier sale order.

Cancellation of Debt Income and IRC Section 108

Outside of bankruptcy, the discharge of indebtedness is generally a taxable event. Internal Revenue Code § 61(a)(11) includes "income from discharge of indebtedness" in gross income. A debtor who settles a $100,000 loan for $20,000 ordinarily recognizes $80,000 of cancellation-of-debt (COD) income.

IRC § 108(a)(1)(A) carves out a bankruptcy exclusion: COD income is excluded from gross income if the discharge "occurs in a title 11 case." A title 11 case means a case under the Bankruptcy Code in which the taxpayer is under the jurisdiction of the court and the discharge is granted by the court or is pursuant to a plan approved by the court. The exclusion applies to discharge in Chapter 7, 11, 12, and 13.

The exclusion is not free. IRC § 108(b) requires the debtor to reduce specified tax attributes by the amount of excluded COD income, in the following order: (1) net operating losses; (2) general business credits; (3) minimum tax credits; (4) capital loss carryovers; (5) basis in property; (6) passive activity loss and credit carryovers; and (7) foreign tax credit carryovers. The reduction is made as of the first day of the taxable year following the discharge year. The debtor may elect under § 108(b)(5) to apply the reduction first against property basis.

The interplay matters most acutely in Chapter 11 reorganizations of solvent businesses with substantial NOLs. The bankruptcy discharge protects the debtor from current taxation on the COD but consumes the very NOLs that would otherwise shelter future operating income. Tax counsel and bankruptcy counsel must coordinate plan design to optimize attribute preservation.

Dischargeability of Tax Debts: Sections 523(a)(1) and (a)(7)

The Bankruptcy Code does not extend the discharge to all tax claims. Section 523(a)(1) carves out three principal categories of non-dischargeable tax debt for individual debtors:

  • § 523(a)(1)(A): Taxes of the kind and for the periods specified in § 507(a)(3) or § 507(a)(8), whether or not a claim is filed or allowed. This sweep captures most recent income taxes, withholding taxes, and employment taxes within statutorily-defined lookback periods.
  • § 523(a)(1)(B): Taxes for which a required return (or equivalent report or notice) was not filed or was filed late and within two years before the petition date. This is the "late-filed return" exception, which has generated substantial circuit-level litigation over what counts as a "return."
  • § 523(a)(1)(C): Taxes with respect to which the debtor "made a fraudulent return or willfully attempted in any manner to evade or defeat such tax." Civil tax fraud findings transfer into the bankruptcy court for purposes of this exception.

Section 523(a)(7) addresses penalty claims. It excepts from discharge any debt "to the extent such debt is for a fine, penalty, or forfeiture payable to and for the benefit of a governmental unit, and is not compensation for actual pecuniary loss." The provision contains a safe harbor for tax penalties relating to a tax that is itself dischargeable and that was assessed more than three years before the petition; such penalties are dischargeable even if the predicate tax is non-dischargeable for other reasons. Interest tracks the underlying tax: dischargeable if the tax is dischargeable, non-dischargeable otherwise.

Practitioners should run a tax-dischargeability analysis on every individual case with material tax liability before filing. The three-year, two-year, and 240-day lookback periods under § 507(a)(8) interact with tolling provisions under § 507(a)(8) flush language and the Internal Revenue Code's statute-of-limitations rules.

Priority of Tax Claims Under Section 507

Section 507(a)(8) confers eighth-priority status on a defined set of unsecured tax claims of governmental units. The eighth priority is junior to administrative expenses, wage claims, contributions to employee benefit plans, and certain other categories, but is senior to general unsecured claims. The principal sub-categories include:

  • Income or gross-receipts taxes for a taxable year ending on or before the petition date, for which a return was last due within three years of the petition;
  • Property taxes incurred before the commencement of the case and last payable without penalty within one year before the petition;
  • Trust-fund taxes (the employer's share of withholding) that the debtor was required to collect or withhold;
  • Employment taxes on wages, salaries, or commissions earned within three years of the petition;
  • Excise taxes on specified transactions within the same general lookback windows; and
  • Customs duties within applicable lookback periods.

In Chapter 13, § 1322(a)(2) requires the plan to provide for the full payment of all priority claims unless the holder agrees to different treatment. Eighth-priority tax claims must therefore be paid in full over the plan term. In Chapter 11, § 1129(a)(9)(C) requires deferred cash payments equal to the present value of priority tax claims over a period not exceeding five years from the petition date.

Secured tax claims (typically tax liens recorded before the petition) ride outside the § 507 priority scheme and are addressed under the secured-claim provisions of §§ 506 and 1129(b). The interaction between recorded tax liens and the debtor's exemptions, particularly under state homestead law, is a recurring source of complexity in consumer cases.

Tax Refunds as Property of the Estate

Section 541(a)(1) defines property of the estate to include "all legal or equitable interests of the debtor in property as of the commencement of the case." Pre-petition tax refunds, including refunds of taxes withheld during the year of filing for the pre-petition portion of that year, are property of the estate.

In Segal v. Rochelle, 382 U.S. 375 (1966), decided under the Bankruptcy Act of 1898, the Supreme Court held that a tax-loss-carryback refund was property of the estate even though the right to the refund did not mature until after filing. The principle carried forward into the Bankruptcy Code through the broad § 541(a)(1) definition and the Supreme Court's general directive in United States v. Whiting Pools, Inc., 462 U.S. 198 (1983), that the estate's reach is to be construed broadly.

For individual debtors, the practical result is that the Chapter 7 trustee may demand turnover of the pre-petition portion of any tax refund received for the year of filing. The portion is typically computed pro rata by reference to the petition date within the tax year. The debtor may exempt some portion of the refund under federal or state exemption schemes (a wildcard exemption is the most common shelter), but any unexempted portion belongs to the estate.

In Chapter 13, tax refunds are generally treated as disposable income subject to the plan; many courts and districts have local rules or standing orders requiring debtors to turn over tax refunds above a threshold each year of the plan.

The Automatic Stay and Tax Collection Activity

The automatic stay of § 362(a) halts most tax-collection activity by the IRS and state taxing authorities upon the petition. Levies, garnishments, lien recordings, and collection-due-process proceedings are stayed. Section 362(b)(9) carves out a limited set of permissible activities: the issuance of a notice of tax deficiency, an audit, a demand for tax returns, and the assessment of any tax. The IRS may continue to determine and assess tax liability during the case; it simply cannot collect outside the bankruptcy process.

Section 505(a) confers concurrent jurisdiction on the bankruptcy court to determine the amount or legality of any tax. The debtor or the trustee may invoke § 505 to litigate disputed pre-petition tax liability in bankruptcy court rather than in the Tax Court or in a federal district court refund suit. The choice has strategic implications for evidence, discovery, and appellate review.
