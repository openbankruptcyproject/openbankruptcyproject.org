KRPC 7.2 / MRPC 4-7.2: Advertising

The architecture of permitted advertising media, the structured exceptions for payment connected to recommendations, and the name-and-address attribution requirement.

Verbatim text of KRPC 7.2 (Kansas)

(a) Subject to the requirements of Rules 7.1 and 7.3, a lawyer may advertise services through written, recorded or electronic communication, including public media.

(b) A lawyer shall not give anything of value to a person for recommending the lawyer's services except that a lawyer may
(1) pay the reasonable costs of advertisements or communications permitted by this Rule;
(2) pay the usual charges of a legal service plan or a not-for-profit or qualified lawyer referral service;
(3) pay for a law practice in accordance with Rule 1.17; and
(4) refer clients to another lawyer or a nonlawyer professional pursuant to an agreement not otherwise prohibited under these Rules that provides for the other person to refer clients or customers to the lawyer, if (i) the reciprocal referral agreement is not exclusive, and (ii) the client is informed of the existence and nature of the agreement.

(c) Any communication made pursuant to this Rule shall include the name and office address of at least one lawyer or law firm responsible for its content. Kan. Sup. Ct. R. 226, KRPC 7.2.

Missouri Rule 4-7.2

Missouri Rule 4-7.2 follows the same structure, with detailed elaboration in subdivisions addressing specific media and disclosure requirements. Both rules track ABA Model Rule 7.2. The 2018 Model Rule revisions reorganized parts of the former 7.2 framework; jurisdictional adoptions vary in detail. Practitioners should consult the current Kansas and Missouri text and any state bar guidance for current-form requirements.

What 7.2 enables

Rule 7.2 is the affirmative authorization for lawyer advertising. Read with Rule 7.1, it establishes that lawyers may advertise their services across any medium of communication, subject to the truthful, non-misleading standard of 7.1 and the direct-contact constraints of 7.3. The rule's architecture has three pillars:

  • Subdivision (a) authorizes advertising through written, recorded, or electronic communication, including public media. The medium-neutral phrasing is deliberate; the rule does not bar any particular advertising medium.
  • Subdivision (b) states the general restriction on payment to third parties for recommending lawyer services, then enumerates the exceptions that structure how such payment may permissibly occur.
  • Subdivision (c) imposes the attribution requirement: every 7.2 communication must include the name and office address of at least one lawyer or law firm responsible for its content.

Structure of the permitted advertising media

The medium-neutral authorization in 7.2(a) covers, without limitation:

  • Print: newspaper advertising, magazine advertising, brochures, directory listings, mailers
  • Broadcast: television commercials, radio spots, podcast sponsorships
  • Outdoor: billboards, transit advertising, bus stop displays
  • Internet and digital: firm websites, blog posts, social-media profiles and posts, online video, search-engine advertising, display advertising, sponsored content
  • Telephone directory: yellow-pages listings, online directory profiles
  • Office and signage: building signage, office collateral, presentation materials

Each medium is permitted; each is subject to the 7.1 truthful, non-misleading standard, the 7.3 limits on direct solicitation, and the 7.2(c) name-and-address attribution. The medium-neutral framing was a deliberate Model Rule choice intended to avoid having the rule become obsolete with each new technology.

Payment for recommendations: the structured exceptions

Subdivision (b) states a general restriction on giving anything of value to a person for recommending the lawyer's services, and then enumerates four exceptions that structure how value may permissibly flow in connection with recommendations. Each exception is itself a permitted commercial arrangement; together they form the affirmative payment architecture.

(b)(1) - Paying for advertising

The lawyer may pay the reasonable costs of advertisements or communications permitted by the rule. This exception covers all paid advertising: print, broadcast, digital, outdoor, sponsored content, search-engine advertising, and any other paid placement. Payment to an advertising vendor for placement of the lawyer's ad is permitted because the payment is for the advertising service, not for a recommendation.

(b)(2) - Lawyer referral services and legal service plans

The lawyer may pay the usual charges of a legal service plan or a not-for-profit or qualified lawyer referral service. A qualified lawyer referral service is one that meets the criteria of the state bar or other authority empowered to qualify such services. The exception affirmatively authorizes participation in:

  • Bar-sponsored lawyer referral services that route inquiries to participating member lawyers
  • Legal service plans (group legal plans, employer-sponsored legal benefits, prepaid plans) in which the lawyer participates as a panel member
  • Not-for-profit referral services operated by community organizations, legal-aid affiliates, or similar entities

The exception is structured around the institutional character of the referring entity, not around the absence of payment; participating lawyers commonly pay membership fees, per-referral fees, or percentage-based participation costs, and these payments are within the exception.

(b)(3) - Sale of a law practice

The lawyer may pay for a law practice in accordance with Rule 1.17. This exception accommodates the transfer of a practice from a retiring or relocating lawyer to a purchasing lawyer, where the purchase price reflects in part the value of the practice's existing client relationships. Rule 1.17 imposes detailed conditions on practice sales (sale of an entire area of practice, notice to clients, fee limits) that operate as the substantive framework within which 7.2(b)(3) applies.

(b)(4) - Reciprocal referral arrangements

The lawyer may enter a reciprocal referral arrangement with another lawyer or with a nonlawyer professional, subject to two conditions: the agreement must not be exclusive, and the client must be informed of the existence and nature of the agreement. The exception affirmatively authorizes:

  • Lawyer-to-lawyer reciprocal referral relationships across complementary practice areas (e.g., a business litigation practice and a transactional tax practice)
  • Lawyer-to-nonlawyer reciprocal referrals with related professionals (e.g., a bankruptcy practice and a credit counselor, a personal-injury practice and a medical provider, an estate-planning practice and a financial planner)

The non-exclusivity condition ensures the arrangement does not foreclose the participants from working with other professionals; the disclosure condition ensures the client knows the structural relationship behind the recommendation.

Reading (b) as architecture

The four (b) exceptions are not loopholes; they are the framework. The (b) structure recognizes four legitimate commercial channels through which value flows in connection with lawyer marketing, and authorizes each on its own terms. Paying an advertising vendor is permitted because the payment is for advertising. Paying a qualified referral service is permitted because the service operates within a regulated framework. Paying for a practice is permitted because Rule 1.17 supplies the substantive guardrails. Reciprocal referrals are permitted on the non-exclusive, disclosed terms the rule specifies. Each is a designed commercial structure, not an exception to a default prohibition.

The name-and-address attribution requirement

Subdivision (c) requires every communication made pursuant to the rule to include the name and office address of at least one lawyer or law firm responsible for its content. The attribution requirement performs three functions:

  • Accountability: A recipient who has a question, a concern, or a complaint can identify the lawyer responsible for the communication.
  • Regulatory traceability: A disciplinary authority reviewing a communication can identify the lawyer responsible for ensuring 7.1 and 7.2 compliance.
  • Anti-deception baseline: The requirement that the communication identify a real lawyer at a real address prevents the use of anonymous or untraceable marketing channels for lawyer services.

The requirement is satisfied by including the lawyer or firm name and the office street address. A website URL or email address alone does not satisfy the requirement; a physical office address must appear. The requirement is satisfied if the address is for any office of the firm (a multi-office firm may use a single principal-office address).

Two pattern situations

Digital advertising campaign

A firm running a search-engine advertising campaign for a consumer practice area is engaged in 7.2 advertising. Each ad must comply with 7.1 (truthful, non-misleading content), must not constitute prohibited direct contact under 7.3, must include the firm name and office address (typically through the linked landing page, which itself qualifies as the communication for attribution purposes), and may permissibly involve payment to the advertising platform under 7.2(b)(1). Reciprocal referral arrangements with credit-counseling services or other related professionals operate under 7.2(b)(4), with disclosure to the referred client.

Bar referral panel participation

A lawyer who joins a state bar lawyer referral panel is paying for participation in a qualified lawyer referral service. The participation fee, the per-referral fee, and any percentage charge taken by the bar from successful matches are all within 7.2(b)(2). The bar's published guidance typically describes how the program qualifies as a "lawyer referral service" under the rule, and conscientious participation requires reviewing that guidance and maintaining membership in good standing.

Special-purpose communications

The rule applies to communications that promote the lawyer's services; it does not generally reach internal client communications, communications to other lawyers about co-counsel arrangements, court filings, and similar non-marketing speech. Statements made in the course of representing a client (such as a press statement issued in connection with active litigation) are generally governed by Rules 3.6 and 3.8, not by Rule 7.2, though where the statement crosses into promotion of the lawyer's services it can become subject to 7.2 as well.

Practical impact

Rule 7.2 enables lawyer advertising as a settled feature of legal-services markets. Two recurring observations:

  • The medium is open; the substance is regulated. A lawyer may advertise in any medium subject to the 7.1 content standard. The rule does not require pre-approval, does not restrict the medium, and does not require a particular format for the ad itself.
  • Payment exceptions structure the commercial environment. The four (b) exceptions are the framework within which paid marketing, panel participation, practice transitions, and reciprocal referrals operate. Each is a recognized commercial channel that the rule explicitly authorizes on its specified terms.
