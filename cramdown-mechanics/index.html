11 U.S.C. Section 1129(b) Chapter 11 Cramdown

Cramdown Mechanics Under Section 1129(b)

The fair-and-equitable test for secured, unsecured, and equity classes; the absolute priority rule; the three sub-paths of Section 1129(b)(2)(A) for secured cramdown; and the new-value corollary after LaSalle.

What cramdown does

Cramdown is the mechanism by which a Chapter 11 plan can be confirmed over the dissent of one or more classes of creditors or interest holders. Without cramdown, Section 1129(a)(8) would require every impaired class to accept the plan; in practice no large case could be confirmed because at least one impaired class almost always dissents. 11 U.S.C. Section 1129(b) permits confirmation despite dissent if two conditions are met: at least one impaired class has accepted the plan (the impaired-accepting-class requirement, sometimes called the "consenting class" requirement, derived from Section 1129(a)(10)), and the plan is "fair and equitable" and "does not discriminate unfairly" with respect to each dissenting impaired class.

The phrase "fair and equitable" is a term of art with class-specific content. Section 1129(b)(2) supplies a non-exhaustive definition for three categories - secured claims, unsecured claims, and interests - and the case law has refined what each category requires.

The unfair-discrimination prong

The "does not discriminate unfairly" prong of Section 1129(b)(1) is distinct from "fair and equitable" but is often discussed alongside it. The prong compares treatment among classes of similar priority. A plan that pays one class of unsecured creditors 80 cents on the dollar while paying another class of equal-priority unsecured creditors 5 cents on the dollar must justify the disparity by reference to a legitimate basis - typically a difference in the legal nature of the claims (for example, a tort claim versus a trade claim) or a contribution the favored class is making to the reorganization.

Courts use various tests to evaluate unfair discrimination. Some apply a four-factor inquiry asking whether there is a reasonable basis for the discrimination, whether the debtor cannot confirm without it, whether the discrimination is proposed in good faith, and whether the degree of discrimination is proportional. Others apply a rebuttable presumption against discrimination where the disparity in treatment exceeds defined thresholds. The doctrine is unsettled and litigated heavily in cases involving separately classified trade claims, indemnity claims, and similarly situated bondholder pools.

Fair and equitable - secured classes: the three sub-paths

Section 1129(b)(2)(A) prescribes three sub-paths by which a plan can be fair and equitable with respect to a dissenting secured class. The drafter must pick one; the sub-paths are not freely interchangeable. The Supreme Court confirmed in RadLAX Gateway Hotel, LLC v. Amalgamated Bank, 566 U.S. 639 (2012), that the specific governs the general - a plan involving a sale of collateral must use the sale-specific sub-path and cannot relabel the transaction to escape its requirements.

Sub-path (i): retention of lien plus deferred cash payments

The class retains its lien on the collateral, and each holder receives deferred cash payments totaling at least the allowed amount of the secured claim with a present value, as of the effective date of the plan, equal to at least the value of the collateral. The present-value requirement is the cramdown-interest-rate problem analyzed in Till v. SCS Credit Corp., 541 U.S. 465 (2004), where a plurality of the Supreme Court endorsed a "formula" approach starting from the prime rate and adjusting upward for the risk of nonpayment. The Court's footnote 14 reserved the question whether an "efficient market" rate should govern in Chapter 11 - a reservation that has divided the circuits in the years since.

If the secured class has elected under Section 1111(b)(2), sub-path (i) is modified: the aggregate face amount of the payments must equal the entire allowed claim, while the present value need only equal the collateral value. The election thus adds a face-amount floor on top of the present-value floor.

Sub-path (ii): sale free and clear with credit-bid right

The collateral is sold free and clear of the lien under Section 363(k), with the secured creditor's right to credit-bid preserved, and the lien attaches to the proceeds. The credit-bid right - the right to bid the face amount of the secured claim rather than cash - is the secured creditor's structural protection against undervaluation by the auction process. The creditor whose collateral is being auctioned can always outbid an undervalued cash bid by bidding the full allowed claim amount, taking the collateral itself if no third party will pay fair value.

The credit-bid right may be capped "for cause" under Section 363(k), but the cause must be substantial. Lower-court rulings have capped credit bids where the secured creditor's lien is subject to a bona fide dispute, where the credit bid would chill the auction in ways that would impair recovery, or where the creditor has engaged in inequitable conduct. Routine cramdown sales preserve the credit bid at face value.

Sub-path (iii): indubitable equivalent

The class receives the "indubitable equivalent" of its secured claim. This is the catch-all path, drawn from Judge Learned Hand's 1935 opinion in In re Murel Holding Corp., 75 F.2d 941 (2d Cir. 1935). The indubitable-equivalent standard requires that the substitute consideration both compensate the creditor for the present value of its claim and assure the creditor of the safety of its principal. The doctrine is analyzed in depth in the indubitable-equivalent deep dive. After RadLAX, sub-path (iii) cannot be used as a workaround to strip the credit-bid right that sub-path (ii) provides; sales of collateral must go through sub-path (ii).

Fair and equitable - unsecured classes: the absolute priority rule

Section 1129(b)(2)(B) defines fair and equitable for a dissenting class of unsecured claims. The class must either receive property of a value, as of the effective date, equal to the allowed amount of its claims, or - if it receives less - no junior class may receive or retain any property "on account of" its junior claim or interest. This second branch is the absolute priority rule.

The absolute priority rule embeds the bankruptcy ordering of claims into plan confirmation. Equity holders cannot retain their shares if the unsecured class is being paid less than in full and dissents. The rule was a fixture of equity-receivership practice long before the 1978 Bankruptcy Code, traceable through Northern Pacific Railway Co. v. Boyd, 228 U.S. 482 (1913), and carried forward into Chapter X of the 1898 Act and then into Chapter 11.

The new-value corollary

The new-value corollary is the most contested doctrine in modern cramdown law. The corollary holds that equity holders who contribute new value - cash or property - to a reorganization may retain their interests over a dissenting unsecured class, on the theory that their retention is "on account of" the new contribution rather than "on account of" their pre-petition ownership.

The Supreme Court declined to decide whether the new-value corollary survived the 1978 Code in Norwest Bank Worthington v. Ahlers, 485 U.S. 197 (1988). It returned to the doctrine in Bank of America Nat'l Trust & Sav. Ass'n v. 203 N. LaSalle St. P'ship, 526 U.S. 434 (1999), and held that an exclusive new-value plan - one in which the debtor-equity holders proposed to retain their interests in exchange for a new contribution without exposing the opportunity to competing bids - violated the absolute priority rule. The Court emphasized that retaining equity through an uncompeted-for option constituted retention "on account of" the prior interest because the option itself had value that the equity holders received only because of their ownership position.

After LaSalle, a new-value plan must either expose the equity opportunity to the market through a competing-bid mechanism (such as a Section 363 auction) or allow other interested parties to propose a competing plan during the period the debtor proposes new-value retention. The Court did not formally adopt the new-value corollary; it left open the possibility that a properly market-tested new-value contribution might satisfy the absolute priority rule. Lower courts have generally accepted that a market-tested new-value plan can confirm, though the doctrine remains formally unresolved at the Supreme Court level.

Fair and equitable - equity classes

Section 1129(b)(2)(C) defines fair and equitable for a dissenting class of interests. The class must either receive property equal to the greatest of the allowed amount of any fixed liquidation preference, any fixed redemption price, or the value of the interest - or no class junior may receive or retain anything on account of its interest. The provision matters most in cases with preferred-stock classes or multi-tier equity capital structures, where the priorities among equity tranches mirror the priorities among debt tranches.

In single-tier-equity cases (the typical small-business Chapter 11), Section 1129(b)(2)(C) collapses into the practical question whether the equity class receives anything. If the equity class receives nothing and is therefore deemed to reject under Section 1126(g), the plan need not provide for its retention at all and the absolute priority rule is satisfied vacuously.

The impaired-accepting-class requirement

Section 1129(a)(10) requires that, if any class of claims is impaired under the plan, at least one impaired class of claims (other than insiders) must accept the plan. The requirement is jurisdictional in the sense that a plan without an accepting impaired class cannot be confirmed even if every other element of Section 1129 is satisfied.

The requirement constrains class-designation gerrymandering. A debtor cannot create an artificial impaired class - for example, by impairing a single trade claim by a token amount - solely to manufacture an accepting class. Courts evaluating manufactured-acceptance challenges look to whether the impairment is "real" and whether the classification serves legitimate plan purposes, with significant variance among the circuits. The Eighth Circuit's analysis in In re Windsor on the River Associates, 7 F.3d 127 (8th Cir. 1993), held that artificial impairment violates the good-faith requirement of Section 1129(a)(3). Other circuits have read the impaired-accepting-class requirement narrowly, accepting any real impairment as sufficient.

The valuation problem at the heart of cramdown

Every cramdown sub-path turns on a valuation question. Secured-class cramdown under sub-path (i) requires valuing the collateral to set the present-value floor. Unsecured-class cramdown under the absolute priority rule requires valuing the reorganized enterprise to determine whether the unsecured class is in fact being paid less than in full. The indubitable-equivalent sub-path requires valuing whatever substitute consideration is being offered.

Valuation in Chapter 11 is litigated through dueling expert reports, with debtors typically pressing low collateral values and high enterprise values (to satisfy unsecured creditors with smaller stakes while limiting secured cramdown payments) and creditors pressing the opposite. Courts apply weighted-average-cost-of-capital and discounted-cash-flow methodologies for going concerns, comparable-sales and capitalization-rate analyses for real estate, and orderly-liquidation-value or replacement-cost analyses for equipment. The valuation date is generally the effective date of the plan, which adds a forecasting dimension to disputes confirmed shortly before plan effectiveness.

Sequencing under Section 1129(b). Cramdown is conditional, not automatic. The plan proponent must first satisfy every requirement of Section 1129(a) except (a)(8) (universal class acceptance). The proponent must then prove (1) at least one impaired class accepted, (2) the plan does not discriminate unfairly against dissenting classes, and (3) the plan is fair and equitable as to each dissenting class under the class-specific tests of Section 1129(b)(2). Failure on any element defeats confirmation; the debtor's remedy is amendment and re-solicitation or dismissal.
