11 U.S.C. Section 552 - Postpetition Effect of Security Interest

The Code's general rule cutting off pre-petition floating liens at the petition date, the carefully drafted proceeds-and-products exception, and the equitable "equities of the case" override.

What Is Section 552?

Section 552 controls the post-petition reach of a pre-petition security interest. The default rule is severe: property the debtor acquires after the petition date is free of any security interest created by a pre-petition agreement. This rule directly attacks the so-called "floating lien" - the Article 9 device by which a lender obtains a continuously refreshed security interest in the debtor's inventory and receivables as they turn over. Without Section 552, a pre-petition floating-lien lender would automatically acquire a lien on the debtor's post-petition inventory and receivables, often capturing the entire going-concern value generated by the bankruptcy estate's continued operations.

The provision is paired with a narrow but commercially essential exception: where the post-petition property is the proceeds, products, offspring, or profits of pre-petition collateral, the pre-petition security interest follows the value across the petition date.

Official citation: 11 U.S.C. § 552

The General Rule: Section 552(a)

Section 552(a) provides: "Except as provided in subsection (b) of this section, property acquired by the estate or by the debtor after the commencement of the case is not subject to any lien resulting from any security agreement entered into by the debtor before the commencement of the case."

The rule is categorical for non-proceeds collateral. A pre-petition security agreement granting a lien in "all inventory now owned or hereafter acquired" does not, on its own terms, encumber inventory the debtor acquires after the petition. The "hereafter acquired" language is rendered ineffective as to the post-petition acquisition by federal bankruptcy law, overriding state-law floating-lien doctrine.

The Proceeds Exception: Section 552(b)(1)

Section 552(b)(1) provides: "Except as provided in sections 363, 506(c), 522, 544, 545, 547, and 548 of this title, if the debtor and an entity entered into a security agreement before the commencement of the case and if the security interest created by such security agreement extends to property of the debtor acquired before the commencement of the case and to proceeds, products, offspring, or profits of such property, then such security interest extends to such proceeds, products, offspring, or profits acquired by the estate after the commencement of the case to the extent provided by such security agreement and by applicable nonbankruptcy law, except to any extent that the court, after notice and a hearing and based on the equities of the case, orders otherwise."

Two conditions: (1) the pre-petition security agreement must by its terms reach proceeds, products, offspring, or profits of the pre-petition collateral, and (2) the property at issue must in fact qualify as proceeds, products, offspring, or profits under applicable non-bankruptcy law (typically UCC Article 9 for personal-property collateral).

The Rents Exception: Section 552(b)(2)

Section 552(b)(2) adds a parallel exception for rents and hotel revenues: "If the debtor and an entity entered into a security agreement before the commencement of the case and if the security interest created by such security agreement extends to property of the debtor acquired before the commencement of the case and to amounts paid as rents of such property or the fees, charges, accounts, or other payments for the use or occupancy of rooms and other public facilities in hotels, motels, or other lodging properties, then such security interest extends to such rents and such fees, charges, accounts, or other payments acquired by the estate after the commencement of the case to the extent provided in such security agreement," subject to the equities-of-the-case override.

Section 552(b)(2) was added in 1994 to overrule judicial decisions that had treated hotel room revenues as accounts (subject to 552(a) cutoff) rather than as rents (preserved by 552(b)). The amendment is critical to single-asset real-estate cases and hospitality bankruptcies, where the going-concern value depends almost entirely on continuing room revenue.

The "Equities of the Case" Override

Both Section 552(b)(1) and 552(b)(2) contain an "except to any extent that the court, after notice and a hearing and based on the equities of the case, orders otherwise" clause. This equitable override allows the court to reduce or eliminate the secured creditor's claim to post-petition value where the estate, the debtor's labor, the debtor's professionals' work, or other unencumbered sources contributed meaningfully to the value of the proceeds, products, offspring, profits, or rents.

The classic application: the lender holds a pre-petition lien on accounts receivable; post-petition, the estate ships product (manufactured in part from raw materials purchased post-petition with unencumbered cash, plus post-petition labor, post-petition utilities, etc.), generating new receivables. The lender claims those receivables as proceeds of pre-petition inventory. The court may, under the equities clause, allocate some of that value away from the lender to compensate the estate for its post-petition contribution to the collateral's enhancement.

Interaction with Cash Collateral and DIP Financing

Section 552's cutoff of post-petition floating liens explains why secured lenders so aggressively seek replacement liens and adequate protection in cash-collateral and DIP-financing orders. Under Section 363, the estate cannot use cash collateral without consent or court order; the lender's leverage to demand replacement liens on post-petition assets stems precisely from the fact that, but for the negotiated grant, Section 552(a) would deprive the lender of any post-petition reach. DIP-financing orders typically include extensive provisions cross-collateralizing the pre-petition and post-petition exposure, with court approval of the specific 552(b) treatment of proceeds and the waiver or limitation of equities-of-the-case challenges.

Common Procedural Postures

  • Cash-collateral motions where the debtor seeks authority to use receivables collections that the lender claims as proceeds of pre-petition inventory;
  • Adversary proceedings to determine the validity, priority, and extent of a security interest in post-petition assets;
  • Equities-of-the-case challenges by the trustee or unsecured creditors' committee seeking to reduce the lender's recovery on post-petition proceeds;
  • Confirmation objections where the secured lender's plan treatment turns on the scope of its 552(b) reach;
  • Plan effective-date litigation over whether specific assets are pre-petition collateral, proceeds of pre-petition collateral, or post-petition free assets.
