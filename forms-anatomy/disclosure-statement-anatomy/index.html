Anatomy of a Chapter 11 Disclosure Statement

The mandatory elements under 11 U.S.C. Section 1125(b), the required content under Federal Rule of Bankruptcy Procedure 3017, the treatment-of-claims sections, plan-feasibility analysis, liquidation analysis, risk factors, valuation analysis, and voting procedures.

What a disclosure statement is

A Chapter 11 disclosure statement is the document a plan proponent must distribute to creditors and equity-security holders entitled to vote on a plan of reorganization before solicitation of acceptances can begin. Its statutory purpose, fixed by 11 U.S.C. Section 1125(b), is to provide "adequate information" - defined by Section 1125(a)(1) as information "of a kind, and in sufficient detail, as far as is reasonably practicable in light of the nature and history of the debtor and the condition of the debtor's books and records" that would enable "a hypothetical investor of the relevant class" to make an informed judgment about the plan.

The disclosure statement is not the plan; it is the explanatory document that accompanies the plan and persuades (or fails to persuade) the parties who will vote. The plan itself is a separate document setting forth the legal effect of confirmation. The two documents are typically filed together and processed sequentially: the court must approve the disclosure statement before the plan proponent may solicit votes on the plan.

Mandatory elements under Section 1125

Section 1125(a)(1) provides the adequate-information standard but leaves the content unenumerated; courts apply a non-exclusive checklist developed in the case law. The checklist most often referenced derives from In re Metrocraft Pub. Servs., Inc., 39 B.R. 567 (Bankr. N.D. Ga. 1984), and similar early decisions; the elements have since been substantially codified for Subchapter V cases at 11 U.S.C. Section 1190 and reflected in local-rule disclosure-statement checklists.

The core mandatory elements are: (1) the events leading to the bankruptcy filing; (2) a description of the debtor's business, organization, and management; (3) the status of pending litigation, including potential claims against insiders and avoidance actions; (4) the source of information used to prepare the disclosure statement; (5) projected financial information sufficient to permit evaluation of feasibility; (6) the assets and liabilities of the debtor; (7) the relationship of the debtor to affiliates and insiders; (8) accounting and valuation methods used; (9) future management of the debtor; (10) summary and analysis of the plan; (11) the estimated administrative expense burden; (12) collectability of any accounts receivable; (13) tax consequences to the debtor and to creditors; and (14) a liquidation analysis showing the value creditors would receive in a hypothetical Chapter 7 liquidation.

Required content under FRBP 3017

Federal Rule of Bankruptcy Procedure 3017 governs the procedural processing of the disclosure statement. Key procedural requirements: the proponent files the proposed disclosure statement and serves it on the U.S. Trustee, debtor, creditors' committee, indenture trustees, and the Securities and Exchange Commission (where relevant). The court fixes a hearing on the adequacy of the disclosure statement; objections must be filed at least seven days before the hearing. Following approval, the proponent transmits the approved disclosure statement, plan, and ballot to all parties entitled to vote, with a court-fixed deadline for return of ballots.

FRBP 3017(d) governs ballot solicitation: the ballot must identify the class to which the creditor's claim has been assigned, the amount of the claim deemed allowed for voting purposes, the alternatives presented (accept or reject), and the deadline for return. FRBP 3017(e) authorizes the court to designate a balloting agent for large cases; the agent receives, tallies, and certifies the ballots to the court.

Treatment of claims and interests sections

The treatment-of-claims section is the operative substance of the plan as summarized in the disclosure statement. It identifies each class of claims and interests and describes how each class will be treated under the plan. Classes are required by Section 1122 to be composed of claims or interests "substantially similar" to others in the same class. The conventional class structure in a corporate Chapter 11 case runs: administrative expense claims (unclassified, paid in full at confirmation); priority tax claims (unclassified); secured claims (often each secured creditor in its own class); unsecured priority claims (e.g., wage claims, deposit claims); general unsecured claims (often a single large class); subordinated claims (e.g., damages from rescission of equity securities under Section 510(b)); and equity interests.

For each class, the disclosure statement must state whether the class is impaired (its rights are altered by the plan), the proposed treatment (lump sum, installment payments, equity conversion, reinstatement, or other), the estimated recovery percentage, and the source of funds for that recovery. Unimpaired classes are deemed to accept the plan and do not vote. Impaired classes whose treatment is "deemed rejection" (no consideration provided) similarly do not vote and the plan must satisfy cramdown standards under Section 1129(b) as to those classes.

Plan-feasibility analysis

The feasibility analysis demonstrates that the plan is "not likely to be followed by the liquidation, or the need for further financial reorganization, of the debtor" - the standard set by Section 1129(a)(11). The analysis typically takes the form of projected financial statements (balance sheet, income statement, cash flow statement) for a period of three to five years post-confirmation, supported by narrative explanation of assumptions and sensitivities.

The projections must show that the reorganized debtor will generate sufficient cash flow to make the payments required under the plan as they come due. Significant assumptions - revenue growth rates, gross margin, capital expenditure, working-capital cycle, refinancing of exit-financing maturities - should be stated explicitly so creditors can evaluate them. Sensitivity analysis (alternative-case projections showing the impact of plan-breaking variances) is good practice and is sometimes required by court order in larger cases.

Liquidation analysis

The liquidation analysis demonstrates that each class of impaired creditors will receive under the plan at least as much as they would receive in a hypothetical Chapter 7 liquidation - the "best interests test" set by Section 1129(a)(7). The analysis takes the form of a balance sheet that estimates the orderly-liquidation value of the debtor's assets, subtracts the costs of administering a Chapter 7 case (Chapter 7 trustee fees, professional fees, wind-down costs), and allocates the net proceeds in order of statutory priority.

The output is a per-class recovery percentage in liquidation, compared in the disclosure statement against the per-class recovery percentage under the plan. If any impaired class would receive more in liquidation than under the plan, the plan fails the best-interests test as to that class and cannot be confirmed without that class's affirmative acceptance.

Risk factors section

The risk-factors section identifies the principal risks that could prevent realization of the projected outcomes. The required candor is real; a risk factor section that omits material adverse contingencies invites disapproval of the disclosure statement and, in subsequent securities litigation, can found liability for misrepresentation in the issuance of new equity or debt instruments under the plan.

Common risk-factor categories in a Chapter 11 disclosure statement: (1) failure of the plan to be confirmed (alternative outcomes if confirmation is denied); (2) inability to obtain exit financing on the assumed terms; (3) operational risks (loss of key customers, regulatory changes, supply disruptions); (4) litigation risks (pending claims not provided for in the projections); (5) tax risks (uncertainty about the treatment of cancellation-of-indebtedness income, net-operating-loss preservation under Section 382 of the Internal Revenue Code); (6) market risks (interest-rate, commodity-price, foreign-exchange exposure); and (7) execution risks specific to the plan structure.

Valuation analysis section

Where the plan distributes new equity or new debt securities, the disclosure statement must include a valuation analysis estimating the value of those securities. The valuation is typically prepared by the proponent's financial advisor and presents three principal methodologies: (1) discounted cash flow (DCF) based on the projections in the feasibility analysis; (2) trading multiples of comparable public companies; and (3) precedent transactions in comparable industries. The three methods yield a valuation range; the analysis selects a point estimate or narrower range and explains the selection.

Valuation matters because the distribution-percentage figures in the treatment sections often depend on it. If new equity is distributed in satisfaction of a claim, the recovery percentage is the value of the equity divided by the claim amount. Disputes over valuation can drive disputes over plan confirmation; an unsecured-creditor class arguing the new equity is worth more than the proponent's valuation will simultaneously argue that the plan undervalues its recovery and that confirmation should be denied or modified.

Voting procedures

The voting-procedures section explains how the recipient should complete and return the ballot. It identifies the class to which the recipient's claim has been assigned; the deadline for return; the address (postal, e-mail, or balloting-agent portal) for return; the contact information for the balloting agent; the rule for spoiled or untimely ballots; and the disposition of ballots returned without a vote indicated. Confirmation requires that at least one impaired class accept the plan (Section 1129(a)(10)), so the voting procedures are not pro forma - they generate the data on which confirmation depends.

A class is deemed to accept the plan if creditors holding at least two-thirds in amount and more than one-half in number of the allowed claims of that class actually voting affirmatively vote in favor (Section 1126(c)). Equity classes accept if creditors holding at least two-thirds in amount of the allowed interests actually voting affirmatively vote in favor (Section 1126(d)). Non-voting creditors are not counted toward either denominator.

The disclosure statement is the document that drives the vote. Plan terms are determinative of legal effect, but the disclosure statement is what creditors actually read. A plan with strong economics described by a disclosure statement that fails the adequate-information standard will not be solicited; conversely, a marginal plan presented through a fully candid disclosure statement may attract enough acceptances to clear cramdown thresholds.
