Assumption and Rejection

Section 365 Executory Contracts

Section 365 gives the trustee or debtor-in-possession a powerful triage tool: the ability to assume contracts and leases that benefit the estate, reject those that burden it, and (subject to limits) assign assumed contracts to third parties. The framework rests on the half-century-old Countryman definition of an executory contract and has spawned specialized rules for real estate, intellectual property, collective bargaining, and retiree benefits.

About section 365

The Bankruptcy Code does not define "executory contract." The accepted working definition comes from Vern Countryman's 1973 article: an executory contract is one under which the obligations of both parties are so far unperformed that the failure of either to complete performance would constitute a material breach excusing the performance of the other. The functional test is what matters: a contract is executory if rejection would relieve the estate of material unperformed obligations.

Assumption requires the estate to cure existing defaults (with limited exceptions for ipso facto and "non-monetary historical" defaults under section 365(b)(2)), to compensate for actual pecuniary loss, and to provide adequate assurance of future performance. Rejection is treated as a breach immediately before the petition date, generating a general unsecured rejection-damages claim under section 502(g). Rejection of certain contracts triggers special treatment: real-property leases are subject to the section 502(b)(6) cap on landlord damages; intellectual-property licenses are subject to the section 365(n) preservation right for licensees; and personal-property leases of motor vehicles are subject to the section 365(p) special rule.

The Code carves out four categories from the general assumption-rejection rules: section 365(c)(1) anti-assignment for personal-services contracts and non-delegable rights; section 365(c)(2) for financial accommodations; section 1113 for collective bargaining agreements (requiring negotiated modification before rejection); and section 1114 for retiree benefits (requiring a similar negotiation track for post-employment health and welfare benefits).

Section 365 deep-dives

  • Section 365 FrameworkThe Countryman definition of executory contract, the assumption requirements (cure, compensation, adequate assurance), the rejection-as-breach rule, and the rejection-damages claim under section 502(g).
  • Commercial Real Estate LeasesThe section 365(d)(4) 120-day assumption-or-rejection deadline for non-residential real estate, the section 502(b)(6) landlord-damages cap, and the assignment of shopping-center leases under section 365(b)(3).
  • Intellectual Property LicensesThe section 365(n) preservation right that protects non-debtor licensees on rejection, the Mission Product Holdings v. Tempnology trademark rule, and the assumption of executive software and patent licenses.
  • Section 1113 Collective Bargaining AgreementsThe pre-rejection procedural prerequisites under section 1113, the nine-factor Bildisco-superseded standard, and the timeline for negotiation, proposal, and court hearing.
  • Section 1114 Retiree BenefitsThe parallel section 1114 framework for modifying retiree health and welfare benefits in chapter 11, including the authorized representative requirement and the modification standard.
Deadline trap: Section 365(d)(4) gives the trustee or debtor-in-possession 120 days from the order for relief to assume or reject a non-residential real-property lease. The court can extend the deadline by 90 days for cause on motion filed before the original deadline. Further extensions require landlord consent. Missing the deadline operates as automatic rejection.
