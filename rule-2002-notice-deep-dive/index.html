Federal Rule of Bankruptcy Procedure 2002

Rule 2002: Notice Mechanics Deep-Dive

The 21-day baseline under Rule 2002(a), abbreviated-notice carve-outs under 2002(b), the section 341 meeting notice under 2002(d), interaction with Rule 7004 service, and mailing-list maintenance under 2002(g).

The architecture of Rule 2002

Federal Rule of Bankruptcy Procedure 2002 governs the notice mechanics that move a bankruptcy case from filing to confirmation, sale, or dismissal. Where Rule 7004 governs service in adversary proceedings and contested matters, Rule 2002 governs the broader category of notice to creditors, equity holders, the United States trustee, and other parties in interest. The two regimes interact: a paper served in a contested matter under Rule 7004 frequently also requires Rule 2002 notice to the broader case constituency, and one regime does not substitute for the other.

The rule is long and subsection-dense. The operative architecture, however, is straightforward: Rule 2002(a) sets the 21-day baseline; Rule 2002(b) requires longer notice for plan and disclosure-statement matters; Rule 2002(c) carves abbreviated notice for routine motions; Rule 2002(d) addresses equity-security-holder notice; Rule 2002(f) lists the specific events that trigger notice to all creditors; and Rule 2002(g) governs the address used for notice and the maintenance of the case mailing matrix.

The 21-day notice baseline under Rule 2002(a)

Rule 2002(a) requires the clerk - or, in some districts, the moving party - to give the debtor, the trustee, all creditors, and indenture trustees at least 21 days' notice by mail of the following events:

  • The meeting of creditors under 11 U.S.C. Section 341
  • A proposed use, sale, or lease of property of the estate other than in the ordinary course of business, unless the court for cause shown shortens the time or directs another method of giving notice
  • The hearing on approval of a compromise or settlement of a controversy other than approval of an agreement under Rule 4001(d)
  • The hearing on the dismissal of the case or the conversion of the case to another chapter
  • The time fixed to accept or reject a proposed modification of a plan
  • The hearing on a proposed modification of a Chapter 12 or Chapter 13 plan
  • The time fixed for filing proofs of claim under Rule 3003(c)
  • The time fixed for objection to a debtor's claim of exemptions

The 21-day period runs from the date of mailing, not the date of receipt. The mailing must be addressed to the address specified in the mailing-matrix rules of Rule 2002(g).

Abbreviated notice under Rule 2002(b) and (c)

Rule 2002(b) requires 28 days' notice to all creditors of the time fixed for filing objections to and the hearing on approval of a disclosure statement under 11 U.S.C. Section 1125 and confirmation of a Chapter 9, 11, or 12 plan. The 28-day window reflects the higher stakes and the need for creditors to evaluate a complex disclosure statement before objecting.

Rule 2002(c) provides important carve-outs from the 21-day baseline. Notice need not be sent to all creditors of every motion; instead, Rule 2002(c)(1) limits the notice obligation for routine matters - employment of professionals, certain fee applications, and stay-relief motions - to the entities the rule specifies (the debtor, trustee, U.S. trustee, and committee). Rule 2002(c)(2) authorizes the court to limit notice further "for cause shown" - the procedural device that supports "limited notice" motions in larger cases where mailing to every creditor would be prohibitively expensive and largely useless.

Practitioners should resist the assumption that Rule 2002(c) authorizes general truncation of notice. The carve-out is narrow and event-specific; matters affecting creditor distributions generally remain subject to the 21-day baseline notwithstanding 2002(c).

The section 341 meeting notice under Rule 2002(d)

Rule 2002(d) governs notice to equity security holders. In Chapter 11 cases involving a corporate or limited-liability debtor, equity holders are entitled to the same notice as creditors of the section 341 meeting, the time for filing objections to and the hearing on approval of a disclosure statement, the time for filing acceptances or rejections of a plan, and the hearing on confirmation.

The section 341 meeting notice itself - typically issued by the United States trustee or the clerk on Official Form 309 (with variants for each chapter) - communicates the meeting date, the deadline for filing proofs of claim, the deadline for objecting to discharge, and the deadline for objecting to claims of exemption. Each of those deadlines is independently load-bearing; an error in the 309 notice can become a due-process problem if a creditor later argues that the notice was insufficient to bar a late claim or a late discharge objection.

Interaction with Rule 7004 service

Rule 7004 governs service of summons and process in adversary proceedings, and Rule 9014 imports much of Rule 7004 into contested matters. Rule 2002, by contrast, governs notice in administrative contexts that do not require summons-and-complaint formality.

The interaction matters because a single event may trigger both regimes. A motion for sale free and clear under 11 U.S.C. Section 363(f), for example, is a contested matter; the motion must be served on the parties whose interests are to be extinguished under the Rule 7004 service rules incorporated by Rule 9014, and the broader case constituency is entitled to Rule 2002(a)(2) notice of the proposed sale. Substituting one for the other is procedurally defective. A movant who serves only by Rule 2002 mailing on a secured creditor whose lien is to be stripped fails the Rule 7004 service requirement; a movant who serves the secured creditor under Rule 7004 but omits the Rule 2002 mailing fails the broader notice requirement.

Mailing-list maintenance under Rule 2002(g)

Rule 2002(g) is the rule that determines where Rule 2002 notice is sent. The provision is structured around a hierarchy:

  • Rule 2002(g)(1) directs that notice be sent to the address listed by a creditor or indenture trustee on a request filed with the court, designating where notices should be sent
  • Rule 2002(g)(2) directs that, absent such a request, notice be sent to the address in the proof of claim filed by the creditor
  • Rule 2002(g)(3) directs that, absent both a request and a proof of claim, notice be sent to the address shown on the list of creditors or schedules
  • Rule 2002(g)(4) requires that, with respect to a notice of the section 341 meeting, notice be sent to the address shown on the list of creditors or schedules, regardless of any later-filed request or proof of claim

Rule 2002(g)(5) addresses the "preferred address" mechanism for certain large institutional creditors that file a notice of preferred address under 11 U.S.C. Section 342(f) - a creditor that registers a preferred address is entitled to notice at that address rather than the address shown on the schedules.

The mailing matrix is the operational substrate of Rule 2002. If the matrix is incomplete or out-of-date, the notice that issues under Rule 2002(a) is not effective, and the deadlines triggered by that notice may be unenforceable against a creditor who did not receive the notice the rule requires.

Notice to the United States trustee

Rule 2002(k) requires that the United States trustee receive copies of all notices sent under Rule 2002(a)(2) through (a)(8), (b), (f)(1), (f)(2), (f)(4), (f)(6), (f)(7), (q), and (p). The provision exists to preserve the U.S. trustee's monitoring role under 28 U.S.C. Section 586; a movant who serves creditors but omits the U.S. trustee creates a procedural defect that is independently raisable.

Notice by publication and equivalents

Rule 2002(l) authorizes the court, on motion or sua sponte, to direct notice by publication if it finds that notice by mail is impracticable or that it is desirable to supplement the notice. Publication is not a default; it is a court-authorized supplement triggered by a particularized showing. Service by electronic means is governed by Rule 9036 and is increasingly the operational reality - the National Creditor Registration Service operated by the federal bankruptcy courts is the principal mechanism by which sophisticated institutional creditors elect to receive Rule 2002 notice electronically.

Common procedural posture

Section 363 sale notice

A trustee proposes to sell estate property outside the ordinary course. Rule 2002(a)(2) requires 21 days' notice to all creditors. The trustee must mail the notice to every address on the Rule 2002(g) matrix and serve any creditor whose lien is to be extinguished under the Rule 7004 service rules. The notice must describe the property, the proposed price, and the deadline for objections.

Plan confirmation notice

A Chapter 11 debtor seeks confirmation of a plan. Rule 2002(b) requires 28 days' notice to all creditors of the disclosure-statement hearing and the confirmation hearing. The notice must specify the deadlines for filing objections, the deadlines for casting ballots, and the date and time of the hearing. Omission of any of these elements is correctable only by an amended notice that restarts the 28-day clock.

Practical impact

Rule 2002 is the rule most often violated unintentionally, because its requirements are dispersed across two dozen subsections and because the Rule 2002(g) mailing-matrix obligation is administrative rather than substantive. Defects in Rule 2002 notice rarely surface during the case; they surface when a creditor argues post-confirmation that the discharge or the sale order does not bind it because notice was insufficient. The remedy is reopening, a costly and time-consuming process that the precise compliance the rule requires at the front end would have avoided.

Notice and service are different regimes. Rule 2002 governs notice; Rule 7004 governs service. Compliance with one does not establish compliance with the other. A motion in a contested matter typically requires both, and the failure to satisfy either is independently fatal.
