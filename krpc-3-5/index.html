KRPC 3.5 / MRPC 4-3.5: Impartiality and Decorum of the Tribunal

Tribunal-interaction protocols - when ex parte contact is authorized, the juror-contact framework before and after discharge, and decorum during proceedings.

Tribunal-interaction protocols that 3.5 permits

Rule 3.5 is a protocol document for the lawyer's interaction with judges, jurors, and the tribunal generally. Read affirmatively, it identifies the channels through which counsel may properly contact a judicial officer, the conditions under which contact with jurors and prospective jurors is appropriate (including the substantially expanded contact authorized after discharge), and the standards of decorum that protect both the lawyer's effective advocacy and the tribunal's authority.

The rule operates as an authorization regime. When counsel follows the protocols - notice to opposing counsel, contact through the clerk, conduct within the rules of the tribunal - the resulting interaction is fully protected. The rule's narrow prohibitions exist to define the protocols, not to restrict the substantial communication the rules of procedure and judicial administration make available.

Verbatim text of KRPC 3.5 (Kansas)

A lawyer shall not:

(a) seek to influence a judge, juror, prospective juror or other official by means prohibited by law;

(b) communicate ex parte with such a person during the proceeding unless authorized to do so by law or court order;

(c) communicate with a juror or prospective juror after discharge of the jury if: (1) the communication is prohibited by law or court order; (2) the juror has made known to the lawyer a desire not to communicate; or (3) the communication involves misrepresentation, coercion, duress or harassment; or

(d) engage in conduct intended to disrupt a tribunal. Kan. Sup. Ct. R. 226, KRPC 3.5.

Missouri Rule 4-3.5

Missouri Rule 4-3.5 is substantively identical to KRPC 3.5 and tracks ABA Model Rule 3.5. The structure is the same: subdivision (a) reaches improper influence; subdivision (b) addresses ex parte contact during the proceeding (with the affirmative carve-in for contact authorized by law or court order); subdivision (c) governs post-discharge juror contact (preserving the substantial zone of permitted contact); subdivision (d) addresses tribunal disruption.

Judicial-contact framework under 3.5(b)

The ex parte prohibition in subdivision (b) is bounded by two important authorization clauses: contact "authorized by law" and contact authorized "by court order." Together they cover a substantial set of permitted interactions that counsel routinely engages in:

  • Emergency ex parte motions. Temporary restraining orders, ex parte seizure orders, and similar emergency relief are obtained through ex parte applications expressly authorized by procedural rules.
  • In camera review. When counsel submits material for the court's review outside the presence of opposing parties, the contact is authorized by the order directing the review.
  • Routine scheduling contact through the clerk. Most jurisdictions permit (and many require) counsel to coordinate scheduling with the court's clerk or judicial assistant.
  • Ministerial inquiries. Questions about the status of an order, the location of a filed paper, or local procedure are generally treated as authorized ministerial contact.
  • Settlement conferences with the judge. Where a court holds settlement conferences ex parte by design, the contact is authorized by the court's procedure.

The Comment to Model Rule 3.5 expressly recognizes that procedural rules and statutes authorize many forms of ex parte contact, and that the lawyer's duty is to operate within the authorized channels - notifying opposing counsel when notification is required, providing on-the-record submissions when required, and following the procedural framework the contact rests upon.

Improper-influence framework under 3.5(a)

Subdivision (a) prohibits seeking to influence a judge, juror, prospective juror, or other official "by means prohibited by law." The affirmative content is significant: every means of influence that is not prohibited by law remains available - oral argument, written briefing, evidentiary presentation, requests for clarification, motions for reconsideration, and every other procedural tool the system provides. Counsel's job is to influence the tribunal through these legitimate means; the rule simply requires that the means be legitimate.

Juror-contact framework

The rule addresses three temporal phases of juror contact:

Before the jury is empaneled (voir dire)

Voir dire is the structured opportunity counsel has to communicate with prospective jurors. The rule does not restrict the substance of voir dire questioning beyond the limits the tribunal imposes; counsel may explore prospective jurors' backgrounds, experiences, and views to the extent the court permits.

During the proceeding

Counsel may not communicate ex parte with sitting jurors during the proceeding, but again the limitation is one of channel rather than substance. Communication through the court's questions and instructions, written submissions of proposed jury instructions, and arguments in open court are all means by which counsel properly communicates with the jury during the proceeding.

After discharge of the jury

Subdivision (c) preserves a substantial zone of permitted post-discharge contact. Counsel may communicate with discharged jurors so long as the communication is not prohibited by law or court order, the juror has not indicated a desire not to communicate, and the communication does not involve misrepresentation, coercion, duress, or harassment. Permitted post-discharge contact includes:

  • Conversations to understand the jury's reasoning that may inform appellate review or future advocacy
  • Discussions about the presentation of evidence to inform future trial practice
  • Research into juror experience for academic or improvement purposes
  • Identification of grounds for new trial or motion-for-judgment-as-a-matter-of-law where appropriate

Affirmative read-out. The post-discharge juror-contact zone is substantial. Counsel who approaches discharged jurors respectfully, with prior court awareness where local practice requires it, and who honors any expressed wish not to talk, operates within the protected zone the rule preserves.

Decorum framework under 3.5(d)

Subdivision (d) addresses conduct "intended to disrupt a tribunal." The standard is intent-based. Vigorous argument, persistent objection, repeated motions for reconsideration, and forceful cross-examination are not "intended to disrupt" - they are intended to advocate. The Comment to Model Rule 3.5 makes the distinction explicit: the rule does not require the lawyer to be passive in the face of an adverse ruling or to abandon advocacy techniques because they are uncomfortable for the tribunal. Disruption is the deliberate impairment of the tribunal's functioning, not the inconvenience of zealous advocacy.

The Comment also recognizes that counsel may stand by an objection, may insist on the right to be heard, and may make a record for appellate review even over the tribunal's resistance. These are decorous activities in the relevant sense.

Common procedural posture

Emergency TRO application

Counsel files an application for a temporary restraining order on grounds requiring ex parte relief. The proceeding is authorized by Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 65(b) and analogous state rules. The ex parte presentation, including any in-camera disclosure of confidential material, is within the affirmative zone of 3.5(b).

Post-trial juror interview

After a verdict, counsel reaches out to a discharged juror to understand the jury's deliberations. The juror agrees to speak. No court order forbids the conversation, no rule of the jurisdiction prohibits it, and counsel conducts the interview with civility and without coercion. The conversation falls within the post-discharge zone 3.5(c) preserves.

Relationship to other rules

  • Rule 3.3 - Candor toward the tribunal; the lawyer's duty in any tribunal contact
  • Rule 3.5(c) - Read in conjunction with local rules on post-trial juror contact (many federal districts have local rules requiring leave of court)
  • Rule 4.4 - Respect for rights of third persons; harassment limitation parallels 3.5(c)(3)
  • Rule 8.4(d) - Conduct prejudicial to the administration of justice; severe disruption may also implicate
  • Judicial codes of conduct - The judge's parallel ethical regime governs the judge's side of any contact

Practical impact

The lawyer who follows the established protocols - notifying opposing counsel when contact requires notice, going through the clerk for scheduling, presenting emergency motions through the procedures the rules authorize, conducting voir dire within the court's framework, conducting post-discharge juror contact respectfully - operates entirely within the zone 3.5 protects. The rule's prohibitions reach a narrow set of conduct (improper influence, unauthorized contact, juror harassment, deliberate tribunal disruption) that is far outside ordinary practice.
