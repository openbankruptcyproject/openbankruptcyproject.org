11 U.S.C. Section 348 - Effect of Conversion

What happens when a bankruptcy case is converted from one chapter to another: deadlines, property of the estate, claims, exemptions, and the Supreme Court's Harris v. Viegelahn rule on undistributed plan payments.

What Is Section 348?

Section 348 controls what happens, doctrinally, when a bankruptcy case is converted from one chapter of the Code to another (most commonly Chapter 13 to Chapter 7, or Chapter 11 to Chapter 7). It is the procedural connective tissue that decides what stays and what changes when a debtor's case shifts gears: which deadlines reset, which property is in the converted estate, what the claims look like, and how the trustee in the new chapter handles money and assets accumulated under the old one.

Official citation: 11 U.S.C. § 348

The Petition Date Is Preserved: Section 348(a)

Section 348(a) is the foundational rule: conversion does not change the date of the filing of the petition, the date of commencement of the case, or the order for relief. The original petition date stays anchored. This matters for preference look-back periods, eligibility computations, fee-disclosure deadlines, the 180-day domicile rule under Section 522(b)(3)(A), and many other date-driven inquiries that look back from the petition.

Deadlines That Do Reset: Section 348(b)

Section 348(b) carves out specific Code provisions where the "order for relief" reference is replaced by "conversion." These include Section 701 (interim trustee), Section 702 (election of trustee), Section 727(a)(10) (waiver of discharge), and certain other Chapter 7 and Chapter 13 mechanics. Practically, conversion to Chapter 7 triggers a new Section 341 meeting under the converted Chapter 7 trustee, even though the underlying petition date is unchanged.

New Bar Dates and Deadlines: Section 348(c)

Section 348(c) provides that the conversion order itself triggers new proof-of-claim, Section 341 meeting, dischargeability complaint, and other operational deadlines under Bankruptcy Rule 1019. Creditors who appeared in the prior chapter generally must monitor the converted case for new bar dates.

Postpetition Claims: Section 348(d)

Section 348(d) addresses claims arising after the original petition but before conversion (other than administrative claims). These claims "shall be treated for all purposes as if such claim had arisen immediately before the date of the filing of the petition." This rule allows postpetition-but-preconversion creditors to participate in the converted Chapter 7 distribution as if they were prepetition creditors, except for taxes and other categories the statute excludes.

Termination of Trustee Services: Section 348(e)

Section 348(e) provides that conversion terminates the service of any trustee or examiner serving in the case before conversion. The new chapter brings a new trustee (in Chapter 7) or restores the debtor to debtor-in-possession status (in conversions to Chapter 11). The departing trustee retains the duty to file a final report and account.

Property of the Estate on Conversion from Chapter 13: Section 348(f)

Section 348(f) is the most-litigated subsection. When a Chapter 13 case is converted to another chapter, the property of the converted estate consists of property of the estate as of the date of filing of the petition that remains in the possession of or is under the control of the debtor on the date of conversion. Property acquired postpetition during the Chapter 13 case does not transfer to the converted Chapter 7 estate unless the conversion is in bad faith (348(f)(2)).

This rule rewards debtors who attempt Chapter 13 in good faith and ultimately convert; postpetition earnings, tax refunds based on postpetition wages, and assets purchased with postpetition wages stay with the debtor in the converted Chapter 7. It is also the foundation for the Supreme Court's analysis in Harris v. Viegelahn.

Harris v. Viegelahn and Undistributed Plan Payments

In Harris v. Viegelahn, 575 U.S. 510 (2015), the U.S. Supreme Court held that when a Chapter 13 case is converted to Chapter 7 in good faith, the Chapter 13 trustee must return to the debtor any undistributed plan payments held by the trustee at the time of conversion. The Court reasoned that Section 348(f) defines the converted estate to exclude postpetition wage payments and that requiring distribution of those funds to creditors after conversion would frustrate the statute.

This decision resolved a circuit split that had produced inconsistent outcomes for debtors converting Chapter 13 cases. The practical effect is that debtors who convert in good faith get their accumulated-but-undistributed plan payments back rather than having them swept into creditor distributions.

Major Doctrinal Cases

  • Harris v. Viegelahn, 575 U.S. 510 (2015) - Undistributed Chapter 13 plan payments return to the debtor on good-faith conversion.
  • In re Lybrook, 951 F.2d 136 (7th Cir. 1991) - Pre-348(f) decision later codified holding that property acquired postpetition in Chapter 13 stays with debtor on conversion.
  • In re Michael, 699 F.3d 305 (3d Cir. 2012) - Discussed Section 348(f)(2) bad-faith exception that brings postpetition assets into the converted estate.
  • In re Bobroff, 766 F.2d 797 (3d Cir. 1985) - Foundational pre-348(f) discussion of property-of-the-estate consequences on conversion.
  • In re Markosian, 506 B.R. 273 (B.A.P. 9th Cir. 2014) - Applied 348(f)(2) bad-faith exception following debtor concealment.
  • In re Calder, 973 F.2d 862 (10th Cir. 1992) - Discussed deadlines that restart on conversion versus those measured from the original petition.
