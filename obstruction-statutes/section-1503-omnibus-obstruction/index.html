18 U.S.C. Section 1503 - Omnibus Obstruction of Court Proceedings

Doctrinal overview of the omnibus clause: the pending-judicial-proceeding requirement, the corrupt-endeavor standard, the juror and officer protections, and the line between Section 1503 and Sections 1505 and 1512.

Home › Obstruction Statutes › 18 U.S.C. Section 1503 - Omnibus Obstruction

What Is Section 1503?

18 U.S.C. Section 1503 is the historic federal obstruction-of-justice statute, tracing its roots to the Act of March 2, 1831. It protects the integrity of pending judicial proceedings by criminalizing interference with grand or petit jurors, court officers, and - through its omnibus clause - any "corrupt" endeavor to influence, obstruct, or impede the due administration of justice. The statute carries a statutory maximum of ten years of imprisonment, with elevated maximums for offenses involving killing or attempted killing.

Official citation: 18 U.S.C. § 1503

Statutory Structure

Section 1503(a) contains two operative components. The first half enumerates specific protected persons and prohibited acts: corrupt or threatening conduct directed at a grand or petit juror, an officer in or of any court of the United States, a magistrate judge, or a commissioner; and retaliation against any such person on account of the performance of official duties. The second half - the omnibus clause - reaches anyone who "corruptly or by threats or force, or by any threatening letter or communication, influences, obstructs, or impedes, or endeavors to influence, obstruct, or impede, the due administration of justice." Section 1503(b) supplies the penalty schedule, with enhanced maximums where killing or attempted killing is involved.

The Pending-Judicial-Proceeding Requirement

The omnibus clause of Section 1503 reaches conduct affecting the "due administration of justice." Federal courts have consistently held that this language requires the existence of a pending federal judicial proceeding at the time of the obstructive conduct. A merely contemplated investigation, an inchoate matter that has not yet generated court process, or an executive-branch inquiry that is not itself a judicial proceeding does not satisfy the pending-proceeding element. See Pettibone v. United States, 148 U.S. 197 (1893), and modern circuit applications.

The pendency requirement is important because it marks the dividing line between Section 1503 and other obstruction statutes. Conduct affecting an executive-branch agency proceeding - for example, a regulatory inquiry, an SEC investigation, or other administrative adjudication - is generally outside Section 1503 and is instead governed by Section 1505. Conduct directed specifically at a witness, victim, or informant - whether or not a proceeding is pending - is generally governed by Section 1512.

The Nexus Element: United States v. Aguilar

In United States v. Aguilar, 515 U.S. 593 (1995), the Supreme Court held that the omnibus clause requires a "nexus" between the defendant's conduct and the pending judicial proceeding. The act must have "a relationship in time, causation, or logic with the judicial proceedings" such that it has the "natural and probable effect" of interfering with the due administration of justice. A defendant's conduct must be such that he or she knew, or had reason to know, that the conduct was likely to affect the proceeding.

The nexus requirement filters out conduct that is too attenuated from the pending matter to be considered an obstruction. Statements made to an investigator who may or may not later testify before a grand jury, without more, do not satisfy the nexus requirement. The defendant must contemplate the particular proceeding the conduct will affect.

"Corruptly" and the Mens Rea Inquiry

The omnibus clause requires that the defendant act "corruptly." Courts have generally defined this as acting "with an improper purpose, personally or by influencing another, including making a false or misleading statement, or withholding, concealing, altering, or destroying a document or other information." See model jury instructions in several circuits drawing on Section 1515(b), which defines "corruptly" for purposes of Section 1512.

Although Section 1515(b)'s definition applies textually to Section 1512, federal courts have frequently looked to it as a guide in the Section 1503 setting. The Supreme Court in Arthur Andersen LLP v. United States, 544 U.S. 696 (2005), emphasized that "knowingly... corruptly persuade" requires "consciousness of wrongdoing," and lower courts have applied similar reasoning to the "corruptly" element of Section 1503. The endeavor must be undertaken with the conscious purpose of obstructing justice; ordinary advocacy, the legitimate assertion of legal rights, and good-faith argumentation are not within the statute's reach.

"Endeavor" Verb: Inchoate Liability

Section 1503 reaches not only completed obstruction but also any "endeavor" to obstruct. The "endeavor" verb makes the statute an inchoate offense: the government need not prove that the defendant's conduct actually impeded the proceeding, only that it was undertaken with the purpose, and had the natural and probable effect, of obstruction. United States v. Russell, 255 U.S. 138 (1921), is the classic articulation. This feature distinguishes Section 1503 from many other federal offenses, which require completed acts.

Juror-Protection Clause

The first portion of Section 1503(a) provides specific protection for grand and petit jurors. It criminalizes corrupt or threatening attempts to influence, intimidate, or impede any grand or petit juror, as well as retaliation against any juror on account of any verdict or indictment assented to. The juror-protection clause has independent operative force; conduct directed at a juror need not also satisfy the broader nexus and pending-proceeding requirements of the omnibus clause to be chargeable under the specific-juror branch.

The clause likewise protects officers of the court and magistrate judges from corrupt or threatening efforts to influence their official conduct. The protection extends to retaliation undertaken after the fact, recognizing that the threat of post-decision retribution can chill the independent exercise of judicial and quasi-judicial functions.

Distinguishing Section 1503 from Section 1512

The most frequently litigated boundary in this corner of Title 18 is between Section 1503's omnibus clause and 18 U.S.C. Section 1512, the witness-tampering statute. Section 1512 was enacted as part of the Victim and Witness Protection Act of 1982 and was substantially expanded by the Sarbanes-Oxley Act of 2002. It contains comprehensive prohibitions on tampering with witnesses, victims, or informants, including conduct that occurs before any proceeding has commenced and conduct that involves official proceedings as defined in Section 1515.

Federal courts are divided on whether Section 1503 retains independent reach over witness-related conduct in light of Section 1512's coverage. The majority view is that Section 1503's omnibus clause continues to reach witness-related obstruction outside the specific procedures Section 1512 governs, but the precise allocation varies by circuit.

Distinguishing Section 1503 from Section 1505

The Section 1503 / Section 1505 line is set by the type of proceeding. Section 1503 reaches obstruction of pending federal judicial proceedings. Section 1505 reaches obstruction of pending proceedings before a federal department or agency and obstruction of certain congressional inquiries. The two statutes do not overlap; conduct affecting an administrative proceeding is generally not chargeable under Section 1503, and conduct affecting a judicial proceeding is generally not chargeable under Section 1505. The choice of statute is dictated by the forum in which the obstructed proceeding is pending.

Penalty Structure

Section 1503(b) sets the maximum term of imprisonment at ten years for most violations. Where the offense results in killing or attempted killing of a person, the maximum is the term provided for first-degree or second-degree murder or for the offense of attempted murder, as the case may be. Fines are imposed under 18 U.S.C. Section 3571.
