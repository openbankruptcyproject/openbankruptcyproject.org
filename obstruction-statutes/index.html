Title 18 Companion Statutes

Obstruction Statutes

Bankruptcy fraud rarely travels alone. The same conduct that violates 18 U.S.C. section 152 or 18 U.S.C. section 157 will often also implicate one or more of the general federal obstruction statutes - sections 1001, 1503, 1505, and 1623 - which reach false statements and obstructive conduct directed at federal courts, federal agencies, and the United States Trustee Program. These four guides explain how each statute reaches into the bankruptcy system.

About the obstruction toolkit

The four statutes catalogued here are the federal prosecutor's general-purpose obstruction toolkit. Each operates on a different jurisdictional axis. Section 1001 reaches false statements made in any matter within the jurisdiction of the executive, legislative, or judicial branch - a sweep that covers communications with the United States Trustee, with the Office of the United States Attorney, and with federal investigative agencies. Section 1503 (the so-called omnibus clause) reaches corrupt endeavors to influence, obstruct, or impede the due administration of justice in pending federal judicial proceedings, which includes pending bankruptcy cases. Section 1505 reaches obstruction directed at pending federal agency proceedings, including United States Trustee inquiries and EOUST oversight reviews. Section 1623 criminalizes false statements made under oath in any proceeding before or ancillary to a federal court or grand jury, including 341 meetings and Rule 2004 examinations.

None of these statutes requires proof of an underlying bankruptcy violation. Each is independently chargeable, and each carries substantial maximum sentences - typically five to twenty years per count. The mental-state requirements vary: section 1001 and section 1623 require knowingly making a materially false statement; section 1503 and section 1505 require corruptly endeavoring to obstruct. Materiality and corrupt intent are the most frequently litigated elements.

The deep-dives below examine each statute in turn, surveying the elements, the Supreme Court and circuit case law, the materiality doctrine, and the practical considerations that govern when these statutes are charged alongside the bankruptcy-specific fraud provisions.

Section-by-section deep-dives

  • 18 U.S.C. Section 1001 - False StatementsThe general federal false-statements statute reaching any materially false statement, concealment, or fraudulent representation in a matter within the jurisdiction of the executive, legislative, or judicial branch.
  • 18 U.S.C. Section 1503 - Omnibus ObstructionThe omnibus obstruction-of-justice clause reaching corrupt endeavors to influence, obstruct, or impede the due administration of justice in pending federal judicial proceedings.
  • 18 U.S.C. Section 1505 - Agency ObstructionThe companion provision reaching obstruction directed at pending proceedings before federal departments, agencies, and committees, including United States Trustee inquiries.
  • 18 U.S.C. Section 1623 - False Statements Before AgencyKnowingly false statements made under oath in proceedings before or ancillary to a federal court or grand jury, including 341 meetings and Rule 2004 examinations.
Procedural note: The general five-year statute of limitations of 18 U.S.C. section 3282 governs each of these offenses. Materiality is an element of every charge under section 1001 and section 1623 (United States v. Gaudin, 515 U.S. 506 (1995)) and is decided by the jury, not the court.
