18 U.S.C. Section 1505 - Obstruction of Federal Agency Proceedings

Doctrinal overview of obstruction directed at pending proceedings before federal departments, agencies, and congressional inquiries. The Department-or-Agency definition, the corrupt-endeavor element, and the line between Section 1505 and the Sarbanes-Oxley anti-shredding statute.

What Is Section 1505?

18 U.S.C. Section 1505 criminalizes corrupt interference with pending proceedings before federal departments, agencies, and congressional committees. The statute fills the gap left by Section 1503, which is limited to obstruction of judicial proceedings. Where the proceeding is administrative or legislative rather than judicial, Section 1505 supplies the operative federal obstruction prohibition. The base statutory maximum is five years of imprisonment, enhanced to eight years for offenses involving domestic or international terrorism.

Official citation: 18 U.S.C. § 1505

Statutory Structure

Section 1505 has two operative paragraphs. The first paragraph addresses obstruction of proceedings arising under the Antitrust Civil Process Act, prohibiting the withholding, falsification, removal, or destruction of documentary material required to be produced under that Act. The second paragraph is the principal operative provision and reaches anyone who "corruptly, or by threats or force, or by any threatening letter or communication influences, obstructs, or impedes or endeavors to influence, obstruct, or impede the due and proper administration of the law under which any pending proceeding is being had before any department or agency of the United States, or the due and proper exercise of the power of inquiry under which any inquiry or investigation is being had by either House, or any committee of either House or any joint committee of the Congress."

"Department or Agency": Defining the Forum

The term "department or agency of the United States" is broadly construed. It encompasses the executive departments enumerated in 5 U.S.C. Section 101 (Treasury, Justice, Defense, and the other principal departments), independent regulatory agencies, and the wide range of federal administrative bodies that conduct adjudicatory or investigative proceedings under federal law. The Securities and Exchange Commission, the Internal Revenue Service, the Federal Trade Commission, the Department of Health and Human Services Office of Inspector General, and similar entities all routinely conduct proceedings within the meaning of Section 1505.

Federal bankruptcy proceedings present a question of statutory taxonomy. The bankruptcy courts are units of the federal district courts under 28 U.S.C. Section 151, and proceedings before a bankruptcy judge are judicial proceedings within the meaning of Section 1503. However, certain adjudicative and oversight functions discharged by other components of the bankruptcy system - including the offices of the United States Trustee under 28 U.S.C. Section 581 et seq., which exercise executive-branch oversight under the Attorney General - are agency functions and may fall within Section 1505 where the conduct targets those functions rather than the court itself. The precise allocation depends on the specific forum and function targeted; the same factual conduct may implicate Section 1503, Section 1505, or both depending on the obstructed proceeding.

The Pending-Proceeding Requirement

Like Section 1503, Section 1505 requires that the proceeding be "pending" at the time of the obstructive conduct. The statute is forward-looking only in the sense that the obstruction need not have succeeded; the proceeding itself must already exist. Federal courts have held that an investigation that has not yet produced any official agency action - no formal order of investigation, no subpoena, no notice of hearing - is generally not "pending" within the meaning of Section 1505. The dividing line is often technical, and circuits differ on the precise moment at which an agency's information-gathering crosses into a "pending proceeding."

The pending-proceeding requirement is one of the principal reasons Congress enacted 18 U.S.C. Section 1519 as part of the Sarbanes-Oxley Act of 2002. Section 1519 reaches the destruction, alteration, or falsification of records in any matter "in relation to or contemplation of" any federal investigation, agency administration, or bankruptcy case - language that does not require a pending proceeding and extends to inchoate investigations.

The "Corruptly" Element and the Section 1515(b) Definition

The corrupt-endeavor element of Section 1505 was the subject of one of the more consequential statutory amendments in this area of federal law. After the D.C. Circuit's decision in United States v. Poindexter, 951 F.2d 369 (D.C. Cir. 1991), which held that "corruptly" was unconstitutionally vague as applied to the defendant's congressional testimony, Congress enacted 18 U.S.C. Section 1515(b) as part of the False Statements Accountability Act of 1996. Section 1515(b) defines "corruptly" as used in Section 1505 to mean "acting with an improper purpose, personally or by influencing another, including making a false or misleading statement, or withholding, concealing, altering, or destroying a document or other information."

The statutory definition cured the vagueness problem identified in Poindexter by tying the corrupt-purpose element to enumerated categories of conduct. Most courts now treat the Section 1515(b) definition as the operative standard for Section 1505 prosecutions.

"Endeavor" and Inchoate Reach

Section 1505 reaches "endeavors" to obstruct as well as completed obstruction. As in Section 1503, the endeavor verb makes the statute an inchoate offense: the government need not prove that the obstructive act actually impeded the proceeding, only that it was undertaken with the requisite corrupt purpose and had the natural and probable effect of obstruction. Conduct that is too remote from the proceeding to have any realistic effect generally does not satisfy the endeavor element.

Congressional-Inquiry Branch

Section 1505 also reaches corrupt obstruction of "the due and proper exercise of the power of inquiry" by either house of Congress, any committee of either house, or any joint committee. The provision is the principal federal vehicle for prosecuting interference with congressional oversight, investigations, and committee proceedings. The congressional-inquiry branch is structurally parallel to the agency-proceeding branch and incorporates the same corrupt-endeavor standard and pending-proceeding requirement.

Distinction from Section 1519

18 U.S.C. Section 1519, the anti-shredding statute enacted as part of the Sarbanes-Oxley Act of 2002, is closely related to Section 1505 but materially different in scope. Section 1519 criminalizes the knowing destruction, alteration, falsification, mutilation, or concealment of any record, document, or tangible object "with the intent to impede, obstruct, or influence the investigation or proper administration of any matter within the jurisdiction of any department or agency of the United States or any case filed under title 11, or in relation to or contemplation of any such matter or case."

Three differences are important. First, Section 1519 does not require a pending proceeding; it reaches conduct undertaken in "contemplation of" a federal matter. Second, Section 1519 explicitly covers bankruptcy cases filed under Title 11 by its own terms. Third, Section 1519 is limited to document-related conduct - destruction, alteration, falsification, concealment - while Section 1505 reaches a broader range of obstructive acts including threats and influence-peddling. Section 1519 carries a higher statutory maximum (twenty years versus five years for Section 1505).

Prosecutors frequently charge both statutes in document-destruction cases, with Section 1519 supplying the broader temporal reach and higher penalty and Section 1505 supplying the more general obstruction theory tied to specific pending proceedings.

Penalty Structure

The base maximum under Section 1505 is five years of imprisonment, a fine under Title 18, or both. Offenses involving international or domestic terrorism (as defined in 18 U.S.C. Section 2331) carry an enhanced maximum of eight years. Fines are imposed under 18 U.S.C. Section 3571.
