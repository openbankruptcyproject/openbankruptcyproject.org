18 U.S.C. Section 1623 - False Declarations Before Grand Jury or Court

Doctrinal overview of false declarations under oath in federal court and grand jury settings. Narrower setting than Section 1001, the Section 1623(c) inconsistent-declarations theory, abolition of the two-witness rule, and the five-year statutory maximum.

What Is Section 1623?

18 U.S.C. Section 1623 is the modern federal false-declarations statute applicable to testimony, declarations, and depositions given under oath in proceedings before or ancillary to any court or grand jury of the United States. Enacted as part of the Organized Crime Control Act of 1970, Section 1623 was designed to address proof problems that limited the practical reach of the older perjury statute, 18 U.S.C. Section 1621. Section 1623 carries a five-year statutory maximum.

Official citation: 18 U.S.C. § 1623

Setting: Narrower than Section 1001

Section 1623 differs from 18 U.S.C. Section 1001 in two structural ways. First, Section 1623 applies only to declarations made "before or ancillary to any court or grand jury of the United States" - a narrower set of forums than the executive, legislative, and judicial sweep of Section 1001. Second, Section 1623 requires that the declaration be made under oath (or under the equivalent declaration permitted by 28 U.S.C. Section 1746). Section 1001 has no oath requirement and reaches unsworn statements made to federal officers in informal settings.

The narrower setting of Section 1623 comes with procedural advantages discussed below. The trade-off is that Section 1623 is unavailable for the broad range of unsworn false statements that arise in federal investigations and administrative matters; for those settings, Section 1001 remains the operative provision.

Elements: Section 1623(a)

Section 1623(a) provides that "[w]hoever under oath (or in any declaration, certificate, verification, or statement under penalty of perjury as permitted under section 1746 of title 28, United States Code) in any proceeding before or ancillary to any court or grand jury of the United States knowingly makes any false material declaration or makes or uses any other information... knowing the same to contain any false material declaration, shall be fined under this title or imprisoned not more than five years, or both."

The elements are: (1) testimony, declaration, or other information given under oath or pursuant to Section 1746; (2) in a proceeding before or ancillary to a federal court or grand jury; (3) that contains a material false declaration; (4) that the defendant knew was false. The materiality element is governed by the same "natural tendency to influence" standard articulated by the Supreme Court in United States v. Gaudin, 515 U.S. 506 (1995), and is a jury question.

The Inconsistent-Declarations Theory: Section 1623(c)

Section 1623(c) is the provision's most distinctive feature. It permits the government to obtain a conviction by proving that the defendant made two or more declarations under oath in federal proceedings that are "irreconcilably inconsistent" to such a degree that "one of them is necessarily false." The government does not have to specify which declaration is the false one or prove the truth or falsity of either declaration independently.

Section 1623(c) was a direct response to one of the proof difficulties the government faced under Section 1621. Under the older perjury statute, the prosecution had to prove the actual falsity of the specific statement charged. A witness who told one story before the grand jury and a different story at trial could be charged with perjury for one of the statements, but the government had to identify which one was false and prove it false to the standard required by the two-witness rule. Section 1623(c) dispenses with both requirements where the inconsistency itself establishes that perjury occurred. The government may simply prove the two declarations and their irreconcilable inconsistency.

Abolition of the Two-Witness Rule: Section 1623(e)

Section 1623(e) expressly provides that "[p]roof beyond a reasonable doubt under this section is sufficient for conviction. It shall not be necessary that such proof be made by any particular number of witnesses or by documentary or other type of evidence." This provision abolishes the common-law two-witness rule for prosecutions brought under Section 1623.

At common law and under Section 1621, a perjury conviction could not rest on the testimony of a single witness contradicting the defendant's sworn statement; corroboration was required. The two-witness rule was a relic of the heightened evidentiary suspicion historically applied to perjury allegations. Section 1623(e) removed it for prosecutions under the new statute, putting Section 1623 on the same evidentiary footing as ordinary federal criminal offenses. The two-witness rule continues to apply, in modified form, in prosecutions under Section 1621.

Recantation Defense: Section 1623(d)

Section 1623(d) provides a limited recantation defense. Where the defendant, in the same continuous court or grand jury proceeding in which the false declaration was made, admits the falsity of the declaration, the prosecution is barred if (1) the recantation occurred before the falsity has substantially affected the proceeding, and (2) before it has become manifest that the falsity has been or will be exposed.

The recantation defense is conjunctive in most circuits: both conditions must be satisfied. A recantation that occurs only after the falsity has affected the proceeding or after the falsity is likely to be exposed does not qualify. Section 1623(d) is an important procedural protection for witnesses who realize they have testified falsely and wish to correct the record before the prosecution becomes irreversible.

"Ancillary to" Any Court or Grand Jury

Section 1623 reaches declarations made in proceedings "before or ancillary to" a federal court or grand jury. The "ancillary" language extends the statute beyond live testimony in open court to depositions, affidavits, declarations submitted in connection with motion practice, and similar quasi-judicial submissions. The Supreme Court in Dunn v. United States, 442 U.S. 100 (1979), held that an unsworn statement to a private attorney in a setting wholly unconnected to any pending federal proceeding is not "ancillary" to a court or grand jury, but ordinary deposition testimony and affidavits filed in federal proceedings clearly qualify.

Section 1623 versus Section 1621

The relationship between Section 1623 and the older perjury statute, 18 U.S.C. Section 1621, is one of overlapping rather than exclusive coverage. Section 1621 retains broader applicability - it covers any "competent tribunal, officer, or person" empowered to administer oaths, not just federal courts and grand juries - while Section 1623 offers the procedural advantages of the inconsistent-declarations theory and the abolition of the two-witness rule.

Prosecutors typically choose between the two statutes based on the proof posture of the case. Where the false statement was made before a federal court or grand jury and either contradicts another sworn statement or can be proved false through evidence that would not satisfy the two-witness rule, Section 1623 is generally preferred. Where the statement was made before a non-court tribunal authorized to administer oaths or where the prosecution proceeds on a single false statement provable through traditional corroborating evidence, Section 1621 may be used.

Penalty Structure

Section 1623 carries a maximum sentence of five years of imprisonment, a fine under Title 18, or both. There is no enhanced penalty schedule comparable to those in some other Title 18 provisions. Fines are imposed under 18 U.S.C. Section 3571.
