18 U.S.C. Section 1001 - False Statements to the Federal Government

Doctrinal overview of the federal false-statements statute. Three statutory categories, the materiality element, the holding of Brogan v. United States, and the five-year statutory maximum.

Home › Obstruction Statutes › 18 U.S.C. Section 1001 - False Statements

What Is Section 1001?

18 U.S.C. Section 1001 is the principal federal false-statements statute. It criminalizes the knowing and willful submission of materially false information to the federal government within any matter within the jurisdiction of the executive, legislative, or judicial branch of the United States. The provision is one of the most broadly applicable criminal statutes in the federal code and is regularly charged in matters ranging from federal-program fraud to false statements made during the course of a federal investigation.

The statute's reach is unusual in two respects. First, it covers statements made outside any formal judicial proceeding, including informal interviews with federal agents. Second, it reaches concealment by trick, scheme, or device as well as affirmative misstatements and the use of false writings. Each of these categories operates independently, and a single course of conduct may satisfy more than one.

Official citation: 18 U.S.C. § 1001

Three Statutory Categories: Section 1001(a)(1)-(3)

Section 1001(a) enumerates three discrete categories of prohibited conduct. Each is independently chargeable, and the elements of each differ in subtle but consequential ways.

Section 1001(a)(1) - Concealment

Subsection (a)(1) criminalizes "falsif[ying], conceal[ing], or cover[ing] up by any trick, scheme, or device a material fact." Liability under this branch requires proof of an affirmative act of concealment together with a legal duty to disclose the concealed fact. Mere passive nondisclosure, in the absence of a duty arising by statute, regulation, contract, or the nature of the proceeding, is generally not chargeable under (a)(1).

Section 1001(a)(2) - False Statements and Representations

Subsection (a)(2) is the most frequently charged branch. It reaches "any materially false, fictitious, or fraudulent statement or representation." It applies to oral statements, written statements, and statements made through any other medium. There is no requirement that the statement be made under oath, and there is no requirement that the person to whom the statement is made be a law-enforcement officer.

Section 1001(a)(3) - False Writings and Documents

Subsection (a)(3) reaches the act of "mak[ing] or us[ing] any false writing or document knowing the same to contain any materially false, fictitious, or fraudulent statement or entry." This branch is commonly charged in matters involving forged or altered documents submitted to federal agencies, false certifications on federal forms, and the submission of falsified records to support claims for federal benefits or program funds.

The Materiality Element

All three branches of Section 1001(a) require that the false statement, concealment, or writing concern a "material" fact. The Supreme Court has defined materiality in this context as a statement having "a natural tendency to influence, or [be] capable of influencing, the decision of the decisionmaking body to which it was addressed." See United States v. Gaudin, 515 U.S. 506 (1995). Materiality is determined as an objective matter; whether the statement actually influenced the decision is not the test.

Importantly, materiality is an element of the offense and must be submitted to the jury, not decided by the court as a matter of law. Gaudin held that the Sixth Amendment requires jury determination of materiality in a Section 1001 prosecution. Statements that are trivial, immaterial to the agency function at issue, or wholly outside the scope of the matter under inquiry do not satisfy the materiality element.

Brogan v. United States and the "Exculpatory No" Doctrine

For several decades, a number of federal circuits recognized an "exculpatory no" doctrine under which a simple, unelaborated denial of wrongdoing to a federal investigator was held outside the reach of Section 1001. The Supreme Court abolished the doctrine in Brogan v. United States, 522 U.S. 398 (1998). The Court held that the text of Section 1001 contains no exception for exculpatory denials and that courts have no authority to engraft one. After Brogan, a knowing and willful false denial made to a federal agent in response to a question about a material matter is a chargeable offense under (a)(2), without regard to whether the denial was offered to avoid self-incrimination.

Brogan did not displace the Fifth Amendment privilege against self-incrimination. A person questioned by a federal agent retains the right to remain silent. The holding is narrower: silence is protected; an affirmative false denial is not.

Jurisdictional Element: "Any Matter Within the Jurisdiction"

Section 1001(a) requires that the false statement, concealment, or writing be made "in any matter within the jurisdiction of the executive, legislative, or judicial branch of the Government of the United States." This jurisdictional element is interpreted broadly. The statement need not be made directly to a federal agency; statements made to state or private entities that are administering federal programs or that are required to report to federal authorities can satisfy the jurisdictional element. See United States v. Rodgers, 466 U.S. 475 (1984).

Section 1001(b) carves out a narrow exception for statements, representations, writings, or documents submitted by a party or counsel to a judge or magistrate in a judicial proceeding. This carve-out does not extend to ancillary submissions, court-required filings outside the immediate adjudication, or statements made to court personnel other than the judge.

Section 1001(c) addresses statements within the jurisdiction of the legislative branch, including statements made in matters within the jurisdiction of congressional committees and offices, with specific definitions of administrative matters and investigations or reviews.

Mens Rea: Knowingly and Willfully

Section 1001(a) requires that the defendant act "knowingly and willfully." Federal courts have generally interpreted "knowingly" to mean that the defendant was aware of the falsity of the statement, and "willfully" to mean that the defendant acted deliberately and with knowledge that the conduct was unlawful, although the precise formulation of the willfulness instruction varies among circuits. The government need not prove that the defendant knew of the specific statute or the specific federal jurisdiction implicated.

Penalty Structure

The base statutory maximum under Section 1001(a) is five years of imprisonment, a fine under Title 18, or both. The maximum is enhanced to eight years for offenses involving international or domestic terrorism (as defined in 18 U.S.C. Section 2331) and for certain offenses involving human trafficking, sex offenses against minors, and related enumerated conduct. The statute also authorizes the imposition of a fine in accordance with the general fine provisions of 18 U.S.C. Section 3571.

Section 1001 is regularly applied in conjunction with United States Sentencing Guidelines Section 2B1.1 (for false statements that cause loss to the government or a victim) and Section 2J1.3 (for perjury-like conduct under certain circumstances).

Relationship to Other Federal Statutes

Section 1001 overlaps substantially with several other federal criminal statutes, and prosecutors frequently choose among them based on the specific factual setting:

  • 18 U.S.C. Section 1621 - General perjury statute, requiring an oath before a competent tribunal, officer, or person.
  • 18 U.S.C. Section 1623 - False declarations before a grand jury or court, narrower in setting but offering procedural advantages including a relaxed two-witness rule.
  • 18 U.S.C. Section 1505 - Obstruction of agency and congressional proceedings.
  • 18 U.S.C. Section 1519 - Destruction, alteration, or falsification of records in federal investigations and bankruptcy.
  • 18 U.S.C. Section 287 - False, fictitious, or fraudulent claims against the United States.

Section 1001's breadth - reaching unsworn statements outside formal proceedings - makes it the default federal false-statement charge in many investigative settings, while sister statutes apply where the procedural context (oath, grand jury, agency proceeding, document destruction) calls for a more specific provision.
