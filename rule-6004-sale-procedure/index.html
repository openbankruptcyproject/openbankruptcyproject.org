Federal Rule of Bankruptcy Procedure 6004

Rule 6004: Sale Procedure (363 Sales)

21-day notice, free-and-clear sales under 6004(c), stalking-horse and bidding procedures under 6004(f), good-faith findings under section 363(m), and the credit-bid framework under section 363(k).

The architecture of Rule 6004

Federal Rule of Bankruptcy Procedure 6004 is the procedural rulebook for sales, uses, and leases of estate property under 11 U.S.C. Section 363. The rule operates downstream of the substantive Section 363 framework: Section 363 authorizes the trustee (or debtor in possession) to sell estate property outside the ordinary course of business with court approval, and Rule 6004 specifies the procedure by which that approval is sought and granted.

The rule has seven operative subdivisions. Rule 6004(a) ties notice into Rule 2002. Rule 6004(b) provides the procedure for objections. Rule 6004(c) governs the free-and-clear sale mechanism keyed to Section 363(f). Rule 6004(d) addresses sales of personally identifiable information. Rule 6004(e) addresses sales of property under $2,500. Rule 6004(f) provides the procedure for the sale itself, including bidding procedures. Rule 6004(g) governs documentation of the sale.

The 21-day notice baseline under Rule 6004(a)

Rule 6004(a) cross-references Rule 2002(a)(2), (c)(1), (i), and (k) for notice of a proposed use, sale, or lease of property other than in the ordinary course of business. The baseline is 21 days' notice to all creditors. The notice must describe the property to be sold, the proposed terms, and the deadline for objections. Where the sale is "private" rather than public, Rule 6004(c) requires that the notice describe the terms of the proposed sale with sufficient particularity to allow informed objection.

The 21-day window can be shortened only "for cause shown." The "for cause" determination is fact-specific; courts have shortened the period for emergencies (perishable inventory, rapidly depreciating assets, time-sensitive transactions), but routine sales are expected to proceed on the standard window.

Free-and-clear sales under Rule 6004(c)

Section 363(f) authorizes the sale of estate property "free and clear" of interests held by third parties - typically liens, security interests, and other encumbrances - if one of five statutory grounds is satisfied:

  • Applicable nonbankruptcy law permits sale of the property free and clear of the interest
  • The holder of the interest consents
  • The interest is a lien and the price at which the property is to be sold is greater than the aggregate value of all liens on the property
  • The interest is in bona fide dispute
  • The holder of the interest could be compelled, in a legal or equitable proceeding, to accept a money satisfaction of the interest

Rule 6004(c) is the procedural rule for invoking Section 363(f). The rule requires that the motion to sell free and clear be served on the parties whose interests are to be extinguished, with notice of the time fixed for filing objections. The interests, once extinguished, attach to the proceeds of sale - the "interests follow proceeds" principle that protects the substantive entitlement while permitting the transactional clearing of title.

Service on lienholders is service, not notice. A motion to sell free and clear of a lien must be served on the lienholder under Rule 9014's incorporation of Rule 7004; mere Rule 2002 mailing to the creditor matrix is insufficient. A free-and-clear sale order entered without proper Rule 7004 service on a lienholder is vulnerable to collateral attack.

Stalking-horse bidder and bidding procedures under Rule 6004(f)

Rule 6004(f) governs the procedure for the sale itself. Most non-trivial bankruptcy sales proceed through a two-step process. First, the trustee or debtor in possession identifies a "stalking-horse" bidder - a buyer who has negotiated terms with the seller and is prepared to bind itself to the sale subject to higher and better offers from other bidders. Second, the court approves "bidding procedures" - typically including a minimum overbid increment, a deadline for competing bids, a deposit requirement, and conditions on the form of competing bids - and conducts an auction.

Bidding procedures are typically approved by a separate order entered before the sale-approval hearing itself. The two-step structure protects all participants: the stalking-horse bidder receives the certainty of going-forward bidding procedures and (often) a break-up fee in the event of overbid; competing bidders receive notice and a defined process for submitting bids; the estate receives the benefit of competitive pricing.

Break-up fees - payments to the stalking-horse bidder if the asset is ultimately sold to a competing bidder - are themselves subject to bankruptcy-court approval. The case law evaluates break-up fees against an "actual and necessary" standard under 11 U.S.C. Section 503(b), with consideration of whether the fee was the inducement that secured the stalking-horse commitment and whether the fee is reasonable in relation to the value the stalking-horse provided.

The good-faith finding under Section 363(m)

Section 363(m) provides that "the reversal or modification on appeal of an authorization under [Section 363(b) or (c)] does not affect the validity of a sale or lease under such authorization to an entity that purchased or leased such property in good faith, whether or not such entity knew of the pendency of the appeal, unless such authorization and such sale or lease were stayed pending appeal."

The Section 363(m) "good-faith" finding is the structural feature that gives 363 sales their commercial finality. Once a sale is consummated to a good-faith purchaser and is not stayed pending appeal, the sale stands even if the underlying authorization is later overturned. This finality is what permits sophisticated buyers to participate in bankruptcy sales without the discount that residual reversal risk would otherwise impose.

The "good-faith" finding is not automatic. The case law requires the bankruptcy court to make a particularized finding on the record, typically addressing whether the purchase was at arm's length, whether the purchaser had knowledge of any defects in the sale process, whether the purchase price was the result of competitive bidding, and whether any insider relationships affected the transaction. A sale order that omits a Section 363(m) finding - or that recites the finding in conclusory terms unsupported by record evidence - is vulnerable.

The credit-bid framework under Section 363(k)

Section 363(k) authorizes a secured creditor whose claim is secured by a lien on the property being sold to "bid at such sale, and, if such holder of such claim purchases such property, [to] offset such claim against the purchase price of such property." The mechanism - "credit bidding" - allows the secured creditor to bid up to the full amount of its allowed claim without putting cash on the table.

The credit bid serves two functions. First, it protects the secured creditor against a sale that would dilute its collateral position by allowing the creditor to set a floor at the full amount of its lien. Second, it creates a market-discipline mechanism: a credit-bidding secured creditor that does not believe the property is worth at least its claim amount will not credit-bid, and the willingness of a credit-bidding secured creditor to bid the full claim provides a signal of value.

Section 363(k) credit-bidding may be limited or denied "for cause." The case law characterizes "cause" narrowly; allegations of inequitable conduct by the secured creditor or substantial uncertainty about the validity of the underlying lien are typical bases. The Supreme Court's decision in RadLAX Gateway Hotel, LLC v. Amalgamated Bank, 566 U.S. 639 (2012), confirms that the credit-bid right also constrains the structure of confirmation in Chapter 11: a plan that purports to sell collateral free and clear without permitting the secured creditor to credit bid does not satisfy the "fair and equitable" requirement of Section 1129(b)(2)(A).

Common procedural posture

Chapter 7 trustee asset sale

A Chapter 7 trustee identifies estate equipment subject to a perfected lien held by a secured creditor. The trustee files a motion under Rule 6004 to sell free and clear under Section 363(f)(3) (price exceeds aggregate liens). The motion is served on the secured creditor under Rule 7004 and noticed to all creditors under Rule 2002. The court holds the hearing, makes a Section 363(m) good-faith finding, and enters the sale order. Liens attach to proceeds.

Chapter 11 going-concern sale

A Chapter 11 debtor proposes to sell its business as a going concern. The debtor files a motion to approve bidding procedures, identifies a stalking-horse bidder with a break-up fee, and proposes an auction schedule. The court approves the bidding procedures by a first-day order, the auction proceeds on the scheduled date, and the court enters a sale order with a Section 363(m) finding approving the sale to the winning bidder.

Practical impact

Rule 6004 governs one of the most consequential transactions in any bankruptcy case. A sale order entered without satisfying the Rule 6004 procedural requirements - 21-day notice, proper service on lienholders, supported Section 363(m) finding - is vulnerable to collateral attack. A sale order entered with full procedural compliance is largely bulletproof, even on appeal, because the Section 363(m) finality protection insulates the consummated transaction.

The Section 363(m) finding is the load-bearing element. Without an articulated good-faith finding on the record, the sale lacks the appellate finality that gives 363 sales their commercial value. Practitioners drafting sale orders should ensure that the Section 363(m) finding is supported by specific record evidence - not merely recited as a conclusion.
