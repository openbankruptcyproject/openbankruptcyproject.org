KRPC 6.5 / MRPC 4-6.5: Nonprofit and Court-Annexed Limited Legal Services Programs

An affirmative framework for short-term limited legal services delivered through nonprofit and court-annexed programs, with modified conflict-of-interest rules tailored to the practice.

Verbatim text of KRPC 6.5 (Kansas)

(a) A lawyer who, under the auspices of a program sponsored by a nonprofit organization or court, provides short-term limited legal services to a client without expectation by either the lawyer or the client that the lawyer will provide continuing representation in the matter:
(1) is subject to Rules 1.7 and 1.9(a) only if the lawyer knows that the representation of the client involves a conflict of interest; and
(2) is subject to Rule 1.10 only if the lawyer knows that another lawyer associated with the lawyer in a law firm is disqualified by Rule 1.7 or 1.9(a) with respect to the matter.

(b) Except as provided in paragraph (a)(2), Rule 1.10 is inapplicable to a representation governed by this Rule. Kan. Sup. Ct. R. 226, KRPC 6.5.

Missouri Rule 4-6.5

Missouri Rule 4-6.5 is substantively parallel to KRPC 6.5 and tracks ABA Model Rule 6.5. The rule provides an affirmative framework that authorizes short-term limited legal services through nonprofit and court-annexed programs and modifies the conflict-of-interest rules to make that practice feasible.

The structural premise

The Comment to Model Rule 6.5 frames the rule's purpose: legal-services programs that provide short-term assistance - drop-in advice clinics, courthouse self-help kiosks, telephone-hotline programs, bar-sponsored brief-advice events - meet a substantial access-to-justice need. Persons facing legal problems often benefit substantially from a brief consultation with a lawyer even where ongoing representation is neither contemplated nor feasible. Without a tailored framework, however, the conflict-of-interest rules designed for full representation would make this category of practice impractical: a volunteer lawyer at a drop-in clinic cannot run a full firm-wide conflict check against each client who walks through the door for a fifteen-minute consultation.

Rule 6.5 resolves the impracticality by establishing a tailored conflict-of-interest framework for this discrete practice category. Within the framework's conditions, lawyers may provide short-term limited legal services without the full conflict-check apparatus that ordinary representation requires.

The framework's threshold conditions

The rule's modified conflict framework applies to representations that satisfy three threshold conditions. Where any condition is not met, the ordinary conflict-of-interest rules apply.

Auspices of a nonprofit or court program

The services must be provided "under the auspices of a program sponsored by a nonprofit organization or court." The auspices requirement ensures that the lawyer is operating within an institutional framework with its own intake, screening, and follow-up processes. The institutional framework is what allows the modified conflict regime to function: the program, rather than the individual volunteer lawyer, holds the structural responsibility for matter management and post-encounter handoff. Eligible auspices include bar-sponsored pro bono clinics, court-affiliated self-help centers, civil legal aid drop-in programs, and analogous structured initiatives.

Short-term and limited

The services must be "short-term limited legal services." The Comment to Model Rule 6.5 frames this as a single discrete encounter - typically a single meeting or telephone consultation - rather than continuing representation. The duration is qualitative rather than measured by a numerical limit. Services that are properly understood as brief advice or limited assistance fall within the framework; services that develop into ongoing representation move outside the framework and into the ordinary conflict regime.

No expectation of continuing representation

Neither the lawyer nor the client expects the lawyer to provide continuing representation in the matter. The mutual-expectation condition operates as the structural distinction between a Rule 6.5 encounter and ordinary representation. The lawyer's role is bounded at the outset to the short-term limited service. Where the client's matter requires continuing work, the program's protocols typically include referral mechanisms - to a staff lawyer, to a private-bar referral service, or to another resource - and the volunteer lawyer's role concludes at the referral.

The modified conflict regime

Within the threshold conditions, Rule 6.5 modifies the application of the ordinary conflict-of-interest rules in three respects.

6.5(a)(1) - Knowledge-based application of Rules 1.7 and 1.9(a)

Rules 1.7 (current-client conflict) and 1.9(a) (former-client conflict) apply only where the lawyer knows that the representation involves a conflict of interest. The ordinary "should know" standard is replaced by an actual-knowledge standard. The Comment to Model Rule 6.5 frames the modification as a recognition that the time-compressed nature of short-term services does not permit comprehensive conflict screening. A volunteer lawyer who proceeds in good faith without conducting a firm-wide conflict check is within the rule even if a comprehensive check would have revealed an issue, provided the lawyer does not in fact know of a conflict.

6.5(a)(2) - Knowledge-based imputation

Rule 1.10 (imputed disqualification) applies only where the volunteer lawyer knows that another lawyer in the firm is disqualified under Rule 1.7 or 1.9(a) with respect to the matter. The ordinary firm-wide imputation regime - in which a conflict held by any firm lawyer disqualifies all firm lawyers - is replaced by an actual-knowledge standard. The volunteer lawyer is not charged with constructive knowledge of every firm-wide conflict; the volunteer lawyer is subject to imputation only where the volunteer lawyer actually knows of the colleague's disqualification.

6.5(b) - Inapplicability of Rule 1.10 outside the knowledge channel

Except as provided in (a)(2), Rule 1.10 is inapplicable to a representation governed by Rule 6.5. The provision insulates the volunteer lawyer's firm from broader imputation effects flowing from the short-term encounter. The matter the volunteer lawyer handles does not, by virtue of the brief consultation, become a firm-wide matter subject to ordinary imputation in subsequent firm work.

The framework rests on the auspices of the program. The modified conflict regime is available because the program supplies the institutional structure - intake, screening, follow-up, and referral - that would otherwise have to be supplied by full firm-conflict procedures. The framework operates within structured programs; it is not a free-standing modification of the conflict rules for any short-term encounter.

Common program structures

Courthouse self-help clinic

A court-annexed self-help center hosts a weekly drop-in clinic at which volunteer lawyers provide brief advice to self-represented litigants on civil matters. Each consultation is bounded - typically twenty to thirty minutes - with no continuing-representation expectation. The clinic's intake form documents the matter category and confirms the limited-services framing. Volunteer lawyers provide advice within the Rule 6.5 framework, with referrals provided by the clinic for matters requiring ongoing representation.

Nonprofit bankruptcy brief-advice program

A nonprofit organization sponsors a periodic brief-advice program at which volunteer lawyers meet with prospective debtors for a one-time consultation on bankruptcy options. The program supplies intake forms, eligibility screening, and referral protocols for clients who proceed to file. The volunteer lawyer's role concludes at the end of the consultation; the conflict modifications of Rule 6.5 apply to the encounter, and ongoing representation, if any, is handled separately through the lawyer's regular engagement procedures.

The interaction with ongoing representation

A consultation that begins within the Rule 6.5 framework may, at the lawyer's and client's mutual election, transition into ongoing representation. The transition moves the matter outside the Rule 6.5 framework and into the ordinary engagement framework, with the full conflict-of-interest rules applying from the point of transition forward. The Rule 6.5 modifications do not carry over into the ongoing representation; that representation is structured and governed like any other.

The transition is typically marked by a separate engagement document, completion of the firm's ordinary conflict-checking process, and formal acceptance of the matter under the firm's intake procedures. Until the transition occurs, the encounter remains within the Rule 6.5 framework.

The role of program intake and referral processes

Program intake and referral processes are structural complements to Rule 6.5. The program's processes typically address:

  • Confirmation of the limited-services framing with the client at intake
  • Brief categorical screening for obvious conflicts that the program can identify from intake information
  • Information barriers between the volunteer lawyer's encounter and other program activities
  • Referral protocols for clients whose matters require continuing representation
  • Documentation of the encounter sufficient to permit a follow-up referral lawyer to step in without re-creating intake

These processes do not appear in the text of Rule 6.5, but they are the institutional infrastructure that supports the rule's operation.

Relationship to other rules

  • Rule 1.7 - Conflict with current clients; applies only with actual knowledge under 6.5(a)(1)
  • Rule 1.9 - Conflict with former clients; subdivision (a) applies only with actual knowledge under 6.5(a)(1)
  • Rule 1.10 - Imputed disqualification; modified under 6.5(a)(2) and largely inapplicable under 6.5(b)
  • Rule 1.2(c) - Limited-scope representation; the underlying authorization for limited services to which Rule 6.5 adds the conflict-rule modifications
  • Rule 6.1 - Voluntary pro bono publico service; the broader pro bono framework
  • Rule 6.3 - Membership in legal services organization; the governance-side framework

Continuing nature of the framework's threshold conditions

The three threshold conditions are continuously evaluated through the encounter. A consultation that begins within the framework but that develops into something materially different - because the lawyer agrees to continuing representation, because the program's auspices end mid-encounter, or because the limited-services framing is otherwise abandoned - moves outside the framework when the precipitating change occurs. The lawyer's evaluation of the framework's applicability is anchored to the encounter as actually conducted, not to its initial design.

Practical impact

Rule 6.5 carries several recurring practical consequences:

  • The rule is an affirmative authorization. The modified conflict regime makes structured short-term services feasible at scale. The rule's design is to enable rather than to restrict.
  • Knowledge is the operative standard. Under 6.5(a)(1) and 6.5(a)(2), the ordinary conflict-screening obligations are replaced by an actual-knowledge standard. Volunteer lawyers acting in good faith without comprehensive firm-wide checking are within the rule provided they do not in fact know of a conflict.
  • The framework depends on program structure. The auspices requirement is structural. Lawyers providing services outside a sponsoring program operate under the ordinary conflict rules; the modified regime is available only within structured programs.
