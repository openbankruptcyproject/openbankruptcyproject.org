11 U.S.C. Section 327 - Employment of Professional Persons

Plain-English guide to court approval of attorneys, accountants, and other professionals retained to assist the trustee or debtor in possession.

What Is Section 327?

Section 327 governs the employment of professional persons by the trustee or debtor in possession in a bankruptcy case. It is the gateway provision for retaining attorneys, accountants, appraisers, financial advisors, investment bankers, auctioneers, and other professionals whose services are necessary to administer the estate. Court approval under Section 327 is a precondition to compensation under Section 330.

The trustee or debtor in possession initiates employment by filing an application accompanied by a verified statement disclosing the professional's connections with the debtor, creditors, and other parties in interest. The disclosure requirement is codified in Federal Rule of Bankruptcy Procedure 2014.

Official citation: 11 U.S.C. § 327

The Disinterestedness Requirement: Section 327(a)

Section 327(a) authorizes the trustee, with the court's approval, to employ professional persons "that do not hold or represent an interest adverse to the estate, and that are disinterested persons, to represent or assist the trustee in carrying out the trustee's duties under this title."

"Disinterested person" is defined in Section 101(14) to mean a person that:

  • Is not a creditor, equity security holder, or insider.
  • Is not and was not, within 2 years before the date of the filing of the petition, a director, officer, or employee of the debtor.
  • Does not have an interest materially adverse to the interest of the estate or any class of creditors or equity security holders, by reason of any direct or indirect relationship to, connection with, or interest in, the debtor, or for any other reason.

"Adverse interest" is a related but distinct concept. A professional may not hold or represent an adverse interest, which includes representing parties whose interests conflict with those of the estate.

Special-Purpose Counsel: Section 327(e)

Section 327(e) provides a narrower route for retention. The trustee may employ, "for a specified special purpose, other than to represent the trustee in conducting the case, an attorney that has represented the debtor, if in the best interest of the estate, and if such attorney does not represent or hold any interest adverse to the debtor or to the estate with respect to the matter on which such attorney is to be employed."

Section 327(e) is commonly used to retain pre-petition counsel for limited matters such as ongoing litigation, real-estate transactions, intellectual-property prosecution, or specialized regulatory matters. The disinterestedness requirement of 327(a) does not apply to 327(e) retentions; the test is only whether the attorney holds an interest adverse to the estate with respect to the specific matter.

Disclosure Under Rule 2014

Federal Rule of Bankruptcy Procedure 2014 requires the employment application to be accompanied by a verified statement disclosing "all of the person's connections with the debtor, creditors, any other party in interest, their respective attorneys and accountants, the United States trustee, or any person employed in the office of the United States trustee." The disclosure obligation is continuous: subsequent connections that come to the professional's attention must be promptly supplemented.

Inadequate or untimely Rule 2014 disclosure is a frequent source of fee disputes. Courts have broad discretion to deny or disgorge compensation under Section 329 and Section 330 where disclosure failures are material, even where the underlying conflict would not have disqualified the professional if disclosed.

Sanctions for Unauthorized Employment

A professional that performs services without prior court approval under Section 327 is generally not entitled to compensation, although courts may grant nunc pro tunc (now-for-then) approval where the failure to obtain prior approval is the result of "extraordinary circumstances." The Supreme Court tightened the nunc pro tunc standard in Roman Catholic Archdiocese of San Juan v. Acevedo Feliciano, 589 U.S. 57 (2020), and many circuits have correspondingly narrowed retroactive approval for bankruptcy professionals.

Counsel must take care to file employment applications promptly and to monitor compliance, including for newly added affiliates and successor entities, to avoid retroactive-approval problems.

Related Bankruptcy Code Sections

This section operates in concert with several other provisions of the Bankruptcy Code:

  • Section 329 - Debtor transactions with attorneys and fee review
  • Section 503 - Administrative expenses including professional fees
  • Section 507 - Priority of administrative-expense claims
  • Section 1183 - Subchapter V trustee retention of professionals

Understanding how these sections interact is important for debtors, creditors, trustees, and counsel navigating a bankruptcy case.
