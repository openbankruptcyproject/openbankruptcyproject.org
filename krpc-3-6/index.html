KRPC 3.6 / MRPC 4-3.6: Trial Publicity

Public-statement safe harbors, the right-to-reply framework, the substantial-likelihood-of-material-prejudice standard, and the Gentile First Amendment architecture.

Public-statement safe harbors that 3.6 protects

Rule 3.6 is best read as a public-statement architecture. It identifies what counsel may say to the public, to the press, and through other channels of mass dissemination at every stage of a matter. The rule names categories of statements that are categorically safe harbors, preserves an affirmative right to reply to publicity initiated by others, and applies the constitutional standard of "substantial likelihood of material prejudice" articulated by the Supreme Court of the United States in Gentile v. State Bar of Nevada, 501 U.S. 1030 (1991).

The rule's affirmative content is broad: routine case-status statements, descriptions of the claim and defenses, information already on the public record, statements warning of imminent danger, and reply statements protecting the client from prejudicial publicity initiated by others. These categories cover the substantial majority of statements counsel makes about pending matters.

Verbatim text of KRPC 3.6 (Kansas)

(a) A lawyer who is participating or has participated in the investigation or litigation of a matter shall not make an extrajudicial statement that the lawyer knows or reasonably should know will be disseminated by means of public communication and will have a substantial likelihood of materially prejudicing an adjudicative proceeding in the matter.

(b) Notwithstanding paragraph (a), a lawyer may state:
(1) the claim, offense or defense involved and, except when prohibited by law, the identity of the persons involved;
(2) information contained in a public record;
(3) that an investigation of a matter is in progress;
(4) the scheduling or result of any step in litigation;
(5) a request for assistance in obtaining evidence and information necessary thereto;
(6) a warning of danger concerning the behavior of a person involved, when there is reason to believe that there exists the likelihood of substantial harm to an individual or to the public interest; and
(7) in a criminal case, in addition to (1) through (6): (i) the identity, residence, occupation and family status of the accused; (ii) if the accused has not been apprehended, information necessary to aid in apprehension of that person; (iii) the fact, time and place of arrest; and (iv) the identity of investigating and arresting officers or agencies and the length of the investigation.

(c) Notwithstanding paragraph (a), a lawyer may make a statement that a reasonable lawyer would believe is required to protect a client from the substantial undue prejudicial effect of recent publicity not initiated by the lawyer or the lawyer's client. A statement made pursuant to this paragraph shall be limited to such information as is necessary to mitigate the recent adverse publicity. Kan. Sup. Ct. R. 226, KRPC 3.6.

Missouri Rule 4-3.6

Missouri Rule 4-3.6 is substantively identical to KRPC 3.6 and tracks ABA Model Rule 3.6. Both rules incorporate the Gentile framework as the constitutional baseline.

The seven categorical safe harbors of 3.6(b)

Subdivision (b) catalogs the categories of statements that are categorically protected. These are not "exceptions" to a rule that otherwise forbids speech; they are an affirmative description of the speech that is plainly safe. They include:

  1. Claim, offense, defense, and identity of parties. Counsel may describe what the case is about and (subject to applicable confidentiality law) who is involved.
  2. Information contained in a public record. Anything on the public docket - filed papers, court orders, transcripts - may be quoted or summarized without further limitation.
  3. That an investigation is in progress. Counsel may confirm that a matter is under investigation.
  4. Scheduling or result of any step in litigation. Hearing dates, motion outcomes, trial scheduling - all may be described.
  5. Request for assistance in obtaining evidence. A public call for witnesses, documents, or information falls within the safe harbor.
  6. Warning of danger. Where there is reason to believe substantial harm threatens, counsel may publicly warn.
  7. Criminal-case identifying and apprehension information. Six specific categories of identifying and procedural information about criminal defendants and arrests.

The right-to-reply framework under 3.6(c)

Subdivision (c) is the affirmative right to reply. It preserves counsel's ability to defend the client from publicity counsel did not initiate. Three operative conditions:

  • A reasonable lawyer must believe the reply is required to protect the client
  • The publicity being responded to must be recent and not initiated by the lawyer or client
  • The reply must be limited to information necessary to mitigate the adverse publicity

The right-to-reply framework recognizes that publicity often flows in only one direction - from prosecutors, from opposing parties, from media reports based on partial information, or from public officials with their own platforms. Counsel for the affected client retains the affirmative ability to respond, and that response is part of the rule's protected zone.

The Gentile framework

The Supreme Court's decision in Gentile v. State Bar of Nevada, 501 U.S. 1030 (1991), supplies the constitutional architecture for Rule 3.6. The Court held that a State may regulate lawyer speech about pending cases under a "substantial likelihood of material prejudice" standard - a standard the Court found constitutionally adequate as applied to lawyers because of the lawyer's role in the administration of justice. At the same time, the Court emphasized that the rule must give clear notice of what is permitted, and that vague safe-harbor provisions may not satisfy due process.

Several propositions follow from Gentile and from the cases applying it:

  • The constitutional standard is "substantial likelihood of material prejudice," not "any possibility of prejudice"
  • The safe harbors of subdivision (b) and the right-of-reply of subdivision (c) must be applied with sufficient clarity to give counsel fair notice of what is permitted
  • The First Amendment interest at stake includes the lawyer's interest in speaking, the client's interest in having an advocate publicly, and the public's interest in information about matters of public concern
  • Restrictions on lawyer speech must be narrowly tailored to the State's interest in fair trials

Affirmative read-out. Counsel who restricts public statements to the seven categorical safe harbors, or who responds to publicity initiated by others within the right-of-reply framework, operates inside the protected zone the rule preserves. The constitutional standard sets the outer perimeter; the safe harbors describe the safe interior; the right of reply describes the protective response.

The substantial-likelihood-of-material-prejudice standard

The general prohibition in subdivision (a) reaches only extrajudicial statements that (i) the lawyer knows or reasonably should know will be disseminated by public communication and (ii) will have a substantial likelihood of materially prejudicing an adjudicative proceeding. Three operative components:

  • Knowledge or reasonable awareness of dissemination. Private statements, statements to other lawyers in the matter, and statements not reasonably expected to be publicly disseminated are not reached.
  • Substantial likelihood. The standard is not "any possibility" but "substantial likelihood" - a heightened threshold the Supreme Court endorsed in Gentile.
  • Material prejudice to an adjudicative proceeding. The prejudice must be to the proceeding itself, not generalized public concern.

Common procedural posture

Press conference at filing

Counsel files a civil complaint and holds a press conference describing the claim, the parties, the relief sought, the hearing schedule, and the filing date. Each of these falls squarely within subdivisions (b)(1) and (b)(4). The press conference is fully within the protected zone.

Reply to adverse press release

An opposing party issues a press release characterizing counsel's client unfavorably. Counsel makes a public statement, limited to factual correction, describing what the public record shows. The statement is within the right-of-reply zone subdivision (c) protects.

Public-record statements as a primary safe harbor

Subdivision (b)(2) - statements of information contained in a public record - is in practice the broadest of the safe harbors. Every filed paper, every docket entry, every court order, every transcript of a public proceeding, every public regulatory filing constitutes a public record for this purpose. Counsel may summarize, quote, link to, or otherwise convey public-record information freely. The Comment to Model Rule 3.6 emphasizes that the public has a legitimate interest in this information and that counsel has a corresponding ability to convey it.

Relationship to other rules

  • Rule 3.3 - Candor toward the tribunal; statements made in court are governed by 3.3 rather than 3.6
  • Rule 3.4 - Fairness to opposing party; 3.4(e) governs in-court references to inadmissible matter
  • Rule 3.8(f) (prosecutors) - Special restrictions on prosecutor extrajudicial statements
  • Rule 1.6 - Confidentiality; public statements must respect client-information limits
  • Rule 8.4 - Misconduct; dishonest public statements may implicate 8.4(c)

Practical impact

The lawyer who restricts public statements to the seven categorical safe harbors and the right-of-reply framework operates within a wide protected zone. Most counsel will find that the cases they handle and the statements they wish to make fall comfortably inside the safe harbors. The constitutional standard articulated in Gentile ensures that the rule cannot be applied in a vague or expansive manner that would chill protected speech.
