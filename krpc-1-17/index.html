KRPC 1.17 / MRPC 4-1.17: Sale of Law Practice

The four conditions enabling practice transition, the client-continuity architecture, and the seller-plus-buyer-plus-transition framework.

Verbatim text of KRPC 1.17 (Kansas)

A lawyer or law firm may sell or purchase a law practice, or an area of law practice, including good will, if the following conditions are satisfied:

(a) The seller ceases to engage in the private practice of law, or in the area of practice that has been sold, from an office that is within the geographic area in which the practice has been conducted;

(b) The entire practice, or the entire area of practice, is sold to one or more lawyers or law firms;

(c) The seller gives written notice to each of the seller's clients regarding:
(1) the proposed sale;
(2) the client's right to retain other counsel or to take possession of the file; and
(3) the fact that the client's consent to the transfer of the client's files will be presumed if the client does not take any action or does not otherwise object within ninety (90) days of receipt of the notice; and

(d) The fees charged clients shall not be increased by reason of the sale. Kan. Sup. Ct. R. 226, KRPC 1.17.

Missouri Rule 4-1.17

Missouri Rule 4-1.17 is substantively parallel and tracks ABA Model Rule 1.17. The rule's purpose, articulated in Comment [1], is to permit the sale of a practice so that retiring or transitioning lawyers can realize the value they have built, while protecting clients with a structured transition architecture.

The four conditions 1.17 enables for practice transition

Rule 1.17 operates as a permission structure. By satisfying four conditions, a lawyer or firm acquires a clean path to transfer a practice that would otherwise be encumbered by the personal nature of the lawyer-client relationship. The four conditions function as enabling architecture rather than as a list of restrictions.

(a) Seller cessation

The seller ceases the private practice (or the area of practice sold) from an office within the geographic area in which the practice has been conducted. The condition ensures the transferred clients are actually transferring, not merely being marketed to a different firm letterhead.

(b) Entire-practice or entire-area scope

The sale must cover the entire practice, or the entire area of practice. Selective transfer of valuable matters while retaining others is not permitted. The condition prevents cherry-picking that would leave behind the matters least attractive to a buyer.

(c) Client-notice framework

Written notice to each affected client, identifying the proposed sale, the right to retain other counsel or take possession of the file, and the 90-day passive-consent window. Notice is the client's mechanism for accepting, redirecting, or terminating the transition.

(d) Fee maintenance

Fees charged to existing clients may not be increased by reason of the sale. The condition prevents the transaction from being financed by client overcharge. The client cannot be made worse off as a price-payer by the change in attorney.

Client-continuity architecture

The four conditions together form the client-continuity architecture. The architecture's central insight is that clients do not transfer automatically with the sale of a practice; the lawyer-client relationship is personal, and the rules of professional conduct do not permit the client to be treated as transferable property. Rule 1.17 substitutes a structured-consent mechanism for ordinary commercial conveyance.

The mechanism has three moving parts:

  1. Written notice initiates the transition and creates a documentary record of what each client was told
  2. Affirmative options - the right to retain other counsel or take possession of the file - preserve client autonomy
  3. The 90-day passive-consent window establishes a default that permits orderly transition without requiring affirmative client action for every file

The 90-day window is structural: it gives the client a meaningful opportunity to evaluate the transition, while preventing the practice from being permanently encumbered by clients who do not respond. The Comment to Model Rule 1.17 emphasizes that the notice should be clear enough to make the choice meaningful, including identification of the buyer and information sufficient for the client to assess the substitution.

Seller, buyer, and transition framework

The rule allocates responsibilities across three roles - seller, buyer, and transition - each with its own competence and continuity obligations.

Seller obligations

  • Cease the practice (or area) from within the geographic area, satisfying condition (a)
  • Sell the entire practice or area, satisfying condition (b)
  • Issue the written notice to every affected client, satisfying condition (c)
  • Refrain from raising fees by reason of the sale, satisfying condition (d)
  • Continue to honor confidentiality under Rule 1.6 through and beyond the transition

Buyer obligations

  • Accept the practice or area in its entirety, including matters that may be less commercially attractive
  • Honor existing client expectations, including fee structures, through condition (d)
  • Apply competent representation under Rule 1.1 to each acquired matter, including matters in unfamiliar substantive areas (which may require declining or referring particular matters rather than acquiring incompetence)
  • Conduct conflicts analysis under Rule 1.7 and Rule 1.9 against the existing client roster of the acquiring firm

Transition-period architecture

The 90-day notice window is itself a transition period. During the window:

  • Active matters require diligence under Rule 1.3 regardless of whether the file ultimately transfers; deadlines do not pause for the notice window
  • Communication under Rule 1.4 continues with the responsible lawyer - the seller before transfer, the buyer after, or the file's new lawyer if the client elects to retain other counsel
  • If the client elects to take possession of the file or retain different counsel, the file-handling duty under Rule 1.16(d) applies; the file is returned to the client or transmitted to successor counsel
  • Confidentiality under Rule 1.6 governs the information flow throughout, including what the buyer may review during due diligence

Jurisdiction continuity and geographic scope

Condition (a) requires cessation from an office "within the geographic area in which the practice has been conducted." The geographic-scope language preserves the client-protection purpose: a seller who continues practicing in the same area would frustrate the structured-transition mechanism by remaining available as the client's lawyer of habit. The geographic scope is calibrated to the practice's actual market.

Jurisdiction continuity also matters for the buyer. A buyer admitted in a different jurisdiction must address admission status before acquiring matters that require local practice. The interaction with Rule 5.5 on unauthorized practice and with Rule 8.5 on disciplinary authority must be analyzed at the front end of the transaction.

The "good will" recognition

The rule's permission to include "good will" in the sale was a significant addition. Earlier versions of the professional conduct rules either prohibited the sale of a practice altogether or permitted only the sale of tangible assets. The current rule recognizes that the value of an established practice - the relationships, the referral network, the reputation - is real and may be transferred for value, subject to the four conditions. The good-will recognition is what makes the rule operationally useful; without it, the practical incentive to comply with the four conditions would be limited.

Relationship to Rule 1.16 (Termination)

A sale under Rule 1.17 effects a termination of the seller's representation of clients who elect to retain other counsel or take possession of their files, and a transition of representation to the buyer for clients who passively consent. The file-return and orderly-transition duties under Rule 1.16(d) apply to the non-transferring clients. Rule 1.17 is best understood as a structured-1.16 procedure for a whole practice, with the notice framework providing the client-protection architecture that ordinary 1.16 termination would otherwise require client-by-client.

Relationship to Rules 1.6, 1.7, and 1.9

Confidentiality under Rule 1.6 governs what the buyer may learn during due diligence. Conflicts analysis under Rule 1.7 and Rule 1.9 must be conducted by the buyer against its existing client roster before the transition is complete; matters that would create a conflict cannot simply be acquired through the 1.17 framework. The interaction is structural: the four conditions of 1.17 do not relieve any other rule's requirements.

Practical impact

Rule 1.17 enables what would otherwise be impossible - the orderly transfer of a law practice for value - by substituting structured client-protection architecture for ordinary commercial conveyance. The four conditions, taken together, allow retiring or transitioning lawyers to realize the value they have built while preserving the client's autonomy and continuity of representation. The rule rewards practices that operate with the kind of file organization, client communication, and fee discipline that make the 1.17 conditions naturally satisfiable; practices that have not invested in those infrastructures find the conditions harder to meet at the moment of transition.
