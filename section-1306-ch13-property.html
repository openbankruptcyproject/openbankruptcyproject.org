11 U.S.C. Section 1306 - Property of the Chapter 13 Estate

Plain-English guide to Section 1306: how the Chapter 13 estate captures post-petition property and wages, why the debtor stays in possession, and the post-confirmation property circuit split.

What Is Section 1306?

Section 1306 is Chapter 13's analogue to Section 541. Section 541 defines what becomes property of the estate at the commencement of any bankruptcy case. Section 1306 extends and modifies that definition specifically for Chapter 13 by sweeping in post-petition property and the debtor's post-petition wages, and by confirming that the debtor (not a trustee) remains in possession of estate property in the ordinary case.

This structural design is what makes the Chapter 13 wage-earner plan possible: the estate continuously refreshes itself with post-petition income, which the debtor then commits to the plan over a three-to-five-year period.

Official citation: 11 U.S.C. § 1306

Section 1306(a): Two Add-Ons to Section 541

Section 1306(a) provides that property of the estate includes, in addition to the property specified in Section 541:

  • 1306(a)(1): All property of the kind specified in Section 541 that the debtor acquires after the commencement of the case but before the case is closed, dismissed, or converted to a case under Chapter 7, 11, or 12, whichever occurs first.
  • 1306(a)(2): Earnings from services performed by the debtor after the commencement of the case but before the case is closed, dismissed, or converted, whichever occurs first.

The first add-on captures post-petition inheritances, lawsuit recoveries, tax refunds, lottery winnings, and other windfalls. The second add-on captures the debtor's wages and other compensation - the lifeblood of the wage-earner plan.

Why Section 541 Excludes Wages and Section 1306 Includes Them

In a Chapter 7 case, Section 541 generally does not capture post-petition earnings of an individual debtor (Section 541(a)(6) carves out "earnings from services performed by an individual debtor after the commencement of the case"). This is why a Chapter 7 debtor can keep all post-petition wages and start fresh. In a Chapter 13 case, Section 1306(a)(2) overrides that exclusion: post-petition earnings are explicitly swept into the estate, which makes them available for plan funding.

The same structural difference holds in Chapter 12 (Section 1207) for family farmers and fishermen, and in Subchapter V of Chapter 11 (where Section 1186 brings post-petition earnings of an individual into the estate).

Section 1306(b): Debtor Remains in Possession

Section 1306(b) provides: "Except as provided in a confirmed plan or order confirming a plan, the debtor shall remain in possession of all property of the estate." This is the Chapter 13 parallel to Section 1107's DIP authority - it ensures the debtor can continue to use, occupy, and operate estate property during the case. The standing trustee under Section 1302 does not displace the debtor's possession; the trustee's role is plan disbursement and oversight, not direct possession.

The Post-Confirmation Property Circuit Split

One of the most-litigated questions in Chapter 13 practice is what happens to estate property after confirmation. Section 1327(b) provides that the confirmation of a plan vests all of the property of the estate in the debtor, "except as otherwise provided in the plan or the order confirming the plan." Section 1306(a) continues to add property to the estate "until the case is closed, dismissed, or converted." How these two provisions interact has produced a long-standing circuit split, with three main approaches:

  • Estate-replenishment approach: Property the debtor acquires after confirmation continues to enter the estate under Section 1306(a) and is then vested in the debtor on a rolling basis.
  • Estate-termination approach: Section 1327(b) terminates the estate at confirmation; post-confirmation property belongs to the debtor outright, with no continuing estate.
  • Estate-preservation approach: The estate continues until the case closes; Section 1327(b) vests property in the debtor for possession but does not eliminate the estate for purposes of Section 1306 or Section 362.

Different circuits have adopted different approaches; practitioners must consult the law of their circuit. The dispute matters most for the automatic stay's application to post-confirmation acquired property, the trustee's standing to recover post-confirmation transfers, and the treatment of post-confirmation tax refunds and inheritances.

Tax Refunds, Inheritances, and Lawsuit Recoveries

Chapter 13 trustees routinely demand turnover of post-petition tax refunds as estate property under Section 1306(a)(1). Many districts have local rules or standing orders that automatically capture tax refunds above a specified threshold for plan distribution. Inheritances received post-petition are also estate property under Section 1306(a)(1); the Section 541(a)(5) 180-day inheritance window of Chapter 7 does not apply to Chapter 13 - any post-petition inheritance during the plan term is in. Pre-petition causes of action remain estate property under Section 541(a)(1); post-petition-arising causes of action enter the estate under Section 1306(a)(1).

Conversion and Dismissal

If a Chapter 13 case is converted to Chapter 7, Section 348(f)(1)(A) provides that property of the estate in the converted case consists of property of the estate as of the date of filing of the petition (not including post-petition acquisitions), unless the conversion is in bad faith. This conversion rule is critical: it generally preserves the Chapter 7 debtor's right to keep post-petition wages and acquisitions even though those items were estate property in the Chapter 13. If a case is dismissed, Section 349(b)(3) revests property of the estate in the debtor as it stood pre-confirmation.
