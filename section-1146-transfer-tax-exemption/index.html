11 U.S.C. Section 1146 - Transfer Tax Exemption Under a Confirmed Plan

Plain-English guide to the Bankruptcy Code's exemption from state and local stamp, recording, and transfer taxes for instruments transferring property under a confirmed Chapter 11 plan, including the Florida v. Piccadilly Cafeterias narrowing.

What Is Section 1146?

Section 1146(a) of the Bankruptcy Code exempts instruments that transfer property "under a plan confirmed under section 1129" from state and local stamp taxes, recording taxes, transfer taxes, and similar levies. The exemption is a federal preemption of state and local revenue measures that would otherwise impose meaningful costs on the asset transfers that implement a confirmed reorganization or liquidation plan.

The economic stakes can be substantial. Real-property transfer taxes in some jurisdictions exceed one percent of the consideration paid. For a plan that contemplates the transfer of a real-estate portfolio worth tens or hundreds of millions of dollars, the tax savings from Section 1146(a) materially affect creditor recoveries. The exemption is therefore one of the most consequential plan-implementation provisions in the Code.

Official citation: 11 U.S.C. § 1146

Section 1146(a): The Text of the Exemption

Section 1146(a) provides: "The issuance, transfer, or exchange of a security, or the making or delivery of an instrument of transfer under a plan confirmed under section 1129 of this title, may not be taxed under any law imposing a stamp tax or similar tax."

The exemption covers two distinct categories. The "issuance, transfer, or exchange of a security" picks up plan-related securities transactions, complementing the Section 1145 registration exemption. The "making or delivery of an instrument of transfer" picks up deeds, assignments, bills of sale, and similar documents that transfer real or personal property pursuant to a plan.

The exempted taxes are those of the "stamp tax or similar tax" family - state and local impositions denominated as transfer taxes, recording taxes, documentary stamp taxes, real-estate transfer taxes, conveyance taxes, and similar levies. The exemption does not reach property taxes (which are ad valorem rather than transfer-based), sales taxes on the underlying goods, income taxes triggered by the transaction, or other taxes outside the stamp-tax family.

Florida v. Piccadilly Cafeterias: The Pre-Confirmation Carve-Out

The Supreme Court's decision in Florida Department of Revenue v. Piccadilly Cafeterias, Inc., 554 U.S. 33 (2008), is the single most important judicial gloss on Section 1146(a). The case resolved a circuit split over whether the "under a plan confirmed" language requires that the transfer actually occur after plan confirmation or whether it suffices that the transfer is contemplated by or part of the eventual plan.

The Court held - by a 7-2 majority - that the exemption applies only to transfers made under a plan that has already been confirmed at the time of the transfer. The plain meaning of "under a plan confirmed under section 1129" requires confirmation as an antecedent to the transfer, not a future event. Transfers made before confirmation - including the increasingly common practice of selling substantially all of a debtor's assets in a pre-confirmation Section 363 sale - are not covered by the exemption.

The decision narrowed Section 1146(a) substantially. Before Piccadilly Cafeterias, several circuits had read the exemption broadly to cover Section 363 sales that were authorized by an interim order with the expectation that they would be incorporated into a later-confirmed plan. After Piccadilly Cafeterias, those sales are subject to state and local transfer taxes unless the parties succeed in another tax-avoidance structure.

Practical impact: Many large Chapter 11 cases now resolve through 363 sales followed by plan confirmation as a "wind-down" mechanism. After Piccadilly Cafeterias, the 363 transfer itself does not enjoy 1146(a) treatment - only post-confirmation transfers (such as distributions of remaining assets under the plan) do. Sale-process bidders should account for the transfer-tax liability in their bids.

What Counts as "Under" a Confirmed Plan

Post-Piccadilly Cafeterias, the operative question for most transfer-tax disputes is whether a given transfer is genuinely "under" a confirmed plan. Two scenarios commonly arise.

Scenario 1: Plan-Mandated Post-Confirmation Sales

A plan may provide that certain assets will be sold post-confirmation by a liquidating trustee, plan administrator, or reorganized debtor to fund plan distributions. Sales pursuant to such plan provisions are squarely within the exemption: the transfer occurs after confirmation, and the transfer is necessary to implement plan provisions. Most bankruptcy courts hold that the exemption applies whether the buyer is identified in the plan or selected through a post-confirmation sale process.

Scenario 2: Post-Confirmation Refinancings and Restructurings

A plan may contemplate that the reorganized debtor will refinance its real-property portfolio, transfer assets to newly-formed subsidiaries, or implement other post-emergence transactions. The exemption typically applies to transactions that are specifically provided for in the plan or are reasonably necessary to consummate the plan. Transactions that are merely permitted by the plan but not required by it are more contested - some courts apply the exemption broadly, others require a tighter nexus.

Section 1146(b): Reduction of Transfer-Tax Refund Claims

Section 1146(b) addresses the related question of whether the debtor or the estate may seek a refund of taxes already paid under state or local law. The provision generally permits the court to refund taxes that were paid under protest where the underlying obligation was discharged or otherwise affected by the plan. The procedural mechanics interact with state-law refund procedures, and counsel should be careful to preserve the federal claim through proper protest filings at the time of payment.

State Resistance and Federal Preemption

States have, at various points, attempted to impose taxes on plan-related transfers under theories that the Bankruptcy Code does not preempt their revenue measures. The Supreme Court's decision in California State Board of Equalization v. Sierra Summit, Inc., 490 U.S. 844 (1989), and other authorities generally confirm that 1146(a) is a valid exercise of federal preemption authority where its terms are satisfied. The dispute is no longer whether 1146(a) preempts state tax laws when it applies, but whether it applies to a given transaction at all - a question now substantially controlled by Piccadilly Cafeterias.

Practical Drafting Considerations

Plan drafters who want to maximize 1146(a) coverage typically structure significant asset transfers as post-confirmation transactions, even when the underlying economic deal is negotiated earlier. The mechanics include identifying assets to be transferred in the plan and disclosure statement, defining the transfer mechanism, and specifying that transfers will occur after the effective date.

Where pre-confirmation sales are unavoidable, sale orders are sometimes drafted to incorporate provisions that the transaction be "effectuated under and pursuant to" the eventually-confirmed plan, but such provisions do not retroactively cure the timing problem identified in Piccadilly Cafeterias. Most counsel today treat pre-confirmation 363 sales as subject to transfer taxes and price the obligation accordingly.

Buyers in post-confirmation sale processes should verify that the seller (debtor, reorganized debtor, or liquidating trustee) holds clear authority under the confirmed plan and that the transfer documents reference the plan, the confirmation order, and Section 1146(a). Recording officers occasionally require this paperwork to process exemption claims at the time of recording.

Related Bankruptcy Code Sections

Section 1146 operates in concert with several other provisions of the Bankruptcy Code:

  • Section 363 - Use, sale, or lease of property (the pre-confirmation sale mechanism)
  • Section 1129 - Plan confirmation (the predicate for 1146(a))
  • Section 1141 - Effect of confirmation
  • Section 1145 - Securities Act exemption (the companion plan-implementation exemption)
  • Section 1123 - Contents of a plan

Understanding how these sections interact is important for debtors, creditors, trustees, asset buyers, and counsel structuring or implementing a Chapter 11 plan that contemplates real-property or other taxable transfers.
