KRPC 6.3 / MRPC 4-6.3: Membership in Legal Services Organization

A lawyer's authorized participation as director, officer, or member of a legal services organization that serves persons other than the lawyer's clients, subject to two protective conditions.

Verbatim text of KRPC 6.3 (Kansas)

A lawyer may serve as a director, officer or member of a legal services organization, apart from the law firm in which the lawyer practices, notwithstanding that the organization serves persons having interests adverse to a client of the lawyer. The lawyer shall not knowingly participate in a decision or action of the organization:
(a) if participating in the decision or action would be incompatible with the lawyer's obligations to a client under Rule 1.7; or
(b) where the decision or action could have a material adverse effect on the representation of a client of the organization whose interests are adverse to a client of the lawyer. Kan. Sup. Ct. R. 226, KRPC 6.3.

Missouri Rule 4-6.3

Missouri Rule 4-6.3 is substantively parallel to KRPC 6.3 and tracks ABA Model Rule 6.3. The rule operates as an affirmative authorization for lawyer participation in the governance of legal services organizations, even where those organizations serve persons whose interests are adverse to the lawyer's clients. The authorization is then qualified by two narrowly defined protective conditions.

The structural premise

The Comment to Model Rule 6.3 frames the rule's purpose: legal services organizations - civil legal aid, public-interest legal centers, indigent-defense entities, court-affiliated legal services programs, bar-sponsored access-to-justice initiatives - depend on the participation of practicing lawyers in their governance and operations. The contributions practicing lawyers bring include subject-matter expertise, bar-network connections, fundraising capacity, and the credibility that comes with active practice. Without their participation, the organizations cannot operate effectively.

At the same time, the lawyers most useful to such organizations are typically engaged in active practice, in which they represent clients whose interests will sometimes be adverse to those of the persons the organization serves. The rule resolves the tension by authorizing the participation as the default and then articulating the discrete circumstances in which the lawyer's role on the organization must be limited.

The affirmative grant

The rule's opening sentence is the affirmative grant: a lawyer may serve as director, officer, or member of a legal services organization, apart from the law firm in which the lawyer practices, notwithstanding that the organization serves persons having interests adverse to the lawyer's clients. Several features of the grant warrant attention:

  • "May serve." The grant is permissive. The rule does not require lawyer participation; it authorizes it.
  • "Director, officer, or member." The grant covers the full range of governance and membership roles - board service, executive committee positions, officer roles, advisory roles, and general organizational membership.
  • "Apart from the law firm." The grant addresses participation in organizations separate from the lawyer's firm. The provision distinguishes organizational governance from in-firm practice.
  • "Notwithstanding that the organization serves persons having interests adverse to a client of the lawyer." The grant explicitly authorizes participation even where the organization's client base includes interests adverse to the lawyer's clients. The rule recognizes that this is the norm rather than the exception in active practice.

The two protective conditions

The grant is qualified by two protective conditions that limit what the lawyer may do within the organization. The conditions are narrowly drawn and operate at the level of specific decisions or actions, not at the level of overall participation. The lawyer remains a director, officer, or member; the conditions identify the discrete decisions in which the lawyer does not participate.

6.3(a) - Compatibility with Rule 1.7 obligations

The lawyer shall not knowingly participate in a decision or action of the organization if participating would be incompatible with the lawyer's obligations to a client under Rule 1.7. The condition imports the current-client conflict framework. The compatibility inquiry asks whether the lawyer's vote, recommendation, or other contribution to the decision would place the lawyer in conflict with the duties owed to a current client.

Compatibility is evaluated decision by decision. A board member who has a Rule 1.7 conflict with respect to a particular matter the board is considering recuses from that matter; the same member may participate in other matters in which no such conflict exists. The condition operates as a discrete-decision recusal rule, not as a categorical disqualification from board service.

6.3(b) - Material adverse effect on organizational clients

The lawyer shall not knowingly participate in a decision or action that could have a material adverse effect on the representation of an organizational client whose interests are adverse to a client of the lawyer. The condition extends the protective framework to the organization's clients: even where the lawyer's participation would not violate Rule 1.7 vis-a-vis the lawyer's own client, the lawyer abstains from decisions that would materially adversely affect the organization's representation of a person whose interests are adverse to the lawyer's client.

The "could have a material adverse effect" standard is forward-looking and probabilistic. The decision need not be certain to produce the effect; the standard is whether the effect is a material adverse possibility. The "material" qualifier excludes trivial or speculative effects.

Recusal is from the decision, not from the organization. The two conditions operate as discrete-decision recusal requirements. A lawyer who recuses from particular decisions because of (a) or (b) remains an active director, officer, or member of the organization with respect to all other matters within the organization's work. The structural feature is preservation of broad participation with narrow recusal in protected categories.

The "apart from the law firm" framing

The grant authorizes participation in organizations "apart from the law firm." The framing distinguishes the lawyer's role on the legal services organization from the lawyer's practice within the firm. The organization is its own institutional entity, governed by its own board, employing its own staff, and representing its own client base. The lawyer's role within the organization is governed by the organization's bylaws and policies, with Rule 6.3 supplying the professional-conduct anchor on the lawyer-conduct side.

Several practical features follow from this framing:

  • The organization's representations of its own clients are governed by the organization's own conflict-checking procedures and counsel-assignment practices. Those representations do not, by virtue of the lawyer's board membership, become representations by the lawyer or the lawyer's firm.
  • The lawyer's board role does not, by itself, give the lawyer's firm access to confidential information about the organization's clients. The organization's confidentiality framework operates independently.
  • The organization may handle its own conflict-checking with respect to board members by procedures that exclude board members from particular matter assignments or that segregate matter information from board-level decisions.

Common organizational structures

Civil legal aid board service

A lawyer in private practice serves on the board of a civil legal aid organization that provides representation to low-income individuals in landlord-tenant, consumer, family, and bankruptcy matters. The lawyer's firm represents landlords, consumer lenders, and creditors in some of these categories. Rule 6.3 authorizes the board service. The lawyer recuses from board decisions that bear directly on a particular matter in which the organization is opposing the lawyer's firm (e.g., a strategic decision about a categorical litigation campaign that would affect a pending firm matter), and otherwise participates fully in the organization's governance.

Pro bono coordinating organization

A lawyer serves as an officer of a bar-sponsored pro bono coordinating organization that places matters with volunteer lawyers across multiple practice areas. The lawyer's organizational role - recruiting volunteers, setting program priorities, fundraising - is broadly authorized by Rule 6.3 and rarely implicates either (a) or (b). The organization's individual-matter decisions are made by program staff and the volunteer lawyers who accept placements, not by the officer-lawyer.

Recordkeeping at the organization level

While Rule 6.3 itself does not impose recordkeeping requirements, organizations with active board members in private practice commonly maintain procedures that document compliance with the rule's protective conditions:

  • Conflict-disclosure forms that board members complete at intake and update periodically
  • Pre-meeting conflict-checking against the organization's matter docket
  • Recusal records noting which board members abstained from which decisions, with the recusal ground identified
  • Information-barrier procedures separating board-level discussion from matter-level case information when warranted

These procedures support both the individual lawyer's compliance with Rule 6.3 and the organization's institutional governance.

Relationship to other rules

  • Rule 1.7 - Conflict of interest with current clients; imported by 6.3(a)
  • Rule 1.6 - Confidentiality; relevant where organizational information might bear on a firm matter or vice versa
  • Rule 1.10 - Imputed disqualification; relevant in evaluating whether the lawyer's organizational role affects the firm's conflict analysis
  • Rule 6.5 - Nonprofit and court-annexed limited legal services programs; the related framework for the lawyer's direct service through such programs

Continuing nature of the inquiry

The compatibility analysis under 6.3(a) and the material-adverse-effect analysis under 6.3(b) are not one-time inquiries. They are conducted with respect to each decision the organization addresses in which the lawyer's participation is contemplated. A lawyer with a continuing board role re-evaluates each board agenda for items implicating the conditions and recuses as appropriate.

Practical impact

Rule 6.3 carries several recurring practical consequences:

  • Participation is the affirmative default. The rule's grant is permissive and broad. Lawyers in private practice may participate fully in the governance of legal services organizations even where their firm's client base produces some overlap with the organization's client base.
  • Recusal is matter-specific. The two protective conditions operate at the decision level, not at the membership level. A lawyer with a 6.3(a) or 6.3(b) issue on a particular matter recuses from that matter and continues in the role generally.
  • The organization and the lawyer's firm remain institutionally separate. The "apart from the law firm" framing preserves the organization's institutional independence. The organization handles its own representations through its own conflict-checking and counsel-assignment procedures.
