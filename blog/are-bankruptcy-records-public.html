Are Bankruptcy Records Public? Yes, Under 11 USC 107(a)

Federal bankruptcy filings are public records by statute and by Supreme Court precedent. The only narrow remedy that operates on the underlying docket is FRBP 9037(d).

Published May 18, 2026

People are often surprised to learn that the bankruptcy case they filed shows up on a Google search. The reason is structural: federal bankruptcy filings are public records by federal statute, not by editorial choice of any particular website. This post walks through the statute, the controlling Supreme Court case, what is and is not redactable, and the one procedural path that actually operates on the underlying court record.

The Statutory Rule: 11 USC 107(a)

The starting point is 11 USC 107(a). It is short and unambiguous:

"Except as provided in subsections (b) and (c) and subject to section 112, a paper filed in a case under this title and the dockets of a bankruptcy court are public records and open to examination by an entity at reasonable times without charge."

Three things follow from this. First, the default for everything filed in a bankruptcy case is public. Second, the publicness is conferred by federal statute, not by any third-party website. Third, the right of access is free at the point of use ("without charge").

The Common-Law Anchor: Nixon v. Warner Communications

The statutory rule is reinforced by a common-law right of public access to judicial records that the Supreme Court confirmed in Nixon v. Warner Communications, 435 U.S. 589 (1978). The Court held that the public has a general right "to inspect and copy public records and documents, including judicial records and documents." This common-law right operates independently of any particular statute. Even in a hypothetical world where 11 USC 107(a) did not exist, the Nixon presumption would still apply to bankruptcy filings as judicial records.

The two anchors together make bankruptcy public records doubly insulated from informal removal demands. A request to take a filing offline because it is "embarrassing" or "could affect a career" runs into both the federal statute and binding Supreme Court precedent.

What Counts as "Public" in Practice

The official source is PACER (Public Access to Court Electronic Records). PACER is the federal judiciary's electronic record system; anyone with an account can read filings on any federal bankruptcy case. PACER charges per page (currently $0.10 per page, capped at $3 per document).

Because the statutory rule makes the data public, a number of mirror archives have grown up around PACER. The largest is the CourtListener RECAP Archive, operated by the 501(c)(3) Free Law Project. RECAP republishes PACER documents for free at the point of use. The Open Bankruptcy Project is a similar mirror: a 501(c)(3) that indexes federal bankruptcy dockets so users can find their case (or a relative's case, or a creditor's case) without paying PACER fees.

None of these mirrors create the publicness. They republish what is already public by statute. Asking a mirror to remove a case but leaving the official PACER docket untouched accomplishes nothing structurally - the next pull from any mirror will re-introduce the same information from the same source.

The One Path That Operates on the Record: FRBP 9037(d)

The Federal Rules of Bankruptcy Procedure include a narrow remedy that does operate on the underlying court file. Rule 9037 governs how protected personal identifiers must appear in filings.

Subsection (a) sets the front-end rule. When filing a document in a bankruptcy case, the filer may include only:

  • The last four digits of a Social Security number or taxpayer-identification number.
  • The year of an individual's date of birth.
  • A minor's initials only.
  • The last four digits of a financial-account number.

Subsection (d) is the back-end remedy. When a document was filed without the required redactions, the court may, on motion, order the filer to redact and refile, restrict remote electronic access to the unredacted document, file a redacted version with the unredacted version filed under seal, or seal a specific document for cause shown.

What 9037(d) Does Not Do

A Rule 9037(d) motion is not a vehicle for hiding the existence of a bankruptcy filing. The case caption, the parties' names, the chapter, the filing date, the dockets, and the docket entries remain public after a successful 9037(d) motion. What changes is that protected personal identifiers (full SSN, full date of birth, account numbers, minor children's names) are removed from individual documents.

For anything beyond protected identifiers, the bar is much higher. 11 USC 107(b) permits the court to protect "trade secrets or confidential research, development, or commercial information" and to keep "scandalous or defamatory matter" out of public view. Section 107(c) addresses information that would create "undue risk of identity theft or other unlawful injury to the individual or the individual's property." Sealing under these provisions requires a specific factual showing, not a generalized concern about reputation.

Practical Takeaway

Three points stand out for anyone whose bankruptcy filing has surfaced in a Google search:

  • Existence of the filing is not removable. The case caption and filing date are public by federal statute and Supreme Court precedent. No mirror, court order, or removal request changes that as a structural matter.
  • Personal identifiers may be redactable. If full SSN, full date of birth, account numbers, or a minor child's name appears in any filed document, a FRBP 9037(d) motion can have those redacted from the official record. Mirrors will pick up the redacted version on their next pull.
  • Removal demands sent to mirrors do not reach PACER. The publicness lives at the federal court. Mirrors republish what PACER publishes. The procedural remedy lives at the court that holds the docket - not at any mirror.

If a bankruptcy is causing real-world harm - employment-decision discrimination, denial of credit beyond what the FCRA reporting periods would predict, identity-theft exposure - the path runs through the bankruptcy court that issued the docket, not through informal contact with a republisher. The Open Bankruptcy Project, like every other PACER mirror, follows whatever the underlying federal court orders.

Need to look up a specific case? You can search OBP's free index of federal bankruptcy filings, or read the full docket on CourtListener or PACER.

For questions about removing protected identifiers from a specific case, consult a bankruptcy attorney who can prepare a Rule 9037(d) motion in the court that holds the docket.
