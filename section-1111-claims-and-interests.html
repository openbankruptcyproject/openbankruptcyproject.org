11 U.S.C. Section 1111 - Claims and Interests in Chapter 11

Two distinct rules in one section: deemed-filed scheduled claims, and the 1111(b) election that lets an undersecured creditor convert its deficiency into a fully secured claim.

Home › Statutes › 11 U.S.C. Section 1111 - Claims and Interests

What Is Section 1111?

Section 1111 contains two unrelated but procedurally important rules that apply only in Chapter 11 cases. Subsection (a) addresses how scheduled claims are treated for proof-of-claim purposes; subsection (b) creates the famous "1111(b) election" that allows an undersecured creditor to elect to have its entire claim treated as secured for plan-distribution purposes.

Official citation: 11 U.S.C. § 1111

Section 1111(a): Deemed-Filed Claims

In Chapter 7 and Chapter 13 cases, every creditor who wishes to participate in distributions must file a proof of claim. In Chapter 11, Section 1111(a) provides a meaningful shortcut: a claim or interest scheduled by the debtor is "deemed filed" under Section 501 unless the debtor has scheduled the claim as disputed, contingent, or unliquidated. A creditor whose claim appears in the debtor's schedules in an undisputed, fixed, liquidated amount need not file a proof of claim to share in distributions.

This rule reduces administrative friction in large cases and shifts responsibility for accurate scheduling onto the debtor. Where the debtor schedules a claim as disputed, contingent, or unliquidated, the creditor must file a timely proof of claim to participate. The proof-of-claim bar date set under Bankruptcy Rule 3003(c)(3) governs the deadline.

Section 1111(b): The Undersecured Creditor Election

Section 1111(b) is one of the most analytically complex provisions in the Bankruptcy Code. It addresses what happens to the deficiency portion of an undersecured creditor's claim in Chapter 11.

By default, under Section 506(a), an undersecured creditor's claim is bifurcated: a secured claim equal to the value of the collateral, plus an unsecured deficiency claim. The unsecured deficiency is typically treated with general unsecured claims and may receive only pennies on the dollar.

Section 1111(b) gives the undersecured creditor two options:

Option 1: Recourse Treatment (1111(b)(1)(A))

The creditor accepts the bifurcation. It has an allowed secured claim equal to collateral value, and an allowed unsecured deficiency claim that will be treated like other unsecured claims. Under 1111(b)(1)(A), even nonrecourse claims are treated as if they had recourse against the debtor, so the deficiency portion can vote and share in unsecured distributions. (Exception: claims subject to a sale under Section 363 or under the plan are not converted.)

Option 2: The 1111(b) Election (1111(b)(2))

The creditor elects to have its claim treated as fully secured. The unsecured deficiency disappears. In return, the plan must provide for payments with a present value at least equal to the collateral value, but with total nominal payments at least equal to the full claim amount.

This election is most powerful when collateral is expected to appreciate, when the debtor proposes a quick payoff of only the secured-claim value, or when the creditor wants to lock in the right to recover the full debt if the debtor later sells the collateral or refinances. The trade-off is that the creditor surrenders its unsecured vote and its share of unsecured distributions.

When the Election Is Available

The 1111(b) election is available only to a class of claims whose collateral has a value as of the petition date that is less than the amount of the secured creditor's allowed claim. It is unavailable where (a) the creditor's interest is of inconsequential value, or (b) the creditor has recourse against the debtor and the property is to be sold under Section 363 or under the plan.

The election is a class election made under Bankruptcy Rule 3014: a class of secured creditors makes the election by majority vote in number and at least two-thirds in amount. In practice this typically means a single-creditor class because most undersecured collateral pools have only one lender.

Interaction with Cramdown Under Section 1129(b)

The 1111(b) election directly affects the cramdown calculus under Section 1129(b). A class that has made the election cannot be crammed down using the standard 1129(b)(2)(A)(i) deferred-cash-payments test alone if the present value of those payments equals only the collateral value: the plan must also satisfy the nominal-total requirement of Section 1111(b)(2). Plans that attempt to strip down an elected class by paying only collateral value over time will fail confirmation.

Conversely, debtors with appreciating collateral can defeat an electing class's leverage by paying off the secured claim quickly (within months), in which case the higher nominal total is achieved by simple full payment of the collateral value.

Major Doctrinal Cases

  • In re Tampa Bay Associates, Ltd. (Bankr. M.D. Fla. 1989) and progeny analyzed when the election is unavailable because property is "of inconsequential value."
  • In re 222 Liberty Associates (Bankr. E.D. Pa. 1989) discussed the proper present-value discount rate for measuring the 1111(b)(2)(A)(i) "value, as of the effective date" requirement.
  • In re Atlanta Southern Business Park, Ltd. (Bankr. N.D. Ga. 1996) addressed the election in the context of a single-asset real estate debtor.
  • In re SM 104 Limited (Bankr. S.D.N.Y. 1995) is a leading discussion of strategic considerations in making or declining the election.
  • In re Westwood Plaza Apartments, Ltd. (Bankr. E.D. Tex. 1993) addressed Rule 3014 procedure and the timing of the election relative to the disclosure-statement hearing.

The 1111(b) election is also analyzed extensively in Collier on Bankruptcy and in the practitioner literature surrounding single-asset real estate cases under Section 101(51B).
