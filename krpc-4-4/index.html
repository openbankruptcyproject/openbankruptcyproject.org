KRPC 4.4 / MRPC 4-4.4: Respect for Rights of Third Persons

The lawyer's duties to non-clients during the course of representation, including the substantial-purpose limitation on means and the inadvertent-document procedure.

Verbatim text of KRPC 4.4 (Kansas)

(a) In representing a client, a lawyer shall not use means that have no substantial purpose other than to embarrass, delay, or burden a third person, or use methods of obtaining evidence that violate the legal rights of such a person.

(b) A lawyer who receives a document or electronically stored information relating to the representation of the lawyer's client and knows or reasonably should know that the document or electronically stored information was inadvertently sent shall promptly notify the sender. Kan. Sup. Ct. R. 226, KRPC 4.4.

Missouri Rule 4-4.4

Missouri Rule 4-4.4 is substantively parallel to KRPC 4.4 and tracks ABA Model Rule 4.4. The rule frames the lawyer's relationship to non-clients during the course of representation. Subdivision (a) calibrates the lawyer's choice of means by reference to substantial purpose and to the legal rights of third persons. Subdivision (b) establishes a specific procedure for handling inadvertently transmitted documents and electronic information.

The structural premise

The Comment to Model Rule 4.4 frames the rule's premise: the duty of zealous representation to one's client operates within a framework that respects the legal rights of others. The rule does not catalog all of those rights - it directs the lawyer outward to the substantive law that defines them and provides a professional-conduct anchor for compliance.

The rule is structured permissively rather than as a general standard of civility. Lawyers may pursue vigorous representation - including conduct that incidentally burdens or inconveniences third persons - so long as that conduct has a substantial purpose tied to the representation and the methods used to obtain evidence stay within the bounds the law sets for the rights of others.

4.4(a) - The substantial-purpose framework

Subdivision (a) operates as a means-calibration rule. It contains two distinct provisions:

The "no substantial purpose" branch

The first branch directs that the lawyer shall not use means that have "no substantial purpose other than to embarrass, delay, or burden a third person." The framing is in the negative, but its operational effect is to identify a permitted zone. Conduct that has any substantial purpose tied to the representation - obtaining relevant evidence, advancing a legal position, protecting a client right, satisfying a procedural requirement - is within the rule even where it incidentally burdens a third person. The prohibition applies only where the lawyer's means have no purpose other than to impose burden, delay, or embarrassment.

The "substantial" qualifier is significant. Marginal or pretextual purposes do not satisfy the standard. The substantial-purpose inquiry is objective: would a reasonable lawyer perceive a legitimate purpose for the means employed, beyond the imposition of burden?

The "legal rights" branch

The second branch directs that the lawyer shall not use methods of obtaining evidence that violate the legal rights of third persons. The provision incorporates the body of substantive law that defines third-person rights in evidence-gathering contexts - privacy statutes, anti-eavesdropping laws, computer-access statutes, statutes governing recording of communications, rules governing access to medical and financial records, and analogous bodies of law. The professional-conduct dimension overlays the substantive law: methods that violate the underlying law are also a violation of 4.4(a).

By inverse, the rule recognizes that methods of evidence gathering authorized by law - formal discovery, public-record requests, properly noticed subpoenas, interviews conducted with informed consent, observation of public conduct - are within the rule's permission, even where the resulting evidence is unfavorable to or causes inconvenience to the third person.

4.4(b) - The inadvertent-document procedure

Subdivision (b) addresses the specific situation of a document or electronically stored information that a lawyer receives and that the lawyer knows or reasonably should know was inadvertently sent. The rule establishes a single procedural obligation: prompt notification of the sender.

The triggering conditions

  • Receipt of a document or ESI relating to the representation. The rule applies to materials the receiving lawyer comes into possession of through the conduct of the representation - email transmissions, file productions, fax communications, electronic file transfers, and analogous channels.
  • Knowledge or constructive knowledge of inadvertence. The lawyer must know, or reasonably should know, that the transmission was inadvertent. Constructive knowledge can arise from indicia on the face of the transmission (an obviously misdirected email, a privilege-log entry mistakenly included in a production, an attached file that does not match the body of the message) or from the surrounding circumstances.

The required action

The action the rule requires is prompt notification of the sender. The rule does not, in its text, address what the receiving lawyer must do with the materials themselves - whether to return, sequester, delete, or read - and leaves those questions to substantive law and to the lawyer's professional judgment in light of the duties owed to the receiving lawyer's own client. The Comment to Model Rule 4.4 notes that the rule's text addresses only the notification obligation; broader questions of return, use, or disqualification are governed by other law and by the lawyer's evaluation of countervailing duties.

The "reasonably should know" element is objective. Subdivision (b)'s notification duty attaches where the inadvertence is reasonably apparent, even if the receiving lawyer has not in fact recognized it. A document that is obviously misdirected, or a production that obviously includes materials the producing party intended to withhold, triggers the duty regardless of the receiving lawyer's subjective state of mind.

The "no substantial purpose" inquiry in practice

The substantial-purpose analysis under 4.4(a) considers conduct in light of its connection to the representation. Several categories of conduct routinely satisfy the substantial-purpose test:

  • Discovery requests tied to the issues in the case. A document request, deposition notice, or subpoena that seeks evidence relevant to a claim or defense has a substantial purpose tied to the representation, even where compliance imposes burden on a third party. Proportionality and other discovery-rule limits operate independently of Rule 4.4.
  • Subpoenas for records held by third parties. Records subpoenas issued through proper procedural channels have a substantial purpose where the records are relevant to the matter.
  • Witness interviews. Interviewing a witness who has information relevant to the matter has a substantial purpose, conducted within the bounds of Rule 4.2 (represented persons) and Rule 4.3 (unrepresented persons).
  • Public-record requests. Requests to government agencies under public-records statutes have a substantial purpose when the records bear on the matter.

Common procedural contexts

Inadvertent disclosure in document production

A producing party transmits a large document production that, on review, contains materials marked privileged on the producing party's privilege log. The receiving lawyer's reasonable review reveals the discrepancy. Subdivision (b) requires prompt notification of the sender. The receiving lawyer then evaluates the producing party's clawback procedure under the applicable procedural rules and the standing order in the case, and acts in accordance with those external frameworks while satisfying the rule's notification requirement.

Subpoena to a non-party records custodian

A lawyer issues a properly noticed subpoena to a third-party records custodian (medical provider, bank, employer) for records relevant to the client's claims or defenses. The subpoena imposes compliance burden on the custodian. The substantial-purpose inquiry under 4.4(a) is satisfied by the relevance of the records to the matter; the legal-rights inquiry is satisfied by the use of an authorized procedural channel (the subpoena) issued in conformity with the applicable rule, with any privacy-statute requirements satisfied separately.

Relationship to evidence-gathering and discovery rules

Rule 4.4 operates alongside the body of substantive and procedural law governing evidence gathering. The lawyer's professional-conduct compliance with 4.4(a) is assessed against the backdrop of the procedural rules that authorize particular methods (formal discovery rules, subpoena procedures, third-party records procedures) and the substantive rules that limit them (privilege, privacy statutes, anti-eavesdropping statutes). Conformity with the operative substantive and procedural framework is the typical path to compliance with 4.4(a)'s legal-rights branch.

Practical impact

Rule 4.4 carries several recurring practical consequences:

  • Substantial purpose is the affirmative key. Lawyers conducting vigorous representation are within the rule so long as the means employed have a substantial purpose tied to the representation. The rule does not require minimization of all burden to third persons; it requires that burden not be the sole or principal purpose.
  • Methods of evidence gathering are bounded by external law. The "legal rights" branch of 4.4(a) directs lawyers to the substantive law governing the rights of third persons in evidence-gathering contexts. Compliance with the operative substantive framework is the typical path to compliance with the rule.
  • Inadvertent-document handling is procedurally specific. Subdivision (b) imposes a discrete notification obligation when the conditions are met. Questions of return, use, or disqualification are governed by other law.
