Rule of Professional Conduct KRPC 3.1 / MRPC 4-3.1

KRPC 3.1 / MRPC 4-3.1: Meritorious Claims and Contentions

The good-faith litigation architecture - basis in law or fact, room for good-faith arguments to extend or change the law, and the constitutional carve-out for criminal defense.

Good-faith litigation architecture that 3.1 protects

Rule 3.1 is best read as an architecture for legitimate advocacy. It defines the floor at which advocacy enjoys full protection: any claim or defense with a non-frivolous basis in existing law and the facts, or any claim or defense that rests on a good-faith argument for an extension, modification, or reversal of existing law. The rule is permissive at its core - it preserves the lawyer's freedom to advance every meritorious position and every novel position that is honestly grounded in research and reflection.

The rule's affirmative function appears throughout published authority: it identifies what advocacy is protected, what theories are open to counsel, and what room exists for argument that the prevailing rule should change. Courts and bar authorities have repeatedly emphasized that 3.1 is not designed to penalize bold theory or unsettled questions - it is designed to give those theories a stable home so long as they are honestly held and minimally supported.

Verbatim text of KRPC 3.1 (Kansas)

A lawyer shall not bring or defend a proceeding, or assert or controvert an issue therein, unless there is a basis in law and fact for doing so that is not frivolous, which includes a good-faith argument for an extension, modification or reversal of existing law. A lawyer for the defendant in a criminal proceeding, or the respondent in a proceeding that could result in incarceration, may nevertheless so defend the proceeding as to require that every element of the case be established. Kan. Sup. Ct. R. 226, KRPC 3.1.

Missouri Rule 4-3.1

Missouri Rule 4-3.1 is substantively identical to KRPC 3.1 and tracks ABA Model Rule 3.1. Both rules cast the duty in terms of what advocacy is permitted, with the negative formulation (no frivolous claims) serving only to mark the outer edge of the protected zone.

The non-frivolous-basis standard, read affirmatively

Counsel may bring or defend any proceeding for which there is a non-frivolous basis in law and fact. That single sentence opens a wide field. A claim is non-frivolous when a reasonable lawyer, after such inquiry as is reasonable under the circumstances, could conclude that the claim has at least colorable support. The standard does not require certainty of success; it does not require freedom from doubt; it does not require that the claim be the strongest claim available.

The Comment to Model Rule 3.1 emphasizes that the filing of an action or defense is not frivolous merely because the facts have not first been fully substantiated, or because the lawyer expects to develop vital evidence only by discovery. Counsel may file based on the information available at filing, then develop the evidentiary record through the discovery tools the court system provides for that purpose. The rule respects the reality that civil litigation is in part an information-gathering exercise.

Good-faith argument for an extension, modification, or reversal

The second clause of the rule is the engine of legal development. Every published change in doctrine - every overruled precedent, every recognized cause of action, every newly limited defense - began as a good-faith argument that the existing rule should change. Rule 3.1 protects that argument explicitly.

To qualify, the argument must be (a) made in good faith and (b) directed at extending existing law (applying a principle to a new context), modifying existing law (refining the rule's contours), or reversing existing law (overturning a precedent the lawyer believes is wrongly decided). Each of these is an affirmatively protected mode of advocacy. The Comment notes that the lawyer's belief need not be objectively certain; the belief must be honestly held and grounded in some recognized methodology of legal reasoning.

Practical implications:

  • Cases of first impression are protected. Where no on-point authority exists, counsel may proceed on principled extension from analogous authority.
  • Circuit splits create space. Where authority is divided, the choice to advance the position adopted by some courts and rejected by others is not frivolous.
  • Doctrinal evolution is contemplated. Arguments that an older rule should yield to a newer principle, or that statutory interpretation should be updated, are within the protected zone.

The criminal-defense carve-out under 3.1

The rule's final sentence creates a specific zone of expanded protection for criminal-defense counsel and respondents in proceedings that could result in incarceration. Defense counsel may require that every element of the case be established by the government, irrespective of counsel's personal assessment of the defendant's guilt. This is not a side note - it is a constitutional accommodation embedded in the rule.

The carve-out reflects the structural premise of the adversary criminal system: the government bears the burden, and the defendant's right to require proof of every element is part of the due-process architecture. Defense counsel honors that architecture by holding the government to its proof, and Rule 3.1 makes clear that doing so is not a violation of the meritorious-claim duty even where counsel privately doubts the strength of the defense.

Affirmative read-out. Rule 3.1 should be understood as protecting four categories of advocacy: (1) claims and defenses with conventional support in law and fact; (2) claims and defenses with colorable support pending discovery; (3) good-faith arguments to extend, modify, or reverse existing law; and (4) the full defense of any criminal or incarceration-eligible respondent. These four categories cover the substantial majority of advocacy that counsel will ever undertake.

Common procedural posture

Filing on incomplete evidence

Counsel files a complaint based on information known at intake plus reasonable inferences, with the understanding that discovery will refine the factual record. Under 3.1 and its Comment, this is protected. The duty is to have a reasonable basis at filing, not to have completed the discovery the case is designed to develop.

Advancing a novel constitutional theory

Counsel raises a constitutional argument that no controlling court has accepted. Under the good-faith-argument clause, the theory is protected so long as it is honestly grounded in recognized legal reasoning and the lawyer holds the position in good faith. Doctrinal evolution depends on exactly this kind of advocacy.

Reasonable inquiry as the gatekeeping concept

The Comment ties the non-frivolous-basis standard to "such inquiry into the facts and law as is reasonable under the circumstances." Reasonable inquiry is the operational gatekeeper between the protected zone and the very narrow zone the rule excludes. The depth of inquiry expected scales with the stakes of the matter, the time available before filing, and the access counsel has to the relevant facts and authorities.

An emergency filing on a temporary restraining order does not require the same depth of pre-filing investigation as a complex commercial complaint filed under no time pressure. The rule is calibrated to context, and the Comment expressly recognizes that pre-filing investigation may be limited where the circumstances justify limitation.

Relationship to Rule 11 / Rule 9011

Rule 3.1 is the professional-responsibility analog to Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 11 and Federal Rule of Bankruptcy Procedure 9011. Each operates as a check on filings without basis, and each preserves the same affirmative zone for good-faith argument. A filing that satisfies Rule 9011's certification requirements - that it is not being presented for an improper purpose, that the legal contentions are warranted by existing law or by a non-frivolous argument for changing the law, that the factual contentions have evidentiary support or are likely to have such support after discovery - will ordinarily satisfy Rule 3.1 as well.

Practical impact

The most important practical consequence of Rule 3.1 is the breadth of the protected zone. The rule does not require counsel to be right; it requires counsel to have a non-frivolous basis or a good-faith argument. Aggressive theory, novel theory, theory inviting doctrinal change, and full defense in criminal matters all sit comfortably within the affirmative architecture the rule establishes.

  • Aggressive does not equal frivolous. Vigorous advocacy that pushes the boundaries of existing doctrine is protected so long as the push is grounded.
  • Losing does not equal frivolous. The outcome of a case is not the measure of whether the case was meritorious to bring or defend at the time of filing.
  • The criminal-defense carve-out is absolute as to elements. Defense counsel may always require proof of every element of the prosecution's case.
