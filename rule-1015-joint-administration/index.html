Federal Rule of Bankruptcy Procedure 1015

Rule 1015: Consolidation or Joint Administration of Cases

The distinction between joint procedural administration and substantive consolidation, the Eagle-Picher factors, and the limits joint administration places on creditor rights.

Verbatim text of Rule 1015(a) and (b)

(a) Cases Involving Same Debtor. If two or more petitions are pending in the same court by or against the same debtor, the court may order consolidation of the cases.
(b) Cases Involving Two or More Related Debtors. If a joint petition or two or more petitions are pending in the same court by or against (1) a husband and wife, or (2) a partnership and one or more of its general partners, or (3) two or more general partners, or (4) a debtor and an affiliate, the court may order a joint administration of the estates. Fed. R. Bankr. P. 1015(a)-(b).

The two concepts are different

Federal Rule of Bankruptcy Procedure 1015 contains two operative provisions that practitioners frequently elide but that have very different legal consequences:

  • Rule 1015(a) - Consolidation of cases involving the same debtor. When duplicate petitions are filed by or against a single debtor in the same court, the court may consolidate them into a single case. This is administratively unremarkable.
  • Rule 1015(b) - Joint administration of related debtors. When separate petitions are filed by related debtors (spouses, partnership and general partners, multiple general partners, or a debtor and an affiliate), the court may order that the cases be administered jointly. The estates remain legally separate.

Neither subdivision authorizes the much more powerful equitable remedy of substantive consolidation, which actually merges the estates and pools their assets and liabilities. Substantive consolidation is not a creature of Rule 1015. It is an equitable remedy derived from the bankruptcy court's general equitable powers under 11 U.S.C. Section 105(a), governed by independent case-law standards, and requires an evidentiary showing of specific factors quite different from the routine showing required for joint administration.

Joint administration under Rule 1015(b)

Joint administration is a procedural mechanism that authorizes the court and parties to administer related cases together for efficiency. The hallmarks of joint administration are:

  • A single docket or interlinked docket for the related cases
  • Joint hearings on common matters
  • Combined notices to creditors when the matter is common to multiple estates
  • A single set of professionals (subject to retention orders in each case) where appropriate
  • One captioned filing serving multiple estates

Joint administration does not do any of the following:

  • Pool the assets of the related estates
  • Make the creditors of one estate creditors of another
  • Subject one estate's assets to the claims of another estate's creditors
  • Permit cross-estate setoff that would not otherwise be available
  • Eliminate the separate corporate or legal identity of the related debtors

Rule 1015(c) - Joint administration procedures

Rule 1015(c) authorizes the court to enter orders as necessary to carry out the joint administration. These commonly include orders permitting the use of combined captions, authorizing combined service lists, designating a lead case for docketing purposes, and addressing the maintenance of separate accounting and tax records sufficient to permit later disentanglement of the estates.

Substantive consolidation - the Eagle-Picher factors

Substantive consolidation is fundamentally different from joint administration. It is the merger of two or more estates into a single estate, treating the consolidated debtors as one entity for all purposes of the bankruptcy case. Inter-debtor claims are extinguished; assets are pooled; creditors share in the pool without regard to which debtor was originally obligated.

Because substantive consolidation can dramatically affect creditor recoveries (creditors of an asset-rich entity may see their recoveries diluted by claims against an asset-poor affiliate), courts require a substantial evidentiary showing. The factors articulated in In re Eagle-Picher Industries, Inc., 192 B.R. 903 (Bankr. S.D. Ohio 1996), have been widely adopted or paralleled by other courts:

The seven Eagle-Picher factors

  1. The presence or absence of consolidated financial statements
  2. The unity of interests and ownership between the various corporate entities
  3. The existence of parent and intercorporate guarantees on loans
  4. The degree of difficulty in segregating and ascertaining individual assets and liabilities
  5. The transfer of assets without observance of corporate formalities
  6. The commingling of assets and business functions
  7. The profitability of consolidation at a single physical location

Other courts apply different formulations. The Second Circuit's framework, articulated in Union Sav. Bank v. Augie/Restivo Baking Co. (In re Augie/Restivo), 860 F.2d 515 (2d Cir. 1988), reduces the analysis to two principal inquiries: (i) whether creditors dealt with the entities as a single economic unit and did not rely on their separate identity in extending credit, or (ii) whether the affairs of the debtors are so entangled that consolidation will benefit all creditors.

The Third Circuit's articulation in In re Owens Corning, 419 F.3d 195 (3d Cir. 2005), emphasized that substantive consolidation should be used "sparingly," recognized the doctrine's potential to override the corporate separateness on which creditors relied when extending credit, and disapproved a broad-brush "deemed consolidation" approach used in some prior cases.

Joint-administration limitations on creditor rights

Even after joint administration is ordered, creditors retain the rights they would have had in a stand-alone case against the particular debtor on whose obligations they are creditors. Specifically:

Voting on plans

In a plan context, each debtor's classes vote separately on plans applicable to that debtor. A creditor of debtor A does not vote on a plan applicable to debtor B unless the creditor also holds a claim against B (e.g., through a guarantee).

Distribution priority

Distributions from each estate are made from that estate's assets to that estate's creditors in accordance with the priority scheme of 11 U.S.C. Section 507 and the absolute-priority rule. A creditor of debtor A does not share in distributions from debtor B's assets unless the creditor also holds a claim against B.

Setoff and recoupment

Setoff under 11 U.S.C. Section 553 requires mutuality - the obligations being set off must be between the same parties in the same capacity. Joint administration does not create mutuality where it did not exist before; a debt owed to debtor A cannot be set off against a debt that the creditor owes to debtor B.

Avoidance actions

Avoidance actions under 11 U.S.C. Sections 547, 548, and 544 are brought on behalf of the particular estate that received the transfer. The trustee for jointly administered estates must allocate any recoveries to the proper estate.

Common procedural posture

Affiliated corporate group filing

A parent corporation and its operating subsidiaries each file separate Chapter 11 petitions on the same day. The lead case typically files a motion for joint administration under Rule 1015(b)(4) (debtor and affiliates) on the first day. The motion proposes the lead case, sets out the proposed combined caption, and recites that joint administration is sought for procedural convenience only and does not constitute or seek substantive consolidation.

Spousal joint case

Spouses file a joint petition under 11 U.S.C. Section 302. Although a joint petition is technically a single filing, Rule 1015(b)(1) provides the analytical framework for treating the joint case as two estates jointly administered. The trustee or court may, on a particularized showing, order substantive consolidation of the spousal estates if the equitable factors are met.

Practical impact

The joint-administration order is among the most routine first-day orders in a corporate Chapter 11 case. It is, however, frequently confused with substantive consolidation by parties who assume that "joint administration" pools the estates. Counsel and creditors must understand that the routine first-day joint-administration order is procedural only, and that any subsequent attempt at substantive consolidation will require a separate motion, evidentiary record, and application of the Eagle-Picher (or analogous circuit) factors.

Joint administration is administrative; substantive consolidation is substantive. The standard joint-administration order entered in the first days of a related-debtor case does nothing to affect the legal separation of the estates. A creditor who wants the protection of separate estates need not object to joint administration; a creditor who wants the benefits of pooling must move for substantive consolidation under the Eagle-Picher factors.
