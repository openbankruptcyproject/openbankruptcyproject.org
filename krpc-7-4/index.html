KRPC 7.4 / MRPC 4-7.4: Communication of Fields of Practice and Specialization

An affirmative framework authorizing lawyers to communicate the fields of law in which they practice, with specific provisions for patent, admiralty, and certified-specialist designations.

Verbatim text of KRPC 7.4 (Kansas)

(a) A lawyer may communicate the fact that the lawyer does or does not practice in particular fields of law.

(b) A lawyer admitted to engage in patent practice before the United States Patent and Trademark Office may use the designation "Patent Attorney" or a substantially similar designation.

(c) A lawyer engaged in Admiralty practice may use the designation "Admiralty," "Proctor in Admiralty" or a substantially similar designation.

(d) A lawyer shall not state or imply that a lawyer is certified as a specialist in a particular field of law, unless:
(1) the lawyer has been certified as a specialist by an organization that has been approved by an appropriate state authority or that has been accredited by the American Bar Association; and
(2) the name of the certifying organization is clearly identified in the communication. Kan. Sup. Ct. R. 226, KRPC 7.4.

Missouri Rule 4-7.4

Missouri Rule 4-7.4 is substantively parallel to KRPC 7.4 and tracks the ABA Model Rule framework. The rule operates as an affirmative authorization for lawyer communication of fields of practice, with two specific designations recognized for federally regulated practice categories (patent and admiralty) and a specific condition for use of a "certified specialist" designation.

The affirmative framework

The rule is organized as an affirmative authorization. Subdivision (a) opens with the general permission: a lawyer may communicate that the lawyer does or does not practice in particular fields. The provision authorizes a broad range of communications:

  • Website pages identifying the firm's practice areas
  • Attorney biographies listing the fields in which the lawyer practices
  • Letterhead and business cards identifying practice fields
  • Directory listings categorizing the lawyer by practice area
  • Marketing materials describing the firm's substantive areas of work
  • Statements by the lawyer in client and prospective-client communications about the fields the lawyer handles

The rule's affirmative grant under (a) does not require certification or any specific credential beyond the lawyer's licensure. Communications under (a) are simply descriptive of the lawyer's practice fields and are permitted as such.

7.4(b) - Patent designation

A lawyer admitted to practice before the United States Patent and Trademark Office may use the designation "Patent Attorney" or a substantially similar designation. The provision recognizes the distinctive federally regulated practice category. Patent practice is governed by a federal admission regime maintained by the USPTO; admission requires satisfaction of the agency's scientific or technical training requirements and passage of the agency's examination. A lawyer admitted under that regime is authorized by Rule 7.4(b) to communicate the admission through the designation "Patent Attorney."

The "substantially similar designation" language permits variants such as "Patent Practitioner," "Registered Patent Attorney," and "USPTO-Registered Patent Attorney." The variants must accurately reflect the lawyer's actual USPTO registration; designations that overstate or misstate the registration fall outside the rule's grant.

7.4(c) - Admiralty designation

A lawyer engaged in Admiralty practice may use the designation "Admiralty," "Proctor in Admiralty," or a substantially similar designation. The provision preserves a historical practice category for which traditional designations have long been recognized. Admiralty practice is the field of law governing maritime matters - vessel ownership, marine cargo, marine personal injury, maritime liens, and related matters within the federal admiralty jurisdiction. The designation supports informed selection of counsel for matters falling within the field.

7.4(d) - The "certified specialist" framework

Subdivision (d) addresses use of the "certified specialist" designation. The provision is the rule's most specific element and reflects a deliberate balancing: the rule recognizes the value of credential-based identification for consumers but conditions use of the credential language on two structural elements that ensure the credential is meaningful.

7.4(d)(1) - Approved or accredited certifying organization

The lawyer must have been certified by an organization that has been (a) approved by an appropriate state authority or (b) accredited by the American Bar Association. The condition channels the certifying activity through institutional review. State authorities and the ABA's accreditation framework evaluate certifying organizations for their certification standards, examination integrity, continuing-education requirements, and overall credibility. A "certified specialist" designation under (d)(1) thus carries with it the institutional vouching of the approving or accrediting authority.

7.4(d)(2) - Clear identification of the certifying organization

The name of the certifying organization must be clearly identified in the communication. The condition ensures that the recipient of the communication can evaluate the credential's source. A reference to "certified specialist in [field]" without identification of the certifying organization does not satisfy the condition; the communication must include the organization's name in a manner the recipient can readily perceive.

Subdivision (a) and subdivision (d) are different categories. The general practice-field communication permitted by 7.4(a) does not require certification and is broadly authorized. The "certified specialist" designation under 7.4(d) is a credential statement and is conditioned on the two structural elements of (d)(1) and (d)(2). Lawyers may communicate their fields of practice under (a) without any reference to specialization; the (d) framework applies when the lawyer wishes to use the credential-specific language.

The relationship to Rule 7.1

Rule 7.4 operates within the broader framework of Rule 7.1, which governs communications about the lawyer's services and prohibits false or misleading communications. The two rules work in tandem:

  • Rule 7.4(a) authorizes practice-field communication; Rule 7.1 requires that the communication not be false or misleading. A lawyer's listing of a practice field under (a) is within the rule when the lawyer actually practices in the field.
  • Rules 7.4(b) and (c) authorize specific designations; Rule 7.1 requires the designations to accurately reflect the lawyer's qualifications. The "Patent Attorney" designation under (b) presumes actual USPTO admission; an "Admiralty" designation under (c) presumes actual engagement in the field.
  • Rule 7.4(d) conditions the "certified specialist" designation on the two structural elements; Rule 7.1 requires that the certification statement, including the identifying information for the certifying organization, be accurate.

The institutional review framework for certifying organizations

The "approved by an appropriate state authority" prong of 7.4(d)(1) refers to the state-level approval frameworks that many jurisdictions maintain for certifying organizations. State approval processes typically evaluate:

  • The certifying organization's substantive standards for certification (years of practice, examination requirements, peer review, continuing-legal-education obligations)
  • The certifying organization's procedures for examination administration and grading
  • The certifying organization's recertification requirements ensuring continued competence
  • The certifying organization's governance and conflict-management structures

The "accredited by the American Bar Association" prong refers to the ABA's national accreditation framework for specialty certification programs, which performs analogous evaluations on a national scale. Either approval channel satisfies the condition of (d)(1).

Common practice-area communications

Firm website practice-area listing

A firm's website includes a "Practice Areas" page listing the substantive fields the firm handles - corporate transactions, commercial litigation, employment law, bankruptcy, family law. The listing is a 7.4(a) communication; the firm communicates the fields in which its lawyers practice. The listing is within the rule when the firm actually maintains substantive practice in each listed field. No certification is required, and no "specialist" language is used.

Attorney biography with credential reference

An attorney biography on a firm website identifies the lawyer's primary practice fields and includes a reference to a credential held by the lawyer (for example, a state-approved certification in family law, with the certifying organization clearly named). The practice-field references are governed by 7.4(a); the credential reference is governed by 7.4(d) and includes the certifying-organization identification that (d)(2) requires.

Cross-jurisdiction considerations

Lawyers practicing across jurisdictions communicate their practice fields in materials that may be viewed from any jurisdiction. The Rule 7.4 framework operates alongside the jurisdictional-scope considerations under Rule 5.5 and the bar-admission representations under Rule 7.1: a multi-jurisdiction practice description identifies the fields in which the lawyer practices, the jurisdictions in which the lawyer is admitted, and (where applicable) the certifying organizations that have credentialed the lawyer's specialty designations. Each component of the communication is governed by the applicable rule provisions.

Continuing nature of the inquiry

Communications under Rule 7.4 are subject to ongoing accuracy obligations. A practice-field listing remains within 7.4(a) so long as the lawyer continues to practice in the field; a designation under (b) or (c) remains accurate so long as the lawyer maintains the underlying status (USPTO registration or active admiralty practice); a "certified specialist" designation under (d) remains within the rule so long as the certification remains in good standing and the certifying organization remains approved or accredited. Materials are updated to reflect changes in any of these statuses.

Relationship to other rules

  • Rule 7.1 - Communications about the lawyer's services; the general accuracy framework that overlays Rule 7.4
  • Rule 7.2 - Communications about legal services (advertising); the related provision governing the channels through which communications are made
  • Rule 7.5 - Firm names and letterheads; relevant where the practice-field communication appears on firm-identifying materials
  • Rule 5.5 - Jurisdictional scope; practice-field communications include implicit representations about the jurisdictions in which the lawyer practices
  • Rule 1.1 - Competence; the underlying obligation to provide competent representation in the fields the lawyer holds out as practicing

Practical impact

Rule 7.4 carries several recurring practical consequences:

  • Practice-field communication is broadly authorized. Lawyers and firms communicate the fields in which they practice through the customary channels - websites, biographies, marketing materials, directory listings - within the framework of 7.4(a) and Rule 7.1's accuracy overlay.
  • Specific designations have specific bases. The "Patent Attorney" and "Admiralty" designations are tied to defined practice categories with established federal or specialized frameworks. Use of these designations under (b) and (c) presumes the underlying qualifications.
  • "Certified specialist" requires institutional vouching. The (d) framework conditions the credential designation on certification by an approved or accredited organization and on clear identification of the certifying organization. The two elements ensure that the designation carries substantive content for the recipient.
