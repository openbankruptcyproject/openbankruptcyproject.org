Arbitration in Bankruptcy Proceedings

Core versus non-core analysis under Stern v. Marshall, the McMahon inherent-conflict framework, FAA preemption, and the In re Anderson / In re Belton circuit split on consumer arbitration.

The two competing federal statutes

Arbitration in bankruptcy sits at the intersection of two federal statutes with sharply different policy orientations. The Federal Arbitration Act (FAA), 9 U.S.C. Section 1 et seq., embodies a "liberal federal policy favoring arbitration agreements." The Bankruptcy Code, 11 U.S.C. Section 101 et seq., embodies an equally strong federal policy favoring the centralized resolution of disputes affecting a debtor's estate within a single forum applying uniform federal law.

When a pre-petition contract containing an arbitration clause produces a dispute that arises in or relates to a bankruptcy case, the bankruptcy court must decide whether to enforce the arbitration clause and compel arbitration of the dispute, or to retain the dispute for resolution by the bankruptcy court. The answer turns on the framework established by the Supreme Court in Shearson/American Express, Inc. v. McMahon, 482 U.S. 220 (1987), and refined by the courts of appeals in the bankruptcy context.

The McMahon framework

In McMahon, the Supreme Court held that arbitration of a statutory claim is required by the FAA unless the party opposing arbitration shows that Congress intended in another statute to preclude waiver of judicial remedies for the claim at issue. Congressional intent to preclude arbitration may be inferred from:

  • The text of the other statute
  • The legislative history
  • An inherent conflict between arbitration and the underlying purposes of the other statute

Applied to bankruptcy, the McMahon test asks whether enforcing an arbitration clause as to a particular dispute would create an "inherent conflict" with the underlying purposes of the Bankruptcy Code. The Bankruptcy Code does not, by its text, expressly preclude arbitration. There is no extensive legislative history on the subject. The inquiry therefore typically turns on the inherent-conflict prong - and that prong, in turn, typically turns on whether the dispute is "core" or "non-core" within the meaning of 28 U.S.C. Section 157(b).

Core versus non-core under 28 U.S.C. Section 157

Bankruptcy jurisdiction is allocated by 28 U.S.C. Section 157, which distinguishes between:

  • Core proceedings under Section 157(b) - matters arising under the Bankruptcy Code or arising in a case under the Code. The bankruptcy court has full statutory authority to hear and determine core proceedings and enter final orders. Section 157(b)(2) enumerates a non-exhaustive list of core matters including allowance of claims, confirmation of plans, motions to terminate the automatic stay, proceedings to determine the dischargeability of debts, and avoidance actions.
  • Non-core proceedings under Section 157(c) - matters that are merely "related to" a bankruptcy case. The bankruptcy court hears non-core proceedings but, absent party consent, submits proposed findings of fact and conclusions of law to the district court for de novo review.

The Stern v. Marshall constitutional overlay

The Supreme Court's decision in Stern v. Marshall, 564 U.S. 462 (2011), added a constitutional overlay to the statutory core / non-core framework. Stern held that, even where Congress has designated a matter as "core" under Section 157(b), Article III of the Constitution may forbid the bankruptcy court (a non-Article III tribunal) from entering final judgment on the matter if the claim does not stem from the bankruptcy itself and would not necessarily be resolved in the claims-allowance process. The category of "Stern claims" - statutorily core but constitutionally non-core - includes, most notably, state-law counterclaims of the estate against creditors who have not filed proofs of claim that necessarily implicate the counterclaim.

Post-Stern, the bankruptcy court analyzing an arbitration motion must consider not only the statutory core / non-core line but also the constitutional Article III line, because a "Stern claim" presents fewer reasons to deny arbitration: if Article III itself prevents the bankruptcy court from entering final judgment, the inherent-conflict rationale for refusing to compel arbitration is correspondingly weaker.

Application of McMahon to bankruptcy disputes

The courts of appeals have applied McMahon in the bankruptcy context with reasonable consistency on the framework, though with continuing disagreement on application to specific disputes. The basic framework:

  • Non-core disputes: The presumption of arbitrability is generally enforced. A pre-petition contract dispute between the debtor and a counterparty that arises independently of the bankruptcy filing is typically sent to arbitration if a valid arbitration clause covers the dispute. The Second Circuit's decision in MBNA America Bank, N.A. v. Hill, 436 F.3d 104 (2d Cir. 2006), is representative.
  • Core disputes: The bankruptcy court has discretion to refuse to compel arbitration on a showing of inherent conflict. The court typically considers the centrality of the dispute to the bankruptcy case, the impact on the orderly administration of the estate, the preclusive effect of arbitration on other claims, and whether the dispute requires interpretation of provisions of the Bankruptcy Code itself.

The In re Anderson / In re Belton circuit split on consumer arbitration

The most prominent recent disagreement among the circuits concerns the enforceability of arbitration clauses with respect to claims that a creditor violated the discharge injunction under 11 U.S.C. Section 524.

In re Anderson (Second Circuit)

In In re Anderson, 884 F.3d 382 (2d Cir. 2018), the Second Circuit held that a consumer debtor's claim that a credit-card issuer violated the discharge injunction by continuing to report a discharged debt as outstanding was not arbitrable. The court applied the McMahon framework and found an inherent conflict: the discharge injunction is a creation of the Bankruptcy Code, is enforced through the bankruptcy court's contempt power, and represents the central protection afforded the debtor by the bankruptcy system. Permitting arbitration of discharge-injunction claims would, the court reasoned, undermine the centralized federal interest in uniform enforcement of the discharge.

In re Belton (Second Circuit, related)

The Second Circuit revisited and reinforced its Anderson holding in In re Belton, 961 F.3d 612 (2d Cir. 2020), again refusing to compel arbitration of consumer claims arising from alleged credit-reporting violations of the discharge injunction. Belton emphasized the bankruptcy court's traditional and exclusive jurisdiction over discharge enforcement and the inherent conflict between arbitration and the centralized federal interest.

Contrary authority

Other circuits have been less willing to refuse arbitration of consumer claims that touch on bankruptcy issues. The Fifth Circuit in In re Henry, 944 F.3d 587 (5th Cir. 2019), enforced an arbitration clause as to an FDCPA claim against a debt collector arising from collection of a discharged debt, distinguishing between Section 524 discharge-injunction claims (potentially non-arbitrable) and Fair Debt Collection Practices Act claims (subject to arbitration). The Ninth Circuit's case law similarly takes a more permissive view of arbitration of statutory claims that touch on but are distinct from the discharge injunction itself.

The split is unresolved at the Supreme Court level. Practitioners in consumer bankruptcy disputes involving arbitration clauses must consult the controlling circuit authority, which varies materially in its willingness to refuse arbitration on inherent-conflict grounds.

FAA preemption analysis

A separate but related question is whether the Bankruptcy Code (or state-law bankruptcy-protective statutes) is preempted by the FAA to the extent of any inconsistency. The Supreme Court has held that the FAA preempts state laws that single out arbitration clauses for disfavored treatment. The bankruptcy preemption question is different: it asks whether a federal statute (the Bankruptcy Code) impliedly limits the operation of another federal statute (the FAA) as applied to particular categories of disputes.

The McMahon inherent-conflict analysis is itself the mechanism by which courts have addressed this implied-limitation question. A finding of inherent conflict is, in substance, a finding that the Bankruptcy Code impliedly displaces the FAA as to the particular dispute. Where no inherent conflict is found, the FAA controls and arbitration is compelled.

Procedural posture - the motion to compel arbitration

A party seeking to compel arbitration in a bankruptcy case typically files a motion to compel arbitration and stay or dismiss the bankruptcy-court proceeding. The motion identifies:

  • The arbitration clause and the contract in which it appears
  • The scope of the clause (which categories of disputes it covers)
  • The party's contention that the dispute falls within the clause
  • The applicable arbitration rules (AAA Commercial, JAMS, etc.)
  • The party's analysis under McMahon, including the core / non-core characterization and the inherent-conflict argument (or the absence of inherent conflict)

The opposing party (typically the debtor, trustee, or committee) responds with its analysis of the inherent-conflict prong, identifying the specific aspects of the dispute that implicate central bankruptcy policies. The bankruptcy court rules on the motion, typically after a hearing on the legal issues without an evidentiary record beyond the arbitration agreement itself.

Common dispute categories

Pre-petition contract claims (typically arbitrable)

The debtor's claim that a counterparty breached a pre-petition contract is typically subject to arbitration if the contract contains a valid arbitration clause covering the dispute. The claim is "related to" the bankruptcy case but does not arise under the Bankruptcy Code, and resolution does not implicate central bankruptcy policies.

Discharge-injunction violations (split)

Claims that a creditor violated the Section 524 discharge injunction by continuing to collect a discharged debt. Anderson and Belton hold these claims non-arbitrable in the Second Circuit; other circuits have reached differing results, with the split most pronounced in cases involving collateral statutory claims (FDCPA, state UDAP statutes).

Avoidance actions (generally non-arbitrable)

Preference, fraudulent-transfer, and other avoidance actions under Sections 544-550 are creations of the Bankruptcy Code. They are core proceedings, and the inherent-conflict analysis typically favors retention by the bankruptcy court. The avoidance regime depends on uniform interpretation of bankruptcy-specific concepts (insolvency, reasonably equivalent value, transfer voidable under applicable law) and is integrated with the estate-administration process.

Stay violations (non-arbitrable)

Claims under Section 362(k) for willful violations of the automatic stay turn on the application of a Bankruptcy Code provision and on remedies the Code itself creates. Most courts treat these claims as non-arbitrable on inherent-conflict grounds, parallel to the discharge-injunction analysis.

The arbitrability analysis is dispute-specific, not contract-specific. The same arbitration clause may produce different outcomes as applied to different claims arising under the same contract. A pre-petition breach-of-contract claim and a post-petition discharge-injunction claim may both fall within the clause's scope but receive different treatment under the McMahon inherent-conflict prong. Counsel should analyze each claim separately.
