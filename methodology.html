1. Data Sources

Primary: FJC Integrated Database

Our primary dataset is the Federal Judicial Center (FJC) Integrated Database, the most comprehensive public source of federal bankruptcy filing data available. It contains filing-level records for cases across all 94 federal judicial districts.

Supplementary Sources

  • PACER docket data - case-level docket entries, attorney records, and filed documents collected via the Public Access to Court Electronic Records system. Used for attorney-level analysis and specific case verification.
  • RECAP Archive - free court documents contributed to the Free Law Project's RECAP archive. OBP has donated 549+ cases and 3,100+ files to date.
  • Census Bureau - population data for per-capita filing rate calculations and demographic adjustments.
  • Bureau of Labor Statistics - unemployment rates and economic indicators for contextual analysis.

2. Data Cleaning Procedures

Raw FJC data requires significant cleaning before analysis. Our multi-stage cleaning pipeline addresses known data quality issues:

Court Identifier Standardization

The FJC uses numeric district codes that have changed over time. We maintain a mapping table that resolves all historical code variations to consistent, current district identifiers (e.g., "Western District of Missouri" regardless of whether the record uses the pre-2010 or post-2010 FJC code).

Attorney and Firm Name Normalization

Attorney names in court records contain abbreviations, misspellings, and formatting inconsistencies. We apply fuzzy matching algorithms with manual review thresholds to consolidate name variants (e.g., "Smith Jones" / "Smith Jones LLP" / "Smith, Jones & Associates" all resolve to the same entity). Firm name normalization follows the same process.

Record Deduplication

Cases that appear in multiple fiscal-year data dumps are deduplicated using a composite key of district, case number, and filing date. Multi-district filings are tracked but counted once in national totals.

Integrity Validation

Automated checks flag records with impossible field combinations (e.g., a Chapter 7 case listing a plan confirmation date), missing required fields, or dates outside expected ranges. Flagged records are reviewed manually or excluded with documentation.

All cleaning code is open-source. Every transformation applied to the raw data is documented in our GitHub repository. You can review, audit, or suggest improvements to any cleaning step.

3. Processing Pipeline

From raw FJC download to published finding, data flows through these stages:

1
Ingest: Raw FJC CSV files are loaded into a SQLite database (bankruptcy_master.db). Schema enforces type constraints and referential integrity.
2
Clean: Standardization scripts normalize court codes, attorney names, firm names, and disposition codes. Deduplication removes multi-year overlaps.
3
Enrich: PACER data supplements FJC records with attorney-level detail, docket entries, and filed documents. Census and BLS data are joined for per-capita and economic context.
4
Analyze: Python scripts compute metrics: completion rates, comparative district rankings, outlier detection, regression models. Each script is self-contained with documented inputs and outputs.
5
Validate: Results are cross-checked against known benchmarks (published ABI statistics, FJC annual reports, academic literature). Anomalies trigger manual review.
6
Publish: Verified findings are published with full methodological notes, dataset version, and links to the generating scripts.

4. Statistical Methods

Core Metrics

  • Completion rates: Cases reaching discharge divided by total cases filed, calculated per district, per attorney, per chapter, and per time period.
  • Per-capita filing rates: Filings per 100,000 population, using Census Bureau estimates for the filing year.
  • Prior-filer rates: Percentage of filers in a given district or attorney's caseload who have filed at least one prior bankruptcy case within the statutory lookback period.

Comparative Analysis

  • District comparisons: Ranking all 94 districts on key metrics with percentile scores and confidence intervals.
  • Attorney scorecards: Multi-factor profiles comparing individual attorneys or firms against district and national benchmarks.
  • Time-series analysis: Year-over-year trends with seasonal adjustment where appropriate.

Outlier Detection

  • Z-scores: Standard deviations from the mean for continuous metrics (filing rates, completion rates, fee levels).
  • Percentile rankings: Position within the national distribution for each metric.
  • Composite scoring: Multi-factor "mill probability" scores combining several indicators, with transparent weighting.

What We Do Not Do

We do not impute missing data. We do not extrapolate beyond the dataset's time range. We do not make causal claims without appropriate statistical support - correlation between attorney behavior and case outcomes is reported as correlation, not causation. We do not cherry-pick favorable findings; when results are mixed or inconclusive, we say so.

5. Academic Validation

Our methodology has been reviewed by researchers at multiple institutions. While OBP is not an academic journal and our work has not undergone formal double-blind peer review, we have actively sought and received critical feedback from established scholars in empirical legal studies.

Faculty Engagement

  • UC Berkeley School of Law - Faculty reviewed the core methodology, including the 1328(f) screening approach and prior-filer analysis. No methodological red flags were identified. The reviewer noted the approach was consistent with standard empirical legal research methods and suggested additional data sources (including AACER) for future work.
  • Georgia State University College of Law - Faculty in computational legal studies reviewed the data pipeline and expressed interest in integration with academic research infrastructure. The reviewer noted potential for co-authorship and expansion into longitudinal outcome studies.

Federal Rules Committee

Our empirical findings form the basis for Suggestion 26-BK-3, submitted to the Advisory Committee on Bankruptcy Rules and accepted in March 2026. The suggestion proposes automated screening of statutory discharge bars at the point of case filing. It is currently listed as "pending consideration" by the Committee. A separate procedural submission, Suggestion 26-BK-5 (Rule 9037 / Forms 121 & 309E1 SSN exposure remediation), was accepted in April 2026 and forwarded to the Standing Committee.

Open Invitation

We actively invite academic scrutiny. Researchers can access our complete codebase, reproduce any finding, and contact us at research@openbankruptcyproject.org with questions, corrections, or collaboration proposals. See our Researchers page for data access details.

6. Reproducibility

Every analysis we publish includes:

  • The specific SQL queries or Python scripts used to generate findings
  • The dataset version, date range, and any filtering criteria applied
  • The statistical methods, thresholds, and parameters used
  • The complete results - including null results and findings that cut against our hypotheses

7. Limitations and Caveats

We are transparent about what our data can and cannot tell us:

Selection Bias

The FJC captures only filed cases. We cannot observe people who needed bankruptcy protection but did not file, were unable to afford an attorney, or were deterred by the process. Our data therefore reflects the filing population, not the population in financial distress.

Attorney Quality Inference

We infer attorney quality from observable case outcomes: completion rates, dismissal rates, and compliance with statutory requirements. These are proxies, not direct measures of attorney competence. An attorney with low completion rates may serve clients with inherently more difficult cases. We note this limitation wherever we publish attorney-level findings.

PACER Coverage

PACER data requires case-by-case collection (manual or automated). Our PACER dataset is extensive but not comprehensive. We note which findings rely on PACER data and the coverage level for each analysis.

Historical Data

FJC data before 2008 uses different field definitions and coding schemes. We limit our primary analyses to 2008-2024 to maintain consistency, though we note where longer time series would be informative.

Ecological Fallacy

District-level statistics describe averages. Individual cases within any district may differ significantly from the district average. We avoid drawing individual-case conclusions from aggregate data.

8. Frequently Asked Questions

Where does your data come from?
The Federal Judicial Center (FJC) Integrated Database, containing 4.9 million bankruptcy cases from 2008-2024 across all 94 federal districts. Supplemented with PACER docket data, Census Bureau demographics, and BLS economic indicators.
Can I independently verify your findings?
Yes. All analysis scripts are open-source on GitHub under the openbankruptcyproject organization. The FJC database is publicly available from fjc.gov. You can download the data, run our scripts, and reproduce any finding.
Has this methodology been peer reviewed?
It has been reviewed by faculty at UC Berkeley School of Law and Georgia State University College of Law. No methodological red flags were identified. However, we have not undergone formal double-blind academic peer review. We consider our work pre-publication empirical research and actively invite scrutiny.
Do you accept corrections?
Absolutely. If you find an error in our data or analysis, contact us at research@openbankruptcyproject.org. We issue corrections promptly and credit reporters.
How often is the data updated?
FJC data is updated annually when new fiscal-year releases are published. PACER data is collected on an ongoing basis. Each analysis notes its specific data vintage.
What statutes do you screen for?
Our primary screening covers Sections 1328(f), 727(a)(8), and 109(g) of the Bankruptcy Code - the statutory discharge bars and filing restrictions based on prior case history. We also analyze completion rates, fee patterns, and attorney caseload metrics.
