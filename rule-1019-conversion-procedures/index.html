Federal Rule of Bankruptcy Procedure 1019

Rule 1019: Conversion Procedures from Chapter 11, 12, or 13 to Chapter 7

The post-conversion procedural cascade: new Section 341 meeting, new schedules, restarted claim bar dates, and administrative treatment of post-petition pre-conversion claims.

What Rule 1019 does

Federal Rule of Bankruptcy Procedure 1019 governs the procedural steps that follow when a case originally filed under Chapter 11, 12, or 13 is converted to Chapter 7. Conversion is not a re-filing; the case retains its original case number, original petition date, and original automatic stay. But the change from a reorganization-or-repayment regime to a liquidation regime triggers a substantial procedural reset that Rule 1019 implements in eight numbered paragraphs.

The eight Rule 1019 directives in summary

  1. Lists, inventories, schedules, and statements already on file are deemed filed in the converted case unless the court directs otherwise (Rule 1019(1)(A)).
  2. The debtor must file a schedule of unpaid debts incurred after the original petition but before conversion (Rule 1019(5)(A)). This is the foundation for administrative-claim treatment of post-petition pre-conversion liabilities.
  3. The trustee or debtor in possession from the prior chapter must turn over records and file a final report and account (Rule 1019(2), (5)(B)).
  4. A new Section 341 meeting of creditors is convened by the United States trustee under the Chapter 7 calendar.
  5. New claim bar dates are established running from the date of conversion for purposes of Rule 3002(c) (Rule 1019(2)).
  6. Post-petition pre-conversion claims receive administrative-expense priority under 11 U.S.C. Section 503(b) and are scheduled and noticed accordingly.
  7. The clerk gives notice to entities listed on the schedules of conversion and of the new bar dates.
  8. The Chapter 7 trustee assumes administration of the estate as it exists on the conversion date.

Section 348 - the substantive companion

Rule 1019 is the procedural implementation of 11 U.S.C. Section 348, which is the substantive provision governing the effect of conversion. Section 348(a) preserves the original commencement date for purposes of automatic stay, claim allowance, and avoidance powers, with specific exceptions. Section 348(b) and (c) carve out exceptions for which the "order for relief" date is the conversion date rather than the original petition date. Section 348(d) provides that post-petition, pre-conversion claims are treated as if they arose immediately before the date of the filing of the petition - meaning they are general unsecured claims rather than administrative-expense claims - except for claims that would have been administrative under Section 503(b) in the original chapter, which retain that priority.

The new Section 341 meeting

The United States trustee, upon entry of the order of conversion, schedules a new meeting of creditors under 11 U.S.C. Section 341. The Chapter 7 trustee - typically newly appointed from the panel for the district - presides. The debtor is required to attend, to be sworn, and to be examined under oath. Topics typically covered include:

  • Changes in the debtor's financial circumstances since the original petition
  • Post-petition pre-conversion income and expenses
  • Disposition of estate property between the original petition and conversion
  • Existence and location of non-exempt assets that may be liquidated for distribution
  • Operation of any post-petition business (in a former Chapter 11 or 12 case)
  • Pre-conversion plan payments (in a former Chapter 13 case) and the source of those payments

The new schedules - the conversion schedule

Rule 1019(5)(A) requires the debtor to file, within 14 days of the conversion order, a schedule of unpaid debts incurred after the original petition but before conversion (post-petition pre-conversion debts). This schedule is in addition to the original schedules and is the basis on which the clerk gives notice to those creditors of the converted case and of the new bar dates. Rule 1019(5) also requires the debtor to file:

  • A schedule of property of the estate acquired after the original petition
  • A schedule of executory contracts entered into or assumed after the original petition
  • Any amendments to the original schedules to reflect changes

Local rules in many districts supply forms or templates for the conversion schedule and prescribe additional content (e.g., a post-conversion statement of financial affairs).

Claim bar dates restart

One of the most important practical consequences of Rule 1019 is the restart of the claim bar dates. Under Rule 3002(c), the bar date in a Chapter 7 case is 70 days after the order for relief. For a converted case, the bar date runs from the date of conversion rather than from the original petition. The clerk's notice of conversion must inform creditors of the new bar date and of the need to file proofs of claim if they have not already done so.

Creditors who filed proofs of claim in the prior chapter need not refile; their claims remain on the docket. But creditors whose claims arose after the original petition and before conversion - or who were added to the schedule of post-petition debts - must file claims by the new bar date to participate in distribution.

Post-petition pre-conversion claims as administrative expenses

Section 348(d) preserves administrative-expense status for claims that, in the prior chapter, would have qualified under Section 503(b) - typically claims for actual, necessary costs and expenses of preserving the estate, post-petition trade debt incurred in the ordinary course of operating a Chapter 11 or 12 business, and compensation for services rendered to the estate. These claims are paid out of the Chapter 7 estate in the priority order of Section 507(a)(2) before any distribution to general unsecured creditors.

The interplay between Section 348(d) and the absolute-priority scheme of Section 507 is one of the most procedurally important features of conversion. A creditor who supplied goods or services to a Chapter 11 debtor in possession during the reorganization phase has a strong claim to administrative priority in the Chapter 7 case - but only if the claim is timely filed and properly characterized as a Section 503(b) claim.

Common procedural posture

Chapter 13 conversion to Chapter 7

A debtor whose Chapter 13 plan has failed (missed payments, loss of employment, material change in circumstances) moves to convert to Chapter 7 under 11 U.S.C. Section 1307(a). Upon conversion, the Chapter 13 trustee files a final report. The Chapter 7 trustee is appointed; a new Section 341 meeting is convened; new bar dates run from conversion; and the trustee evaluates the post-petition pre-conversion period for any non-exempt assets acquired during that period that are now property of the Chapter 7 estate under Section 348(f)(2) (in bad-faith conversion) or excluded under Section 348(f)(1) (in good-faith conversion).

Chapter 11 conversion to Chapter 7

A Chapter 11 reorganization fails after substantial post-petition operations. On conversion, the debtor-in-possession's records are turned over to the Chapter 7 trustee. Post-petition trade creditors who supplied the operating business hold Section 503(b) administrative claims that, under Section 348(d), retain administrative priority in the Chapter 7. The trustee evaluates these claims, files a final report, and proposes distribution that pays the administrative class before any distribution to general unsecured creditors.

Practical impact

Conversion to Chapter 7 is a procedural reset that affects every active party. Creditors who relaxed proof-of-claim diligence during the reorganization phase must promptly attend to the new bar date or risk forfeiture of their position. Debtor's counsel must prepare and file the conversion schedule and prepare the debtor for the new Section 341 meeting. The newly appointed Chapter 7 trustee assumes responsibility for any estate property acquired or disposed of during the prior-chapter administration and must rapidly assess what assets remain available for liquidation.

Conversion is not a re-filing. The case retains its original petition date, original automatic stay, and original look-back periods for avoidance purposes. But the procedural reset triggered by Rule 1019 - new Section 341 meeting, new bar date, conversion schedule, administrative treatment of post-petition pre-conversion claims - is substantial enough that practitioners often refer informally to "the converted case" as a distinct procedural entity within the unified case file.
