KRPC 5.5 / MRPC 4-5.5: Jurisdictional Scope of Law Practice

Where a lawyer may practice and under what conditions, including temporary-practice safe harbors and federal and in-house exceptions.

Verbatim text of KRPC 5.5 (Kansas)

(a) A lawyer shall not practice law in a jurisdiction in violation of the regulation of the legal profession in that jurisdiction, or assist another in doing so.

(b) A lawyer who is not admitted to practice in this jurisdiction shall not:
(1) except as authorized by these Rules or other law, establish an office or other systematic and continuous presence in this jurisdiction for the practice of law; or
(2) hold out to the public or otherwise represent that the lawyer is admitted to practice law in this jurisdiction.

(c) A lawyer admitted in another United States jurisdiction, and not disbarred or suspended from practice in any jurisdiction, may provide legal services on a temporary basis in this jurisdiction that:
(1) are undertaken in association with a lawyer who is admitted to practice in this jurisdiction and who actively participates in the matter;
(2) are in or reasonably related to a pending or potential proceeding before a tribunal in this or another jurisdiction, if the lawyer, or a person the lawyer is assisting, is authorized by law or order to appear in such proceeding or reasonably expects to be so authorized;
(3) are in or reasonably related to a pending or potential arbitration, mediation, or other alternative dispute resolution proceeding in this or another jurisdiction, if the services arise out of or are reasonably related to the lawyer's practice in a jurisdiction in which the lawyer is admitted to practice and are not services for which the forum requires pro hac vice admission; or
(4) are not within paragraphs (c)(2) or (c)(3) and arise out of or are reasonably related to the lawyer's practice in a jurisdiction in which the lawyer is admitted to practice.

(d) A lawyer admitted in another United States jurisdiction, and not disbarred or suspended from practice in any jurisdiction, may provide legal services in this jurisdiction that:
(1) are provided to the lawyer's employer or its organizational affiliates and are not services for which the forum requires pro hac vice admission; or
(2) are services that the lawyer is authorized to provide by federal law or other law of this jurisdiction. Kan. Sup. Ct. R. 226, KRPC 5.5.

Missouri Rule 4-5.5

Missouri Rule 4-5.5 is substantively identical to KRPC 5.5 and tracks ABA Model Rule 5.5. The rule frames where a lawyer may practice (within jurisdictions of admission), the conditions under which a lawyer may engage in temporary practice in jurisdictions other than those of admission (the four safe harbors of subdivision (c)), and two structural exceptions for in-house and federal-law work (subdivision (d)).

5.5(a) - Jurisdictional anchor

Subdivision (a) establishes the baseline principle: a lawyer must conform to the regulation of the legal profession in any jurisdiction in which the lawyer practices, and must not assist another in conduct that would violate that jurisdiction's regulation. The rule directs the reader outward to the regulatory authority of each jurisdiction - the admissions regime, the licensure rules, and any related regulatory provisions of that jurisdiction's Supreme Court or equivalent regulatory body define what activity falls inside or outside the regulated profession. Rule 5.5(a) imports those external definitions and makes conformity with them a professional-conduct obligation.

5.5(b) - Systematic presence and holding out

Subdivision (b) addresses two specific situations for lawyers not admitted in the jurisdiction:

  • 5.5(b)(1) - A lawyer not admitted in the jurisdiction may not, except as authorized by the rules or other law, establish an office or other systematic and continuous presence in the jurisdiction for the practice of law. The key qualifiers are "systematic" and "continuous." Episodic activity that fits within a 5.5(c) safe harbor does not establish a systematic presence; opening an office, listing the jurisdiction in firm marketing as a place of practice, or holding regular intake meetings in the jurisdiction generally does.
  • 5.5(b)(2) - A lawyer not admitted in the jurisdiction may not hold out to the public or otherwise represent that the lawyer is admitted there. The duty extends to firm websites, letterhead, marketing materials, social-media profiles, and oral representations. Where a lawyer is admitted in one jurisdiction and licensed only there, materials that imply broader admission misstate the lawyer's authority to practice in the additional jurisdictions named.

5.5(c) - Temporary-practice safe harbors

Subdivision (c) is the rule's affirmative permission provision. It permits a lawyer admitted in another United States jurisdiction (and not disbarred or suspended anywhere) to provide legal services on a temporary basis in the host jurisdiction in four enumerated categories.

5.5(c)(1) - Association with locally admitted counsel

Services undertaken in association with a lawyer admitted in the host jurisdiction who actively participates in the matter. "Actively participates" requires substantive involvement, not nominal sponsorship. Co-counsel arrangements in which the locally admitted lawyer engages with the substance of the matter satisfy the standard; arrangements in which the local lawyer's role is limited to lending a name to a filing generally do not.

5.5(c)(2) - Pending or potential tribunal proceedings

Services in or reasonably related to a pending or potential proceeding before a tribunal in the host jurisdiction or another jurisdiction, where the lawyer or the person the lawyer is assisting is authorized by law or order to appear, or reasonably expects to be so authorized. This safe harbor includes the activity surrounding pro hac vice admission - investigation, fact-gathering, witness interviews, and motion preparation undertaken in reasonable expectation of admission.

5.5(c)(3) - Pending or potential ADR proceedings

Services in or reasonably related to a pending or potential arbitration, mediation, or other alternative-dispute-resolution proceeding, where the services arise out of or are reasonably related to the lawyer's practice in a jurisdiction in which the lawyer is admitted, and the forum does not require pro hac vice admission. The "arise out of or are reasonably related to" standard ties the host-jurisdiction work to the lawyer's home-jurisdiction practice.

5.5(c)(4) - General reasonable-relation safe harbor

Services that do not fall within (c)(2) or (c)(3) but that arise out of or are reasonably related to the lawyer's practice in a jurisdiction of admission. This is the broadest of the four safe harbors and the principal vehicle for transactional and counseling work that crosses jurisdictional lines. The "reasonably related" test is generally satisfied where the host-jurisdiction work is an extension of an established client relationship, involves the law of the lawyer's home jurisdiction, or involves a body of federal law in which the lawyer routinely practices.

"Temporary" is qualitative, not durational. The Comment to Model Rule 5.5 emphasizes that "temporary" is not defined by a numerical limit on days or matters; services that are recurring or extended may still be temporary if they do not amount to a systematic or continuous presence under 5.5(b)(1). The factors include the duration of the engagement, the number of matters in the host jurisdiction, the existence of an office or persistent advertising in the host jurisdiction, and whether the lawyer's host-jurisdiction work is incidental to or central to the lawyer's overall practice.

5.5(d) - In-house and federal-law exceptions

Subdivision (d) authorizes two ongoing (not temporary) categories of practice by a lawyer admitted in another United States jurisdiction:

5.5(d)(1) - In-house counsel

Services provided to the lawyer's employer or its organizational affiliates, where the forum does not require pro hac vice admission. This permits a lawyer admitted in one state to serve as in-house counsel in another state, providing advice to the employer and its affiliates. The exception does not permit the in-house lawyer to provide services to third parties or to appear in proceedings for which the forum requires admission or pro hac vice authorization.

5.5(d)(2) - Federal law and other authorizing law

Services the lawyer is authorized to provide by federal law or by other law of the host jurisdiction. The federal-law branch covers practice before federal agencies and courts under federal admission regimes - federal trademark and patent practice, immigration practice before agencies that authorize practitioners by federal credential, practice before federal courts that maintain their own admission rolls (subject to those courts' admission requirements), and similar federally authorized work. The state-law branch covers any host-jurisdiction statute or rule that affirmatively authorizes practice by lawyers admitted elsewhere - for example, a host-jurisdiction rule permitting limited-scope appearances by out-of-state lawyers in specific matter categories.

Cross-border counsel relationships

Several configurations recur in cross-border legal services and shape the application of Rule 5.5:

Federal-court practice in the lawyer's state of physical residence

A lawyer admitted in State A who maintains an office in State B and whose practice is principally before federal courts in State B operates within a combination of 5.5(b), 5.5(c)(4), and 5.5(d)(2). Federal-court practice is governed by the admission rules of the relevant federal court. The state Rule 5.5 inquiry centers on whether the lawyer's office and practice in State B include legal services governed by State B's body of state law and, if so, whether those services fit within a safe harbor or another authorization.

Co-counsel on a matter pending in the host jurisdiction

A lawyer admitted in State A serves as co-counsel on a matter pending in a State B court alongside a lawyer admitted in State B. The 5.5(c)(1) safe harbor applies if the State B lawyer actively participates. If the State A lawyer also seeks to appear in court, the lawyer obtains pro hac vice admission under State B's rules; pre-admission activity falls within 5.5(c)(2) as work in reasonable expectation of authorization.

The "regulation of the legal profession" reference

Subdivision (a) imports each jurisdiction's external regulatory framework. The scope of activity that the regulatory framework reserves to admitted lawyers, the contours of any exceptions for legal-services entities or limited-license practitioners, and the categories of cross-jurisdiction practice the framework authorizes are matters of state regulation outside the Rules of Professional Conduct. Readers should consult published state regulations and authoritative state-bar guidance for the operative framework in any given jurisdiction.

Rule 5.5 operates within whatever framework the state has adopted. Where a state's framework grants a particular category of authority to practice, the rule recognizes that authority. Where the framework does not, the rule provides a professional-conduct anchor for conformity to the framework's boundaries.

Continuing nature of the inquiry

The conditions of 5.5(c) and 5.5(d) are not satisfied once and then disregarded; they must continue to be satisfied throughout the period of host-jurisdiction work. A safe-harbor engagement that begins as a single matter and grows into a recurring practice may at some point cross from temporary into systematic and continuous under 5.5(b)(1). The Comment to Model Rule 5.5 notes that the inquiry should be revisited as the scope of host-jurisdiction work evolves.

Practical impact

Rule 5.5 is the principal anchor for cross-jurisdiction legal services. Several themes recur:

  • The rule is structurally permissive. Subdivisions (c) and (d) define the conditions under which cross-jurisdiction work is affirmatively authorized. Lawyers and firms operating across jurisdictions should structure engagements to fit within one of the safe harbors or exceptions.
  • Marketing and online presence implicate 5.5(b)(2). Firm websites, attorney bios, and social-media profiles that list jurisdictions of practice are subject to the holding-out limitation. Listings should accurately reflect each lawyer's jurisdictions of admission, and any cross-jurisdiction practice listed should be tied to the relevant safe harbor or exception.
  • Active participation matters under 5.5(c)(1). Where the safe harbor depends on association with locally admitted counsel, the active-participation requirement looks to substance. Co-counsel arrangements that involve nominal sponsorship without engagement do not satisfy the rule's terms.
