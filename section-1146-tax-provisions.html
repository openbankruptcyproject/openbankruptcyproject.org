11 U.S.C. Section 1146 - Special Tax Provisions

Plain-English guide to Section 1146: the stamp-tax exemption for transfers under a confirmed Chapter 11 plan and the prompt-determination procedure for tax claims.

What Is Section 1146?

Section 1146 contains two distinct tax provisions that apply only in Chapter 11. Subsection (a) provides a meaningful exemption from state and local stamp and similar transfer taxes for transactions effected under a confirmed plan. Subsection (b) provides an expedited procedure for the bankruptcy court to determine the debtor's state or local tax liability. Both provisions are technical, narrow, and the source of recurring litigation about scope.

Official citation: 11 U.S.C. § 1146

The Stamp-Tax Exemption: Section 1146(a)

Section 1146(a) provides: "The issuance, transfer, or exchange of a security, or the making or delivery of an instrument of transfer under a plan confirmed under section 1129 of this title, may not be taxed under any law imposing a stamp tax or similar tax." The exemption covers transfer taxes traditionally imposed on the issuance of stock, the recording of deeds, and similar instruments. The Internal Revenue Code's federal income-tax provisions are not affected; Section 1146(a) is solely a state-and-local-tax preemption.

Common applications: real-estate transfer taxes when a debtor conveys property to a buyer under the plan; documentary stamp taxes on the issuance of new equity to creditors under the plan; mortgage-recording taxes on new mortgages issued under the plan.

The "Under a Plan" Limit: Florida Department of Revenue v. Piccadilly

The scope of Section 1146(a) was sharply narrowed by the Supreme Court in Florida Department of Revenue v. Piccadilly Cafeterias, Inc., 554 U.S. 33 (2008). The Court held, 7-2, that the exemption applies only to transfers made "under" - that is, pursuant to - a plan that has already been confirmed under Section 1129. A pre-confirmation sale of substantially all assets, even one approved by the bankruptcy court and integral to the eventual plan, does not qualify for the exemption.

Justice Thomas, writing for the majority, emphasized the ordinary meaning of "under": a transfer is "under" a plan only if it occurs after confirmation and is authorized by the confirmed plan. The result is that most Section 363 sales conducted before confirmation do not enjoy the Section 1146(a) exemption, even when the plan is confirmed weeks later. Practitioners structure transactions to maximize exemption availability by deferring eligible transfers until after confirmation.

"Stamp Tax or Similar Tax"

Litigation has also focused on which state and local taxes qualify as "stamp tax or similar tax." Courts have generally held that taxes imposed on the privilege of transferring property (transfer taxes, documentary stamps, deed taxes) are within the exemption. Taxes imposed on the underlying transaction value or on the parties (sales taxes, gross-receipts taxes, capital-gains-style state taxes) are not, because they are not properly characterized as stamp or similar taxes. The case law is fact-specific and varies by state.

Prompt Determination of Tax Liability: Section 1146(b)

Section 1146(b) provides that the bankruptcy court may authorize the proponent of a Chapter 11 plan to request a determination, limited to questions of law, by a state or local governmental unit charged with responsibility for collection or determination of a tax on or measured by income, of the tax effects of the plan. The provision is a Chapter 11 analogue to Section 505(b), which provides a federal-tax prompt-determination procedure.

Section 1146(b) is sparingly used. It is most often invoked in mass-restructuring cases where the tax consequences of plan implementation are material to feasibility and where state revenue departments are willing to engage. Many states resist Section 1146(b) requests; the procedure does not bind the state if the state declines to respond.

Subchapter V and Section 1146

Section 1146 is part of subchapter III of Chapter 11 and applies to all Chapter 11 cases, including cases proceeding under Subchapter V. The same "under a plan" timing limit applies. In practice, Subchapter V cases are smaller and less likely to involve significant transfer-tax exposure, but the exemption can still meaningfully reduce closing costs on real-estate dispositions effected under a confirmed Subchapter V plan.

Practical Drafting Considerations

To maximize the Section 1146(a) benefit, plan proponents should:

  • Identify all qualifying transfers (deeds, security issuances, mortgages) in the plan;
  • Specify that each transfer is being made "pursuant to and under" the plan;
  • Defer transfers to after confirmation when feasible;
  • Include a confirmation-order finding that the Section 1146(a) exemption applies;
  • Provide a process for the debtor to deliver an exemption letter to recording officers;
  • Anticipate state-tax-authority objections at confirmation.
