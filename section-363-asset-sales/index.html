Selling Property of the Estate

Section 363 Asset Sales

Section 363 governs the sale, use, or lease of estate property - the workhorse mechanism by which value is monetized in Chapter 11 reorganizations, Chapter 7 liquidations, and asset-sale Chapter 11 cases that bypass a confirmed plan. These five deep-dives cover the principal moving parts: the asset purchase agreement, free-and-clear sales under section 363(f), credit bidding under section 363(k), stalking-horse procedures, and the court-approval standards that govern.

About section 363 sales

Section 363(b) authorizes the trustee or debtor-in-possession to use, sell, or lease property of the estate other than in the ordinary course of business after notice and a hearing. Section 363(c) authorizes ordinary-course transactions without court approval. The line between ordinary and extraordinary is fact-specific and litigated regularly. The court-approval standard for non-ordinary transactions is articulated under the Lionel business-judgment test: a sound business reason, fair and reasonable price, accurate notice to creditors, and good-faith negotiation.

Section 363(f) permits the sale of estate property free and clear of liens, claims, encumbrances, and other interests if one of five enumerated conditions is met. The most-used condition is section 363(f)(5), which permits a free-and-clear sale if the interest-holder could be compelled to accept money satisfaction in a legal or equitable proceeding. Section 363(m) provides finality protection for good-faith purchasers: a reversal or modification on appeal does not affect the validity of a sale unless the sale was stayed pending appeal.

The stalking-horse pattern - a pre-auction baseline bid with bid protections (break-up fee, expense reimbursement, minimum overbid) - has become the dominant deal structure in chapter 11 asset sales. Court approval of bid procedures and bid protections is governed by a separate motion, typically heard well in advance of the sale hearing itself, and involves an inquiry into whether the protections are reasonable and necessary to attract the stalking horse.

Section 363 deep-dives

  • Asset Purchase MechanicsStructuring the asset purchase agreement for a section 363 sale: representations, conditions, allocation of cure costs under section 365, "subject to higher and better offers" language, and walk rights.
  • Free and Clear SalesThe five section 363(f) conditions, the special rules for co-owned property under section 363(h), and the treatment of successor-liability claims under the free-and-clear order.
  • Credit Bid MechanicsSection 363(k) credit-bidding rights for secured creditors, the "for cause" basis to limit or strip credit bidding under Fisker, and the interaction with section 1129(b)(2)(A) plan confirmation.
  • Stalking-Horse AuctionsBid-procedure motion drafting, break-up fees and expense reimbursement, minimum-overbid increments, qualified-bidder requirements, and the conduct of the auction itself.
  • Court Approval StandardsThe Lionel business-judgment test for non-ordinary-course transactions, the section 363(m) finality protection for good-faith purchasers, and the sub rosa plan prohibition that limits use of section 363 to circumvent plan confirmation.
Strategic note: Section 363(m) finality protection is potent but not automatic - the purchaser must establish good faith on the record at the sale hearing. Failure to obtain an express finding can leave the sale subject to reversal if the appellant obtains a stay pending appeal.
