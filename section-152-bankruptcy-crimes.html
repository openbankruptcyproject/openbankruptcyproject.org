This page describes 18 U.S.C. § 152 in plain English for educational purposes. The Open Bankruptcy Project does not investigate, accuse, or report any individual under this statute. Federal criminal allegations require federal investigators, federal prosecutors, and grand juries.

What Is 18 U.S.C. Section 152?

Section 152 of Title 18 of the United States Code is the principal federal criminal statute addressing bankruptcy fraud. It is not located in the Bankruptcy Code (Title 11) but in the federal criminal code (Title 18, Chapter 9). It defines eight distinct categories of conduct, each constituting a separate federal felony when committed knowingly and fraudulently in connection with a case under Title 11.

Each offense under Section 152 carries a maximum penalty of imprisonment for five years, a fine under Title 18 (currently up to $250,000 for individuals or twice the gross gain or loss), or both. Convictions are also frequently paired with other federal charges including 18 U.S.C. § 157 (bankruptcy fraud scheme), § 1341 (mail fraud), § 1343 (wire fraud), § 1519 (destruction of records in federal investigations), and § 371 (conspiracy).

Official citation: 18 U.S.C. § 152

The Eight Subsections

Each numbered paragraph of Section 152 defines a distinct offense:

  • 152(1) - Concealment of assets: Knowingly and fraudulently concealing from a custodian, trustee, marshal, or other officer of the court, or from creditors, any property belonging to the estate of a debtor.
  • 152(2) - False oath or account: Knowingly and fraudulently making a false oath or account in or in relation to any case under Title 11.
  • 152(3) - False declaration, certificate, verification, or statement: Knowingly and fraudulently making a false declaration, certificate, verification, or statement under penalty of perjury in or in relation to any case under Title 11.
  • 152(4) - False proof of claim: Knowingly and fraudulently presenting any false claim for proof against the estate of a debtor, or using any such claim in any case under Title 11.
  • 152(5) - Receipt of property with intent to defeat the Code: Knowingly and fraudulently receiving any material amount of property from a debtor after the filing of a case, with intent to defeat the provisions of Title 11.
  • 152(6) - Bribery in connection with the case: Knowingly and fraudulently giving, offering, receiving, or attempting to obtain any money, property, reward, or other compensation for acting or forbearing to act in any case.
  • 152(7) - Fraudulent transfer or concealment in contemplation of bankruptcy: In a personal capacity or as an agent or officer of any person or corporation, in contemplation of a case under Title 11 by or against the person or any other person or corporation, or with intent to defeat the provisions of Title 11, knowingly and fraudulently transferring or concealing any of his property or the property of such other person or corporation.
  • 152(8) - Concealment, destruction, mutilation, falsification, or alteration of records: After the filing of a case under Title 11 or in contemplation thereof, knowingly and fraudulently concealing, destroying, mutilating, falsifying, or making a false entry in any recorded information (including books, documents, records, and papers) relating to the property or financial affairs of a debtor.
  • 152(9) - False filings, certifications, and declarations: After the filing of a case under Title 11, knowingly and fraudulently withholding from a custodian, trustee, marshal, or other officer of the court or a United States Trustee entitled to its possession any recorded information (including books, documents, records, and papers) relating to the property or financial affairs of a debtor.

The "Knowingly and Fraudulently" Mens Rea

Each subsection of Section 152 requires the government to prove the defendant acted "knowingly and fraudulently." This is a heightened mens rea: the defendant must have acted with actual knowledge of the truth and with intent to deceive. Mere negligence, recklessness, or even gross carelessness does not satisfy Section 152. The leading appellate articulation is that the defendant must have acted with "specific intent to defraud," not merely with intent to commit the underlying conduct.

Proof of fraudulent intent is typically circumstantial: the pattern of concealment, the timing of transfers, contradictions between schedules and other documents, post-petition inconsistencies, and the defendant's own statements. Reliance on counsel is a recognized defense if the defendant disclosed all material facts and acted in good-faith reliance on counsel's advice.

Federal Prosecution Mechanics

Section 152 is enforced by the U.S. Attorney's Offices in coordination with the U.S. Trustee Program (Department of Justice, Executive Office for United States Trustees). The U.S. Trustee's Criminal Enforcement Program is the principal source of referrals to U.S. Attorney's Offices for bankruptcy crimes. The Federal Bureau of Investigation and the Internal Revenue Service Criminal Investigation Division (IRS-CI) frequently investigate larger cases.

Referral mechanics typically proceed as follows: (1) the U.S. Trustee's regional office identifies suspected criminal conduct during ordinary case oversight or after a private referral; (2) the matter is forwarded to the U.S. Trustee's Criminal Enforcement Coordinator; (3) the Coordinator presents a written referral to the relevant U.S. Attorney's Office; (4) the U.S. Attorney's Office accepts or declines and, if accepting, assigns to an Assistant U.S. Attorney and federal investigative agency. Grand-jury proceedings, indictment, arraignment, plea negotiations, and trial follow ordinary federal criminal procedure under the Federal Rules of Criminal Procedure.

Statute of Limitations: 18 U.S.C. Section 3284

The general federal statute of limitations for non-capital offenses is five years (18 U.S.C. § 3282). However, 18 U.S.C. § 3284 provides a special concealment-of-assets rule for Section 152(1): "The concealment of assets of a debtor in a case under Title 11 shall be deemed to be a continuing offense until the debtor shall have been finally discharged or a discharge denied, and the period of limitations shall not begin to run until such final discharge or denial of discharge." In other words, the five-year clock on a Section 152(1) concealment charge does not begin to run until the bankruptcy concludes, dramatically extending the practical window for prosecution.

Common Fact Patterns at the Abstract Level

Bankruptcy-fraud prosecutions at the federal level typically involve one or more of the following abstract fact patterns:

  • Omission of an asset from schedules (vehicle, bank account, anticipated inheritance, business interest, lawsuit recovery, cryptocurrency, accounts receivable);
  • Pre-petition transfer of an asset to a friendly party (relative, business associate, shell entity) shortly before filing;
  • Misstatement of income or expenses on Schedules I and J or on the Statement of Financial Affairs;
  • False testimony at a Section 341 meeting of creditors or at a Rule 2004 examination;
  • Post-petition transfer of estate property without authorization (also a civil Section 549 issue);
  • Destruction or alteration of business records relevant to the case;
  • Filing a false proof of claim against the estate.

Civil and Criminal Tracks Are Independent

Conduct that violates Section 152 may also support civil consequences in the bankruptcy case itself: denial of discharge under 11 U.S.C. § 727(a), nondischargeability of specific debts under 11 U.S.C. § 523(a)(2), (4), or (6), revocation of an already-granted discharge, dismissal for cause, and trustee avoidance actions. The civil and criminal tracks operate independently; a denial of discharge does not require a criminal conviction, and a criminal conviction does not automatically resolve civil dischargeability questions (though it may have collateral-estoppel effect).

Related Provisions

  • 18 U.S.C. § 153: Embezzlement against the estate (by trustees, custodians, marshals).
  • 18 U.S.C. § 154: Adverse interest and conduct of officers (trustees, examiners).
  • 18 U.S.C. § 155: Fee agreements in cases under Title 11.
  • 18 U.S.C. § 156: Knowing disregard of bankruptcy law or rule by a bankruptcy petition preparer.
  • 18 U.S.C. § 157: Bankruptcy fraud (the "scheme" statute, separately chargeable).
  • 18 U.S.C. § 158: Designation of U.S. Attorneys and agents of the FBI to address fraud.
  • 18 U.S.C. § 1519: Destruction, alteration, or falsification of records in federal investigations (Sarbanes-Oxley).
  • 11 U.S.C. § 727(a) - Civil denial-of-discharge counterpart.
  • 11 U.S.C. § 523(a) - Civil nondischargeability counterpart.

Topical deep-dives on Title 18 bankruptcy crimes

  • 18 USC 152 - concealment, false oaths and claims, and bribery — the nine numbered offenses under the principal federal bankruptcy-crimes statute, mens rea, sentencing, and prosecutorial framework.
  • 18 USC 153 - embezzlement against the estate — the federal embezzlement statute applicable to bankruptcy trustees, custodians, attorneys, and other estate fiduciaries (five-year maximum).
  • 18 USC 156 - knowing disregard of bankruptcy law — criminal liability for a bankruptcy petition preparer's knowing disregard of the Bankruptcy Code or Rules and interplay with 11 USC Section 110.
  • 18 USC 157 - bankruptcy fraud (scheme to defraud) — the three sub-offenses of the federal bankruptcy-fraud scheme statute (five-year maximum), distinct from the single-act offenses of Section 152.
  • 18 USC 1519 - Sarbanes-Oxley anti-shredding statute — twenty-year maximum for destruction, alteration, or falsification of records in federal investigations including bankruptcy.
