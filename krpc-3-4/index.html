Rule of Professional Conduct KRPC 3.4 / MRPC 4-3.4

KRPC 3.4 / MRPC 4-3.4: Fairness to Opposing Party and Counsel

Permitted evidence handling, the witness-contact framework, the structured rules of discovery participation, and the framework for references to inadmissible matter.

Permitted evidence and witness handling that 3.4 establishes

Rule 3.4 is a structured charter for the lawyer's interaction with evidence, witnesses, and opposing counsel. Read affirmatively, the rule confirms what counsel may do at every step: gather and preserve evidence within the scope of legitimate inquiry, contact witnesses and pay statutorily authorized fees, participate fully in discovery within the framework the rules establish, and make references at trial that fall within the scope of admissible matter or matter that the lawyer reasonably believes will be admitted.

The rule operates as a fairness architecture - a description of how the adversary process is supposed to run when both sides are working within its framework. The narrow set of conduct the rule places outside the framework exists to keep that architecture intact; the much broader zone of permitted conduct is what the rule, in practice, protects.

Verbatim text of KRPC 3.4 (Kansas)

A lawyer shall not:

(a) unlawfully obstruct another party's access to evidence or unlawfully alter, destroy or conceal a document or other material having potential evidentiary value. A lawyer shall not counsel or assist another person to do any such act;

(b) falsify evidence, counsel or assist a witness to testify falsely, or offer an inducement to a witness that is prohibited by law;

(c) knowingly disobey an obligation under the rules of a tribunal except for an open refusal based on an assertion that no valid obligation exists;

(d) in pretrial procedure, make a frivolous discovery request or fail to make reasonably diligent effort to comply with a legally proper discovery request by an opposing party;

(e) in trial, allude to any matter that the lawyer does not reasonably believe is relevant or that will not be supported by admissible evidence, assert personal knowledge of facts in issue except when testifying as a witness, or state a personal opinion as to the justness of a cause, the credibility of a witness, the culpability of a civil litigant or the guilt or innocence of an accused;

(f) request a person other than a client to refrain from voluntarily giving relevant information to another party unless: (1) the person is a relative or an employee or other agent of a client; and (2) the lawyer reasonably believes that the person's interests will not be adversely affected by refraining from giving such information. Kan. Sup. Ct. R. 226, KRPC 3.4.

Missouri Rule 4-3.4

Missouri Rule 4-3.4 is substantively identical to KRPC 3.4 and tracks ABA Model Rule 3.4. The structure is the same: subdivisions (a) through (f) each carve a small zone of unfair conduct out of a much larger zone of permitted advocacy.

Evidence-handling permissions

Counsel may gather, organize, preserve, and present evidence on the client's behalf. The rule confirms two affirmative parameters of that work:

  • Lawful preservation. Counsel may take custody of documents and tangible items having potential evidentiary value and preserve them in the ordinary course of the representation. Maintaining a litigation hold, organizing client documents for production, and securing physical items for forensic examination are all within the protected zone.
  • Lawful access. Counsel may use every method the rules of procedure provide for gathering evidence - requests for production, subpoenas, depositions, requests for admission, third-party document subpoenas, and the like. None of these are obstructive; they are the access mechanisms the system provides.

The narrow zone subdivision (a) places outside the protected sphere is "unlawful" obstruction, alteration, destruction, or concealment - that is, conduct contrary to law, court order, or rule of procedure. The qualifier "unlawful" carries weight; routine evidence handling is plainly not what the rule reaches.

Witness contact framework

Counsel may contact witnesses, interview them, ask them to attend deposition or trial, and pay them lawful witness fees and reasonable expenses. The rule's Comment makes the affirmative framework explicit:

  • Reasonable compensation for time. Counsel may pay a witness reasonable compensation for time lost in attending or testifying, and reasonable expenses incurred
  • Expert-witness compensation. Counsel may pay an expert witness a reasonable fee for the expert's professional services
  • Voluntary interviews. Counsel may seek voluntary interviews with witnesses (subject to Rule 4.2's no-contact rule for represented persons)

The rule places outside the protected zone only payments that operate as inducements to testify in a particular way - that is, payment contingent on content of testimony, or payment beyond what statute permits.

Witness-availability framework under 3.4(f)

Subdivision (f) addresses requests that a person refrain from voluntarily giving information to another party. The default rule is that counsel may not make such requests of non-clients, but the rule expressly permits the request in a defined zone: the person is a relative, employee, or other agent of the client, AND counsel reasonably believes the person's interests will not be adversely affected. This is an affirmative carve-in: within the defined zone, counsel may give such advice on a routine basis as part of representing the organizational or family-related client.

Discovery participation framework under 3.4(d)

Subdivision (d) addresses pretrial procedure and operates on two parallel duties: do not make a frivolous discovery request, and make reasonably diligent effort to comply with a legally proper discovery request. The affirmative content of both duties is broad. Counsel may serve any discovery request grounded in a legitimate theory of the case, and counsel may interpose any objection the rules of procedure provide. The rule simply requires that requests not be frivolous and that responses not be obstructive.

What the rule plainly permits:

  • Asserting privileges (attorney-client, work product, joint-defense, common-interest, statutory privileges)
  • Objecting on grounds of relevance, proportionality, burden, or scope
  • Seeking protective orders or in-camera review where appropriate
  • Negotiating discovery scope and timing with opposing counsel
  • Producing documents subject to confidentiality designations under a stipulated order

Affirmative read-out. Rule 3.4 is a fairness architecture, not a set of traps. The vast majority of evidence handling, witness contact, and discovery participation falls inside the protected zone. The rule's prohibitions exist to keep the architecture intact for everyone.

Tribunal-obligation framework under 3.4(c)

Subdivision (c) prohibits knowing disobedience of a tribunal's rules, but expressly preserves the lawyer's ability to make an open refusal based on an assertion that no valid obligation exists. This is a deliberate design choice: it protects counsel's ability to challenge the validity of an order or rule through appropriate channels - by direct appeal, by mandamus, by interlocutory application - so long as the challenge is made openly rather than through covert non-compliance. Counsel may, for example, refuse to comply with an order counsel believes to be unconstitutional and seek prompt review.

Trial-reference framework under 3.4(e)

Subdivision (e) governs counsel's references at trial. Its affirmative content: counsel may allude to any matter the lawyer reasonably believes is relevant and will be supported by admissible evidence. The standard turns on the lawyer's reasonable belief; certainty is not required. Counsel may preview anticipated evidence in opening statement, may reference exhibits before they are formally admitted (subject to the court's procedures), and may make arguments about the inferences the evidence supports.

The rule also addresses three specific categories of restriction at trial:

  • Assertion of personal knowledge of facts in issue (permitted only when the lawyer testifies as a witness - see Rule 3.7)
  • Statement of personal opinion on the justness of a cause
  • Statement of personal opinion on the credibility of a witness, the culpability of a civil litigant, or the guilt or innocence of an accused

The affirmative space: counsel may argue every inference the evidence supports, may marshal the evidence in any rhetorical structure, and may urge the fact-finder to reach any conclusion the evidence permits. The rule constrains personal-opinion vouching; it does not constrain argument.

Common procedural posture

Litigation hold and document preservation

Counsel issues a litigation hold to the client, organizes preservation of relevant documents, and oversees production through document-by-document privilege review. The entire process is within the protected zone of 3.4(a). Routine preservation and privilege screening are precisely what the rule contemplates.

Expert witness retention

Counsel retains an expert, agrees to a reasonable fee for the expert's professional services and time spent testifying, and prepares the expert through customary expert-witness preparation. The arrangement is plainly within 3.4's permitted framework. The rule prohibits inducement to testify in a particular way; it does not prohibit reasonable compensation for the expert's professional work.

Relationship to other rules

  • Rule 3.3 - Candor toward the tribunal; partial overlap on falsification of evidence
  • Rule 3.5 - Impartiality and decorum of the tribunal
  • Rule 4.4 - Respect for rights of third persons; aligned values
  • Rule 8.4(c) - Conduct involving dishonesty or fraud; reaches some 3.4(a)-(b) violations

Practical impact

Rule 3.4 is the workhorse of day-to-day litigation conduct. Counsel who follows the rules of procedure, asserts colorable objections, preserves documents under reasonable holds, pays expert witnesses customary fees, and argues the inferences the evidence supports operates entirely within the protected zone. The rule's prohibitions reach a narrow class of conduct - knowing destruction, falsification, contingent witness inducement, frivolous discovery practice - that is far outside ordinary practice.
