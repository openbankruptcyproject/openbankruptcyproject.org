Rule of Professional Conduct KRPC 1.5 / MRPC 4-1.5

KRPC 1.5 / MRPC 4-1.5: Fees

The fee architecture the rule permits: reasonable-fee factors, contingent-fee structure, fee-sharing between firms, and the written-agreement requirement.

What Rule 1.5 affirmatively does

Rule 1.5 is the architecture of attorney compensation. It identifies the factors that make a fee reasonable, expressly authorizes contingent fees in matters where the recovery makes contingency workable, permits two unrelated firms to share a fee on a single matter under defined conditions, and requires that the basis or rate of the fee be communicated to the client in writing in the contexts where written confirmation matters most. The rule is enabling: it tells the bar what fee structures are permitted and how to deploy them.

Verbatim text of KRPC 1.5 (Kansas, abbreviated)

(a) A lawyer's fee shall be reasonable. The factors to be considered in determining the reasonableness of a fee include the following: (1) the time and labor required, the novelty and difficulty of the questions involved, and the skill requisite to perform the legal service properly; (2) the likelihood, if apparent to the client, that the acceptance of the particular employment will preclude other employment by the lawyer; (3) the fee customarily charged in the locality for similar legal services; (4) the amount involved and the results obtained; (5) the time limitations imposed by the client or by the circumstances; (6) the nature and length of the professional relationship with the client; (7) the experience, reputation, and ability of the lawyer or lawyers performing the services; and (8) whether the fee is fixed or contingent.

(b) When the lawyer has not regularly represented the client, the basis or rate of the fee shall be communicated to the client, preferably in writing, before or within a reasonable time after commencing the representation.

(c) A fee may be contingent on the outcome of the matter for which the service is rendered, except in a matter in which a contingent fee is prohibited by paragraph (d) or other law. A contingent fee agreement shall be in writing and shall state the method by which the fee is to be determined, including the percentage or percentages that shall accrue to the lawyer in the event of settlement, trial or appeal, litigation and other expenses to be deducted from the recovery, and whether such expenses are to be deducted before or after the contingent fee is calculated.

(e) A division of fee between lawyers who are not in the same firm may be made only if: (1) the division is in proportion to the services performed by each lawyer or each lawyer assumes joint responsibility for the representation; (2) the client is advised of and does not object to the participation of all the lawyers involved; and (3) the total fee is reasonable. Kan. Sup. Ct. R. 226, KRPC 1.5.

Missouri Rule 4-1.5

Missouri Rule 4-1.5 tracks ABA Model Rule 1.5 and adopts the same eight reasonableness factors, the same contingent-fee structure, and the same fee-sharing conditions. Missouri imposes additional written-disclosure requirements at the contingent-fee end of the spectrum.

1.5(a) - The eight reasonableness factors

Subdivision (a) supplies the eight-factor test that determines whether a fee is reasonable. The factors are not weighted; they are read together as a holistic measure of reasonableness in context.

Labor-side factors

Time and labor required (factor 1), the novelty and difficulty of the questions involved (factor 1), the skill required (factor 1), and the time limitations imposed (factor 5). These measure what the work cost the lawyer to perform.

Market-side factors

Customary fee in the locality (factor 3), the amount involved and results obtained (factor 4), and the experience and reputation of the lawyer (factor 7). These measure what the work was worth in the market.

Relationship factors

Nature and length of the professional relationship (factor 6) and the preclusion of other employment (factor 2). These measure the opportunity cost of the engagement and the depth of the lawyer-client relationship.

Structure factor

Whether the fee is fixed or contingent (factor 8). A contingent fee that compensates for the risk of nonrecovery may reasonably exceed an hourly fee that the lawyer would have charged for the same work.

1.5(b) - Communication of the basis or rate

When a lawyer has not regularly represented the client, the basis or rate of the fee must be communicated to the client - preferably in writing in Kansas and required in writing for contingent fees in both jurisdictions - before or within a reasonable time after commencing the representation. The communication describes how the fee will be calculated: hourly rate plus expenses, flat fee for a defined scope, contingent percentage, or some combination.

1.5(c) - The contingent-fee structure

Subdivision (c) expressly authorizes contingent fees in any matter where contingency is not specifically prohibited. The contingent-fee architecture has four required components in the written agreement:

  1. Method of calculation. The agreement must state how the fee is calculated - typically as a percentage of recovery.
  2. Percentages at each stage. If the percentage varies by settlement, trial, or appeal, each percentage must be stated.
  3. Litigation and other expenses. The expenses to be deducted from the recovery must be identified.
  4. Order of operations. The agreement must state whether expenses are deducted before or after the contingent fee is calculated - a distinction that meaningfully changes the client's net recovery.

The contingent fee expands access. Contingency makes representation available to clients who could not pay an hourly rate. The structure aligns the lawyer's compensation with the client's recovery and shifts the risk of nonrecovery to the lawyer. Rule 1.5(c) does not cap the percentage; it requires that the percentage and structure be in writing and that the total fee remain reasonable under the 1.5(a) factors.

Settlement statement at conclusion

Upon conclusion of a contingent-fee matter, the lawyer must provide the client with a written statement showing the outcome, the remittance to the client, the method of determination, and the expenses deducted.

1.5(d) - Matters where contingency is not used

Contingent fees are not used in two specific categories: domestic relations matters where the fee is contingent on securing a divorce or on the amount of alimony or support, and criminal-defense matters. The carve-outs exist to keep the fee structure from interfering with substantive outcomes in matters where contingency would distort the lawyer's incentives.

1.5(e) - Fee sharing between firms

Two unrelated firms may share a single fee on a matter when three conditions are met:

  • Proportional division or joint responsibility. Either each lawyer's share matches the services that lawyer performed, or each lawyer assumes joint responsibility for the representation.
  • Client notice without objection. The client is advised of the participation of all lawyers involved and does not object.
  • Total fee reasonable. The aggregate fee, after splitting, satisfies the 1.5(a) reasonableness factors.

The architecture allows specialty referrals - a generalist refers a complex matter to a specialty firm and remains involved enough to assume joint responsibility - while preserving the client's right to know about and consent to the arrangement.

The engagement-letter checklist

  • Basis or rate. Hourly rate, flat fee, or contingent percentage.
  • Scope. What the fee covers, by reference to Rule 1.2.
  • Expenses. What costs the client pays separately, and whether expenses are deducted before or after a contingent fee is calculated.
  • Billing rhythm. When the client receives bills and what level of detail they contain.
  • Modifications. How a change in fee structure will be handled - by amendment with the client's signed consent.
  • Termination. What happens to the unearned portion of a flat fee or retainer upon termination under Rule 1.16(d).

Continuing nature of the duty

The reasonableness of a fee is evaluated at the time the work is done and again at the time the fee is collected. A fee that was reasonable at the engagement's outset can drift toward unreasonableness if the matter expands and the lawyer does not communicate the change. Rule 1.4's communication duty supplies the running update that keeps Rule 1.5 reasonableness intact.

Practical impact

Rule 1.5 is the rule clients consult when they question a bill and the rule lawyers consult when they design a fee structure. Three takeaways recur:

  • Written agreements are the engine. A written engagement letter that satisfies 1.5(b) prevents most fee disputes before they begin.
  • The eight factors operate as a holistic test. No single factor is decisive; reasonableness is the aggregate impression.
  • Contingency expands access. Subdivision (c) is one of the principal access-to-justice mechanisms in the rules of professional conduct.
