11 U.S.C. Section 526 - Restrictions on Debt Relief Agencies

Plain-English guide to BAPCPA's regulation of debt-relief agencies, including attorneys, who provide bankruptcy assistance to consumer debtors.

What Is Section 526?

Section 526 is one of three related BAPCPA provisions (with Sections 527 and 528) regulating "debt relief agencies." The term is defined in Section 101(12A) to include any person who provides "bankruptcy assistance" to an "assisted person" in return for the payment of money or other valuable consideration. "Assisted person" is defined in Section 101(3) as a person whose debts consist primarily of consumer debts and whose nonexempt property is less than the inflation-adjusted threshold.

The Supreme Court resolved a circuit split on attorney coverage in Milavetz, Gallop & Milavetz, P.A. v. United States, 559 U.S. 229 (2010), holding that attorneys who provide bankruptcy assistance to assisted persons are debt-relief agencies subject to Sections 526, 527, and 528.

Official citation: 11 U.S.C. § 526

Prohibited Conduct: Section 526(a)

Section 526(a) prohibits debt-relief agencies from engaging in four categories of conduct:

  • 526(a)(1): Failing to perform any service the agency informed the assisted person it would provide in connection with a bankruptcy case.
  • 526(a)(2): Making any statement, or counseling or advising any assisted person or prospective assisted person to make a statement, in a document filed in a bankruptcy case that is untrue or misleading, or that upon the exercise of reasonable care should have been known to be untrue or misleading.
  • 526(a)(3): Misrepresenting to any assisted person or prospective assisted person, directly or indirectly, the services that such agency will provide to such person, or the benefits and risks that may result if such person becomes a debtor in a bankruptcy case.
  • 526(a)(4): Advising an assisted person or prospective assisted person to incur more debt in contemplation of filing a bankruptcy case under this title, or to pay an attorney or bankruptcy petition preparer fee or charge for services performed as part of preparing for or representing a debtor in a case.

The Milavetz Court read the (a)(4) prohibition narrowly to apply only to advice to incur additional debt "in contemplation" of bankruptcy, that is, with the actual intent that the debt be discharged. Routine advice to manage existing debt or to obtain financing for legitimate non-bankruptcy purposes is not prohibited.

Mandatory Disclosures: Cross-Reference to Section 527

Although Section 526 focuses on prohibited conduct, related Section 527 imposes affirmative disclosure obligations. Debt-relief agencies must provide assisted persons with specific written notices about the costs of bankruptcy, the consequences of filing inaccurate information, and the availability of pro se filing. Required disclosures include a statement that "An attorney or bankruptcy petition preparer is required to assist [the debtor] in completing certain documents" and specific language about the assisted person's right to choose to file pro se.

Section 528 imposes additional advertising-disclosure requirements, including the mandatory tagline "We are a debt relief agency. We help people file for bankruptcy relief under the Bankruptcy Code." Failure to include this tagline in covered advertising is itself a 526-528 violation.

Enforcement: Section 526(c)

Section 526(c) provides three enforcement mechanisms:

  • 526(c)(1): Contracts that do not comply with the material requirements of Sections 526, 527, and 528 may not be enforced by any federal or state court or any other person, other than the assisted person. The assisted person may enforce the contract.
  • 526(c)(2): A debt-relief agency that is intentionally or negligently in violation may be liable to the assisted person for actual damages, the agency's fees, and reasonable attorneys' fees and costs. A state attorney general or other officer also has standing to sue in parens patriae for residents of the state.
  • 526(c)(5): The bankruptcy court may enjoin violations and impose appropriate civil penalties of up to the agency's gross income from the case for each violation.

The remedy structure is unusual: the assisted person can enforce a non-compliant contract against the agency (compelling performance or refund) but the agency cannot enforce the contract against the assisted person.

Pre-Petition Communication Restrictions

Section 526(a)(4)'s prohibition on advising assisted persons to incur additional debt has practical effects on consumer-bankruptcy counsel. Counsel should not advise a prospective debtor to charge up credit cards or take loans intending to discharge the new debt. Counsel may, however, advise on legitimate management of existing debt, on the timing of filings to maximize exemption planning under Section 522, and on financing necessary post-petition operations of a small business contemplating reorganization.

The boundary between permissible advice and prohibited advice is fact-intensive. Counsel should document the legitimate non-bankruptcy purpose of any borrowing-related advice given before filing.

Related Bankruptcy Code Sections

This section operates in concert with several other provisions of the Bankruptcy Code:

  • Section 329 - Attorney fee disclosure obligations
  • Section 522 - Exemption planning and the assisted-person threshold
  • Section 525 - Discrimination against debtors
  • Section 727 - Chapter 7 discharge sought by assisted persons

Understanding how these sections interact is important for debtors, creditors, trustees, and counsel navigating a bankruptcy case.
