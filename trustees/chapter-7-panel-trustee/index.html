Chapter 7 Panel Trustee Role

Plain-English guide to the appointment, statutory duties, bond, and compensation of the private trustee who administers a Chapter 7 liquidation case.

What Is a Chapter 7 Panel Trustee?

A Chapter 7 panel trustee is a private individual, not a federal employee, who is appointed by the United States Trustee to administer Chapter 7 liquidation bankruptcies in a particular federal judicial district. Panel trustees serve at the pleasure of the United States Trustee under 28 U.S.C. § 586(a)(1), which directs each United States Trustee to "establish, maintain, and supervise a panel of private trustees that are eligible and available to serve as trustees in cases under chapter 7 of title 11."

When a Chapter 7 petition is filed, the United States Trustee assigns an interim trustee from the district panel under 11 U.S.C. § 701. That interim trustee ordinarily becomes the permanent trustee at the conclusion of the meeting of creditors under Section 702 unless creditors holding the requisite claims elect a different qualified person.

Authority: 28 U.S.C. § 586(a)(1); 11 U.S.C. §§ 701, 702.

Panel Selection and Qualifications

Selection to a panel is governed by United States Trustee Program (USTP) regulations at 28 C.F.R. Part 58. Applicants must be individuals (not entities), must be competent to perform the duties of a trustee, and must be physically able to perform the duties. The regulations contemplate background investigation, professional references, and an interview process. The USTP publishes guidance, including the Handbook for Chapter 7 Trustees, that elaborates qualifications and ongoing performance standards.

A panel trustee need not be an attorney. Many panel trustees are attorneys, accountants, or experienced bankruptcy professionals, but non-attorney panel trustees are permitted and common in many districts. A panel trustee who is not an attorney must retain counsel under Section 327 for legal services that require a licensed attorney.

Membership on a panel is not a permanent appointment. The United States Trustee may suspend or remove a panel trustee for cause, including unsatisfactory performance, conflicts of interest, or violation of program rules. Removed trustees may seek administrative review under 28 C.F.R. § 58.6.

The Bond Requirement: Section 322

11 U.S.C. § 322 requires that, before beginning the duties of office, a trustee file with the court a bond in favor of the United States, conditioned on the faithful performance of the trustee's official duties. The amount and sureties of the bond are approved by the United States Trustee. In most districts, panel trustees are covered by a blanket bond purchased by the USTP that applies to all cases assigned to that trustee, with case-specific increases authorized when warranted by the size of the estate.

The bond is the principal financial guarantee available to creditors and the estate if a trustee misappropriates or mismanages assets. Claims on the bond are made directly against the surety. The bond requirement complements the fiduciary duties imposed by Section 704 and the criminal prohibitions on trustee misconduct under 18 U.S.C. § 153.

Statutory Duties: Section 704

11 U.S.C. § 704(a) enumerates the duties of the Chapter 7 trustee:

  • 704(a)(1): Collect and reduce to money the property of the estate for which the trustee serves, and close such estate as expeditiously as is compatible with the best interests of parties in interest.
  • 704(a)(2): Be accountable for all property received.
  • 704(a)(3): Ensure that the debtor performs the intention specified in the statement of intention under Section 521(a)(2)(B).
  • 704(a)(4): Investigate the financial affairs of the debtor.
  • 704(a)(5): Examine proofs of claims and object to the allowance of any improper claim if a purpose would be served.
  • 704(a)(6): Oppose the discharge of the debtor if advisable.
  • 704(a)(7): Furnish information concerning the estate and the estate's administration as requested by a party in interest.
  • 704(a)(8): File periodic reports and summaries of the operation of any business operated by the trustee.
  • 704(a)(9): Make a final report and file a final account of the administration of the estate.
  • 704(a)(10) through (12): Comply with notice and reporting requirements relating to domestic support obligations, post-petition tax returns of debtors, and certain health-care-business transfers.

The trustee may, with court approval under Section 327, employ counsel, accountants, auctioneers, and other professionals to assist in carrying out these duties. The cost of those professionals is paid as an administrative expense under Section 503(b).

Compensation: Sections 326 and 330

Chapter 7 panel trustees are compensated under a two-tier framework. 11 U.S.C. § 326(a) sets a statutory ceiling for trustee compensation, calculated as a graduated percentage of "all moneys disbursed or turned over in the case by the trustee to parties in interest, excluding the debtor, but including holders of secured claims":

  • 25 percent on the first $5,000 or less,
  • 10 percent on amounts in excess of $5,000 but not in excess of $50,000,
  • 5 percent on amounts in excess of $50,000 but not in excess of $1,000,000, and
  • reasonable compensation not to exceed 3 percent of amounts in excess of $1,000,000.

The Section 326(a) percentage is a maximum, not an automatic entitlement. The actual award is determined under Section 330(a), which authorizes "reasonable compensation for actual, necessary services rendered" considering the nature, extent, and value of the services, the time spent, and customary compensation for comparable non-bankruptcy services. The trustee must file a fee application that satisfies the standards of Federal Rule of Bankruptcy Procedure 2016.

In "no-asset" cases (the substantial majority of consumer Chapter 7 filings), there are no funds to disburse, and the trustee receives a flat administrative fee from the filing fee under 28 U.S.C. § 330(b). The amount of that flat fee is set by statute and historically has been $60 per estate, paid out of the filing fee or by court direction.

The Supreme Court held in Lamie v. United States Trustee, 540 U.S. 526 (2004), that debtor's counsel in a Chapter 7 case cannot be paid from estate property under Section 330(a)(1) absent appointment under Section 327. The same rigor applies to all professionals retained on the estate's behalf.

Relationship to Other Parties

The Chapter 7 trustee occupies the central administrative role in the case but interacts with several other parties:

  • The debtor: The trustee is not the debtor's adversary in the abstract, but the trustee's fiduciary duty runs to creditors. The trustee conducts the Section 341 meeting of creditors, evaluates the debtor's schedules, and may pursue avoidance actions against the debtor or related parties.
  • Creditors: The trustee reviews and objects to proofs of claim, distributes estate proceeds under the Section 726 priority scheme, and provides information to creditors on request under Section 704(a)(7).
  • The United States Trustee: The USTP supervises panel trustees, audits their performance, and may move to remove a trustee for cause under Section 324. The USTP also receives the trustee's final reports.
  • The bankruptcy court: The court approves the trustee's professional retentions, fee applications, and sales of estate property under Section 363, and rules on contested matters.
  • Professionals: The trustee selects and supervises counsel, accountants, and other professionals retained under Section 327.

Practical Impact for Debtors and Creditors

For most consumer debtors, the Chapter 7 trustee is the case official the debtor will actually interact with. The Section 341 meeting of creditors, conducted by the trustee, is the most consequential live event in many Chapter 7 cases. The trustee will examine the debtor under oath about the schedules and the disposition of assets, and may follow up with document requests, turnover demands, or adversary proceedings.

For creditors, the trustee is the gatekeeper of estate value. A trustee who actively investigates undervalued assets, fraudulent transfers, or preferential payments can generate distributions in cases that initially looked like no-asset filings. Conversely, a trustee who closes cases without investigation forecloses creditor recovery. Creditor scrutiny of the trustee's final report, and where appropriate motions under Section 324 to remove or under Section 327 to seek special counsel, are the principal tools available to engaged creditors.

Removal and Accountability

11 U.S.C. § 324(a) authorizes the court, after notice and hearing, to remove a trustee, other than the United States Trustee, for cause. Cause includes failure to perform statutory duties, breach of fiduciary duty, conflicts of interest, and incompetence. Removal under Section 324 is distinct from administrative removal from the panel by the United States Trustee.

Trustees are subject to fiduciary liability for negligent or willful breach of duty. The Supreme Court in Mosser v. Darrow, 341 U.S. 267 (1951), articulated the high standard of care imposed on bankruptcy trustees and the personal liability that may attach to a trustee who fails to meet it.
