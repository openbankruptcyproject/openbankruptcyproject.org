Section 02Statutory Role

Chapter 7 panel trustees are appointed case-by-case from a panel maintained by the United States Trustee under 28 U.S.C. § 586(a)(1). At the section 341 meeting of creditors, the panel trustee assigned to a case examines the debtor under oath about the debtor’s assets and liabilities. The trustee’s statutory duties are set out in 11 U.S.C. § 704 and include collecting and reducing to money the property of the estate, investigating the financial affairs of the debtor, examining proofs of claim, opposing discharge if appropriate, and making a final report.

Panel trustees exercise the estate’s avoidance powers under sections 544-553 of the Bankruptcy Code, including the strong-arm power, preferential transfer recovery, fraudulent transfer recovery, and statutory liens avoidance. Section 554 governs abandonment of burdensome property. Compensation in asset cases is calculated under 11 U.S.C. § 326 as a percentage of moneys disbursed to parties in interest (not including the debtor), capped at 25% on the first $5,000, 10% on the next $45,000, 5% on the next $950,000, and 3% above $1,000,000. In no-asset cases the trustee receives a flat $60 statutory fee from the filing fee.

Section 04Compensation Framework

Panel-trustee compensation in asset cases follows the sliding scale of 11 U.S.C. § 326: 25% on the first $5,000 disbursed, 10% on the next $45,000, 5% on the next $950,000, and 3% above $1,000,000. In a no-asset Chapter 7 case the trustee receives only a $60 statutory fee paid from the filing fee. Because the same trustee is paid only on actual distributions, panel-trustee economics differ structurally from fee-for-service professionals.

Section 06Privacy Rule (FRBP 9037) Context

Federal Rule of Bankruptcy Procedure 9037 requires that Social Security Numbers, dates of birth, financial account numbers, and minor-child identifiers be redacted in bankruptcy filings. Trustees commonly encounter unredacted personal identifiers on proofs of claim, schedules, and exhibits. The Federal Judicial Center’s April 2024 empirical study, Unredacted Social Security Numbers in Federal Court PACER Documents, documented systemic compliance shortfalls — particularly in proof-of-claim filings, which the study found to be 98% noncompliant. OBP cites the FJC study only as national context; OBP does not publish per-trustee or per-district Rule 9037 compliance numbers.

Primary source: Unredacted Social Security Numbers in Federal Court PACER Documents (FJC, April 2024).
