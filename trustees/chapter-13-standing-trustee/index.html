Chapter 13 Standing Trustee Role

Plain-English guide to the per-district appointment, statutory duties, fund mechanics, and percentage-fee compensation of the standing trustee in Chapter 13 wage-earner cases.

What Is a Chapter 13 Standing Trustee?

A Chapter 13 standing trustee is a private individual, appointed by the United States Trustee, who administers all Chapter 13 cases (or all cases within a designated geographic subdivision) filed in a particular federal judicial district. Unlike the Chapter 7 panel-based model, where the United States Trustee assigns cases from a roster of trustees, the standing-trustee model concentrates Chapter 13 administration in one trustee (or a small number of trustees) per district under 28 U.S.C. § 586(b).

Section 586(b) authorizes the United States Trustee to "appoint one or more individuals to serve as standing trustee, or designate one or more assistant United States trustees to serve in cases under chapter 12 or 13 of title 11" when the volume of cases warrants. In practice, every federal judicial district has at least one Chapter 13 standing trustee, and high-volume districts have multiple standing trustees serving different geographic divisions.

Authority: 28 U.S.C. § 586(b); 11 U.S.C. § 1302.

Appointment and Qualifications

The standing-trustee model produces an operationally consolidated office. A standing trustee typically employs a permanent staff of accountants, case administrators, and counsel, and operates from dedicated premises. The trustee is not a federal employee; the trustee and the staff are paid from the standing trustee fund described below, not from federal appropriations.

Selection and qualifications are governed by USTP regulations at 28 C.F.R. Part 58. The application process includes a background investigation, professional references, and an interview. As with Chapter 7 panel trustees, a standing trustee need not be an attorney, although many are. A non-attorney standing trustee retains counsel under Section 327 for legal services. The trustee must post a bond under 11 U.S.C. § 322 conditioned on faithful performance of the trustee's duties.

Statutory Duties: Section 1302

11 U.S.C. § 1302(b) enumerates the duties of the Chapter 13 trustee. The trustee must:

  • 1302(b)(1): Perform the duties specified in Sections 704(a)(2), (3), (4), (5), (6), (7), and (9), which incorporate the Chapter 7 duties of accounting for property, ensuring debtor compliance with stated intentions, investigating financial affairs, examining proofs of claim, opposing discharge if advisable, furnishing information, and filing a final report.
  • 1302(b)(2): Appear and be heard at any hearing that concerns the value of property subject to a lien, confirmation of a plan, or modification of the plan after confirmation.
  • 1302(b)(3): Dispose of any moneys paid into the court for the case, as required by the plan, and otherwise comply with the duties imposed by Section 1326.
  • 1302(b)(4): Advise, other than on legal matters, and assist the debtor in performance under the plan.
  • 1302(b)(5): Ensure that the debtor commences making timely payments under Section 1326.
  • 1302(b)(6): If with respect to the debtor there is a claim for a domestic-support obligation, perform the notice duties specified in Section 1302(d).

The advising and assisting function under Section 1302(b)(4) is unique to Chapter 13. The standing trustee is expected to coach the debtor through the multi-year plan, identify modification opportunities, and flag default before it becomes terminal.

Payment, Disbursement, and the Section 1326 Framework

11 U.S.C. § 1326 directs the debtor to commence making payments under the proposed plan no later than 30 days after the petition is filed, and to make such payments to the trustee until the plan is confirmed or denied. The trustee retains the funds, releases approved adequate-protection payments to secured creditors, and, upon confirmation, begins disbursing under the plan's terms.

The standing trustee operates the payment infrastructure for the case. The trustee receives debtor payments (most commonly by wage order under Section 1325(c) or by direct ACH), maintains an accounting of plan compliance, and disburses to creditors in priority order. Creditors holding allowed claims look to the trustee, not to the debtor directly, for plan distributions in most cases. The trustee's records form the core evidence in any dispute over whether the debtor has performed under the plan.

Compensation: Section 1302(d) and the Standing Trustee Fund

28 U.S.C. § 586(e) establishes the compensation framework. The Attorney General, after consultation with the United States Trustee, fixes (1) a maximum annual compensation for each standing trustee and (2) a percentage fee to be collected from all payments received by the trustee under plans in cases administered by that trustee.

The percentage fee is capped by statute at 10 percent of all payments under the plan, but the actual percentage charged in any district is set by the Attorney General to be no higher than necessary to fund (a) the trustee's actual necessary expenses of operation and (b) the trustee's authorized compensation up to the annual ceiling. Many districts operate at percentages well below the 10 percent cap; the precise percentage is reset periodically and published in the standing trustee's operating budget approved by the USTP.

How the math works: If the percentage fee is 7 percent, then on a $500 monthly plan payment, the trustee retains $35 for operations and compensation, and disburses $465 to creditors and priority claimants.

Standing-trustee compensation is not awarded through Section 330 fee applications. Instead, the trustee's individual salary is fixed in advance by the Attorney General, and the trustee's office operating expenses are paid from the fund collected through the percentage fee. Any excess of fund receipts over authorized compensation and necessary expenses is returned to the United States Treasury under Section 586(e)(2)(B)(ii). This structure insulates standing-trustee income from the volume of any single case and from the outcome of any individual fee dispute.

Relationship to Other Parties

The standing trustee occupies a distinctive supervisory and operational role across many cases simultaneously:

  • The debtor: The trustee conducts the Section 341 meeting, reviews plan feasibility, and supervises ongoing compliance. The trustee is the most frequent point of contact for the debtor and debtor's counsel during the 36-to-60-month plan period.
  • Creditors: The trustee processes claims, distributes payments, and may object to claims that are improperly filed or inflated. Secured creditors holding ongoing claims (mortgages, vehicle loans) receive monthly disbursements through the trustee in most plans.
  • The United States Trustee: The USTP supervises the standing trustee, reviews the operating budget, and audits the trustee's office annually. The USTP also reviews chapter-wide compliance with statutory duties.
  • The bankruptcy court: The court confirms or denies plans, rules on contested matters, and grants discharges. The trustee participates as a party in interest at most confirmation and modification hearings.

Practical Impact for Debtors

For Chapter 13 debtors, the standing trustee is the most important non-judicial figure in the case. The trustee will conduct the Section 341 meeting, recommend confirmation or object to it, and supervise plan performance for three to five years. A debtor who falls behind on plan payments will typically be contacted by the trustee's office before any motion to dismiss is filed. The trustee's advising function under Section 1302(b)(4) is an underutilized resource; debtors and counsel who proactively engage with the trustee on modification opportunities, hardship-discharge analysis, and post-confirmation life events generally fare better than those who treat the trustee as adversarial.

Debtors and counsel should understand that the percentage fee is not an extra expense the debtor pays separately. It is a deduction from each plan payment before disbursement. A plan that promises 100 percent payment to unsecured creditors over 60 months at a 7 percent trustee fee builds the trustee's fee into the required monthly payment from the outset.
