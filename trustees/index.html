Bankruptcy Trustees

Several distinct categories of trustee operate within the federal bankruptcy system. Each has a different statutory mandate, compensation structure, supervisory chain, and relationship to the debtor and creditors. These role guides cover the five principal trustee categories, plus the closely-related Chapter 11 examiner.

About the United States Trustee Program

The United States Trustee Program (USTP) is a component of the U.S. Department of Justice established under 28 U.S.C. § 581 to oversee the administration of bankruptcy cases. The program is headquartered in Washington and divided into 21 regions covering 88 federal judicial districts (Alabama and North Carolina retain the legacy Bankruptcy Administrator system). Each region is led by a United States Trustee with assistants who staff district field offices.

The USTP appoints and supervises the private trustees who administer Chapter 7 cases, oversees Chapter 11 debtors-in-possession, monitors Chapter 13 standing trustees, and approves Subchapter V trustees from a regional roster. The U.S. Trustee also has standing under section 307 to appear and be heard on any issue in any bankruptcy case other than to file a plan.

The private trustees in each chapter have substantively different roles. A Chapter 7 panel trustee liquidates non-exempt assets and pursues avoidance actions. A Chapter 13 standing trustee disburses plan payments and supervises plan performance. A Subchapter V trustee facilitates the development of a consensual plan and serves a hybrid oversight role that does not displace the debtor. A Chapter 11 trustee replaces management when appointed under section 1104, while a Chapter 11 examiner investigates conduct without taking operational control.

Common confusion: The U.S. Trustee (the federal official) is not the same as the case trustee. The U.S. Trustee supervises; the panel, standing, Subchapter V, or Chapter 11 trustee administers the individual case.
