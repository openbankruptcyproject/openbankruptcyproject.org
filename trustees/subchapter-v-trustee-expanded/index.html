A New Kind of Bankruptcy Trustee

The Small Business Reorganization Act of 2019 added Subchapter V to Chapter 11 of the Bankruptcy Code and created with it a new species of bankruptcy trustee, codified at 11 U.S.C. § 1183. The Subchapter V trustee is unlike anything else in the Bankruptcy Code. It is not a Chapter 7 trustee taking possession of assets to liquidate them. It is not a Chapter 13 standing trustee disbursing plan payments on a fixed percentage fee. It is not a Chapter 11 trustee displacing management under Section 1104(a). And it is not a Chapter 11 examiner producing a single investigative report. It borrows pieces from each of those roles and adds two functions that have no direct analogue: facilitation of a consensual plan and ongoing monitoring of a debtor that remains in possession.

This page sits alongside, and intentionally focuses on, the operational and comparative dimensions of the Subchapter V trustee role. For the underlying statutory text and a section-by-section walk through Section 1183 itself, see the Section 1183 guide.

Authority: 11 U.S.C. §§ 1181, 1183, 1184, 1185, 1187, 1191; 28 U.S.C. § 586(d).

The Facilitation Function

Section 1183(b)(7), as renumbered after technical amendments to (b)(5), directs the trustee to "facilitate the development of a consensual plan of reorganization." Facilitation is the function that most clearly distinguishes the Subchapter V trustee from every other bankruptcy fiduciary. Neither a Chapter 7 trustee, nor a Chapter 13 trustee, nor a Chapter 11 trustee, nor an examiner is given facilitation as a statutory duty.

In practice, facilitation looks like mediation between the debtor and creditor groups, structured discussion of plan terms, identification of cramdown risk, and assistance in negotiating treatment of secured and priority claims. Many Subchapter V trustees convene informal early calls with the debtor, then with secured creditors, then with significant unsecured creditors, to identify the plausible bargaining range before a plan is filed. The 90-day plan deadline imposed by Section 1189(b) makes early facilitation essential; the trustee who waits for a filed plan to begin facilitation is often too late.

Facilitation is non-decisional. The trustee does not have power to order the parties to settle, does not vote on the plan, and does not bind any party. The trustee's leverage is informational, procedural, and reputational: a trustee who reports to the court that the debtor or a creditor has refused to engage in good faith is a trustee whose report the court will weigh at confirmation under Section 1129(a)(3).

The Monitoring Function

The Subchapter V trustee monitors the debtor's operations without taking them over. Under 11 U.S.C. § 1184, the debtor remains in possession and exercises the rights and powers of a trustee subject to the same fiduciary duties. Section 1183(b)(1) imposes on the trustee a tailored subset of Chapter 7 duties: account for property, examine proofs of claim, oppose discharge if advisable, furnish information, and file required reports.

The monitoring function is principally informational. The trustee reviews operating reports filed by the debtor under Section 1187, scrutinizes cash collateral usage, attends the status conference under Section 1188, and stays current on the debtor's operations between status conferences. The trustee is positioned to flag, early, departures from disclosed financial projections, unauthorized post-petition expenditures, and signs of mismanagement that would justify removal under Section 1185.

Monitoring is not investigation. Section 1183(b)(2) limits investigation duties (Section 1106(a)(3) and (4)) to the extent the court orders. Absent such an order, the Subchapter V trustee is not in the same investigative posture as a Chapter 11 trustee or examiner. Courts can expand the trustee's mandate by order, but expansion is the exception, not the rule.

The Plan-Oversight Function

Plan oversight is the post-confirmation function. Under Section 1191, plans are confirmed either consensually under (a) or nonconsensually under (b). The trustee's post-confirmation role differs accordingly:

  • Consensual confirmation under Section 1191(a): The trustee's service terminates upon substantial consummation of the plan under Section 1183(c)(1). The debtor performs and pays creditors directly. The trustee is out.
  • Nonconsensual confirmation under Section 1191(b): The trustee remains in service to receive payments and disburse them to creditors under Section 1183(b)(3) (consistent with renumbering), unless the plan or confirmation order provides otherwise. The trustee monitors plan performance through the discharge under Section 1192.

This split creates a structural incentive for consensual confirmation that runs in the same direction as the debtor's incentive: both parties want to end the trustee's service early. Section 1183(b)(7)'s facilitation duty draws its sharpest force from this alignment.

Distinguishing the Role From Other Trustees

The Subchapter V trustee occupies a distinct position relative to other bankruptcy fiduciaries:

  • vs. Chapter 7 panel trustee: A Chapter 7 trustee takes possession of and liquidates the estate. A Subchapter V trustee never takes possession in the ordinary course; the debtor remains in possession under Section 1184. The Chapter 7 trustee's job is to maximize liquidation value for creditors; the Subchapter V trustee's job is to facilitate a reorganization that maximizes going-concern value.
  • vs. Chapter 13 standing trustee: A Chapter 13 trustee performs ongoing payment processing for individual wage-earner plans on a percentage fee under 28 U.S.C. § 586(e). A Subchapter V trustee performs payment processing only in nonconsensual cases and is compensated under Section 330 hourly review, not under a percentage fee.
  • vs. Chapter 11 trustee under Section 1104(a): A Chapter 11 trustee displaces management and assumes operating control. A Subchapter V trustee does not displace management; the trustee's leverage is procedural and informational, not custodial. Management can be displaced under Section 1185 for cause, but that is the exception, not the design.
  • vs. Chapter 11 examiner under Section 1104(c): An examiner conducts a discrete investigation and files a written report; the examiner's job is largely one-shot. The Subchapter V trustee has a continuous role across the life of the case, with facilitation, monitoring, and (in nonconsensual cases) plan-payment functions.

Compensation: Section 1183(a) and Section 330

Subchapter V trustee compensation is governed by Section 1183(a) and ultimately by Section 330. The trustee is entitled to reasonable compensation for actual, necessary services rendered, plus reimbursement of actual, necessary expenses. Compensation is generally hourly, supported by detailed time records that satisfy Federal Rule of Bankruptcy Procedure 2016. The Section 326(a) percentage cap that applies to Chapter 7 trustees does not apply to Subchapter V trustees, and the Chapter 13 standing-trustee fund mechanism (28 U.S.C. § 586(e)) does not apply either.

Trustee fees are administrative expenses under Section 503(b)(2) with first priority within the administrative-expense class under Section 507(a)(2). They are paid by the estate. In small cases, trustee fees can be a material part of the administrative-expense projection that determines feasibility; both the trustee and counsel for the debtor should be candid with the court at the status conference about projected fees and the corresponding effect on plan distributions.

Where the trustee makes plan payments under nonconsensual confirmation, the trustee is entitled to additional compensation for that disbursement function. Fee applications are reviewed for reasonableness, with the United States Trustee Program and creditor stakeholders both empowered to object under Sections 330 and 503.

Selection of the Trustee

Subchapter V trustees are appointed under 28 U.S.C. § 586(d), which directs the Attorney General to "establish a panel of private trustees" to serve in Subchapter V cases and provides that "the United States trustee for each judicial district may, subject to the approval of the Attorney General, increase the maximum number of members of the panel as the United States trustee considers to be necessary." Some districts maintain a standing panel from which appointments rotate; others appoint on an ad hoc basis. In either case, the appointee must be a disinterested person within the meaning of Section 101(14) and must accept the appointment in writing.

Qualifications for service vary across districts. Many Subchapter V trustees are practicing bankruptcy attorneys; others are CPAs, financial advisors, or non-attorney professionals with restructuring experience. A non-attorney Subchapter V trustee must retain counsel under Section 327 for legal services. Subchapter V trustees are not federal employees; they serve at the pleasure of the United States Trustee and may be removed administratively.

Practical Impact for Debtors and Creditors

For a small-business debtor, the Subchapter V trustee is the most influential figure in the case after the bankruptcy judge. A trustee who engages early, identifies the plausible deal, and pushes both sides toward it can shorten a case from a year to a few months and convert a contested cramdown into a consensual confirmation. Conversely, a passive trustee can leave the debtor and creditors to negotiate in the absence of structure, increasing the likelihood that the 90-day plan deadline is missed and that the case slips toward conversion or dismissal.

For creditors, the Subchapter V trustee is a channel for raising concerns about debtor performance without filing motions. A creditor who flags a missed operating report, a sudden change in cash collateral usage, or an unexplained insider transaction to the trustee can prompt corrective action without the cost of a contested hearing. Creditors should not assume the trustee will independently identify every problem; the trustee monitors but does not investigate absent a court order.
