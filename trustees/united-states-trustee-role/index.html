United States Trustee Role and Structure

Plain-English guide to the United States Trustee Program: statutory authority under 28 USC 581-589, the 21-region structure, supervision of private trustees, Section 307 standing, and relationship to bankruptcy courts and the Department of Justice.

What Is the United States Trustee Program?

The United States Trustee Program (USTP) is the component of the United States Department of Justice that supervises the administration of bankruptcy cases and the conduct of private trustees in 88 of the 94 federal judicial districts. The exception is the six districts in Alabama and North Carolina, which operate under the older Bankruptcy Administrator program supervised by the Administrative Office of the United States Courts rather than by the Department of Justice.

The USTP was established by the Bankruptcy Reform Act of 1978 as a pilot in select districts and was expanded to nationwide status (with the Alabama and North Carolina exceptions) by the Bankruptcy Judges, United States Trustees, and Family Farmer Bankruptcy Act of 1986. The statutory framework is codified at 28 U.S.C. §§ 581-589a.

Authority: 28 U.S.C. §§ 581-589a; 11 U.S.C. § 307; 28 C.F.R. Part 58.

Statutory Framework: 28 USC 581 through 589a

The chapter 39 framework lays out the structural and operational design of the program:

  • 28 U.S.C. § 581: Establishes 21 regions, each covering one or more federal judicial districts, and authorizes the Attorney General to appoint one United States Trustee for each region.
  • 28 U.S.C. § 582: Authorizes assistant United States trustees in each region.
  • 28 U.S.C. § 586: The core duties section. Section 586(a) imposes specific duties on each United States Trustee, including establishing and supervising the Chapter 7 panel under (a)(1) and the Chapter 13 standing trustees under (a)(2), monitoring the administration of cases under (a)(3), and reviewing fee applications under (a)(3)(A).
  • 28 U.S.C. § 587: Records and reporting requirements.
  • 28 U.S.C. § 589a: Establishes the United States Trustee System Fund, which receives bankruptcy filing fees and quarterly fees from Chapter 11 debtors under 28 U.S.C. § 1930(a)(6) and funds the USTP's operations.

The Executive Office for United States Trustees (EOUST), located in Washington, D.C., provides centralized policy, training, and administrative support for the regional offices. The Director of the EOUST is a Senate-confirmed Presidential appointee. The Attorney General supervises both the EOUST and the regional United States Trustees.

Regional Structure: 21 Regions

The country is divided into 21 USTP regions. Each region is headed by a United States Trustee, appointed by the Attorney General for a five-year term under 28 U.S.C. § 581(b), removable by the Attorney General at any time. Each region operates one or more field offices, typically in the major bankruptcy filing centers within the region. The regions are not contiguous with federal judicial circuits; a single circuit can span multiple regions, and a single region can span multiple circuits.

Each region's field offices are staffed by assistant United States trustees, bankruptcy analysts, attorneys, paralegals, and support personnel. The USTP nationally employs roughly 1,100 personnel. Region-level priorities are set in light of national EOUST guidance, but each United States Trustee has substantial discretion in the deployment of office resources within their region.

The current list of regions, their judicial-district coverage, and field-office addresses is maintained at justice.gov/ust/regions.

Oversight of Private Trustees

The USTP supervises three classes of private trustees: Chapter 7 panel trustees under 28 U.S.C. § 586(a)(1), Chapter 12 and 13 standing trustees under 28 U.S.C. § 586(b), and Subchapter V trustees under 28 U.S.C. § 586(d). For each class, the United States Trustee establishes qualifications, conducts background investigations, maintains the panel or appointment roster, and audits the trustees' performance.

Supervision includes:

  • Annual or periodic audits of the trustee's case files, fee applications, bond, and operating accounts, with formal reports of findings.
  • Review of fee applications in Chapter 7 (under Section 330) and approval of standing-trustee budgets (under 28 U.S.C. § 586(e)(2)).
  • Receipt and review of the final report and final account in each case, including the trustee distribution and the application of funds.
  • Administrative removal from the panel for cause under 28 C.F.R. § 58.6, subject to the trustee's right to administrative review.
  • Initiation of judicial removal proceedings under 11 U.S.C. § 324 for trustees whose conduct warrants court-ordered removal.

The USTP's supervisory role is administrative; it does not have judicial power. Sanctions against private trustees that affect their service in particular cases generally require court action on the USTP's motion or on the motion of a private party.

Section 307 Standing to Be Heard

11 U.S.C. § 307 grants the United States Trustee broad standing in bankruptcy cases: "The United States trustee may raise and may appear and be heard on any issue in any case or proceeding under this title but may not file a plan pursuant to section 1121(c) of this title."

Section 307 is the load-bearing standing statute for USTP appearances. It permits the United States Trustee to appear and to take positions without satisfying the case-by-case standing requirements that ordinarily apply to private parties. The United States Trustee regularly appears on motions for relief from stay, conversion or dismissal motions, fee applications, sale motions under Section 363, plan-confirmation contests, and trustee-appointment motions under Section 1104.

The Supreme Court in Office of the United States Trustee v. John Q. Hammons Fall 2006, LLC, 602 U.S. 487 (2024), discussed the program's institutional role and reaffirmed the importance of the USTP's appearance authority in the bankruptcy system. Siegel v. Fitzgerald, 596 U.S. 464 (2022), held that the disuniform increase in USTP quarterly fees applicable only to USTP-district debtors (and not to Bankruptcy Administrator-district debtors) violated the Bankruptcy Clause's uniformity requirement, prompting subsequent statutory and refund developments.

Appearance and Investigation Functions

The USTP appears and acts across the full life cycle of bankruptcy cases:

  • Filing review: Initial review of petitions, schedules, and statements of financial affairs for completeness and accuracy. Referral of suspected fraud or abuse to appropriate authorities, including USTP Civil Enforcement and the Federal Bureau of Investigation.
  • Section 341 meeting oversight: Although the meeting is conducted by the trustee, the United States Trustee monitors compliance with statutory requirements and may appear directly in significant cases.
  • Means-test enforcement: Review of Chapter 7 cases under Section 707(b) for presumed abuse, and motion practice where indicated.
  • Chapter 11 oversight: Active participation in confirmation, plan-modification, and post-confirmation proceedings. The duty under Section 1104(e), added in 2005, requires the United States Trustee to move for the appointment of a trustee where there are reasonable grounds to suspect specified misconduct by current management.
  • Civil enforcement: The USTP Civil Enforcement Initiative targets fraud, abuse, and unauthorized practice of law in bankruptcy. Outcomes include disgorgement, debarment of professionals, and referrals for criminal prosecution to the United States Attorneys.
  • Criminal referrals: The USTP refers cases of suspected bankruptcy crime under 18 U.S.C. Chapter 9 to the appropriate United States Attorney's office. Most bankruptcy prosecutions under 18 U.S.C. §§ 152, 153, 156, 157, and 1519 originate as USTP referrals.

Relationship to Bankruptcy Courts

The United States Trustee is not a court officer. The USTP sits in the executive branch as a component of the Department of Justice; bankruptcy courts sit in the judicial branch as units of the United States district courts. The two institutions interact constantly but are structurally independent.

Bankruptcy judges do not supervise the United States Trustee, and the United States Trustee does not supervise bankruptcy judges. The United States Trustee appears in court as a party in interest under Section 307. Court orders bind the United States Trustee like any other party. Conversely, the United States Trustee's administrative decisions about panel membership, fee-application objections, and case selection are not subject to ordinary judicial review except as they affect particular cases.

The relationship is collaborative in practice. Many bankruptcy courts and United States Trustees in the same district have long-standing operating protocols for procedural matters (Section 341 meeting scheduling, fee-application formatting, monthly operating reports), and a productive working relationship is a precondition for the efficient administration of a busy bankruptcy calendar.

Relationship to the Department of Justice

The USTP is one of approximately 40 components of the Department of Justice. The Attorney General supervises the program through the Deputy Attorney General. The Director of the EOUST reports up through that chain. Bankruptcy-related criminal prosecutions are handled by the United States Attorneys (separate DOJ components) and, in significant cases, by the Criminal Division at Main Justice.

The USTP's civil enforcement work is coordinated with the United States Attorneys and, where appropriate, the Civil Division. Coordination protocols are governed by internal DOJ guidance, including the United States Attorneys' Manual (now the Justice Manual) provisions on bankruptcy.

Practical Impact for Debtors, Creditors, and Counsel

For most consumer debtors, the United States Trustee will be an invisible institution. The case will be administered by a Chapter 7 or Chapter 13 trustee, and the USTP's role will be felt only in cases where means-test enforcement, fraud concerns, or attorney-fee issues bring the program to the forefront.

For Chapter 11 and Subchapter V debtors, the United States Trustee is a routine participant. The USTP appoints the trustee or examiner under Section 1104(d) where applicable, files quarterly-fee assessments under 28 U.S.C. § 1930(a)(6), reviews monthly operating reports, and takes positions on confirmation and significant motions. Counsel for Chapter 11 debtors should treat the USTP as a permanent counterparty and structure the case accordingly.

For creditors, the United States Trustee is a channel for raising systemic concerns about case administration, trustee performance, professional conduct, and debtor misconduct. The USTP does not act as advocate for any particular creditor, but it can and does take positions that align with creditor interests where the public interest in bankruptcy administration so requires.
