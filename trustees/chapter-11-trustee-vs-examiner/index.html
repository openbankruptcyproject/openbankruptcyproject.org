Chapter 11 Trustee vs Examiner

Plain-English comparison of the two non-debtor fiduciaries the bankruptcy court can install in a Chapter 11 case: a displacing trustee under Section 1104(a) or an investigative examiner under Section 1104(c).

The Default Rule: Debtor in Possession

Chapter 11 begins from a strong default that the debtor remains in possession of the estate. 11 U.S.C. § 1107 vests the debtor in possession with most of the rights, powers, and duties of a trustee. The displacement of the debtor by a trustee, or the appointment of an outside examiner, is an exception to that default and must be affirmatively justified under Section 1104.

Section 1104 sets out two distinct mechanisms for inserting a non-debtor fiduciary into a Chapter 11 case: appointment of a trustee under Section 1104(a) or appointment of an examiner under Section 1104(c). The two roles share an appointment process (Section 1104(d)) but differ sharply in scope and consequence.

Authority: 11 U.S.C. §§ 1104, 1106, 1107.

Appointment of a Chapter 11 Trustee: Section 1104(a)

Section 1104(a) provides two grounds for appointment of a Chapter 11 trustee:

  • 1104(a)(1) - For cause: "for cause, including fraud, dishonesty, incompetence, or gross mismanagement of the affairs of the debtor by current management, either before or after the commencement of the case, or similar cause, but not including the number of holders of securities of the debtor or the amount of assets or liabilities of the debtor."
  • 1104(a)(2) - Best-interests test: "if such appointment is in the interests of creditors, any equity security holders, and other interests of the estate, without regard to the number of holders of securities of the debtor or the amount of assets or liabilities of the debtor."

The Supreme Court has not articulated a uniform test for "cause" under Section 1104(a)(1), and circuit law varies. The moving party generally bears the burden of proof by clear and convincing evidence in most circuits. See In re Sharon Steel Corp., 871 F.2d 1217 (3d Cir. 1989) (collecting authorities). Cause is most commonly found where management has engaged in self-dealing, where post-petition fiduciary failures continue uncorrected, or where pre-petition fraud is implicated in the conduct that produced the bankruptcy.

Section 1104(a)(3), added in 2005, mandates appointment of a trustee in cases of health-care-business debtors when a patient-care ombudsman is in place and certain conditions are met, expanding the prior two-ground framework.

Duties of a Chapter 11 Trustee: Section 1106(a)

11 U.S.C. § 1106(a) imposes broad duties on a Chapter 11 trustee:

  • 1106(a)(1): Perform the duties of a Chapter 7 trustee specified in Sections 704(a)(2), (5), (7), (8), (9), (10), (11), and (12) (accountability, claim review, information furnishing, operating reports, final report, and the domestic-support, tax-return, and health-care notice duties).
  • 1106(a)(2): If the debtor has not done so, file the list, schedule, and statement required by Section 521(a)(1).
  • 1106(a)(3): Except to the extent that the court orders otherwise, investigate the acts, conduct, assets, liabilities, and financial condition of the debtor, the operation of the debtor's business and the desirability of the continuance of such business, and any other matter relevant to the case or to the formulation of a plan.
  • 1106(a)(4): As soon as practicable, file a statement of any investigation conducted under (a)(3), including any fact ascertained pertaining to fraud, dishonesty, incompetence, misconduct, mismanagement, or irregularity in the management of the affairs of the debtor, or to a cause of action available to the estate.
  • 1106(a)(5): As soon as practicable, file a plan, file a report explaining why the trustee will not file a plan, or recommend conversion of the case to Chapter 7, 12, or 13 or dismissal of the case.
  • 1106(a)(6): For any year for which the debtor has not filed a tax return, furnish to taxing authorities the information that may be required.
  • 1106(a)(7): After confirmation of a plan, file such reports as are necessary or as the court orders.

The most consequential of these duties for the existing management is Section 1106(a)(3) investigation. A Chapter 11 trustee is empowered, and indeed required, to investigate pre-petition and post-petition conduct of management and to file a public statement of findings.

Appointment of a Chapter 11 Examiner: Section 1104(c)

Where the case does not warrant the wholesale displacement of management but specific concerns require independent inquiry, the court can appoint an examiner instead. Section 1104(c) provides that, after notice and a hearing, the court shall order the appointment of an examiner to investigate the debtor's acts, conduct, assets, liabilities, and financial condition if:

  • 1104(c)(1): Such appointment is in the interests of creditors, any equity security holders, and other interests of the estate; or
  • 1104(c)(2): The debtor's fixed, liquidated, unsecured debts, other than debts for goods, services, or taxes, or owing to an insider, exceed $5,000,000.

The Section 1104(c)(2) trigger is significant: when unsecured debts exceed the $5 million threshold and a party moves for examiner appointment, the court's appointment authority is mandatory in many circuits, although there is a circuit split on whether the court retains discretion to refuse appointment notwithstanding the statutory shall. See In re Loral Space & Communications, Ltd., 313 B.R. 577 (Bankr. S.D.N.Y. 2004), and Morgenstern v. Revco D.S., Inc., 898 F.2d 498 (6th Cir. 1990) (mandatory reading).

Duties of a Chapter 11 Examiner: Section 1106(b)

Section 1106(b) provides that an examiner appointed under Section 1104(c) "shall perform the duties specified in paragraphs (3) and (4) of subsection (a) of this section, and, except to the extent that the court orders otherwise, any other duties of the trustee that the court orders the debtor in possession not to perform."

The default examiner mandate is therefore limited to Section 1106(a)(3) investigation and Section 1106(a)(4) reporting. The examiner does not displace the debtor in possession, does not file the plan, and does not assume operating control. The examiner's deliverable is a written report, filed publicly on the docket, that informs creditors, the United States Trustee, and the court about the matters investigated. The court can expand the examiner's mandate by separate order, but expansions that effectively make the examiner a shadow trustee are disfavored.

Appointment Process: Section 1104(d)

Section 1104(d) describes the appointment mechanic. After the court orders the appointment of a trustee or examiner, the United States Trustee consults with parties in interest, then appoints, subject to court approval, one disinterested person other than the United States Trustee to serve. The appointee must satisfy the disinterestedness standard of Section 101(14) and be acceptable to the court.

Section 1104(b) provides an alternative election mechanism for trustees. On request of a party in interest made not later than 30 days after the court orders the appointment of a trustee, the United States Trustee shall convene a meeting of creditors for the purpose of electing one disinterested person to serve as trustee in the case. Election is rare; appointment by the United States Trustee under Section 1104(d) is by far the more common path.

Compensation

Both Chapter 11 trustees and examiners are compensated under Section 330 for "reasonable compensation for actual, necessary services rendered" and reimbursement of actual, necessary expenses. The Section 326(a) percentage cap applies to a Chapter 11 trustee (calculated on moneys disbursed), but does not apply to examiners. Both fee streams are paid as administrative expenses under Section 503(b)(2) and have first priority within the administrative-expense priority of Section 507(a)(2).

Both the trustee and the examiner must retain counsel under Section 327 for legal services, with the corresponding disinterestedness and disclosure obligations described in that section.

Choosing Between Trustee and Examiner

Counsel and courts choose between a trustee and an examiner along three axes:

  • Severity of management failure. Fraud, persistent fiduciary breach, or active concealment ordinarily warrants a trustee. Concerns about specific transactions or accounting matters can be addressed through an examiner without disrupting day-to-day operations.
  • Operational continuity. A debtor with going-concern value that depends on continuity of management may be destroyed by displacement; the more value depends on existing relationships and institutional knowledge, the higher the cost of trustee appointment relative to examiner.
  • Speed. A trustee can act on findings immediately because the trustee controls the estate; an examiner produces a report that requires subsequent action by other parties (United States Trustee, creditors, the court). Where time-sensitive recovery is critical, a trustee is generally faster.

Section 1104(e), added in 2005, also imposes on the United States Trustee an affirmative duty to move for the appointment of a trustee where there are reasonable grounds to suspect that current members of the debtor's governing body, the chief executive officer, the chief financial officer, or members of management who selected them participated in actual fraud, dishonesty, or criminal conduct in the management of the debtor or the public reporting of its financial affairs. This statutory duty constrains the United States Trustee independent of any private motion.
