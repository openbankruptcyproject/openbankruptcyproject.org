KRPC 7.1 / MRPC 4-7.1: Communications Concerning a Lawyer's Services

The architecture of permitted communications: truthful content, non-misleading framing, and the conditions for informative messaging.

Verbatim text of KRPC 7.1 (Kansas)

A lawyer shall not make a false or misleading communication about the lawyer or the lawyer's services. A communication is false or misleading if it contains a material misrepresentation of fact or law, or omits a fact necessary to make the statement considered as a whole not materially misleading. Kan. Sup. Ct. R. 226, KRPC 7.1.

Missouri Rule 4-7.1

Missouri Rule 4-7.1 articulates the same standard with elaborating sub-parts addressing comparison claims, results, and specialty designations. Both rules track ABA Model Rule 7.1, the foundational rule of the 7-series and the only rule in the series that applies to all communications concerning lawyer services, regardless of medium or audience.

What 7.1 affirmatively permits

Rule 7.1 is the constitutional charter of lawyer communications. It begins from the premise that lawyers may communicate about their services and structures that communication around a single standard: the communication must be truthful and must not be misleading. Within that standard, the rule permits a wide range of informative content:

  • Identification of the lawyer and firm, including name, address, telephone, email, and website
  • Description of areas of practice, including the matters typically handled and the types of clients typically served
  • Bar admissions and academic credentials, including law school, graduation date, and jurisdictions of admission
  • Professional honors and memberships, including bar leadership roles and bona fide professional recognitions
  • Languages spoken by the lawyer or by staff who routinely interact with clients
  • Fee structure, including hourly rates, flat fees for defined matters, contingent-fee terms, and consultation policies
  • Office hours, locations, and accessibility information for clients with disabilities, language preferences, or scheduling constraints

Each of these categories is informative content the rule treats as presumptively permitted, subject to the single overarching standard of truthful, non-misleading framing.

Structure of the permitted communication standard

The 7.1 standard operates in two pieces:

Truthful as to facts and law

The communication must not contain a material misrepresentation of fact or law. A misrepresentation is "material" if it bears on the recipient's decision-making process about whether to engage the lawyer or how to evaluate the lawyer's services. Errors of detail that do not bear on a reasonable recipient's decision are not material. A statement that the firm was founded in 1985 when the actual founding year was 1984 is unlikely to be material; a statement that the firm has handled "thousands of cases" when the actual number is in the dozens is material because the order-of-magnitude misstatement bears directly on the recipient's evaluation of experience.

Non-misleading as to the whole

The communication must not omit a fact necessary to make the statement considered as a whole not materially misleading. The omission branch is structurally distinct from the affirmative-misrepresentation branch: a communication can be technically accurate sentence-by-sentence but misleading as a whole because of what it leaves out. The standard asks whether a reasonable recipient, considering the communication as it appears in the medium and context where it is delivered, would form a misleading impression.

The "considered as a whole" standard means context and emphasis matter. A footnote disclaimer set in small type at the bottom of an advertisement may not cure a misleading headline; the test is whether the reasonable recipient, reading the communication the way it is actually delivered, takes away an accurate impression.

Conditions for results-related communications

Statements about case results (verdicts, settlements, recoveries, win rates) are permitted under Rule 7.1 when the statement is truthful and accompanied by such context as is necessary to avoid creating an unjustified expectation. The Comment to Model Rule 7.1 identifies the kinds of context the rule contemplates:

  • That results depend on the facts of each case
  • That the result described was achieved in a matter with facts particular to that matter
  • That the result described is not a guarantee or prediction of results in other matters

A truthful statement of a past result, accompanied by clear and conspicuous context of the kind described, is a permitted communication. The same statement without context can be misleading if it leaves the reasonable recipient with an unjustified expectation about likely outcomes in the recipient's own matter.

Conditions for comparison communications

Statements comparing the lawyer's services with the services of other lawyers are permitted when the comparison can be factually substantiated. The standard does not bar comparison; it requires that comparison claims rest on objective, verifiable facts rather than on subjective superlatives.

  • Verifiable comparisons: A statement that the lawyer has tried more cases of a particular type in a particular court than any other lawyer in the past five years is permitted if the count can be substantiated through court records.
  • Substantiated rankings: A statement that the lawyer is listed in a bona fide independent ranking publication (one that uses transparent methodology and is not pay-to-play) is permitted if accurately characterized.
  • Subjective superlatives: Claims such as "the best lawyer in town" or "the most experienced bankruptcy practitioner in the state" without verifiable substantiation are not permitted because they are inherently unverifiable comparative assertions.

Conditions for testimonial and endorsement content

Client testimonials and third-party endorsements are permitted under Rule 7.1 when handled within the truthful, non-misleading framework. The structural conditions are:

  • The testimonial accurately represents the client's actual experience
  • The testimonial does not create an unjustified expectation as to results in other matters
  • Compensation to the speaker, if any, is disclosed
  • The testimonial does not contain content that would itself violate Rule 7.1 if stated by the lawyer (e.g., misleading comparison or results-based claims)

The Comment to Model Rule 7.1 specifically notes that the rule does not categorically prohibit testimonials but requires the same truthful, non-misleading standard applicable to all 7.1 communications. Many jurisdictions also require an explicit context statement accompanying testimonials about case results, paralleling the results-related context requirement.

Specialty and certification designations

Rule 7.1 (and parallel provisions in Rule 7.4 in some jurisdictions) governs statements about specialization. The framework is:

  • Practice-area descriptions are permitted: A lawyer may state that the lawyer's practice is "limited to" or "concentrated in" a particular field. This is not a specialty certification; it is a description of the lawyer's practice scope.
  • Certification by an accredited organization is permitted: A lawyer who is certified as a specialist by an organization accredited by the state bar (or by the ABA in some jurisdictions) may state the certification, identifying the certifying organization.
  • "Specialist" as a bare claim is generally not permitted: A lawyer who is not certified may not call themselves a "specialist" in a field without context, because the term carries an implication of formal credentialing not supported by the bare claim.

Two pattern situations

Practice-area webpage

A firm webpage describing the firm's consumer bankruptcy practice is a paradigmatic 7.1 communication. The page may identify the lawyers, list bar admissions and academic credentials, describe the types of bankruptcy matters handled, identify the courts in which the firm appears, describe the firm's typical fee structure, and provide contact information. Each item is informative content within the rule. The page must not omit material facts (for example, if the firm only handles Chapter 7 and not Chapter 13, the description should not imply broader scope). Past results, if included, require the contextual statements described above.

Directory profile

A lawyer profile in an online directory (state bar lawyer-finder, commercial legal directory, etc.) is a 7.1 communication and is governed by the same standard. The profile may state areas of practice, year admitted, languages, fee policy, and similar information. If the directory's structure invites the lawyer to claim a rating, badge, or "top" designation, the lawyer should ensure that the underlying claim is verifiable and that any context statements required by the directory's terms are accurately presented.

Continuing-accuracy obligation

Communications under 7.1 carry a continuing-accuracy expectation. A statement that was accurate when made may become inaccurate over time as the lawyer's practice changes, bar memberships lapse, certifications expire, or fee policies change. A lawyer who maintains a website, directory profile, or other persistent communication is expected to keep the content current; the persistence of a once-true but now-inaccurate statement can itself become misleading under the rule's "considered as a whole" standard.

Practical impact

Rule 7.1 is the umbrella rule under which all lawyer communications operate. Two recurring observations:

  • The standard is broad and the latitude is wide. Within the truthful, non-misleading framework, lawyers may communicate a great deal of informative content about their services, qualifications, fee structures, and availability. The rule does not bar informative communication; it conditions it.
  • Omissions matter as much as affirmative misstatements. The "considered as a whole" standard reaches communications that are technically accurate but leave a misleading impression. A lawyer designing a communication should consider not only whether each sentence is true but whether the overall takeaway corresponds to reality.
