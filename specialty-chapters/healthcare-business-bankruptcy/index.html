Healthcare Business Bankruptcy

When a hospital, nursing home, hospice, or other "health care business" files bankruptcy, three sections do most of the special work: the Section 333 patient-care ombudsman, the Section 704(a)(12) duty to transfer patients, and the Section 351 protocol for disposal of patient records.

What Makes a Health Care Business Different

A bankruptcy proceeding involving a hospital, nursing home, hospice, ambulatory surgical center, or other healthcare provider is not procedurally different from any other Chapter 7 or Chapter 11 in its broad strokes - the automatic stay, the property of the estate, the priority and claims rules, the plan-confirmation framework all apply identically. What is different is the overlay of patient-protection provisions that Congress added in the 2005 amendments. Those provisions exist because the property of the estate in a healthcare-business case includes operating clinical capacity (admitted patients, ongoing treatment plans, custody of vulnerable individuals) and informational assets (medical records subject to federal and state privacy law) whose mishandling can cause clinical harm or violate other federal statutes.

Three statutory tools carry most of the load: (1) Section 333, which authorizes appointment of a patient-care ombudsman; (2) Section 704(a)(12), which imposes a transfer duty on a trustee or debtor in possession when patients can no longer be cared for in the existing facility; and (3) Section 351, which prescribes how patient records must be handled if the estate's assets are insufficient to pay for indefinite storage.

Official citations: 11 U.S.C. Section 333; 11 U.S.C. Section 351; 11 U.S.C. 704(a)(12); definition at 11 U.S.C. 101(27A).

Who Is a "Health Care Business"? Section 101(27A)

Section 101(27A) defines "health care business" as a public or private entity that is primarily engaged in offering to the general public facilities and services for the diagnosis or treatment of injury, deformity, or disease, or for surgical, drug-treatment, psychiatric, or obstetric care, and includes (among other things) any general or specialized hospital, ancillary ambulatory, emergency, or surgical treatment facility, hospice, home health agency, and other health care institution that is similar to those entities. The definition also expressly includes long-term-care facilities, including skilled nursing facilities, intermediate-care facilities, and assisted-living facilities.

The definition is functional rather than formal. A debtor's status as a health care business does not turn on its tax classification, ownership structure, or whether it accepts Medicare. It turns on whether the debtor is primarily engaged in offering diagnostic or treatment services to the public. A medical-equipment manufacturer is not a health care business under Section 101(27A); a pharmacy that simply fills prescriptions is generally not; a physical-therapy clinic open to the public generally is.

The Patient-Care Ombudsman: Section 333

Section 333(a)(1) provides that if the debtor is a health care business, the court "shall order, not later than 30 days after the commencement of the case, the appointment of an ombudsman to monitor the quality of patient care and to represent the interests of the patients of the health care business unless the court finds that the appointment of such ombudsman is not necessary for the protection of patients under the specific facts of the case." Appointment is the default; non-appointment is the exception that must be affirmatively found.

The ombudsman is appointed by the United States Trustee (subject to court approval) and is typically a person experienced in long-term-care ombudsman work, healthcare administration, or healthcare law. The ombudsman is not a trustee and does not displace management. The ombudsman's role is to monitor the quality of patient care, interview patients and staff, review records, and report findings to the court no less than every 60 days. If the ombudsman determines that the quality of patient care is declining significantly or is otherwise being materially compromised, the ombudsman must file a motion or written report with the court immediately.

Section 333(c) protects the confidentiality of patient information accessed by the ombudsman; the ombudsman may not review confidential patient records without court approval and authorization consistent with applicable non-bankruptcy privacy law (including HIPAA where applicable). The compensation and expense reimbursement of the ombudsman are administrative-priority expenses of the estate under Section 503(b).

Patient Transfers: Section 704(a)(12)

Section 704(a)(12), made applicable in Chapter 11 by Section 1106(a)(1), imposes a transfer duty on the trustee (or debtor in possession) "if the debtor is a health care business, [to] use all reasonable and best efforts to transfer patients from a health care business that is in the process of being closed to an appropriate health care business that - (A) is in the vicinity of the health care business that is closing; (B) provides the patient with services that are substantially similar to those provided by the health care business that is in the process of being closed; and (C) maintains a reasonable quality of care."

The duty is triggered by closure of the health care business or any unit within it that admits patients. The transferee facility must be in the vicinity, must provide substantially similar services, and must maintain a reasonable quality of care. "Vicinity" is judged practically - a 200-mile transfer of an elderly nursing-home resident is generally not a transfer to a vicinity facility; a transfer across town generally is. Substantially similar services means the receiving facility must be capable of providing the same level of clinical care, not merely a different healthcare service of any kind.

Disposal of Patient Records: Section 351

Section 351 governs what happens to patient records when a healthcare-business case ends and the estate cannot afford indefinite storage. The procedure is sequenced.

First, the trustee (or debtor in possession) must publish notice in one or more appropriate newspapers that the records will be destroyed unless claimed within 365 days. The notice must give patients and insurance providers the opportunity to claim their records.

Second, during the 365-day notice period, the trustee must attempt to provide individual notice by certified mail to each patient (and to the patient's most recent insurance carrier) at the last known address.

Third, if the records are not claimed within the 365-day period, the trustee may dispose of them. The disposal protocol depends on the format. Electronic records must be created in a manner that prevents disclosure of confidential patient information (deletion with overwriting, destruction of storage media, or equivalent). Paper records must be destroyed by burning, shredding, or other physical-destruction means designed to prevent reconstruction.

The statute is designed to thread a narrow needle: estates of failed healthcare businesses often have no funds to pay for permanent records-retention contracts, but federal and state law require that medical records not be casually disposed of. Section 351 supplies a federally compliant safe-harbor procedure that satisfies the privacy and records-retention requirements while liberating the estate from open-ended storage obligations.

When the Ombudsman Is Not Necessary

Section 333(a)(1) creates a default of appointment but allows the court to find that "the appointment of such ombudsman is not necessary for the protection of patients under the specific facts of the case." The factor list that has emerged from the case law is generally non-exclusive but commonly includes: the cause of the bankruptcy filing (financial vs. clinical); whether the case is a reorganization or a liquidation; the number of patients served; whether the facility is subject to ongoing oversight by state or federal regulators (a CMS-certified facility, for example, already has the federal survey-and-certification apparatus watching it); whether the debtor has its own internal quality-of-care and compliance program; whether there is any history of patient-care complaints; and whether the debtor is small enough that the cost of an ombudsman would materially erode the estate.

The decision is typically litigated at the outset of the case on the debtor's motion to forgo ombudsman appointment. The U.S. Trustee usually takes a position; if the court denies the motion the ombudsman is appointed within the 30-day default window. Most operating-hospital cases see an ombudsman appointed; small non-clinical providers (for example, a billing service that meets a narrow reading of the definition) more often obtain the no-ombudsman finding.

Operating Issues Specific to Healthcare Bankruptcies

  • Medicare and Medicaid receivables and claims: Provider-agreement obligations, recoupment rights, and program-integrity audits all sit alongside the bankruptcy case. The federal government's setoff and recoupment rights against pending Medicare receivables can materially affect available estate liquidity.
  • State licensure: A state hospital or long-term-care license is generally not transferable as a matter of property of the estate. A sale of substantially all assets typically requires the buyer to obtain its own state license, which can introduce timing risk into a Section 363 sale.
  • Critical-vendor and emergency-services issues: Pharmaceuticals, blood products, dialysis supplies, and the like cannot be interrupted; first-day motions in healthcare cases typically include broader critical-vendor relief than in non-healthcare Chapter 11s.
  • EMTALA and similar federal obligations: The federal Emergency Medical Treatment and Active Labor Act and related obligations continue to apply through the bankruptcy; they are not stayed.
