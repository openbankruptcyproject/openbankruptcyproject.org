Chapter 12 Family Farmer and Fisherman

A purpose-built reorganization chapter for family farms and family fishing operations: who qualifies under Sections 101(18) and 101(19A), how the debt limits work, and how the Section 1225 secured-claim cramdown can rewrite a farm-loan balance to the value of the collateral.

Why Chapter 12 Exists

Chapter 12 was enacted in response to the 1980s farm crisis, when collapsing land values, high interest rates, and weak commodity prices put tens of thousands of American family farms into default. Existing Chapter 11 was too expensive and procedurally heavy for most operating farms, while Chapter 13's debt limits were too low to capture realistic farm balance sheets. Congress created Chapter 12 in 1986 as a temporary measure, repeatedly extended it, and made it permanent in 2005. In 2005 it was also expanded to cover "family fishermen," recognizing that commercial fishing operations faced structurally similar problems: heavy secured debt against specialized depreciating equipment, seasonal cash flow, and limited collateral substitutes.

The structural innovations of Chapter 12 - aggressive secured-debt cramdown, generous plan length, treatment of capital-gains taxes on farm-asset sales as unsecured claims, and a streamlined trustee - all reflect the same underlying judgment: that working farms and fishing operations whose only realistic chance to repay creditors is to keep operating need a procedural environment that lets them keep operating.

Official citation: 11 U.S.C. Chapter 12 (Sections 1201-1232); eligibility definitions at 11 U.S.C. Section 101(18) and 101(19A).

Who Is a "Family Farmer"? Section 101(18)

Section 101(18) defines "family farmer" with two parallel sub-definitions: one for individuals (and spouses) and one for corporations and partnerships. Both versions require (in their current statutory form) that the debtor be engaged in a farming operation, that the debtor's aggregate debts not exceed the statutory cap, that at least 50 percent of the debt (excluding the debt on the principal residence, unless that debt arises from the farming operation) arises out of the farming operation, and that more than 50 percent of the debtor's gross income for the applicable look-back period came from the farming operation.

The aggregate-debt limit is set in the Code at a dollar figure that has been raised multiple times by amendment and is further adjusted for inflation under Section 104. Practitioners should always confirm the current cap against the most recent Section 104 adjustment, because the figure has changed materially over the past decade. Individuals must also be U.S. residents and must satisfy the gross-income and percentage tests on a personal basis; corporate and partnership filers must satisfy parallel structural tests, including that more than 50 percent of the equity be held by one family (and relatives of that family) conducting the farming operation, and that more than 80 percent of the value of corporate or partnership assets be related to the farming operation.

Who Is a "Family Fisherman"? Section 101(19A)

Section 101(19A) defines "family fisherman" using the same architectural template as Section 101(18) but tuned to commercial fishing operations. The definition was added in 2005 to extend Chapter 12 to commercial fishing families that had been ineligible under the original 1986 statute despite facing structurally identical reorganization problems. The aggregate-debt cap for family fishermen has historically been lower than the family-farmer cap; both are subject to Section 104 inflation adjustments. The 50-percent-of-debt-from-fishing-operation test and the 50-percent-of-gross-income-from-fishing-operation test mirror the family-farmer structure.

What "Farming Operation" Covers

Section 101(21) defines "farming operation" inclusively: "farming, tillage of the soil, dairy farming, ranching, production or raising of crops, poultry, or livestock, and production of poultry or livestock products in an unmanufactured state." Courts have repeatedly grappled with edge cases - vertically integrated operations, custom-feeding contracts, hobby farms, large industrial agribusiness operations, and similar - and the case law tends to focus on whether the debtor bears the risks inherent in farming (weather, market, biological cycle) as opposed to operating in an essentially industrial or service capacity. In re Hayes and similar cases illustrate how courts apply the test functionally rather than by reference to formal labels.

The Section 1225 Cramdown - The Signature Tool

The most consequential single provision in Chapter 12 is the secured-claim cramdown under Section 1225(a)(5). It allows the debtor to keep secured collateral over the secured creditor's objection by paying the secured creditor, through the plan, the present value of the allowed secured claim. The "allowed secured claim" is determined by valuing the collateral under Section 506(a). If the collateral is worth less than the contract balance - the typical scenario when land or equipment values have fallen - the contract is bifurcated: the secured portion equals collateral value; the deficiency is an unsecured claim treated alongside other unsecured claims under the plan.

The Section 1225(a)(5)(B) "cramdown" path requires that the secured creditor retain its lien and that the plan provide for payments of a value (as of the effective date of the plan) of not less than the allowed amount of the secured claim. Through this mechanism a $1,200,000 farm-loan balance on land worth $800,000 can be effectively rewritten to an $800,000 secured obligation plus a $400,000 unsecured claim. The unsecured deficiency is paid (if at all) only out of disposable income over the plan term.

The applicable interest rate on the rewritten secured claim is determined under Till v. SCS Credit Corp., 541 U.S. 465 (2004), which adopted a formula-rate approach (the prime rate plus a risk premium). The cramdown plus the Till rate together make Chapter 12 a powerful tool for over-leveraged operations that have a credible operating future.

Plan Length and Disposable Income

A Chapter 12 plan is generally a three-year plan but may extend to five years "for cause." The "disposable income" commitment under Section 1225(b) requires that all of the debtor's projected disposable income to be received during the plan period, after deducting amounts reasonably necessary for the maintenance or support of the debtor or a dependent and amounts reasonably necessary for the continuation, preservation, and operation of the debtor's farming or fishing operation, be applied to make payments to unsecured creditors. As with Subchapter V, the business-expense deduction is critical: the disposable-income computation runs after legitimate operating expenses, not before.

Treatment of Capital-Gains Tax on Farm Asset Sales

One of the more specialized features of Chapter 12 is Section 1232 (added in 2005, amended in 2017), which treats federal, state, and local capital-gains-tax claims arising from the sale of farm assets used in the debtor's farming operation as unsecured claims that are not entitled to priority under Section 507(a)(8) and are dischargeable. The Supreme Court resolved a circuit split on a related question in Hall v. United States, 566 U.S. 506 (2012), and Congress responded by enacting the Family Farmer Bankruptcy Clarification Act of 2017. The practical effect is that a Chapter 12 debtor can sell appreciated farm assets during the case (for example, to right-size the operation or to satisfy a secured creditor) without the resulting capital-gains tax becoming a non-dischargeable priority obligation.

The Chapter 12 Trustee

The Chapter 12 standing trustee has a narrower role than a Chapter 11 trustee. The debtor remains in possession and continues to operate the farm or fishing business. The trustee distributes plan payments, monitors compliance, and may object to claims and plans, but does not displace management. This light-touch structure mirrors Chapter 13 and reflects the same underlying judgment that an operating business reorganizing under tight cash-flow conditions needs continuity of operations.

Discharge in Chapter 12

Section 1228 governs the Chapter 12 discharge. The default discharge under Section 1228(a) is granted after the debtor completes all payments under the plan. The "hardship discharge" under Section 1228(b) is available where the debtor's failure to complete plan payments is due to circumstances for which the debtor should not justly be held accountable, modification of the plan under Section 1229 is not practicable, and unsecured creditors have already received at least as much as they would have received in a hypothetical Chapter 7. As with Subchapter V cramdown discharges, the timing of discharge in Chapter 12 differs materially from immediate-discharge regimes - the debtor must complete the plan term to receive the full discharge benefit.

The scope of the Chapter 12 discharge is broad. It excepts the same categories of debt that are excepted from a Chapter 13 discharge under Section 523(a), but the Chapter 12 discharge is otherwise general. Long-term secured debts paid through a plan but maturing after the plan term remain enforceable on their contract terms; the discharge does not extinguish the underlying lien or accelerate the maturity.

Chapter 12 Compared to Chapter 11 and Chapter 13

  • Versus Chapter 11: Chapter 12 is materially cheaper, faster, and procedurally simpler. There is no creditors' committee, no disclosure-statement requirement, and no absolute-priority rule. Chapter 11 (including Subchapter V) is available to family farmers and fishermen as an alternative when the debt cap is exceeded or when other structural reasons argue against Chapter 12.
  • Versus Chapter 13: Chapter 12's debt caps are much higher than Chapter 13's, and Chapter 12's cramdown of secured claims on principal residence is unconstrained by the Section 1322(b)(2) anti-modification rule that protects home mortgages in Chapter 13. Chapter 13 is generally available only to wage-earning individuals; Chapter 12 is purpose-built for farming and fishing operations whose income is seasonal and tied to commodity cycles.
