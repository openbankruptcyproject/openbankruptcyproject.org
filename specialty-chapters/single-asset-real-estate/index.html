Single Asset Real Estate (SARE)

When a Chapter 11 debtor's only meaningful asset is a single piece of income-producing real property, Section 101(51B) reclassifies the case as SARE and the 90-day adequate-protection deadline of Section 362(d)(3) starts running on day one.

The SARE Designation Matters

Single Asset Real Estate is not a separate chapter of the Bankruptcy Code. SARE is a designation applied to a Chapter 11 case whose debtor satisfies the Section 101(51B) definition. Once the designation attaches, a special creditor-protective deadline kicks in: under Section 362(d)(3), a creditor with a security interest in the SARE real property can obtain stay relief unless, by the later of 90 days after the order for relief or 30 days after the court determines the SARE status, the debtor either (1) files a plan of reorganization that has a reasonable possibility of being confirmed within a reasonable time or (2) begins making monthly payments to the secured creditor at the contract or non-default rate on the value of the creditor's interest.

The original 1994 enactment of SARE applied only to small projects (those with secured debt of $4 million or less). The 2005 amendments removed the debt cap, expanding SARE coverage to large commercial real estate projects. Today the typical SARE case is an office building, a strip-center landlord, a hotel-property single-asset SPE, a multifamily-residential SPE, or a development-land holder. Excluded by statutory definition is real estate that is also being used in an active business by the debtor that goes beyond the operation of the real property itself - most prominently, hotels (which the case law generally treats as not SARE because the hotel operation is itself a substantial business beyond mere real estate operation) and family-farm operations (which are statutorily excluded).

Official citations: 11 U.S.C. Section 101(51B); 11 U.S.C. Section 362(d)(3).

The Section 101(51B) Definition

Section 101(51B) defines "single asset real estate" as "real property constituting a single property or project, other than residential real property with fewer than 4 residential units, which generates substantially all of the gross income of a debtor who is not a family farmer and on which no substantial business is being conducted by a debtor other than the business of operating the real property and activities incidental thereto."

The definition has four operative elements: (1) a single property or project, (2) excluding small (under four-unit) residential real property, (3) generating substantially all of the debtor's gross income, and (4) no substantial business is conducted on the property other than operating the real property itself and incidental activities. The fourth element is the most heavily litigated. The hotel-versus-not question turns on it; so does the question whether an assisted-living facility, a self-storage facility, or a parking-garage operation is SARE. The case law is fact-specific. A debtor that is simply collecting rent from tenants is generally SARE; a debtor whose income depends on operating a substantial service business that happens to be conducted on real property it owns is generally not SARE.

"Project" is broader than "property" - the definition permits a "single project" to include multiple parcels that function as an integrated whole (for example, a master-planned development with several adjacent parcels under common ownership and a single development plan). Conversely, a debtor that owns multiple unrelated properties is generally not SARE even if each property would qualify on its own.

The Section 362(d)(3) Deadline

Section 362(d)(3) is the operative consequence of SARE classification. On a creditor's motion, the court "shall grant relief from the stay" for cause to a creditor whose claim is secured by an interest in SARE real property unless, not later than the date that is the later of (A) 90 days after the entry of the order for relief or (B) 30 days after the court determines that the debtor is subject to Section 362(d)(3), one of the following has occurred:

  1. Plan path: The debtor has filed a plan of reorganization that has a reasonable possibility of being confirmed within a reasonable time, or
  2. Payment path: The debtor has commenced monthly payments that may, in the debtor's sole discretion, be made from rents or other income generated before, on, or after the date of the commencement of the case by or from the property to each creditor whose claim is secured by the real estate (other than a claim secured by a judgment lien or by an unmatured statutory lien), in an amount equal to interest at the then applicable non-default contract rate of interest on the value of the creditor's interest in the real estate.

The 90-day clock is short by the standards of complex Chapter 11. A SARE debtor has roughly three months to either file a plan or begin paying contract-rate interest on the secured value of the property. Failure to satisfy one of the two paths is independent grounds for stay relief.

What the Two Paths Look Like in Practice

The plan path requires more than filing any plan. The plan must have "a reasonable possibility of being confirmed within a reasonable time." Courts have read this requirement strictly. A plan that depends on speculative future financing, a contingent equity infusion, or refinancing in a market that does not exist is generally not a plan with a reasonable possibility of confirmation. The standard is meaningfully tighter than the general feasibility standard at confirmation; the SARE plan must look plausibly confirmable from the moment it is filed.

The payment path requires the debtor to begin paying monthly contract-rate interest on the value of the secured creditor's interest. The value of the secured creditor's interest is determined under Section 506(a) by reference to the value of the collateral - so if the property has fallen in value, the debtor's interest-payment obligation is correspondingly reduced. The interest rate is the non-default contract rate. The payments can come from any source, including rents subject to a cash-collateral order. This is sometimes the more attractive path because it preserves all of the debtor's confirmation optionality.

The Pre-1994 Two-Party Dispute Problem

Before the SARE provisions were enacted in 1994, single-asset real-estate Chapter 11 filings were a perennial complaint of secured creditors. The case typically involved one piece of real estate, one principal secured lender, and a debtor-borrower whose only realistic business was holding that property. There were essentially no unsecured creditors of consequence and no operating business to reorganize. Chapter 11 was used (the creditor complaint went) primarily as a delay device to extract better terms from the lender in what was substantively a two-party dispute. Congress's SARE provisions, refined in 2005, were designed to compress the time available for that kind of use without categorically prohibiting the filing. The 90-day deadline and the two-path safe harbor are the compromise structure.

Strategic Considerations for SARE Debtors

  • SARE designation is not optional: A debtor cannot opt out of SARE classification by declining to identify the case as SARE on its petition. A secured creditor can move for a determination of SARE status, and if the debtor fits Section 101(51B) the designation attaches and the deadline starts running.
  • Valuation is the central battleground: Both paths under Section 362(d)(3) turn on the value of the property. A low valuation makes the interest payment small and reduces the secured creditor's leverage on stay relief; a high valuation does the opposite. SARE debtors and their creditors often litigate valuation early in the case.
  • The plan need not propose a sale: A reorganization-only plan that contemplates a refinance, a new equity infusion, or a workout of the mortgage is permissible if it meets the "reasonable possibility" standard. Many SARE plans propose a sale of the property within the plan because a sale is often the cleanest realistic exit.
  • Subchapter V interaction: A small commercial real estate debtor that satisfies the Subchapter V debt threshold may be both SARE and a Subchapter V debtor. The Section 362(d)(3) deadline runs in either case; Subchapter V's expedited 90-day plan-filing requirement under Section 1189 maps closely to the SARE timeline.

SARE and the Absolute Priority Rule

Outside of Subchapter V, a SARE Chapter 11 confirmation still has to navigate the absolute-priority rule of Section 1129(b)(2)(B)(ii). Because the typical SARE debtor has a single secured creditor, one or two impaired classes, and limited unsecured creditor body, the practical question is whether the equity holders can retain their interests over the dissent of a secured creditor whose claim exceeds the property's value. The "new value corollary" - the doctrine that equity may retain its interest if it contributes "new value" that is substantial, necessary, in money or money's worth, and proportionate to the retained interest - is frequently litigated in SARE cases. The Supreme Court left the new-value corollary's vitality unresolved in Bank of America v. 203 North LaSalle Street Partnership, 526 U.S. 434 (1999), but held that any retention of equity in exchange for new value must be subject to a market test (typically through competitive bidding or some equivalent mechanism). For SARE debtors with equity-holder restructuring proposals, the LaSalle market-test requirement is a regular procedural hurdle.
