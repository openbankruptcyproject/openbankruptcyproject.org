What Is Chapter 9?

Chapter 9 of the Bankruptcy Code provides a specialized reorganization path for an insolvent "municipality." It is one of the smallest chapters by filing volume but among the most legally consequential: every Chapter 9 case touches deep questions about federalism, public services, public pensions, and the limits of federal judicial power over a state's political subdivisions. There were only a few hundred Chapter 9 filings in the entire modern era of the Bankruptcy Code, and many of those were small special-purpose districts rather than cities. The headline cases that the public remembers - Detroit (2013), Stockton (2012), San Bernardino (2012), Jefferson County, Alabama (2011), Vallejo (2008), and Orange County (1994) - are statistical outliers.

The defining structural feature is that Chapter 9 is a reorganization-only chapter. There is no liquidation analogue. A municipality cannot be sold, dissolved by a federal court, or have its assets distributed to creditors the way a Chapter 7 debtor's property is liquidated by a trustee. This is not a drafting oversight; it is constitutional architecture. The federal courts cannot dismantle a state's political subdivision because the Tenth Amendment reserves to the states the power to define and organize their own governments.

Official citation: 11 U.S.C. Chapter 9 (Sections 901-946); eligibility at 11 U.S.C. Section 109(c).

Who Is a "Municipality"? Section 101(40)

Section 101(40) defines "municipality" as a "political subdivision or public agency or instrumentality of a State." That phrase reaches farther than the everyday meaning of "city." Counties, towns, villages, school districts, sewer authorities, water districts, hospital districts, transit authorities, and similar bodies have all qualified at one time or another. What it excludes is also important. A state itself is not a municipality and cannot file Chapter 9. Federal entities cannot file. A private nonprofit hospital that happens to receive public funding is not a municipality. The State of Puerto Rico's debt restructuring proceeded under a different federal statute (PROMESA, 48 U.S.C. Chapter 20) rather than Chapter 9 precisely because the Commonwealth was not, on the better reading of the statute, a "State" for Chapter 9 purposes and so could not authorize its own Chapter 9 filing.

The Five-Part Section 109(c) Eligibility Test

Section 109(c) is the gatekeeper. A would-be Chapter 9 debtor must satisfy all five of the following:

  1. Section 109(c)(1) - Municipality: The debtor must be a municipality as defined by Section 101(40).
  2. Section 109(c)(2) - State authorization: The debtor must be "specifically authorized, in its capacity as a municipality or by name, to be a debtor under such chapter by State law, or by a governmental officer or organization empowered by State law to authorize such entity to be a debtor under such chapter." This is the single most contested element in modern Chapter 9 litigation.
  3. Section 109(c)(3) - Insolvent: The debtor must be "insolvent" as defined in Section 101(32)(C), meaning generally unable to pay debts as they become due or unable to pay debts as they become due within the meaning of cash-flow insolvency.
  4. Section 109(c)(4) - Desire to effect a plan: The debtor must desire to effect a plan to adjust its debts.
  5. Section 109(c)(5) - Pre-petition negotiation or impracticability: The debtor must have either (A) obtained creditor agreement, (B) negotiated in good faith and failed, (C) been unable to negotiate because negotiation was impracticable, or (D) reasonably believed a creditor was about to obtain an avoidable preference.

Each prong is an independent hurdle. A petition that satisfies four out of five fails. Eligibility is typically litigated at the outset, and the burden is on the petitioning municipality to prove each element by a preponderance of the evidence.

The State-Authorization Requirement

The Section 109(c)(2) state-authorization requirement is what most clearly distinguishes Chapter 9 from every other chapter. The default rule across the United States is that a municipality is not authorized to file Chapter 9 unless its home state has affirmatively said so. States fall into roughly four categories: (1) states that broadly authorize municipal filings without precondition; (2) states that authorize filings subject to a state oversight process or gubernatorial approval; (3) states that have specific case-by-case authorization mechanisms; and (4) states that prohibit municipal bankruptcy outright. As a result, the same financially distressed city would have very different options depending on which state line it sits on.

When the City of Detroit filed in 2013, the constitutionality and procedure of Michigan's emergency-manager statute and the governor's authorization were among the central litigated issues. When the City of Stockton, California filed in 2012, California's pre-bankruptcy mediation process under AB 506 was the threshold question. When Bridgeport, Connecticut tried to file in the early 1990s, the absence of state authorization defeated the petition. The state-authorization step is therefore not a formality but the substantive choke point of Chapter 9 access.

No Liquidation, No Sale, No Dissolution

A Chapter 9 case can have only one possible terminus: a confirmed plan of adjustment, dismissal, or conversion (and conversion is not available for Chapter 9). The case cannot end with the court liquidating the municipality's assets. Federal courts cannot abolish, dissolve, or restructure the municipality's governance. They cannot order the sale of public assets without the municipality's consent. They cannot appoint a receiver to run the city. They cannot order tax increases or budget changes.

This is in part a constitutional limit and in part written into the Code. Section 904 ("Limitation on jurisdiction and powers of court") expressly prohibits the bankruptcy court from interfering, by any stay, order, or decree, with: (1) any of the political or governmental powers of the debtor; (2) any of the property or revenues of the debtor; or (3) the debtor's use or enjoyment of any income-producing property, unless the debtor consents. The municipality holds far more practical leverage than a corporate Chapter 11 debtor because the court's powers over it are dramatically narrower.

The Tenth Amendment Backdrop

The constitutional question that runs underneath Chapter 9 is whether the federal bankruptcy power, granted in Article I, Section 8, Clause 4, can reach a state's own political subdivisions at all. The Supreme Court answered yes, but with substantial limits, in United States v. Bekins, 304 U.S. 27 (1938), which upheld a redesigned municipal bankruptcy statute after an earlier version had been struck down in Ashton v. Cameron County Water Improvement District No. One, 298 U.S. 513 (1936). The compromise the Court accepted - and that survives in the modern statute - is that the federal government can give a willing municipality a forum to restructure its debts, but it cannot impose the regime over the objection of the state or the municipality, and the federal court cannot displace the municipality's governmental functions.

Every modern Chapter 9 case operates within that compromise. The state-authorization requirement, the Section 904 limitations on court power, the absence of a trustee with management authority, and the inability to liquidate are all expressions of the same underlying constitutional balance.

Treatment of Public Pensions

One of the most heavily litigated questions in modern Chapter 9 has been whether and how public-employee pension obligations can be modified in a plan of adjustment. The orthodoxy before Detroit was that state constitutional or statutory protections for public pensions placed them outside the reach of federal bankruptcy. The Eastern District of Michigan held in 2013 that pension obligations are contract rights for federal supremacy purposes, that contract rights can be impaired in a federal bankruptcy, and that state constitutional pension protections do not bind the federal bankruptcy court. The Detroit plan ultimately reduced pension benefits through negotiated adjustment, though by less than initial proposals.

Other Chapter 9 cases have reached different practical outcomes through negotiation rather than litigation. The Stockton plan preserved its CalPERS pension obligations while imposing larger haircuts on bond debt; bondholders objected, and the question of whether CalPERS could have been impaired was litigated but not definitively answered on appeal because the parties settled. The legal landscape therefore offers no single bright-line rule about pensions in Chapter 9; the answer turns on the state-law characterization of the pension, the contractual structure, and the negotiation dynamics in the particular case.

The Puerto Rico Framework: PROMESA

Puerto Rico is not a state for Chapter 9 purposes and could not authorize its own Chapter 9 filing under the statute as written. After the Commonwealth's debt crisis escalated, Congress enacted the Puerto Rico Oversight, Management, and Economic Stability Act (PROMESA) in 2016. PROMESA Title III created a separate federal-court debt-adjustment proceeding modeled on Chapter 9 but adapted for a non-state territory, with oversight by the Financial Oversight and Management Board. The Commonwealth and several instrumentalities, including the Puerto Rico Electric Power Authority, restructured tens of billions of dollars of debt under PROMESA between 2017 and 2022.

PROMESA is not a part of the Bankruptcy Code, but a Chapter 9 reader needs to understand it because the doctrines, case law, and many of the practitioners overlap. The eligibility, plan-confirmation, and creditor-protection rules in PROMESA Title III incorporate Chapter 9 provisions by cross-reference.

Plan Confirmation in Chapter 9

The Chapter 9 plan-confirmation standards in Section 943 borrow heavily from Chapter 11 but include several Chapter 9-specific requirements: the plan must comply with the provisions of title 11 made applicable by Section 901; the plan must be in the "best interests of creditors"; the plan must be feasible; the debtor cannot be prohibited by law from taking any action necessary to carry out the plan; and any regulatory or electoral approval necessary under non-bankruptcy law has been obtained or the plan is expressly conditional on such approval. The "best interests" test in Chapter 9 is qualitatively different from its Chapter 7 counterpart because there is no liquidation comparator; courts have applied a "more than the creditors would otherwise receive" standard tied to realistic non-bankruptcy alternatives.
