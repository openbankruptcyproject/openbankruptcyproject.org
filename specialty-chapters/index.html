Specialty Chapters

Most bankruptcy practice involves Chapter 7, Chapter 11, or Chapter 13. But the Bankruptcy Code reserves several chapters and sub-chapters for specialized debtor types - municipalities, family farmers and fishermen, healthcare businesses, single-asset real-estate entities, and stockbroker/commodity-broker liquidations. These five deep-dives explain when each specialty vehicle applies and how its rules differ from the general framework.

About the specialty chapters

The Bankruptcy Code's specialty chapters exist because the standard reorganization or liquidation machinery does not fit every debtor type. Chapter 9 covers municipalities and other governmental units; it is far more limited than chapter 11 because state sovereignty constraints prevent the federal court from interfering with the political or governmental powers of the debtor. Chapter 12 covers family farmers and fishermen with regular annual income; it offers a more debtor-friendly cramdown framework than chapter 11 or chapter 13, reflecting the seasonal nature of agricultural income.

Within chapter 7 and chapter 11, the Code further differentiates by industry. Subchapter III of chapter 7 contains the special rules for stockbroker liquidations under SIPA-coordinated framework, and subchapter IV covers commodity-broker liquidations. Chapter 11 contains streamlined provisions for single-asset real-estate cases (the so-called SARE provisions in sections 101(51B) and 362(d)(3)) and special provisions for healthcare businesses (sections 333, 351, and 1170-1174) that recognize the regulatory and patient-care complications of healthcare reorganizations.

Each specialty regime modifies the standard framework in ways that can be load-bearing for case strategy. Practitioners moving from a general chapter 11 practice into a specialty chapter must reorient to the modified deadlines, the specialized officers (patient-care ombudsmen, stockbroker liquidation trustees), and the substantive carve-outs.

Specialty-chapter deep-dives

  • Chapter 9 Municipal BankruptcyEligibility under section 109(c) including the state-authorization requirement, the limited scope of judicial intervention under section 904, and the plan-confirmation standards adapted for governmental debtors.
  • Chapter 12 Family Farmer and Family FishermanEligibility ceilings, the section 1222(b)(9) cramdown of secured creditors over the value of the collateral, and the simplified plan-confirmation framework relative to chapter 11.
  • Healthcare Business BankruptcyThe patient-care ombudsman required by section 333, transfer of patients under section 704(a)(12) and section 1106(a)(1), and the disposal of patient records under section 351.
  • Single Asset Real Estate (SARE)SARE eligibility under section 101(51B), the section 362(d)(3) accelerated relief-from-stay timeline (90 days to file a plan or commence monthly interest payments), and the litigation strategy for the secured lender.
  • Stockbroker and Commodity Broker LiquidationSubchapter III of chapter 7 (stockbrokers) and subchapter IV (commodity brokers), the priority of customer claims under sections 752 and 766, and the relationship with SIPA proceedings.
Eligibility note: Each specialty chapter has strict eligibility requirements policed by the United States Trustee and challenged by parties in interest. A case filed under the wrong chapter can be dismissed or converted, with significant loss of debtor-friendly procedural advantages.
