Priming Liens under 11 U.S.C. Section 364(d) and Adequate Protection

When a DIP lender can leapfrog an existing secured creditor: the consent path, the contested adequate-protection path, and the Section 361 menu of protections the existing lienholder must receive.

What Is a Priming Lien?

A priming lien is a lien granted to a post-petition DIP lender that ranks senior to, or pari passu with, an already-existing lien on the same collateral. The new lender "primes" the existing secured creditor: when the collateral is later sold or liquidated, the DIP lender is paid first, even though the existing lien predates the DIP loan. In a sense, priming reorders the secured creditor waterfall by judicial decree rather than by consensual intercreditor agreement.

Official citation: 11 U.S.C. § 364(d)

Because priming radically alters the property rights of the existing lienholder, Section 364(d) is hedged with two threshold requirements. The court may approve priming credit only if (i) the trustee is unable to obtain such credit otherwise and (ii) there is adequate protection of the interest of the holder of the lien on the property on which the senior or equal lien is proposed to be granted.

The Two Paths: Consent or Adequate Protection

In practice, priming-lien approvals come in two flavors. The far more common is the consensual priming, where the existing lienholder agrees to subordinate its position in exchange for negotiated concessions. Consent is often documented in the DIP order itself and is sometimes purchased through a paydown of the existing facility, a roll-up of the existing facility into the new DIP, expansion of the existing lender's collateral pool, or other intercreditor accommodations.

The contested path is the non-consensual priming under Section 364(d). Here the debtor must affirmatively prove, by a preponderance of the evidence, both the inability-to-obtain-otherwise prong and the adequate-protection prong. The burden of proof on adequate protection is on the debtor under Section 364(d)(2). Contested primings are rare but consequential: a wrongly-decided priming can wipe out millions of dollars of secured value with no recourse for the displaced lienholder.

The Inability-to-Obtain-Otherwise Prong

The debtor must show that less aggressive financing is unavailable. Courts generally require evidence of a good-faith canvass of the financing market and proof that no lender will extend a Section 364(c) facility (junior liens, super-priority claim) on commercially reasonable terms. A single declined term sheet is not enough; courts typically expect multiple solicitations and a credible explanation of why each was unavailable or unsuitable. The standard does not require the debtor to find the worst possible terms before resorting to priming, but it does require a genuine inability to secure equivalent value through subordinated post-petition credit.

Section 361 Adequate Protection: The Three Statutory Forms

Section 361 sets the menu of adequate-protection devices. In a priming context, the same menu applies:

  • Section 361(1) — Cash payments: Periodic cash payments to the existing lienholder equal to the decrease in the value of its interest caused by the priming or by the use of collateral.
  • Section 361(2) — Additional or replacement liens: A lien on additional or replacement collateral sufficient to compensate for the decrease in value. In a typical priming order, the existing lienholder receives a replacement lien junior to the new DIP lien but senior to other estate property of equivalent value.
  • Section 361(3) — The "indubitable equivalent": Any other relief, other than an administrative-priority claim under Section 503(b)(1), that will result in the realization by the holder of the indubitable equivalent of its interest in the property.

Section 361 expressly forbids the use of a Section 503(b)(1) administrative priority as adequate protection. This is a recognition that an administrative claim against a fragile estate is no substitute for a recorded lien on identifiable collateral.

Equity Cushion as Adequate Protection

The most common adequate-protection theory in contested priming hearings is the equity cushion: if the collateral is worth materially more than the priming and existing debts combined, the existing lienholder is protected by the cushion of unencumbered value. Courts have generally accepted equity cushions in the range of 20 percent or more as sufficient adequate protection, with cushions below 10 percent generally inadequate and the intermediate band requiring a fact-intensive analysis. The valuation of the collateral and the size of the existing and proposed debts are typically the most heavily litigated facts at a contested priming hearing.

An undersecured lienholder cannot be primed without genuine new value flowing back to it; the only "cushion" available in an undersecured posture is whatever new collateral, cash payments, or other consideration the priming arrangement is willing to deliver.

Risk of Failed Adequate Protection: Section 507(b)

If adequate protection later proves inadequate (for example, because the collateral declines in value beyond the cushion or replacement-lien valuation), Section 507(b) gives the existing lienholder a super-priority administrative claim for the shortfall. This claim outranks all other administrative expenses, including the DIP lender's own Section 364(c)(1) super-priority unless specifically subordinated in the DIP order. Section 507(b) is, in effect, the back-stop guarantee that adequate protection will deliver real economic value, not just a paper recital.

DIP orders often include carefully-negotiated waterfalls that govern the relative priority of the DIP super-priority claim and the Section 507(b) claim. Existing lienholders should pay close attention to these provisions before consenting to a priming arrangement.

Evidentiary Burden and Hearing Posture

The debtor bears the burden of proof on adequate protection. Evidence typically takes the form of an appraisal, a financial-advisor declaration on alternative financing efforts, and a budget showing the use of the new financing. The existing lienholder will frequently offer competing valuation evidence. Courts often require live testimony at contested final-DIP hearings and will not rubber-stamp a contested priming on declarations alone.

Where the priming is sought on interim relief, courts limit the interim priming to the amount reasonably necessary to avoid immediate and irreparable harm pending the final hearing, consistent with Federal Rule of Bankruptcy Procedure 4001(c)(2).
