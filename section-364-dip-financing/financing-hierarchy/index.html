11 U.S.C. Section 364 Financing Hierarchy and Approval Procedure

The Section 364(b)/(c)/(d) ladder of escalation, Rule 4001(c) notice and disclosure requirements, the interim/final two-step, and the standard components of a contemporary DIP financing order.

The Statutory Ladder of Escalation

Section 364 is structured as a ladder. Each rung represents a stronger lender protection — and, correspondingly, a greater level of court scrutiny and procedural protection for other creditors. A debtor must show that the rung below is genuinely unavailable before climbing higher. The four-rung framework is:

Official citation: 11 U.S.C. § 364

  1. Section 364(a) — Ordinary-course unsecured credit. No motion, no hearing.
  2. Section 364(b) — Non-ordinary-course unsecured credit. Notice and a hearing; result is Section 503(b)(1) administrative claim.
  3. Section 364(c) — Super-priority claim, lien on unencumbered property, or junior lien on encumbered property. Notice and a hearing; debtor must show inability to obtain Section 503(b)(1)-level credit.
  4. Section 364(d) — Senior or equal (priming) lien on already-encumbered property. Notice and a hearing; debtor must show inability to obtain credit otherwise and adequate protection of the existing lienholder.

Each successive rung incorporates the protections of the rung below and adds new ones. A modern DIP order routinely invokes multiple rungs of the ladder simultaneously: liens on unencumbered property under Section 364(c)(2), junior liens on already-encumbered property under Section 364(c)(3), super-priority administrative claims under Section 364(c)(1), and priming liens on selected categories of collateral under Section 364(d).

Rule 4001(c): Notice and Disclosure

Federal Rule of Bankruptcy Procedure 4001(c) governs motions to obtain credit. The rule requires:

  • Service: Service on the United States Trustee, any committee elected under Section 705 or appointed under Section 1102, the twenty largest unsecured creditors if no committee has been appointed, and any other party the court directs.
  • Concise statement: The motion must contain a concise statement of the relief requested, with the proposed form of order attached.
  • Disclosure of significant provisions: The motion must specifically identify any provision of the type listed in Rule 4001(c)(1)(B), including: grant of priority or a lien on property of the estate under Section 364(c) or (d); adequate-protection or priority of pre-petition claims; determination of validity, enforceability, priority, or amount of a pre-petition claim or lien; waiver or modification of the automatic stay; waiver or modification of any entity's authority to file a plan, seek extensions of exclusivity, or request use of cash collateral; establishment of deadlines for filing a plan of reorganization or for confirmation; waiver or modification of the applicability of nonbankruptcy law relating to perfection of liens; release, waiver, or limitation of any claim or right against any entity; indemnification of any entity; release, waiver, or limitation of any right under Section 506(c); grant of a lien on any claim or cause of action arising under Sections 544-553; and cross-collateralization.
  • Final hearing: A final hearing may not be commenced earlier than 14 days after service of the motion.
  • Interim relief: The court may conduct a preliminary hearing before the 14-day period expires, but may only authorize the obtaining of credit to the extent necessary to avoid immediate and irreparable harm to the estate.

The Interim/Final Two-Step

Nearly every meaningfully-sized DIP facility is approved through a two-step process. On or shortly after the petition date, the debtor files a motion for entry of an interim order and a final order. The interim order, entered at a first-day hearing typically held within one to four business days of the petition, authorizes a limited tranche of borrowing (the amount necessary to fund operations until the final hearing) and grants the interim liens and priority on a limited basis. The final order, entered after the 14-day notice period and a contested final hearing, authorizes the full facility, including any extraordinary provisions deferred from the interim stage.

The interim period is critical for committee formation and creditor diligence. The official committee of unsecured creditors, if appointed, will use the interim period to investigate the proposed financing, negotiate carve-outs, investigation budgets, and challenge periods, and prepare any objections to the final order. The interim/final cadence is the principal mechanism by which Rule 4001(c)'s notice-and-disclosure protections are operationalized.

Standard Components of a Final DIP Order

A typical modern final DIP order runs many pages and contains a recurring set of provisions. The components below are not exhaustive but represent the standard taxonomy:

  • Findings of fact: Good faith of the lender (for Section 364(e) protection), the debtor's inability to obtain alternative financing, the necessity of the financing to avoid immediate and irreparable harm and (in due course) to maximize the value of the estate.
  • Authorization: The debtor is authorized to borrow up to the committed amount and to execute the DIP loan documents.
  • Liens and priorities: Specific Section 364(c)(1), 364(c)(2), 364(c)(3), and (where applicable) 364(d) grants, with descriptions of the affected collateral and the rank of the resulting liens.
  • Adequate protection for primed lenders: Replacement liens, Section 507(b) super-priority, periodic cash payments, and reporting rights for any existing lienholder being primed under Section 364(d) or whose cash collateral is being used.
  • Budget and variance covenants: The approved budget that governs use of the DIP proceeds, the permitted variance, and the rights of the lender if variances are exceeded.
  • Carve-out: The reservation of a portion of the DIP collateral and proceeds for accrued and unpaid professional fees, U.S. Trustee fees, and (sometimes) wind-down expenses, with specified pre- and post-default caps.
  • Milestones: Sale, plan, or restructuring milestones tied to the case timeline; default upon missed milestone.
  • Stipulations and challenge period: Stipulations as to the validity, priority, and amount of the lender's pre-petition claims and liens, subject to a defined challenge period (typically 60-75 days) during which the committee or other parties may bring adversary proceedings to challenge.
  • Section 506(c) and 552(b) waivers: Waiver of the estate's surcharge rights under Section 506(c) and of the equities-of-the-case exception under Section 552(b), subject to Rule 4001(c) disclosure.
  • Investigation budget: A small carve-out (often $25,000 to $250,000) available to the committee for investigation of pre-petition lender claims.
  • Events of default and remedies: A defined cure period for non-payment defaults, notice obligations to the U.S. Trustee and committee before exercising remedies, and the lender's rights upon a continuing default.
  • Section 364(e) good-faith finding: An express finding that the lender extended credit in good faith and is entitled to the protections of Section 364(e).

U.S. Trustee Guidelines and Local Practice

The United States Trustee Program has published guidelines for DIP-financing motions that flag specific provisions as warranting heightened review, including roll-ups, cross-collateralization, milestones perceived as overly aggressive, and short challenge periods. Many districts have local rules or general orders that supplement Rule 4001(c) with district-specific disclosure requirements (for example, a standard form of side-by-side comparison of "extraordinary" DIP provisions). Practitioners should consult both the U.S. Trustee guidelines and applicable local rules in any new district to ensure that the disclosure requirements are satisfied.
