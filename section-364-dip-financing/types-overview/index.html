DIP Financing Types Overview — 11 U.S.C. Section 364

Plain-English overview of the four tiers of post-petition credit available to a debtor in possession: ordinary-course unsecured, court-approved unsecured, administrative-priority and super-priority secured, and priming liens.

What Is Section 364?

Section 364 of the Bankruptcy Code is the gateway through which a debtor in possession (or a Chapter 11 trustee) obtains post-petition credit. Without Section 364, a debtor could not borrow money or buy goods on credit after the petition date, because the automatic stay and the rules of administrative priority would make any new lender's claim subordinate to a host of pre-petition obligations and existing liens. Section 364 solves that problem by creating a tiered system: the more aggressive the financing terms a debtor needs, the higher the level of court scrutiny required.

Official citation: 11 U.S.C. § 364

The four tiers reflect a deliberate ladder of escalation. Each tier responds to the practical reality that a debtor's credit profile in bankruptcy is severely compromised and that lenders will demand progressively stronger protections before extending new money to an insolvent enterprise.

Section 364(a): Ordinary-Course Unsecured Credit

Section 364(a) authorizes a trustee or debtor in possession operating the business under Section 1108 to obtain unsecured credit and incur unsecured debt in the ordinary course of business without notice or a hearing. The resulting claim is treated as an administrative expense under Section 503(b)(1).

This subsection is the workhorse provision that lets an operating debtor buy inventory on net-30 trade terms, accept utility services, and incur day-to-day operating liabilities without running to the bankruptcy court for each transaction. The "ordinary course" limit is fact-bound and turns on both the horizontal test (consistent with industry norms) and the vertical test (consistent with the debtor's own pre-petition practice).

Section 364(b): Court-Approved Unsecured Credit

Section 364(b) covers unsecured credit outside the ordinary course. The court, after notice and a hearing, may authorize the trustee to obtain unsecured credit or to incur unsecured debt other than under Section 364(a). The claim is again treated as a Section 503(b)(1) administrative expense.

This tier is invoked when the debtor needs unsecured credit that does not fit the trade-credit pattern (for example, a large unsecured loan from a parent entity or a strategic partner). The hearing requirement gives creditors and the United States Trustee a chance to object before the obligation is incurred.

Section 364(c): Administrative-Priority and Super-Priority Secured Credit

When the debtor cannot obtain unsecured credit allowable as an ordinary administrative expense, Section 364(c) allows the court, after notice and a hearing, to authorize new credit secured by one or more of three escalating protections:

  • Section 364(c)(1): A super-priority administrative expense having priority over any or all administrative expenses of the kind specified in Sections 503(b) or 507(b);
  • Section 364(c)(2): A lien on property of the estate that is not otherwise subject to a lien; or
  • Section 364(c)(3): A junior lien on property of the estate already subject to a lien.

The hallmark of Section 364(c) is that it gives the new lender a security interest in unencumbered property, or a junior lien on already-encumbered property, without disturbing the position of existing secured creditors. The threshold finding that the debtor could not obtain credit on better terms is essential.

Section 364(d): Priming Liens

Section 364(d) is the most aggressive of the tiers. It authorizes the court to approve credit secured by a senior or equal lien on property already subject to a lien (a "priming" lien) only if (i) the debtor cannot obtain credit otherwise and (ii) there is adequate protection of the interest of the existing lienholder being primed.

Priming a non-consenting secured lender is one of the most consequential remedies available in a bankruptcy case. The existing lienholder's collateral position is subordinated to a new loan, and unless adequate protection (typically a replacement lien, cash payments, or a sufficient equity cushion) is provided, that subordination violates the takings principle that runs through the secured-creditor protections of the Code. The priming-liens deep-dive page covers the adequate-protection mechanics in detail.

Roll-Ups and Cross-Collateralization

Two recurring features of modern DIP facilities sit on top of the basic Section 364 framework and have generated extensive doctrinal controversy:

Roll-ups are provisions in a DIP loan that use new-money advances to pay down a pre-petition claim held by the same lender, converting an unsecured or under-secured pre-petition position into a fully-secured post-petition position. Courts have approved roll-ups where the new money is substantial relative to the rolled-up amount, where unsecured creditors receive meaningful concessions, and where the alternative is liquidation. They are scrutinized closely under the Section 364(c) and (d) standards.

Cross-collateralization is the practice of granting the DIP lender a lien on the debtor's assets to secure not only the new post-petition advances but also the lender's pre-petition claim. The Eleventh Circuit's In re Saybrook Manufacturing Co., 963 F.2d 1490 (11th Cir. 1992) treats cross-collateralization as per se impermissible under Section 364, on the rationale that it improperly elevates a pre-petition unsecured claim ahead of other unsecured creditors. Other circuits apply a balancing test. The cross-collateralization deep-dive page traces the circuit split.

Procedure and Approval Standards

Section 364(b), (c), and (d) all require "notice and a hearing" within the meaning of Section 102(1). Federal Rule of Bankruptcy Procedure 4001(c) lays out the specific notice requirements for motions to obtain credit, including a 14-day notice period and detailed disclosure of significant terms (cross-collateralization, roll-ups, waivers of Section 506(c), liens on avoidance actions, and so forth).

Courts routinely enter an interim DIP order on a short turnaround (often within a few days of the petition) and then schedule a final hearing roughly three weeks later. The interim order is constrained to the financing reasonably necessary to avoid immediate and irreparable harm. The final order resolves all objections and approves the full facility.
