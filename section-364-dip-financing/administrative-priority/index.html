11 U.S.C. Section 364(c) Administrative-Priority and Super-Priority

How Section 364(c) authorizes credit secured by super-priority administrative status, by liens on unencumbered property, and by junior liens on encumbered property — and how it connects with the ordinary administrative-expense regime of Section 503(b)(1).

From Ordinary Admin Expense to Section 364(c)(1) Super-Priority

Section 503(b)(1) creates the ordinary administrative-expense category that covers post-petition costs of preserving the estate, wages and benefits for post-petition services, and similar charges. Administrative expenses are entitled to first priority under Section 507(a)(2) and must be paid in full on the effective date of a confirmed Chapter 11 plan unless their holders agree to different treatment.

Official citation: 11 U.S.C. § 364(c)

For a small post-petition trade obligation, ordinary Section 503(b)(1) administrative status is usually sufficient. For a meaningful DIP loan, it is almost never enough. The lender wants assurance that even in a downside scenario (conversion to Chapter 7, attempted sale at a value insufficient to pay accrued professionals' fees and other administrative claims), the DIP loan will be paid first. Section 364(c)(1) is the mechanism that delivers that assurance.

Section 364(c)(1): Super-Priority Over All Other Admin Expenses

Section 364(c)(1) authorizes the court to approve credit "with priority over any or all administrative expenses of the kind specified in section 503(b) or 507(b) of this title." A claim granted Section 364(c)(1) priority sits above every other administrative expense, including:

  • Section 503(b)(1) post-petition operating costs and wages;
  • Section 503(b)(2) professional-fee awards for debtor and committee counsel and financial advisors;
  • Section 503(b)(3) and (4) substantial-contribution claims and their related fees;
  • Section 503(b)(9) twenty-day pre-petition goods claims;
  • Section 507(b) failed-adequate-protection claims arising from depletion of secured-creditor collateral.

The Section 364(c)(1) priority is itself junior, by statute and by negotiation, to any "carve-out" the DIP order may create. A typical carve-out reserves a defined dollar amount for accrued and unpaid professional fees and U.S. Trustee fees so that the estate can fund its own wind-down even in a default scenario. The size of the carve-out is one of the most heavily-negotiated terms in any DIP order.

Section 364(c)(2): Liens on Unencumbered Property

Section 364(c)(2) authorizes a lien on property of the estate that is not otherwise subject to a lien. This is the standard mechanism for granting the DIP lender a first-priority lien on previously unencumbered assets: free-and-clear accounts receivable, free-and-clear intellectual property, cash and deposit accounts not subject to pre-petition liens, equity interests in non-debtor subsidiaries (subject to applicable restrictions), and similar property. Because no existing lienholder is being displaced, Section 364(c)(2) does not require adequate protection.

Section 364(c)(3): Junior Liens on Encumbered Property

Section 364(c)(3) authorizes a junior lien on property of the estate that is already subject to a lien. This option is used when the debtor's collateral pool is largely encumbered and there is no unencumbered property of meaningful value. The DIP lender takes a second-out or third-out position behind the existing secured creditors and is repaid only after the senior liens are satisfied. Because the existing lienholder's position is unchanged, Section 364(c)(3) does not require adequate protection.

In practice, Section 364(c)(3) is the least common of the three Section 364(c) tiers because the economics are usually unattractive: a lender that is willing to take a junior lien on encumbered property is often also willing to take a Section 364(c)(1) super-priority claim, and the combination of both is the standard structure of a modern DIP facility.

The Threshold Showing: "Unable to Obtain Credit Otherwise"

Section 364(c) is unavailable unless the trustee is "unable to obtain unsecured credit allowable under Section 503(b)(1)." Courts have construed this requirement flexibly: the debtor does not need to canvass the entire credit market exhaustively but must demonstrate a good-faith effort to obtain less-aggressive financing and a credible explanation of why such financing is not available on commercially reasonable terms. A typical first-day declaration from the debtor's chief financial officer or investment banker recites the financing efforts undertaken, the responses received, and the reasons why a Section 364(a) or (b) facility is not feasible.

The threshold showing is generally easier to make in cases where the debtor's principal pre-petition secured lender is the only realistic source of DIP financing, because pre-petition lenders typically condition any DIP facility on Section 364(c) and (d) protections that no third-party lender would forgo.

Section 364(e): Good-Faith Lender Protection

Section 364(e) is the doctrinal back-stop that gives DIP lenders confidence in court-approved priority and lien grants. It provides that the reversal or modification on appeal of an authorization to obtain credit or incur debt, or of a grant of priority or a lien, does not affect the validity of any debt incurred or any priority or lien granted, to an entity that extended credit in good faith. The lender's good-faith reliance on the bankruptcy court order is protected even if the order is later vacated, provided the appeal was not stayed pending review.

Section 364(e) is the reason that interim DIP orders are functionally final: a party objecting to a DIP order generally must obtain a stay pending appeal to preserve any meaningful appellate remedy. Without a stay, the lender will fund the loan and acquire valid liens and priority that cannot be undone by appellate reversal.

Risks to Section 364(c)(1) Priority: Section 507(b) and the Carve-Out

Two principal risks dilute Section 364(c)(1) super-priority. First, Section 507(b) creates a competing super-priority for adequate-protection failures by secured creditors who consented to use of cash collateral or were primed under Section 364(d). The DIP order must specify the relative priority of the Section 364(c)(1) claim and any Section 507(b) claim; many orders rank the DIP super-priority above 507(b), but careful drafting and disclosure are required.

Second, the carve-out for professional fees and U.S. Trustee fees creates an explicit, statutorily-permitted senior claim against the DIP-lender collateral and super-priority. The carve-out is often structured as a "trigger" amount (unlimited fees accrued and unpaid before a default notice, plus a capped amount after the trigger). The interplay between the trigger, the funding mechanism, and the success-fee provisions of professional retention orders is a frequent source of post-effective-date disputes and should be negotiated with care at the DIP-approval stage.
