Cross-Collateralization in DIP Financing — Saybrook and the Circuit Split

When a DIP lender's pre-petition unsecured exposure piggybacks on the post-petition financing collateral: the Eleventh Circuit per se ban, the case-by-case balancing test, and the Second Circuit's interim-relief constraint.

What Is Cross-Collateralization?

Cross-collateralization in DIP financing refers to a provision in the post-petition loan documents that grants the DIP lender liens on the debtor's property to secure not only the new post-petition advances but also the lender's pre-petition claim against the debtor. In its most aggressive form, sometimes called "Texlon-style" cross-collateralization, the pre-petition portion of the lender's exposure is given a lien (or an upgraded lien) on collateral that would otherwise have been available to the general unsecured creditor body.

Underlying provision: 11 U.S.C. § 364(c), (d)

The practical effect is that the DIP lender exits bankruptcy with an enhanced pre-petition position relative to what it had on the petition date, paid for by the bankruptcy estate. The lender argues that this is the price of extending new credit to a distressed borrower. Other constituencies argue that this is exactly the kind of preference-style elevation of one pre-petition unsecured claim over equally-situated others that the Code is designed to prevent.

The Eleventh Circuit Per Se Ban: In re Saybrook Manufacturing Co.

In In re Saybrook Manufacturing Co., 963 F.2d 1490 (11th Cir. 1992), the Eleventh Circuit held that cross-collateralization is per se impermissible under Section 364. The court reasoned that Section 364 authorizes the use of collateral to secure post-petition credit, not to secure pre-petition claims. Allowing a DIP lender to leverage post-petition collateral for the benefit of its pre-petition exposure improperly elevates one unsecured creditor ahead of others outside the strictures of Section 507's priority scheme and the Chapter 11 plan-confirmation process.

The Eleventh Circuit acknowledged that some courts had previously approved cross-collateralization under their general equitable powers, but rejected that rationale, holding that Section 364's specific provisions on post-petition financing displaced any general equitable authority that might otherwise support the practice. The opinion treated cross-collateralization as a form of improper preference and refused to find authority for it anywhere in the Code.

The Pre-Saybrook Balancing Approach

Before Saybrook, several courts had approved cross-collateralization under a multi-factor test sometimes traced to the bankruptcy court opinion in In re Vanguard Diversified, Inc., 31 B.R. 364 (Bankr. E.D.N.Y. 1983), and elaborated in subsequent decisions. The factors typically included:

  • Whether absent the proposed financing the debtor's business would not survive;
  • Whether the debtor is unable to obtain alternative financing on acceptable terms;
  • Whether the proposed lender will not accept less preferential terms;
  • Whether the proposed financing is in the best interest of the estate.

Some courts continue to apply versions of this balancing test outside the Eleventh Circuit, particularly where the cross-collateralization is bounded in scope (for example, limited to the existing under-secured portion of the pre-petition claim), where the DIP loan delivers substantial new value, and where the official unsecured creditors' committee has affirmatively consented. The legality of cross-collateralization is therefore genuinely jurisdiction-dependent.

The Texlon Constraint on Interim Cross-Collateralization

The Second Circuit's earlier decision in In re Texlon Corp., 596 F.2d 1092 (2d Cir. 1979), is the foundational case on cross-collateralization. Texlon did not impose a per se rule, but it sharply criticized the entry of a cross-collateralization order ex parte and without adequate notice or a contested hearing. The case is frequently cited for the proposition that cross-collateralization, if it is to be approved at all, may not be approved at the first-day interim hearing and must instead await a final hearing with full notice to the creditor body.

This is the source of the standard practice that "first-day" interim DIP orders do not grant cross-collateralization or roll-up relief; those features are deferred to the final order. Federal Rule of Bankruptcy Procedure 4001(c)(1)(B) reinforces the constraint by requiring that motions to obtain credit specifically identify any cross-collateralization provision and that interim relief be limited to what is necessary to avoid immediate and irreparable harm.

Roll-Ups as Functional Cross-Collateralization

A modern "roll-up" structure achieves much of the same economic result as cross-collateralization without facing the formal Saybrook prohibition. In a roll-up, the new DIP advances are used (in whole or part) to pay down the lender's pre-petition exposure dollar-for-dollar, after which the previously-pre-petition exposure has been converted into a Section 364 post-petition secured claim. Roll-ups have been approved in many jurisdictions, including some within the Eleventh Circuit, where the new-money component is significant relative to the rolled-up amount and where other creditor constituencies have consented or received adequate concessions.

The distinction between cross-collateralization and a roll-up is one of mechanics rather than economics, and several commentators have noted that the doctrinal gap is increasingly artificial. Courts and creditor committees scrutinize roll-ups for many of the same concerns that animate the cross-collateralization doctrine: disparate treatment of similarly-situated unsecured creditors, foreclosure of plan-confirmation oversight, and entrenchment of the DIP lender as the only viable buyer at any subsequent sale.

Practical Posture: Disclosure and Objection

Federal Rule of Bankruptcy Procedure 4001(c)(1)(B) requires motions to obtain credit to specifically identify cross-collateralization provisions, roll-ups, Section 506(c) waivers, liens on avoidance actions, and other "extraordinary" provisions. The United States Trustee Program's guidelines for DIP-financing motions list these provisions as items meriting heightened review. An official committee of unsecured creditors, where appointed, will routinely negotiate carve-outs, investigation budgets, and challenge periods to mitigate the impact of cross-collateralization or roll-up relief.

In jurisdictions following Saybrook, the answer to a cross-collateralization request is straightforward: the court will not approve it. In other jurisdictions, the practical question becomes whether the financing is sufficiently necessary, the new money sufficiently substantial, and the creditor-body concessions sufficiently meaningful to justify the deviation from ordinary unsecured-creditor parity.
