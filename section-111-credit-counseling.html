11 U.S.C. Section 111 - Approved Credit Counseling Agencies

The statutory regime under which the United States Trustee Program approves and supervises the nonprofit agencies that deliver the pre-filing credit-counseling briefing and the pre-discharge personal-financial-management course.

What Is Section 111?

Section 111 was added by BAPCPA in 2005 to establish the federal approval mechanism for the two consumer-education programs that BAPCPA simultaneously made mandatory for individual debtors: the pre-filing credit-counseling briefing required by Section 109(h), and the pre-discharge personal-financial-management instructional course required by Section 727(a)(11) and Section 1328(g).

The section directs the United States Trustee (or in the Bankruptcy Administrator districts of Alabama and North Carolina, the Bankruptcy Administrator) to maintain publicly available lists of approved providers. Only agencies on those lists may deliver certificates that satisfy a debtor's statutory pre-filing or pre-discharge obligation.

Official citation: 11 U.S.C. § 111

Approval Criteria for Credit-Counseling Agencies: Section 111(c)

Section 111(c) sets minimum standards for approval as a credit-counseling agency. The agency must:

  • Have a board of directors a majority of which are not employed by the agency and will not directly or indirectly benefit financially from the outcomes of the counseling.
  • If a fee is charged, charge a reasonable fee and provide services without regard to ability to pay.
  • Provide for safekeeping and payment of client funds, including an annual audit and the maintenance of a separate trust account for client funds.
  • Provide full disclosures to clients, including the funding sources, counselor qualifications, possible impact on credit reports, and the cost of services.
  • Provide adequate counseling that includes an analysis of the client's current financial condition, the factors that caused the financial condition, and how the client can develop a plan to respond to the financial condition without incurring negative amortization of debt.
  • Provide trained counselors who receive no commission or bonus based on the outcome of counseling services, demonstrate adequate experience and background, and be adequately trained and accredited.

Approval Criteria for Personal-Financial-Management Providers: Section 111(d)

Section 111(d) sets parallel standards for instructional-course providers. Providers must demonstrate they can provide debtor education effectively, have adequate experience and educational background, have adequate facilities, and offer the course at a reasonable cost.

Both credit-counseling and instructional-course approvals are time-limited. The U.S. Trustee Program conducts periodic reapproval reviews and may revoke approval for noncompliance.

Quality Standards and Oversight: Section 111(e)-(g)

Section 111(e) directs the U.S. Trustee Program to take reasonable steps to ensure that services are adequate, qualified counselors are available, and approved agencies and providers comply with the criteria in subsections (c) and (d). The U.S. Trustee Program has used this authority to develop formal quality-service standards, performance audits, and sanctions including suspension and revocation of approval.

Section 111(g) addresses the relationship between approval under this section and state laws regulating credit-counseling and debt-management activities. Federal approval does not displace state licensing or regulatory requirements; agencies generally must satisfy both regimes.

Practical Significance for Debtors

For individual debtors, Section 111 operates invisibly: the debtor receives a list of approved providers from the bankruptcy court or the U.S. Trustee Program, selects one, completes the briefing or course, and receives a certificate. The statutory requirement is satisfied by completion at an approved provider; completion at a non-approved provider does not satisfy the statute regardless of the educational quality.

Limited exceptions to the pre-filing requirement exist under Section 109(h)(3) (exigent circumstances) and 109(h)(4) (incapacity, disability, or active military duty in a combat zone). These exceptions are narrow and require specific documentation.

Major Doctrinal Cases

  • In re Hubel, 395 B.R. 823 (N.D.N.Y. 2008) - Discussed the strictness of the pre-filing briefing requirement and the narrow scope of the Section 109(h)(3) exigent-circumstances exception.
  • In re Falcone, 370 B.R. 462 (Bankr. D. Mass. 2007) - Held that pre-filing counseling completed less than 180 days before the petition did not satisfy the timing requirement.
  • In re Tomco, 339 B.R. 145 (Bankr. W.D. Pa. 2006) - Addressed dismissal of cases filed without compliant pre-filing counseling certificates.
  • In re LaPorta, 332 B.R. 879 (Bankr. D. Minn. 2005) - Early decision on the contours of the BAPCPA pre-filing counseling regime.
  • In re Hess, 347 B.R. 489 (Bankr. D. Vt. 2006) - Discussed Section 109(h)(4) incapacity exception and required documentation.
