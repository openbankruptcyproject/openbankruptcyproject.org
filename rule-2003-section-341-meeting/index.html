Federal Rule of Bankruptcy Procedure 2003

Rule 2003: Meeting of Creditors Under Section 341

Scheduling, presiding officer, examination scope, USTP-required questions, and transcript availability at the meeting of creditors.

Statutory foundation

11 U.S.C. Section 341(a) requires that, in every case under Chapter 7, 11, 12, or 13, the United States Trustee convene and preside at a meeting of creditors within a reasonable time after the order for relief. Section 341(c) prohibits the judge from presiding at or attending the meeting. Federal Rule of Bankruptcy Procedure 2003 implements Section 341 by specifying the timing of the meeting, the procedures for conducting it, the scope of permissible examination, and the record-keeping requirements.

Timing of the meeting

Rule 2003(a) provides timing windows that vary by chapter:

  • Chapter 7 individual case: 21 to 40 days after the order for relief
  • Chapter 7 non-individual case: 21 to 40 days
  • Chapter 11 case: 21 to 40 days (Chapter 11 individual cases) or as ordered by the United States Trustee in Subchapter V and other Chapter 11 cases
  • Chapter 12 case: 21 to 35 days
  • Chapter 13 case: 21 to 50 days

The meeting may be held at a location designated by the United States Trustee. Since 2020, many districts have continued post-pandemic procedures permitting or requiring telephonic or video appearance for routine consumer cases; the U.S. Trustee Program has issued guidance on remote-appearance procedures, and individual district practice varies.

Who presides

The presiding officer at the Section 341 meeting depends on the chapter:

  • Chapter 7: The interim trustee (who normally becomes the permanent trustee under 11 U.S.C. Section 702(d) when no permanent trustee is elected) presides.
  • Chapter 13: The standing Chapter 13 trustee for the district presides.
  • Chapter 12: The standing Chapter 12 trustee presides.
  • Chapter 11 (non-Subchapter V): The United States Trustee or a designee presides. There is no estate-administering trustee unless one is appointed under Section 1104.
  • Chapter 11 Subchapter V: The Subchapter V trustee presides (28 U.S.C. Section 586(b); 11 U.S.C. Section 1183).

Section 341(c) prohibits the bankruptcy judge from presiding at or attending the meeting. The exclusion preserves the judge's neutrality on factual disputes that may emerge from the meeting and that may later require judicial resolution.

Identification, oath, and the USTP-required questions

The Handbook for Chapter 7 Trustees and the Handbook for Chapter 13 Standing Trustees, both issued by the Executive Office for United States Trustees, require trustees to begin the examination of each debtor with a uniform set of identification, identity-verification, and substantive questions. The "USTP-required questions" cover, at minimum:

  • Verification of the debtor's full name, address, and Social Security number against photo identification (driver's license, state identification card, or U.S. passport) and an original Social Security card or other acceptable evidence of the SSN
  • Confirmation that the debtor reviewed and signed the petition, schedules, and Statement of Financial Affairs and that the documents are true and correct to the best of the debtor's knowledge
  • Confirmation of completion of the pre-petition credit-counseling briefing required by 11 U.S.C. Section 109(h)
  • Whether the debtor has filed a prior bankruptcy case within the time periods that affect discharge eligibility under Section 727(a)(8)-(9) (Chapter 7) or that limit access to the automatic stay under Section 362(c)(3)-(4)
  • Whether the debtor has disclosed all assets, all transfers within the lookback windows, and all sources of income
  • Whether the debtor has any pending claims, lawsuits, or other potential causes of action that may belong to the estate
  • Whether the debtor expects any inheritance, life-insurance proceeds, or property-settlement payments within 180 days after the petition date (relevant to 11 U.S.C. Section 541(a)(5))

The trustee then proceeds to chapter-specific questions: in Chapter 7, focus on liquidation assets and dischargeability indicators; in Chapter 13, focus on feasibility of the proposed plan, the debtor's budget, and treatment of secured creditors.

Examination by creditors and the United States Trustee

After the presiding officer's examination concludes, creditors and the United States Trustee may examine the debtor. The scope of examination is governed by Section 343, which requires the debtor to appear and submit to examination under oath at the meeting. The examination scope under Section 343 is broad and includes the operation of the debtor's business, the debtor's financial affairs, and any matter that may affect administration of the estate or the debtor's right to discharge.

Although the scope is broad, creditor examination is not unlimited. The presiding officer has authority to control the orderly conduct of the meeting and may limit cumulative, harassing, or off-topic examination. Rule 2003 does not provide for cross-examination in the trial-procedure sense; the meeting is an examination, not an adversary hearing.

Record of the meeting

Rule 2003(c) requires the presiding officer to make a record of the meeting that includes the time and place, the trustee's report of the examination, and any matters that the parties or the presiding officer specifically request to be recorded. In practice, Section 341 meetings are recorded by audio (or, in some districts, by stenographic reporter) and the recording is preserved by the office of the United States Trustee.

An audio recording or transcript of the meeting is generally available on written request to the U.S. Trustee field office or the trustee's office that conducted the meeting. The U.S. Trustee Program publishes a uniform request procedure; requesters typically pay a duplication fee. The 341 transcript is frequently used as an evidentiary record in later contested matters - for example, a debtor's testimony at the 341 meeting may be used as a prior inconsistent statement under Federal Rule of Evidence 613 if the debtor takes a different position at a later contested-matter hearing.

Adjournment and continuation

Rule 2003(e) authorizes the presiding officer to adjourn the meeting from time to time by oral announcement at the meeting without further written notice. An adjournment may be necessary when:

  • The debtor fails to appear, requiring rescheduling and a renewed appearance demand
  • The debtor's identification or other verification documents are not available
  • Significant amendments to the schedules or SOFA are required and the trustee defers examination until the amendments are filed
  • The debtor's responses suggest the need for production of additional documents
  • Sub V or Chapter 11 cases involve substantial creditor questioning that cannot be completed in the original time slot

A continued 341 meeting restarts the clock for various deadlines tied to the "conclusion" of the meeting - most importantly, the deadline for filing a complaint objecting to discharge or to determine dischargeability under Rule 4004 and Rule 4007 runs from the first date set for the meeting, but the deadline for filing objections to claimed exemptions under Rule 4003(b)(1) runs from the conclusion of the meeting.

Common procedural posture

Asset case discovered at the meeting

A Chapter 7 case initially designated "no asset" becomes an asset case when the trustee's examination reveals an undisclosed account, a pre-petition transfer, or a non-exempt asset. The trustee adjourns the meeting, files a notice of assets with the bankruptcy court, and proceeds with administration. Creditors then receive a Section 341(d) notice to file proofs of claim.

Debtor non-appearance

The debtor fails to appear at the scheduled meeting. The trustee continues the meeting and notifies the debtor. A second non-appearance is grounds for the trustee to recommend dismissal under 11 U.S.C. Section 707(a)(1) (Chapter 7) or Section 1307(c)(1) (Chapter 13) for unreasonable delay. In Chapter 13, the standing trustee will typically move to dismiss for failure to appear.

Subchapter V status conference under Section 1188

For Subchapter V cases, the Section 341 meeting is supplemented by the status conference required by 11 U.S.C. Section 1188, held within 60 days after the order for relief, at which the debtor must report on the efforts to obtain a consensual plan and the court enters scheduling orders. The Section 341 meeting and the Section 1188 conference are distinct events: the 341 meeting is creditor-facing and conducted by the Subchapter V trustee; the 1188 conference is court-facing and conducted by the bankruptcy judge.

The 341 transcript is a public-record evidentiary anchor. Testimony at the meeting is under oath and is recorded. A party who later takes a position inconsistent with that testimony in a contested matter may be impeached with the recording, and material false statements at the meeting may be prosecuted under 18 U.S.C. Section 152(2) or referred for denial of discharge under 11 U.S.C. Section 727(a)(4).
