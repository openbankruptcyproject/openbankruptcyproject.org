11 U.S.C. Chapter 15

Chapter 15: Cross-Border Insolvency Recognition

The Section 1515 petition for recognition of a foreign proceeding, the Section 1517 recognition factors, the foreign main / foreign nonmain distinction, and the Section 1506 public-policy exception.

The origin of Chapter 15

Chapter 15 was added to the Bankruptcy Code by the Bankruptcy Abuse Prevention and Consumer Protection Act of 2005 (BAPCPA) as the U.S. enactment of the UNCITRAL Model Law on Cross-Border Insolvency (1997). Chapter 15 replaced former Section 304 - a sparse, equitable provision authorizing limited ancillary relief in aid of foreign proceedings - with a comprehensive recognition-and-cooperation framework drawn directly from the Model Law's articles.

The Model Law's stated objectives, incorporated into 11 U.S.C. Section 1501, include: cooperation between U.S. courts and foreign courts in cross-border cases; greater legal certainty for trade and investment; fair and efficient administration of cross-border insolvencies that protects the interests of all creditors; protection and maximization of the value of the debtor's assets; and facilitation of the rescue of financially troubled businesses.

The Section 1515 petition for recognition

(a) A foreign representative applies to the court for recognition of a foreign proceeding in which the foreign representative has been appointed by filing a petition for recognition.
(b) A petition for recognition shall be accompanied by - (1) a certified copy of the decision commencing such foreign proceeding and appointing the foreign representative; (2) a certificate from the foreign court affirming the existence of such foreign proceeding and of the appointment of the foreign representative; or (3) in the absence of evidence referred to in paragraphs (1) and (2), any other evidence acceptable to the court of the existence of such foreign proceeding and of the appointment of the foreign representative. 11 U.S.C. Section 1515(a)-(b).

The petition initiates the ancillary case. It must be filed by a "foreign representative" as defined in 11 U.S.C. Section 101(24) - a person or body, including one appointed on an interim basis, authorized in a foreign proceeding to administer the reorganization or liquidation of the debtor's assets or affairs or to act as a representative of the foreign proceeding. The foreign representative is the petitioner; the U.S. debtor (if any U.S. presence exists) is not.

Section 1515(c) requires that the petition be accompanied by a statement identifying all foreign proceedings, in any jurisdiction, with respect to the debtor that are known to the foreign representative. The statement is meant to give the U.S. court the global picture before recognition is decided.

The Section 1517 recognition factors

Subject to section 1506, after notice and a hearing, an order recognizing a foreign proceeding shall be entered if - (1) such foreign proceeding for which recognition is sought is a foreign main proceeding or foreign nonmain proceeding within the meaning of section 1502; (2) the foreign representative applying for recognition is a person or body; and (3) the petition meets the requirements of section 1515. 11 U.S.C. Section 1517(a).

Recognition under Section 1517 is mandatory if the three factors are satisfied and the public-policy exception of Section 1506 does not apply. The statute uses "shall be entered," not "may be entered." This is a deliberate departure from the discretionary equitable framework of former Section 304. The U.S. court's role at the recognition stage is narrow: confirm that the foreign proceeding exists, that the foreign representative was duly appointed, and that the proceeding qualifies as foreign main or foreign nonmain under Section 1502.

The two recognition tracks - foreign main and foreign nonmain

Section 1517(b) directs the court to enter the recognition order with the proper characterization:

Foreign main proceeding

Recognized as such if it is pending in the country where the debtor has the center of its main interests ("COMI"). The presumption under 11 U.S.C. Section 1516(c) is that the debtor's registered office is the COMI in the absence of evidence to the contrary. Recognition as foreign main triggers automatic relief under Section 1520.

Foreign nonmain proceeding

Recognized as such if the foreign proceeding is pending in a country where the debtor has an "establishment" - any place of operations where the debtor carries out a nontransitory economic activity. 11 U.S.C. Section 1502(2). Recognition as foreign nonmain does not trigger Section 1520 automatic relief; available relief is limited to the discretionary relief under Section 1521.

The distinction is doctrinally load-bearing. See the foreign main versus foreign nonmain companion page for the substantive effects of the two classifications.

The Section 1506 public-policy exception

Nothing in this chapter prevents the court from refusing to take an action governed by this chapter if the action would be manifestly contrary to the public policy of the United States. 11 U.S.C. Section 1506.

Section 1506 is the only generally-applicable safety valve in the recognition framework. It is intentionally narrow. The word "manifestly" was carried over verbatim from the UNCITRAL Model Law and is meant to limit refusal to the most extreme cases - those where recognition would offend fundamental U.S. public policy in a clear and unmistakable way. The U.S. courts have applied the exception sparingly.

In In re Ephedra Products Liability Litigation, 349 B.R. 333 (S.D.N.Y. 2006), the court rejected a Section 1506 challenge to recognition of a Canadian Companies' Creditors Arrangement Act (CCAA) proceeding, holding that procedural differences between U.S. and Canadian insolvency systems - even substantial ones - do not amount to a "manifest" public-policy violation. The standard requires more than the absence of an equivalent U.S. procedure; it requires that the action sought be offensive to a fundamental U.S. interest.

The leading early-application case, In re Bear Stearns High-Grade Structured Credit Strategies Master Fund, Ltd., 374 B.R. 122 (Bankr. S.D.N.Y. 2007), aff'd, 389 B.R. 325 (S.D.N.Y. 2008), denied recognition altogether on COMI grounds - the funds had been organized in the Cayman Islands but had no economic activity there beyond the registered office - and signaled that the recognition gate, not the Section 1506 exception, is where most close calls will be resolved.

Comity, cooperation, and Section 1525

Recognition under Chapter 15 unlocks the court's authority to grant cooperation under 11 U.S.C. Section 1525 and Section 1526. The U.S. court "shall cooperate to the maximum extent possible" with foreign courts and foreign representatives, including by direct communication. The cooperation framework treats the U.S. case as ancillary to the foreign main proceeding wherever possible, with the U.S. court's role being to assist - not to displace - the foreign court's administration of the estate.

In In re SPhinX, Ltd., 351 B.R. 103 (Bankr. S.D.N.Y. 2006), aff'd, 371 B.R. 10 (S.D.N.Y. 2007), the court denied foreign-main recognition of a Cayman Islands proceeding because the COMI in fact lay elsewhere, but recognized the proceeding as foreign nonmain and granted significant discretionary cooperation. The case illustrates that the choice between foreign main and foreign nonmain is rarely the choice between recognition and rejection; it is the choice between mandatory and discretionary relief.

Relief on recognition

Recognition opens the door to three categories of relief:

  1. Section 1519 - provisional relief between filing and recognition. Where urgently needed to protect the debtor's assets or the creditors' interests, the court may grant relief including a stay against execution against the debtor's assets in the United States and entrustment of administration of the debtor's U.S. assets to the foreign representative.
  2. Section 1520 - automatic effects of recognition of a foreign main proceeding. Sections 361 and 362 of the Code (adequate protection and the automatic stay) apply with respect to the debtor and its U.S. property; Sections 363, 549, and 552 (use, sale, lease, and postpetition transactions) apply; and the foreign representative may operate the debtor's business.
  3. Section 1521 - discretionary relief after recognition. Available regardless of whether the recognition is as foreign main or foreign nonmain, including extending the provisional Section 1519 relief, suspending the right to transfer assets, examining witnesses, entrusting administration to the foreign representative, and granting any additional relief that may be available to a U.S. trustee, except certain categories reserved by Section 1521(a)(7).

Foreign-proceeding doctrinal landmarks

In re Bear Stearns High-Grade Structured Credit Strategies (2007)

The Southern District of New York's denial of recognition to two Cayman-organized hedge funds because their COMI was not in the Cayman Islands. The case established that the Section 1516(c) presumption of COMI at the registered office is rebuttable and that the court must independently analyze where the debtor's center of main interests actually lies.

In re SPhinX, Ltd. (2006)

Decision adjacent to Bear Stearns denying foreign-main recognition on COMI grounds but granting foreign-nonmain recognition based on the existence of an establishment in the foreign jurisdiction. Demonstrated that the foreign-main / foreign-nonmain distinction is operative even where the registered-office presumption is rebutted.

In re Stanford International Bank Limited (2012)

The competing Antiguan and U.S. receiverships over the Stanford Ponzi-scheme assets produced extended litigation over recognition, COMI, and cooperation. The Fifth Circuit's decision in In re Stanford International Bank, Ltd., 681 F.3d 240 (5th Cir. 2012), upheld the bankruptcy court's denial of recognition to the Antiguan proceeding on the ground that the Antiguan liquidators had not carried their burden of establishing Antigua as the debtor's COMI under the totality of the circumstances.

In re ABC Learning Centres Ltd. (2013)

The Third Circuit's decision in In re ABC Learning Centres Ltd., 728 F.3d 301 (3d Cir. 2013), addressed the Section 1506 public-policy exception in the context of an Australian liquidation. The court held that the Australian receivership did not offend U.S. public policy notwithstanding procedural and substantive differences from Chapter 7, reinforcing the narrow construction of Section 1506.

Procedural framework

A Chapter 15 case is commenced by filing the Section 1515 petition in the United States Bankruptcy Court for the district where the debtor has its principal place of business or principal assets in the United States, or, in the absence of either, in any district. See 28 U.S.C. Section 1410. The Federal Rules of Bankruptcy Procedure govern the proceeding generally, with Rule 1007(a)(4) and Rule 2002(q) supplying particular Chapter 15 requirements.

Recognition is a gate, not a result. The Section 1517 order does not dispose of the case or distribute assets. It is the threshold determination that opens the door to either automatic relief (under Section 1520, for a foreign main proceeding) or discretionary relief (under Section 1521, for either type of recognized proceeding). Post-recognition, the foreign representative typically returns to court to seek the specific relief needed to administer the debtor's U.S. assets.
