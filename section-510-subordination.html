11 U.S.C. Section 510 - Subordination of Claims and Interests

Three regimes for ranking claims behind others: contractual subordination agreements, mandatory subordination of securities-rescission claims, and the equitable subordination doctrine.

What Is Section 510?

Section 510 is the principal Bankruptcy Code provision authorizing the demotion of one claim or interest behind others in the distribution priority. It contains three independent regimes: (a) enforcement of pre-petition contractual subordination agreements; (b) mandatory subordination of claims arising from the purchase or sale of securities of the debtor or an affiliate; and (c) equitable subordination of allowed claims and interests based on misconduct.

Subordination is a powerful remedy: a subordinated claim retains its allowed status but is paid only after the senior class (or specified senior creditor) is paid in full. In an insolvent estate, subordination commonly means the subordinated creditor receives nothing.

Official citation: 11 U.S.C. § 510

Contractual Subordination: Section 510(a)

Section 510(a) provides: "A subordination agreement is enforceable in a case under this title to the same extent that such agreement is enforceable under applicable nonbankruptcy law." The Code thus respects intercreditor agreements rather than overriding them. A bondholder who has contractually agreed to be paid only after senior bondholders is held to that bargain in bankruptcy.

Common forms include intercreditor agreements between senior and subordinated lenders, subordinated indenture provisions in publicly issued debt, and turnover provisions requiring junior creditors to deliver any received distributions to senior creditors until the senior debt is paid in full. Disputes typically center on the scope of the senior debt, the trigger for payment turnover, and the enforceability of voting waivers under Section 1126.

Securities Subordination: Section 510(b)

Section 510(b) imposes mandatory subordination of claims arising from "the rescission of a purchase or sale of a security of the debtor or of an affiliate of the debtor, for damages arising from the purchase or sale of such a security, or for reimbursement or contribution allowed under section 502 on account of such a claim." Such claims are subordinated to all claims or interests senior to or equal to the claim or interest represented by the security; if the security is common stock, the subordinated claim has the same priority as common stock (i.e., last).

The policy rationale is that equity investors should not be able to convert their risk-bearing investment into a creditor claim through securities-fraud or rescission theories. Allowing pari passu treatment would let shareholders share in the recovery pool ahead of creditors who never bore the equity risk in the first place. The leading appellate authority (most often cited from In re Telegroup, Inc., 281 F.3d 133 (3d Cir. 2002)) construes 510(b) broadly to capture indirect damages claims and breach-of-contract claims tied to the issuance.

Equitable Subordination: Section 510(c)

Section 510(c) codifies the equitable-subordination doctrine: "Notwithstanding subsections (a) and (b) of this section, after notice and a hearing, the court may - (1) under principles of equitable subordination, subordinate for purposes of distribution all or part of an allowed claim to all or part of another allowed claim or all or part of an allowed interest to all or part of another allowed interest; or (2) order that any lien securing such a subordinated claim be transferred to the estate."

The classic articulation, from In re Mobile Steel Co., 563 F.2d 692 (5th Cir. 1977) - widely followed in other circuits - requires three findings:

  1. The claimant must have engaged in some type of inequitable conduct;
  2. The misconduct must have resulted in injury to the creditors of the bankrupt or conferred an unfair advantage on the claimant; and
  3. Equitable subordination of the claim must not be inconsistent with the provisions of the Bankruptcy Code.

For insiders (officers, directors, controlling shareholders, affiliates), courts apply heightened scrutiny to their dealings with the debtor. For non-insiders, the standard is significantly more demanding, generally requiring proof of gross misconduct akin to fraud, spoliation, or moral turpitude.

Recharacterization Distinguished

Equitable subordination should not be confused with recharacterization. Recharacterization treats what is labeled "debt" as equity from its inception - meaning the claim is not a claim at all, but an equity interest. Subordination, by contrast, accepts the claim as a claim but pushes it back in the priority queue. Recharacterization rests on factors developed in tax and corporate-law cases (capitalization, intent of the parties, source of repayment, identity of interests between lender and borrower, etc.), while subordination focuses on post-loan misconduct and resulting harm.

Some circuits authorize recharacterization under Section 105(a), others under Section 502(b)'s incorporation of state law. The Supreme Court has not resolved the split, and practitioners frequently plead recharacterization and equitable subordination as alternative or successive theories.

Common Procedural Postures

Subordination disputes typically arise in adversary proceedings under Federal Rule of Bankruptcy Procedure 7001(8) (proceedings to subordinate claims). Standing to seek equitable subordination is most clearly held by the trustee or debtor in possession; whether individual creditors have standing to assert derivative subordination claims depends on the jurisdiction and on whether the trustee has refused to act. Common factual settings include:

  • Insider loans made when the debtor was undercapitalized;
  • Insider claims arising from self-dealing transactions, dividend recapitalizations, or below-market guarantees;
  • Lender liability claims where the senior lender exercised excessive control over the debtor's operations;
  • Fraudulent-transfer-related claims where the recipient of a voidable transfer also holds a contract claim against the estate;
  • Rescission claims by recent purchasers of stock or notes alleging issuance fraud.
