KRPC 6.2 / MRPC 4-6.2: Accepting Appointments

The professional baseline of accepting tribunal appointments and the three categories of good cause that authorize a lawyer to seek to avoid an appointment.

Verbatim text of KRPC 6.2 (Kansas)

A lawyer shall not seek to avoid appointment by a tribunal to represent a person except for good cause, such as:
(a) representing the client is likely to result in violation of the Rules of Professional Conduct or other law;
(b) representing the client is likely to result in an unreasonable financial burden on the lawyer; or
(c) the client or the cause is so repugnant to the lawyer as to be likely to impair the client-lawyer relationship or the lawyer's ability to represent the client. Kan. Sup. Ct. R. 226, KRPC 6.2.

Missouri Rule 4-6.2

Missouri Rule 4-6.2 is substantively parallel to KRPC 6.2 and tracks ABA Model Rule 6.2. The rule frames the professional baseline of acceptance of tribunal appointments and identifies the categories of good cause that authorize a lawyer to seek to avoid a particular appointment.

The structural premise

The Comment to Model Rule 6.2 frames the underlying premise: legal representation is the principal mechanism through which the rights and interests of persons before tribunals are protected, and the responsibility to provide that representation is shared across the lawyers of the bar. Tribunal-appointment systems - whether under federal indigent-defense statutes, state public-defender systems, court-appointed-counsel rosters in civil matters, or court-appointed guardian-ad-litem arrangements - operate by distributing appointments among the lawyers eligible to receive them. The rule operates as the professional-conduct anchor for that distributive system.

The rule's affirmative orientation is that acceptance of appointment is the baseline professional posture. Lawyers in good standing whose practice profile makes them eligible for appointment under the operative appointment regime accept appointments as a routine incident of practice. The rule then identifies the categories of good cause that authorize departure from the baseline.

The "good cause" framework

The rule is structured as a baseline plus enumerated categories. The baseline directs that a lawyer shall not seek to avoid an appointment "except for good cause." The enumerated categories that follow - subdivisions (a), (b), and (c) - are introduced by "such as," indicating that they are illustrative rather than exhaustive. Each enumerated category, however, articulates a distinct ground recognized by the rule itself; other grounds may qualify as good cause where they are analogous in character and weight.

6.2(a) - Rule or law violation

The first enumerated ground for good cause is that the representation is likely to result in a violation of the Rules of Professional Conduct or other law. The category is the broadest of the three and encompasses several recurring fact patterns:

  • Conflict of interest under Rules 1.7 or 1.9. Where the appointment would place the lawyer in a position that violates the current-client or former-client conflict rules, the lawyer's representation would be precluded by those rules, and good cause is established for purposes of 6.2(a).
  • Lack of competence under Rule 1.1. Where the matter requires expertise the lawyer does not possess and cannot reasonably acquire within the time frame of the representation, accepting the appointment would risk a Rule 1.1 violation. The Comment to Model Rule 6.2 notes that mere unfamiliarity with the subject matter does not establish good cause if the lawyer can become competent within reasonable time and effort; the category is reserved for situations in which competence cannot be achieved.
  • Jurisdictional limitations under Rule 5.5. Where the appointment would require practice in a jurisdiction in which the lawyer is not authorized to practice and no safe harbor applies, good cause is established.
  • Other-law constraints. Where another body of law (federal court admission rules, state-licensure requirements for particular matter categories, specialized-practice authorization regimes) precludes the lawyer's participation, that external constraint qualifies under 6.2(a).

6.2(b) - Unreasonable financial burden

The second enumerated ground for good cause is that the representation is likely to result in an unreasonable financial burden on the lawyer. The category recognizes that appointed representation is often compensated below market rates - or, in some appointment regimes, on a pro bono or modest-honorarium basis - and that there is a point at which the financial impact of a particular appointment exceeds the baseline burden the lawyer accepts as part of the shared appointment system.

The standard is "unreasonable," not merely "burdensome." The Comment to Model Rule 6.2 frames the inquiry by reference to the magnitude of the financial impact on the lawyer's practice (a single substantial matter at compensation well below the lawyer's typical engagement may qualify; a routine appointment at standard appointment-roster compensation typically does not), the lawyer's overall appointment load (a lawyer who has recently completed several appointments may have a stronger case than a lawyer who has received few), and the resources of the lawyer or firm relative to the demands of the matter (a sole practitioner's response to a complex appointment differs from a large firm's).

6.2(c) - Impairing repugnance

The third enumerated ground for good cause is that the client or the cause is so repugnant to the lawyer as to be likely to impair the client-lawyer relationship or the lawyer's ability to represent the client. The category recognizes that effective representation depends on the lawyer's ability to advocate competently for the client's interests and that there is a threshold of personal disagreement beyond which that ability is materially impaired.

The standard incorporates two important qualifiers:

  • "So repugnant." The repugnance must be substantial. Ordinary distaste for a particular cause or client does not establish good cause; the rule contemplates a strong reaction that rises above the lawyer's normal range of acceptable representations.
  • "Likely to impair." The repugnance must be likely to impair the relationship or the representation. The inquiry is forward-looking and tied to the probable effect on the representation, not to the lawyer's preferences as such.

The Comment to Model Rule 6.2 emphasizes that representation of an unpopular client or cause is generally not by itself good cause under (c). The professional ethos the rule articulates is that lawyers regularly represent persons and causes that draw public criticism, and that doing so is part of the function the bar performs. Subdivision (c) is reserved for the narrower category in which the lawyer's personal reaction to the client or cause is so strong as to actually impair the representation.

The three categories are illustrative. The rule's "such as" phrasing recognizes that good cause may also be established by grounds not specifically enumerated. The illustrations provide guidance on the character and weight required; analogous grounds (e.g., severe illness, a non-conflict personal relationship to a witness or party of a kind that materially affects the lawyer's capacity) may qualify under the same standard.

The character of the appointment regime

The procedural framework through which appointments are made and through which a lawyer raises good cause is defined by the operative appointment regime rather than by Rule 6.2. Federal criminal appointments under the Criminal Justice Act, state public-defender systems, court-appointed counsel under bankruptcy provisions, court-appointed guardian-ad-litem regimes, and civil-appointment systems each have their own procedures for assignment, conflict-checking, and motion practice on appointment-avoidance grounds. Rule 6.2 provides the professional-conduct anchor for the lawyer's compliance with the operative regime's procedures.

Common procedural contexts

Conflict-based motion to be relieved

A lawyer receives an appointment in a matter that, on conflict check, reveals a current-client conflict under Rule 1.7. The lawyer promptly notifies the appointing tribunal under the operative procedure and moves to be relieved. The 6.2(a) ground (likely rule violation) is established by the conflict, and the tribunal's procedure provides the channel for substitution. The lawyer's professional-conduct posture under 6.2 is satisfied by raising the ground through the appropriate procedure rather than by declining the appointment unilaterally.

Capacity-based application for relief

A lawyer carrying a heavy appointment load receives a further appointment that, by virtue of its complexity and the lawyer's existing commitments, would constitute an unreasonable financial burden under 6.2(b). The lawyer applies to the appointing authority for relief on that ground, providing documentation of current load and the new matter's projected demands. The appointing authority evaluates the application under the operative regime's standards.

Relationship to the broader pro bono and access framework

Rule 6.2 sits within a larger framework that encourages voluntary pro bono service (addressed by Rule 6.1) and that authorizes a range of structured limited-services activities (addressed by Rules 6.3 and 6.5). The rule operates at the intersection of those voluntary frameworks and the formal appointment system: where appointment occurs through a tribunal under an operative appointment regime, Rule 6.2 governs the lawyer's professional-conduct response. Voluntary pro bono and structured limited-services activities are governed by their own respective rules and do not implicate the appointment-avoidance question that Rule 6.2 addresses.

Practical impact

Rule 6.2 carries several recurring practical consequences:

  • Acceptance is the baseline. Lawyers eligible for appointment under an operative appointment regime accept appointments as a routine incident of practice. The rule recognizes the appointment system as a function the bar shares.
  • Good cause runs through enumerated and analogous grounds. The three enumerated grounds - rule or law violation, unreasonable financial burden, impairing repugnance - articulate the character and weight the rule contemplates. Analogous grounds may qualify where they meet the same standard.
  • The procedural channel is provided by the operative appointment regime. Good cause is raised through the tribunal's procedure for appointment review. The rule's professional-conduct standard runs alongside that procedure.
