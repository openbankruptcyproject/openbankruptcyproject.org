KRPC 7.3 / MRPC 4-7.3: Direct Contact with Prospective Clients

The compliant channels for direct outreach: written and recorded communication, prior-relationship and institutional exceptions, and labeling conventions.

Verbatim text of KRPC 7.3 (Kansas)

(a) A lawyer shall not by in-person, live telephone or real-time electronic contact solicit professional employment from a prospective client when a significant motive for the lawyer's doing so is the lawyer's pecuniary gain, unless the person contacted: (1) is a lawyer; or (2) has a family, close personal, or prior professional relationship with the lawyer.

(b) A lawyer shall not solicit professional employment from a prospective client by written, recorded or electronic communication or by in-person, telephone or real-time electronic contact even when not otherwise prohibited by paragraph (a), if: (1) the prospective client has made known to the lawyer a desire not to be solicited by the lawyer; or (2) the solicitation involves coercion, duress or harassment.

(c) Every written, recorded or electronic communication from a lawyer soliciting professional employment from a prospective client known to be in need of legal services in a particular matter shall include the words "Advertising Material" on the outside envelope, if any, and at the beginning and ending of any recorded or electronic communication, unless the recipient of the communication is a person specified in paragraphs (a)(1) or (a)(2).

(d) Notwithstanding the prohibitions in paragraph (a), a lawyer may participate with a prepaid or group legal service plan operated by an organization not owned or directed by the lawyer that uses in-person or telephone contact to solicit memberships or subscriptions for the plan from persons who are not known to need legal services in a particular matter covered by the plan. Kan. Sup. Ct. R. 226, KRPC 7.3.

Missouri Rule 4-7.3

Missouri Rule 4-7.3 follows the same architecture with detailed Missouri-specific elaboration on written-communication labeling and the institutional exceptions. Both rules track ABA Model Rule 7.3. The 2018 revisions to the Model Rule reorganized terminology; jurisdictional adoptions vary.

The affirmative architecture: what 7.3 permits

Rule 7.3 is best read by inverting the conventional summary. The rule permits a broad universe of direct contact with prospective clients and identifies a narrow set of channels that carry heightened risk of overreach. Understanding the affirmative architecture clarifies what the rule actually does:

  • Written communication is broadly permitted. A lawyer may send a letter, an email, a postcard, a brochure, or other written material to a prospective client, subject to the labeling requirement in subdivision (c) and the consent-and-coercion limits in subdivision (b). Written communication is the rule's primary affirmative channel because the recipient can review and discard at the recipient's pace, without the pressure of a live interaction.
  • Recorded communication is broadly permitted. A recorded voicemail, an audio or video message, or a similar non-live recorded contact is permitted on the same terms as written communication.
  • Electronic non-real-time communication is broadly permitted. Email, asynchronous chat, and similar electronic channels that do not involve real-time live interaction are permitted on the same terms.
  • Live contact with specified categories is permitted. In-person, telephone, and real-time electronic contact is permitted with lawyers, with family members, with persons in a close personal relationship with the lawyer, and with persons with a prior professional relationship.
  • Group and prepaid plan outreach is permitted. Subdivision (d) explicitly authorizes lawyer participation with prepaid or group legal service plans operated by entities not owned or directed by the lawyer, including plans whose marketing involves direct contact with prospective plan members.

The narrow zone: live, pecuniary-motivated solicitation of strangers

The rule's central restriction in subdivision (a) reaches a specifically defined zone: in-person, live telephone, or real-time electronic contact, where a significant motive is pecuniary gain, with persons who do not fall within the lawyer-relationship, family, close-personal, or prior-professional-relationship categories. Each element is necessary:

  • Live interaction: The rule's concern is the live-interaction pressure of in-person, real-time telephone, or real-time electronic contact. Written, recorded, and asynchronous electronic contact is not live and is not within (a).
  • Pecuniary motive: The rule applies where a significant motive is pecuniary gain. Contact motivated by genuine pro bono interest, public-service intent, or non-pecuniary professional concern is outside (a).
  • Stranger recipient: The rule applies to persons outside the enumerated relationship categories. Contact with lawyers, family, close personal contacts, and prior-professional-relationship contacts is outside (a).

Conditions for written, recorded, and electronic communication

Written, recorded, and electronic communication is permitted as a direct-outreach channel and is subject to two structural conditions:

The labeling convention

Communications directed to a prospective client known to be in need of legal services in a particular matter must include the words "Advertising Material" on the outside envelope (for mailed materials) and at the beginning and ending of recorded or electronic communications. The labeling requirement:

  • Signals to the recipient that the material is solicitation rather than personal correspondence
  • Permits the recipient to discard the communication unopened if not interested
  • Creates a clear regulatory marker that distinguishes the communication from non-solicitation contact

The labeling requirement does not apply to communications with the four enumerated relationship categories (lawyers, family, close personal, prior professional), reflecting the rule's judgment that the heightened-context value of the label is not needed within those relationships.

Consent and non-harassment

Subdivision (b) imposes two cross-cutting limits that apply to written, recorded, electronic, and live contact alike:

  • Communication must cease if the prospective client has indicated a desire not to be solicited by the lawyer.
  • Communication must not involve coercion, duress, or harassment regardless of channel.

These limits are channel-neutral baselines; they apply whether the communication is a single letter or a sustained outreach campaign.

The relationship exceptions to live contact

Subdivision (a)'s carve-outs identify four categories where live contact is permitted because the existing relationship supplies the contextual protection the rule otherwise relies on the medium to provide:

Lawyer-to-lawyer contact

A lawyer may directly contact another lawyer to discuss potential representation, co-counsel arrangements, referrals, or specialty consultations. The lawyer recipient possesses the training to evaluate the proposal without protection from live-interaction pressure.

Family and close personal relationships

A lawyer may directly contact a family member or a person in a close personal relationship to offer representation. The existing personal relationship supplies the contextual familiarity that makes the live contact appropriate.

Prior professional relationships

A lawyer may directly contact a person with whom the lawyer has a prior professional relationship. This category includes former clients, persons the lawyer has previously represented in unrelated matters, persons who have consulted the lawyer in a professional capacity (even where no formal representation followed), and similar pre-existing professional ties. The exception affirmatively enables continuity of relationship: a former client who later faces a new matter can be contacted by the lawyer who previously represented them.

Institutional channels: prepaid and group legal service plans

Subdivision (d) is the affirmative authorization for lawyer participation with prepaid and group legal service plans. The structural elements:

  • The plan must be operated by an organization not owned or directed by the lawyer (the plan must be an independent organizational structure, not a marketing wrapper for the lawyer's practice).
  • The plan may use in-person or telephone contact to solicit memberships or subscriptions.
  • The contact must be for plan enrollment, not for representation in a particular matter the prospective member is known to need.

The exception affirmatively enables a substantial category of legal-services delivery: employer-sponsored group plans, union legal benefits, organization-affiliated plans, prepaid consumer plans, and similar arrangements that make routine legal services available to broad populations through a subscription model.

Court-appointed and lawyer-referral routing

Direct contact in connection with court appointments and lawyer-referral-service routing operates outside the (a) restriction in practice, because contact through those channels is initiated by the appointing or referring entity rather than by the lawyer's direct solicitation. A lawyer who accepts a court appointment to represent an indigent defendant, or who receives a referral through a qualified bar referral service, and who then contacts the new client to initiate representation, is not making the kind of "in-person solicitation" the rule addresses; the lawyer is making contact with a client whose engagement was initiated by an institutional process.

Two pattern situations

Mailing to public-record contacts

A lawyer who learns from public records that a person is facing a particular legal situation (e.g., a court filing, a property action, a published notice) and who wishes to offer representation may send a written communication. The communication must comply with Rule 7.1's truthful, non-misleading standard, must include the "Advertising Material" label on the envelope and (if relevant) in the communication, must comply with the consent-and-non-harassment limits of 7.3(b), and must include the name-and-address attribution of Rule 7.2(c). The written-mailing channel is the rule's principal affirmative pathway for outreach to identified prospects.

Reconnection with former client

A lawyer who represented a client in a closed matter and who later wishes to offer representation in a new matter is squarely within the prior-professional-relationship exception. The lawyer may telephone, email, or meet the former client to discuss the new matter without 7.3(a) restriction. The (b) consent-and-non-harassment limits still apply, and the lawyer must respect any prior request by the client not to be contacted.

The role of the labeling convention

The "Advertising Material" label is the structural counterpart to the asynchronous channel. Written, recorded, and electronic communications are broadly permitted in part because the recipient retains control over the interaction. The label reinforces that control by giving the recipient an immediate signal to evaluate, accept, or discard the communication. The label converts the communication into a clearly identified outreach the recipient can engage with on the recipient's own terms.

Online forms and inbound solicitation

Rule 7.3 governs lawyer-initiated direct contact with prospective clients. It does not generally reach inbound contact: a prospective client who completes a contact form on the lawyer's website, sends an email inquiry, calls the lawyer's office, or initiates a chat with the firm's website chat function is the initiating party, and the lawyer's responsive communication is not "solicitation" within the rule. The rule's concern is the live-interaction pressure of lawyer-initiated stranger outreach, not the lawyer's response to prospective-client inquiry.

Practical impact

Rule 7.3 has often been described in terms of what it prohibits; read carefully it is better understood by what it permits. Two recurring observations:

  • Written, recorded, and electronic outreach is broadly available. Lawyers may engage in substantial direct outreach to prospective clients through asynchronous channels, subject to truthful content, attribution, and the "Advertising Material" labeling convention. The rule does not restrict the volume or reach of properly labeled written outreach.
  • The relationship and institutional exceptions enable continuity and access. The prior-professional-relationship exception preserves continuity of representation across matters; the prepaid and group plan authorization opens access for populations served through subscription models; the court-appointment and qualified-referral pathways operate outside the (a) restriction in practice.
