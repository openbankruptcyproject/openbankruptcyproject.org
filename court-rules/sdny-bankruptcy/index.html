U.S. Bankruptcy Court for the Southern District of New York - Local Rules Guide 2026

First published 2026-05-22/Last modified 2026-05-22/CC-BY-4.0

Abstract

The United States Bankruptcy Court for the Southern District of New York (Bankr. S.D.N.Y.) is one of the busiest and most procedurally sophisticated bankruptcy courts in the United States, with a long-standing concentration of large Chapter 11 reorganizations and cross-border insolvency proceedings under Chapter 15. The court's local rules are codified as the Local Bankruptcy Rules for the Southern District of New York (S.D.N.Y. L.B.R. 1001-1 through 9077-1) and are supplemented by an extensive body of general orders and judge-specific case management procedures. This guide summarizes the local-rule features that most often diverge from federal default practice.

Court Identification and Coverage

The Southern District of New York comprises the New York counties of New York (Manhattan), Bronx, Westchester, Rockland, Putnam, Orange, Dutchess, and Sullivan. The bankruptcy court maintains divisional offices at:

  • Manhattan - Alexander Hamilton U.S. Custom House, One Bowling Green, New York, NY 10004. Principal courthouse and the venue for most large Chapter 11 filings.
  • White Plains - 300 Quarropas Street, White Plains, NY 10601. Serves Westchester and the lower Hudson Valley.
  • Poughkeepsie - 355 Main Street, Poughkeepsie, NY 12601. Serves the mid-Hudson Valley.

The court is staffed by Article I bankruptcy judges appointed under 28 U.S.C. § 152 by the United States Court of Appeals for the Second Circuit. The chief-judge designation and complete judicial roster are published on the court's website.

Local Rule Numbering Scheme

The court uses the standard four-digit scheme keyed to the Federal Rules of Bankruptcy Procedure:

  • L.B.R. 1007-2 - affidavit pursuant to local rule (the "First Day" affidavit standard in Chapter 11)
  • L.B.R. 2014-1 - employment of professionals
  • L.B.R. 2016-1 - compensation of professional persons
  • L.B.R. 2016-2 - U.S. Trustee guidelines and fee committee procedures (in larger cases)
  • L.B.R. 2090-1 - attorneys; admission to practice
  • L.B.R. 9013-1 - motions, applications, objections, responses
  • L.B.R. 9037-1 - redaction and restriction of public access

Attorney Admission: L.B.R. 2090-1

Local Bankruptcy Rule 2090-1 governs attorney admission to practice before the court. The rule provides two categories of admission:

  • Standing admission. An attorney admitted to practice in the United States District Court for the Southern District of New York is automatically admitted to practice in the bankruptcy court.
  • Pro hac vice admission. An attorney admitted to practice and in good standing in another United States court (district court, court of appeals, or state court of last resort) may be admitted to practice in a particular case on motion filed by an attorney of record under L.B.R. 9077-1.

The court requires that each attorney appearing in a case maintain an individual CM/ECF login. Pro hac vice admission requires the sponsoring attorney to remain available for service and responsible for compliance with local rules.

Motion Practice and Hearings

L.B.R. 9013-1 prescribes the form and content of motions and objections, including:

  • Required components: notice of motion, motion or application, supporting affidavit or declaration, memorandum of law (in any motion involving disputed questions of law), and a proposed order.
  • A standard notice period of 14 days for hearings on motions (subject to particular rule modifications and to FRBP 2002 notice requirements).
  • Specific requirements for "First Day" pleadings in Chapter 11, including the L.B.R. 1007-2 affidavit by an officer of the debtor.
  • Judge-specific chambers procedures published on the court's website that supplement (and in some respects override) generic motion-practice procedures.

Hearings in large Chapter 11 cases are typically scheduled as omnibus hearing dates set by the court in the initial case management order. Routine matters proceed on negative notice when properly noticed under FRBP 2002 and the applicable judge's procedures. Telephonic and video appearance is available for non-evidentiary hearings.

Complex Chapter 11 Procedures

The Bankr. S.D.N.Y. has developed extensive procedures for "complex" or "mega" Chapter 11 cases that are routinely incorporated through initial case-management and procedural orders. Common features include:

  • Omnibus hearing dates fixed in advance for the duration of the case.
  • Approval of streamlined notice and service procedures, often through a court-approved claims and noticing agent.
  • "First Day" relief packages addressing cash management, employee compensation, taxes, utilities, and critical vendor payments.
  • Fee committees or fee examiners appointed under standing or case-specific orders to review professional compensation applications in cases over a designated threshold.
  • Case-specific protective orders governing discovery and confidential information.

These procedures are not codified as local rules but are routinely entered through orders that have over time hardened into a recognized "S.D.N.Y. complex case protocol." Counsel should review the assigned judge's chambers procedures and recent large-case orders to understand the protocol's current contours.

Fee Disclosure and Compensation Procedures

L.B.R. 2016-1 supplements FRBP 2016 by prescribing the form and content of compensation applications. Applications must comply with the U.S. Trustee's compensation guidelines and, in large cases, with the local "Fee Committee Order" if one has been entered. The S.D.N.Y. court is one of the leading users of fee examiners and fee committees, and counsel filing in a complex case should anticipate independent review of monthly and interim compensation requests.

FRBP 2016(b) supplemental-disclosure obligations apply with full force; the court enforces strictly the obligation to disclose any change in compensation arrangements within 14 days of the change.

Electronic Filing (CM/ECF)

All documents must be filed electronically through CM/ECF except those specifically excluded by general order or judge-specific procedure. The court's electronic filing system is at ecf.nysb.uscourts.gov. Document size is capped at the court's published ECF limit per upload; oversized exhibits must be filed in compliant segments. Filings must comply with FRBP 9037 redaction requirements, and L.B.R. 9037-1 supplements the federal rule with district-specific procedures for sealing and restricting public access.

General Orders

The court maintains an extensive catalogue of General Orders ranging from M-100-series administrative orders to more recent M-500-series substantive orders. Notable categories include:

  • General Orders governing electronic filing requirements and CM/ECF user obligations
  • General Orders implementing fee guidelines and fee-committee procedures
  • General Orders on Chapter 13 administration and uniform plan provisions
  • General Orders implementing Federal Rule changes at the district level
  • Case-management procedures adopted as default approaches in complex cases

The complete catalogue is posted on the court's website. Counsel should consult the current catalogue and the assigned judge's chambers procedures before relying on any federal default procedure.

Practical Impact for Counsel

Three features of S.D.N.Y. practice most often surprise counsel from other districts:

The L.B.R. 1007-2 affidavit is mandatory in Chapter 11. Every Chapter 11 case filed in the district requires an affidavit by a debtor representative on the first day setting forth the structure of the debtor's businesses, the circumstances leading to the filing, and the relief sought on a first-day basis. The affidavit's content and structure are prescribed in detail by L.B.R. 1007-2.

Judge-specific chambers procedures matter as much as the local rules. Each judge of the S.D.N.Y. publishes chambers procedures that supplement (and sometimes modify) generic local-rule procedures, including specific requirements for proposed orders, hearing requests, and motion-practice format. Counsel should review the assigned judge's procedures at the outset of any case.

Fee committees and fee examiners are common in large cases. Unlike many districts where U.S. Trustee review is the principal compensation check, S.D.N.Y. large cases routinely include an independent fee committee or fee examiner empowered to review and negotiate fee applications. Counsel should plan for the additional review layer and the associated time and cost.
