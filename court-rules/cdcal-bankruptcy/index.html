U.S. Bankruptcy Court for the Central District of California - Local Rules Guide 2026

First published 2026-05-22/Last modified 2026-05-22/CC-BY-4.0

Abstract

The United States Bankruptcy Court for the Central District of California (Bankr. C.D. Cal.) is the largest bankruptcy court in the United States by case volume. The district encompasses the bulk of the Los Angeles metropolitan area and the Inland Empire. The court's local rules are codified as the Local Bankruptcy Rules for the Central District of California (LBR 1001-1 through LBR 9075-1) and are unusually detailed in their integration with mandatory court-approved form orders. This guide summarizes the local-rule features that most often diverge from federal default practice.

Court Identification and Coverage

The Central District of California comprises Los Angeles, Orange, Riverside, San Bernardino, Santa Barbara, San Luis Obispo, and Ventura counties. The bankruptcy court maintains five divisional offices:

  • Los Angeles Division - Edward R. Roybal Federal Building and U.S. Courthouse, 255 East Temple Street, Los Angeles, CA 90012.
  • Santa Ana Division - Ronald Reagan Federal Building, 411 West Fourth Street, Santa Ana, CA 92701.
  • San Fernando Valley Division - 21041 Burbank Boulevard, Woodland Hills, CA 91367.
  • Riverside Division - 3420 Twelfth Street, Riverside, CA 92501.
  • Northern Division - 1415 State Street, Santa Barbara, CA 93101.

Bankruptcy judges are Article I judges appointed under 28 U.S.C. § 152 to fourteen-year terms by the United States Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit. The court's chief-judge designation and full judicial roster are published on its website.

Local Rule Numbering Scheme

The court uses the standard four-digit scheme keyed to the Federal Rules of Bankruptcy Procedure, with court-approved form orders identified by an "F" prefix. Representative rules:

  • LBR 1002-1 - commencement of case (with mandatory case-cover sheet)
  • LBR 2014-1 - employment of professionals
  • LBR 2016-1 - compensation of professionals
  • LBR 2090-1 - attorneys; admission to practice
  • LBR 2091-1 - withdrawal of attorney
  • LBR 9013-1 - motions and applications
  • LBR 9013-3 - notice of motion - mandatory form requirement
  • LBR 9037-1 - redaction and restriction of public access

The district is distinctive in maintaining a comprehensive set of court-approved mandatory and optional forms (the "F" forms), many of which counsel must use in lieu of attorney-drafted versions. Most motions, notices, and proposed orders in routine matters must use the form prescribed by the court.

Attorney Admission: LBR 2090-1

LBR 2090-1 governs attorney admission to practice before the court. The rule provides:

  • Standing admission. An attorney admitted to practice in the United States District Court for the Central District of California is automatically admitted to practice in the bankruptcy court.
  • Pro hac vice admission. An attorney admitted to practice and in good standing in another United States court may apply for pro hac vice admission in a particular case under C.D. Cal. L.R. 83-2.1 (district court rule), through a sponsoring attorney of record.

The court requires each attorney appearing in a case to maintain an individual CM/ECF login. The sponsoring attorney in a pro hac vice admission remains responsible for compliance with local rules.

Motion Practice and Hearings

LBR 9013-1 prescribes the form and content of motions; LBR 9013-3 imposes a mandatory notice-of-motion form requirement. The district is unusually rigorous in enforcing form compliance: a noncompliant motion may be denied or set aside even where the substance of the relief sought is unobjectionable.

Hearings are scheduled on the assigned judge's calendar. Each judge publishes self-calendaring rules and posted hearing dates; counsel select an available date and notice the motion accordingly. Negative-notice procedures are common for routine matters. Default notice period is 21 days under FRBP 9014, modified by particular rules and judge-specific procedures.

The court was an early and aggressive adopter of remote-appearance technology. Most non-evidentiary hearings are conducted by Zoom or telephone; in-person attendance is required only when the assigned judge directs it. Evidentiary hearings and contested confirmation hearings are typically conducted in person at the appropriate divisional courthouse.

The Court-Approved Form System

The C.D. Cal. mandatory-form system is the district's most distinctive procedural feature. Court-approved forms are designated with an "F" prefix (e.g., F 9013-1.1.MOTION) and are posted on the court's website. Counsel must use:

  • Mandatory forms for filings where the local rules specifically require the court-approved form (e.g., motions for relief from stay, motions to extend time, certain notices).
  • Optional forms where the court provides a form that counsel may use but is not required to use, typically for routine matters.

The mandatory-form regime substantially reduces the variance in motion-practice content across the district. It also imposes a significant initial training cost for counsel new to practice in the district. The forms catalogue is updated periodically; counsel should download the current version from the court's website rather than reusing prior-case templates.

Withdrawal of Attorney: LBR 2091-1

LBR 2091-1 governs withdrawal of an attorney's appearance. The rule requires:

  • A motion served on the client and on all parties who have entered an appearance.
  • Compliance with the court-approved mandatory form for withdrawal notices and motions.
  • Court order before withdrawal becomes effective.
  • For artificial-entity clients (corporations, LLCs, partnerships), substitution of successor counsel before withdrawal is granted, consistent with the federal common-law rule that artificial entities cannot proceed pro se.

The rule applies to the individual attorney's appearance. A firm's employment under 11 U.S.C. § 327, where applicable, is separately governed and separately terminable by order modifying or vacating the employment order.

Fee Disclosure and Compensation

LBR 2014-1 supplements FRBP 2014 by requiring a verified statement disclosing all connections to parties in interest, the debtor, the United States Trustee, and any person employed in the office of the United States Trustee. Compensation applications under LBR 2016-1 must comply with U.S. Trustee guidelines and the court-approved mandatory form for fee applications. The court enforces fee-application formatting requirements strictly; noncompliant applications may be set aside.

FRBP 2016(b) supplemental-disclosure obligations apply with full force. The "Rights and Responsibilities" agreement is required in Chapter 13 cases between debtor and debtor's counsel, governing the scope of services and fees.

Electronic Filing (CM/ECF)

All documents must be filed electronically through CM/ECF except those specifically excluded by standing order. The court's electronic filing system is at ecf.cacb.uscourts.gov. Document size is capped at the court's published ECF limit per upload. Filings must comply with FRBP 9037 redaction requirements, supplemented by LBR 9037-1 procedures for restricted public access.

General Orders

The court maintains a catalogue of General Orders covering recurring procedural matters and case-administration issues. Active categories include:

  • General Orders governing electronic filing and remote-appearance procedures
  • General Orders implementing Chapter 13 administration and the Rights and Responsibilities agreement
  • General Orders adopting and updating the mandatory court-approved forms
  • General Orders implementing Federal Rule changes at the district level
  • Judge-specific procedures posted on the court's website

The complete list of General Orders and judge-specific procedures is on the court's website. Counsel should consult the current catalogue before relying on any federal default procedure.

Practical Impact for Counsel

Three features of C.D. Cal. bankruptcy practice most often surprise counsel from other districts:

Mandatory form compliance is dispositive. The court's mandatory-form regime is rigorously enforced. Substantively correct motions filed on noncompliant forms can be denied or set aside, regardless of the merits. Counsel must obtain and use the current "F" forms from the court website for every filing where a mandatory form exists.

Each judge's self-calendaring procedures vary. The court does not centrally assign hearing dates. Counsel select an available date from the assigned judge's posted calendar and notice the motion for that date. The judge-specific calendar conventions and self-calendaring rules are essential reading at the outset of any case.

Ninth Circuit Bankruptcy Appellate Panel review available. Unlike many circuits, the Ninth Circuit maintains a Bankruptcy Appellate Panel. Appeals from the C.D. Cal. bankruptcy court may be taken either to the Ninth Circuit BAP or to the U.S. District Court for the Central District of California under 28 U.S.C. § 158(c). The choice is made by election under the rules; the appellee may consent or opt out.
