U.S. Bankruptcy Court for the Southern District of Texas - Local Rules Guide 2026

First published 2026-05-22/Last modified 2026-05-22/CC-BY-4.0

Abstract

The United States Bankruptcy Court for the Southern District of Texas (Bankr. S.D. Tex.) became one of the leading venues for large Chapter 11 cases in the United States over the past decade. The court's Complex Chapter 11 Procedures and the Houston Division's specialized complex-case panel attract energy, healthcare, and retail debtors from across the country. The local rules are codified as the Bankruptcy Local Rules for the Southern District of Texas (BLR 1001-1 through BLR 9077-1) and are supplemented by General Orders and the published Complex Chapter 11 Procedures. This guide summarizes the local-rule features that most often diverge from federal default practice.

Court Identification and Coverage

The Southern District of Texas comprises 43 counties in southeast Texas, including the Houston, Galveston, Brownsville, Corpus Christi, McAllen, and Laredo metropolitan areas. The bankruptcy court maintains divisional offices at:

  • Houston Division - 515 Rusk Avenue, Houston, TX 77002. Principal courthouse and venue for the bulk of the district's complex Chapter 11 filings.
  • Corpus Christi Division - 1133 N. Shoreline Boulevard, Corpus Christi, TX 78401.
  • Brownsville Division - 600 East Harrison Street, Brownsville, TX 78520.
  • McAllen Division - 1701 W. Business Highway 83, McAllen, TX 78501.
  • Galveston Division - 601 Rosenberg Street, Galveston, TX 77550.
  • Laredo Division - 1300 Victoria Street, Laredo, TX 78040.

Bankruptcy judges are Article I judges appointed under 28 U.S.C. § 152 to fourteen-year terms by the United States Court of Appeals for the Fifth Circuit. The court's chief-judge designation and judicial roster are published on its website.

Local Rule Numbering Scheme

The court uses the four-digit scheme keyed to the Federal Rules of Bankruptcy Procedure, designated "BLR":

  • BLR 1007-1 - schedules, statements, and lists
  • BLR 2014-1 - employment of professionals
  • BLR 2016-1 - compensation of professionals
  • BLR 2090-1 - attorneys; admission to practice
  • BLR 2091-1 - withdrawal of appearance
  • BLR 9013-1 - motion practice
  • BLR 9037-1 - redaction and restriction of public access

The court's Complex Chapter 11 Procedures are not codified as numbered local rules but operate as a standing operating framework adopted in case-management orders issued at the outset of qualifying cases.

Attorney Admission: BLR 2090-1

BLR 2090-1 governs attorney admission to practice. The rule provides:

  • Standing admission. An attorney admitted to practice in the United States District Court for the Southern District of Texas is automatically admitted to practice in the bankruptcy court.
  • Pro hac vice admission. An attorney admitted to practice and in good standing in another United States court may apply for pro hac vice admission in a particular case through a sponsoring local counsel of record, under the district court's pro hac vice rules.

Each attorney must maintain an individual CM/ECF login. Sponsoring local counsel in a pro hac vice admission remains responsible for compliance with local rules throughout the engagement.

Motion Practice and Hearings

BLR 9013-1 prescribes the form and content of motions. Motions must state with particularity the grounds for relief, attach a proposed order, and be set for hearing on the assigned judge's calendar with notice consistent with FRBP 2002. Many routine matters proceed on negative notice when properly noticed; the court's procedural orders identify specific motion categories eligible for negative-notice treatment.

The Houston Division is unusually flexible with hearing scheduling, particularly in complex Chapter 11 cases, where omnibus hearing dates are typically set in advance and "first day" relief is heard on an expedited basis. Telephonic and video appearance is available for non-evidentiary hearings; contested evidentiary hearings and confirmation hearings are typically in person.

Complex Chapter 11 Procedures

The Bankr. S.D. Tex. is one of the leading venues in the country for large Chapter 11 cases. The court's published Procedures for Complex Chapter 11 Cases (the "Complex Case Procedures") govern qualifying cases. A case qualifies as "complex" if it meets the criteria set forth in the Complex Case Procedures (typically, debt above a threshold, public securities, or other indicia of complexity). On qualification:

  • The case is assigned to a judge from the court's designated complex-case panel.
  • An initial case-management order is entered incorporating omnibus hearing dates, streamlined notice procedures, and modified motion-practice requirements.
  • The case typically uses an approved claims and noticing agent.
  • "First day" relief - cash management, employee compensation, taxes, utilities, critical vendors - is heard on an expedited basis.
  • Fee committees or fee examiners may be appointed for independent compensation review.

The Houston Division panel of judges experienced in complex Chapter 11 reorganizations has been a principal draw for large filings. The Complex Case Procedures are posted on the court's website and are periodically updated.

Withdrawal of Appearance: BLR 2091-1

BLR 2091-1 governs withdrawal of an attorney's appearance. The rule requires:

  • A motion served on the client and on all parties who have entered an appearance.
  • Court order before the withdrawal becomes effective.
  • In cases where the client is an artificial entity (corporation, LLC, partnership), substitution of successor counsel before withdrawal is granted, consistent with the federal common-law rule that artificial entities cannot proceed pro se.

The rule applies to the individual attorney's appearance. A firm's employment under 11 U.S.C. § 327, where applicable, is separately governed by the order of employment and is separately terminable by order modifying or vacating the employment order.

Fee Disclosure and Compensation

BLR 2014-1 supplements FRBP 2014 by requiring a verified statement disclosing all connections to parties in interest, the debtor, the United States Trustee, and any person employed in the office of the United States Trustee. The court applies the disclosure obligation strictly and expects supplemental disclosures upon any change.

Compensation applications under BLR 2016-1 must comply with the U.S. Trustee's compensation guidelines and itemize time entries with sufficient particularity to permit independent review. In complex Chapter 11 cases, compensation procedures often include a fee committee or fee examiner under the initial case-management order. The court enforces the U.S. Trustee's billing guidelines and any case-specific compensation procedures alike.

FRBP 2016(b) supplemental-disclosure obligations apply with full force across all chapters; the court enforces strictly the obligation to disclose any subsequent compensation arrangement within 14 days.

Electronic Filing (CM/ECF)

All documents must be filed electronically through CM/ECF except those specifically excluded by general order. The court's electronic filing system is at ecf.txsb.uscourts.gov. Document size is capped at the court's published ECF limit per upload. Filings must comply with FRBP 9037 redaction requirements; BLR 9037-1 supplements with district-specific procedures for restricting public access and sealing.

General Orders

The court maintains a catalogue of General Orders addressing recurring procedural matters and case-administration issues. Active categories include:

  • General Orders adopting and updating the Complex Chapter 11 Procedures
  • General Orders governing electronic filing and CM/ECF use
  • General Orders implementing Chapter 13 administration
  • General Orders implementing Federal Rule changes at the district level
  • Judge-specific case-management orders that serve as the operative framework in complex cases

The complete catalogue is on the court's website. Counsel should consult both the current General Orders and the assigned judge's recent case-management orders before relying on any federal default procedure.

Practical Impact for Counsel

Three features of S.D. Tex. bankruptcy practice most often surprise counsel from other districts:

The Complex Case Procedures dominate large-case practice. The published procedures, supplemented by initial case-management orders, materially modify routine motion-practice requirements, notice periods, and hearing-calendar mechanics in qualifying cases. Counsel filing in a complex case should treat the Complex Case Procedures as the operative procedural framework alongside the BLRs.

Houston Division concentration of complex-case judges. The court's complex-case panel sits primarily in Houston. Counsel filing complex cases in other divisions should anticipate the possibility of transfer or coordinated case management with the Houston complex-case judges.

Fifth Circuit has no Bankruptcy Appellate Panel. Bankruptcy appeals are taken to the U.S. District Court for the Southern District of Texas under 28 U.S.C. § 158(a), with further review available in the Fifth Circuit Court of Appeals. There is no BAP option.
