U.S. Bankruptcy Court for the District of Kansas - Local Rules Guide 2026

First published 2026-05-22/Last modified 2026-05-22/CC-BY-4.0

Abstract

The United States Bankruptcy Court for the District of Kansas (D. Kan. Bankr.) sits as a unit of the United States District Court for the District of Kansas under 28 U.S.C. § 151. Its local bankruptcy rules are codified in a four-digit scheme keyed to the Federal Rules of Bankruptcy Procedure (D. Kan. Bankr. L.R. 1001.1 through 9037.1) and supplemented by standing orders posted on the court's website. This guide summarizes the local-rule features that most often diverge from federal default practice in the district.

Court Identification and Coverage

The District of Kansas is a single-district federal jurisdiction encompassing the entire State of Kansas (105 counties). The bankruptcy court maintains divisional offices at:

  • Topeka - Frank Carlson Federal Building, 444 SE Quincy, Topeka, KS 66683. Principal courthouse and clerk's office for the central and northeastern divisions.
  • Kansas City, Kansas - Robert J. Dole United States Courthouse, 500 State Avenue, Kansas City, KS 66101. Serves the Wyandotte and Johnson County metropolitan area.
  • Wichita - U.S. Courthouse, 401 N. Market Street, Wichita, KS 67202. Serves south-central Kansas.

The court is staffed by Article I bankruptcy judges appointed under 28 U.S.C. § 152 to fourteen-year terms by the United States Court of Appeals for the Tenth Circuit. The current chief-judge designation and full judicial roster are published on the court's website.

Local Rule Numbering Scheme

The District of Kansas uses the four-digit scheme keyed to the Federal Rules of Bankruptcy Procedure, with a period rather than a hyphen separating the federal-rule digits from the subsection number. Representative rules:

  • D. Kan. Bankr. L.R. 1007.1 - schedules, statements, and lists
  • D. Kan. Bankr. L.R. 2014.1 - employment of professionals
  • D. Kan. Bankr. L.R. 2016.1 - compensation of professionals
  • D. Kan. Bankr. L.R. 9010.1 - representation, entry and withdrawal of appearance
  • D. Kan. Bankr. L.R. 9013.1 - motion practice
  • D. Kan. Bankr. L.R. 9037.1 - redaction and restriction of public access

A small set of legacy three-digit numbers persists for general administrative provisions. Where a federal rule has no local counterpart, the federal rule controls.

Attorney Admission and Bar Status

Practice in the D. Kan. Bankruptcy Court requires admission to the bar of the United States District Court for the District of Kansas. Admission rules are governed by D. Kan. Rule 83.5 of the district court's local rules. Standing admission is open to attorneys licensed and in good standing with the Supreme Court of Kansas, with reciprocal admission available to out-of-state attorneys subject to the conditions in Rule 83.5. Pro hac vice admission is available on motion of local counsel of record, who must remain available throughout the engagement.

Each attorney filing electronically must maintain an individual CM/ECF account. Shared logins are prohibited and may result in administrative discipline.

Motion Practice and Hearings

Local Rule 9013.1 supplements Federal Rule of Bankruptcy Procedure 9013 by requiring motions to state with particularity the grounds for the relief requested and to be accompanied by a proposed order. The District of Kansas has historically used a "Notice of Opportunity for Hearing" mechanism that combines the motion and notice into a single filing for matters that may be granted without hearing absent objection.

The standard objection period for negative-notice matters is twenty-one (21) days under FRBP 9014, with shorter periods authorized for specific categories by standing order. The court permits telephonic and video appearance for non-evidentiary hearings; contested evidentiary hearings are typically conducted in person at the appropriate divisional courthouse.

Chapter 13 cases are heard on calendar at the divisional courthouses according to a regular rotation. The court has approved a uniform Chapter 13 plan form and a uniform confirmation order form, both posted on the website. Counsel filing a non-standard plan must explain the deviation in the body of the plan or in a separate motion.

Withdrawal of Appearance: D. Kan. Bankr. L.R. 9010.1

The District of Kansas combines its representation, entry, and withdrawal provisions in a single local rule, D. Kan. Bankr. L.R. 9010.1. The withdrawal provisions impose three operative requirements that parallel the practice in most federal bankruptcy courts:

  • Withdrawal of a counsel of record requires leave of court, granted on motion.
  • The motion must be served on the client and on all parties who have entered an appearance.
  • Where the client is a corporation, partnership, limited liability company, or other artificial entity, the order granting withdrawal will typically be conditioned on the entry of successor counsel, because such entities cannot proceed pro se in federal court.

The successor-counsel requirement traces to the federal common-law rule that artificial entities must appear through licensed counsel. See Rowland v. California Men's Colony, 506 U.S. 194 (1993). The same successor-counsel posture applies to debtors in possession in Chapter 11 and Subchapter V cases.

Withdrawal under L.R. 9010.1 operates at the level of the individual attorney's appearance. It does not, standing alone, terminate a firm's employment as a professional person under 11 U.S.C. § 327; that authorization runs to the firm under the order of employment and is terminated only by a separate order modifying or vacating the employment order.

Employment of Professionals and Fee Disclosure

D. Kan. Bankr. L.R. 2014.1 supplements FRBP 2014 by requiring that applications to employ professional persons under 11 U.S.C. § 327 include a verified statement of all connections to parties in interest, the debtor, the United States Trustee, and any person employed in the office of the United States Trustee. Supplemental disclosures are mandatory upon any change in circumstances.

Compensation applications under L.R. 2016.1 must comply with United States Trustee guidelines and must itemize time entries with sufficient particularity to permit independent review. Fee applications in Chapter 11 and Subchapter V cases are routinely set for hearing.

Under FRBP 2016(b), debtor's counsel in any case must file a statement disclosing compensation paid or agreed to be paid in connection with the case and must file a supplemental statement within 14 days of any subsequent payment or agreement not previously disclosed. The District of Kansas enforces the supplemental-disclosure obligation strictly across all chapters.

Electronic Filing (CM/ECF)

All documents must be filed electronically through CM/ECF except documents specifically excluded by standing order (sealed documents, oversized exhibits, and certain pro se filings). The court's electronic filing system is at ecf.ksb.uscourts.gov. Document size is capped at the court's published ECF limit per upload. Filings must comply with FRBP 9037 redaction requirements; the court will restrict public access to filings that contain unredacted personal identifiers pending remediation.

Standing Orders and General Orders

The District of Kansas Bankruptcy Court maintains a catalogue of standing and general orders covering recurring procedural questions. Active categories include:

  • Standing orders adopting the uniform Chapter 13 plan form and uniform confirmation order
  • Standing orders governing telephonic and video appearance procedures
  • Standing orders authorizing negative-notice treatment for specified motion categories
  • Standing orders implementing fee-disclosure procedures and U.S. Trustee compensation guidelines
  • General orders implementing changes to the Bankruptcy Code and Federal Rules at the district level

The complete list and full text of currently effective standing orders is on the court's website. Counsel should review the current catalogue before relying on any default federal procedure, because a standing order may have altered the default.

Practical Impact for Counsel

Three features of District of Kansas bankruptcy practice most often surprise counsel from other districts:

Withdrawal requires leave of court and successor counsel for entities. The L.R. 9010.1 framework requires court order on noticed motion; in cases involving corporate, LLC, or partnership debtors, successor counsel must be in place before withdrawal will be granted. The corporate pro se bar is rigorously applied, and an entity debtor left without counsel can face conversion or dismissal.

Section 327 employment is firm-level, distinct from individual appearance. An employment order entered under 11 U.S.C. § 327 names a firm. Departure of the lead individual attorney from the firm does not terminate the firm's authorization to render compensable services to the estate. Conversely, an individual attorney whose appearance is on the docket but whose firm has not been employed under § 327 is not authorized to bill the estate.

No Tenth Circuit Bankruptcy Appellate Panel. Unlike the Eighth, Ninth, and First Circuits, the Tenth Circuit does not maintain a bankruptcy appellate panel. Bankruptcy appeals are taken to the United States District Court for the District of Kansas under 28 U.S.C. § 158(a), with further review available in the Tenth Circuit Court of Appeals.
