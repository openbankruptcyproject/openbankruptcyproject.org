U.S. Bankruptcy Court for the Western District of Missouri - Local Rules Guide 2026

First published 2026-05-22/Last modified 2026-05-22/CC-BY-4.0

Abstract

The United States Bankruptcy Court for the Western District of Missouri (W.D. Mo. Bankr.) sits as a unit of the United States District Court for the Western District of Missouri under 28 U.S.C. § 151. Its local rules are codified in a four-digit numbering scheme derived from the Federal Rules of Bankruptcy Procedure (LBR 1001-1 through LBR 9037-1) and supplemented by a body of standing orders and general orders. This guide summarizes the rules that most often diverge from federal default practice for attorneys appearing in the district.

Court Identification and Coverage

The Western District of Missouri encompasses 66 counties spanning roughly the western half of the State of Missouri, including the Kansas City metropolitan area, Springfield, St. Joseph, Joplin, and Jefferson City. The bankruptcy court maintains divisional offices in:

  • Kansas City (Western Division) - 400 East 9th Street, Kansas City, MO 64106. Principal courthouse and clerk's office.
  • Springfield (Southern Division) - 222 North John Q. Hammons Parkway, Springfield, MO 65806.
  • St. Joseph (St. Joseph Division) - hearings held periodically at the federal courthouse in St. Joseph.
  • Jefferson City (Central Division) - hearings held periodically at the federal courthouse in Jefferson City.

The court is staffed by Article I bankruptcy judges appointed under 28 U.S.C. § 152 to fourteen-year terms by the United States Court of Appeals for the Eighth Circuit. The official roster of sitting judges and current chief-judge designation is published on the court's website.

Local Rule Numbering Scheme

The court adopts the four-digit numbering scheme recommended by the Administrative Office of the U.S. Courts. The first four digits track the relevant Federal Rule of Bankruptcy Procedure; the suffix denotes the local subsection. For example:

  • LBR 1007-1 - schedules and statements (tracks FRBP 1007)
  • LBR 2014-1 - employment of professionals (tracks FRBP 2014)
  • LBR 2016-1 - compensation of professionals (tracks FRBP 2016)
  • LBR 2019-1 - attorneys: withdrawal and substitution (formerly LBR 2091-1; renumbered in the Local Rules adopted January 5, 2026)
  • LBR 9013-1 - motion practice generally (tracks FRBP 9013)
  • LBR 9037-1 - redaction of personal identifiers (tracks FRBP 9037)

Where a federal rule has no local counterpart, the federal rule governs without supplementation. Local rules supplement but cannot override the Federal Rules of Bankruptcy Procedure or the Bankruptcy Code.

Attorney Admission and Bar Status

Practice before the W.D. Mo. Bankruptcy Court requires admission to the bar of the United States District Court for the Western District of Missouri. The district's general bar admission rules are found at W.D. Mo. L.R. 83.5. Standing admission is open to attorneys licensed and in good standing with the Supreme Court of Missouri (or any other state bar, subject to reciprocal practice rules). Out-of-district attorneys may seek pro hac vice admission on motion of a local counsel of record, who remains responsible for compliance with local rules throughout the engagement.

The bankruptcy court does not maintain a separate bankruptcy-specific bar; admission to the district court bar carries with it the right to appear in bankruptcy proceedings. Each attorney filing electronically must obtain an individual CM/ECF login from the clerk's office; shared logins are prohibited.

Motion Practice and Hearings

Most contested motions in the district are heard on the chambers calendar of the assigned bankruptcy judge. Local Rule 9013-1 requires that motions state with particularity the grounds for the relief requested, attach a proposed order, and be accompanied by a notice of opportunity to object where the relief is of a kind that may be granted without hearing absent objection. The court's standard notice period for negative-notice motions is twenty-one (21) days, consistent with FRBP 9013 and 9014.

The court has issued standing orders authorizing routine matters - most cash collateral stipulations, agreed extensions of bar dates, and certain claim-objection categories - to be heard on negative notice. Counsel should consult the court's posted procedural orders for the current list of matters that may proceed on negative notice in a given case type.

Telephonic and video appearances are available for most non-evidentiary hearings under the court's continuing standing orders adopted during the public-health emergency and retained as ordinary practice. Evidentiary hearings and contested confirmation hearings are typically conducted in person at the courthouse, subject to the assigned judge's preferences.

Withdrawal and Substitution: LBR 2019-1 (formerly 2091-1)

The local rule on attorney withdrawal is among the district's most consequential for ongoing case staffing. In the Local Rules adopted January 5, 2026, the court renumbered the withdrawal rule from LBR 2091-1 to LBR 2019-1 (Attorneys – Withdrawal and Substitution); citations to 2091-1 in filings predating that adoption refer to the same rule. The operative subsection, W.D. Mo. Bankr. L.R. 2019-1(A), provides:

An attorney of record may withdraw only by Court permission, by an order entered after service of notice of withdrawal on the client, other counsel of record, the trustee, if any, and the United States Trustee. An order will not be entered until new counsel enters an appearance, except for good cause.

Three operative features control. First, withdrawal is by Court permission, not unilateral notice. Second, notice of withdrawal must be served on the client, other counsel of record, the trustee (if any), and the United States Trustee. Third, the order will not be entered until successor counsel appears, except on a showing of good cause — a condition that carries particular weight where the client cannot proceed pro se (corporate, LLC, partnership debtors and similar artificial entities). Rule 2019-1(B) separately permits substitution by notice where new counsel is employed in the same law firm as the attorney of record.

This rule operates at the level of the individual attorney's appearance. Where a law firm represents the debtor under an employment order entered pursuant to 11 U.S.C. § 327, the firm's authorization to render compensable services to the estate is separately governed by the § 327 order and is not terminated by an individual attorney's withdrawal of appearance.

Employment of Professionals and Fee Disclosure

LBR 2014-1 supplements Federal Rule of Bankruptcy Procedure 2014 by requiring that applications to employ professional persons under 11 U.S.C. § 327 include a verified statement disclosing all connections with the debtor, creditors, any other party in interest, their respective attorneys and accountants, the United States Trustee, and any person employed in the office of the United States Trustee. Supplemental disclosures are required whenever circumstances change.

Compensation applications under LBR 2016-1 must comply with the United States Trustee's compensation guidelines for that fiscal year and must itemize time entries with sufficient particularity to permit independent review. Fee applications in Chapter 11 and Chapter 12 cases are typically set for hearing on the chambers calendar regardless of whether objections are filed.

Federal Rule of Bankruptcy Procedure 2016(b) requires debtor's counsel in any case to file a statement disclosing compensation paid or agreed to be paid by or on behalf of the debtor for services rendered in contemplation of or in connection with the case, and to file a supplemental statement within 14 days of any payment or agreement not previously disclosed. The W.D. Mo. court enforces the supplemental-disclosure obligation strictly.

Electronic Filing (CM/ECF)

All documents must be filed electronically through the court's CM/ECF system except for documents specifically excluded by standing order (typically: documents under seal, paper exhibits exceeding ECF size limits, and pro se filings by debtors without electronic-filing privileges). The court's CM/ECF system is accessible at ecf.mowb.uscourts.gov.

Document size is capped at the court's posted ECF limit per upload. Large exhibits must be split into compliant segments and filed as separate docket entries. Filings must comply with Federal Rule of Bankruptcy Procedure 9037 redaction requirements; the court will, on motion or sua sponte, restrict access to filings containing unredacted personal identifiers pending remediation.

Standing Orders and General Orders

The court issues standing orders to address recurring procedural matters and general orders for case-administration purposes that apply across all cases in the district. Categories of standing orders presently in force include:

  • Standing orders authorizing negative-notice procedures for specified categories of motions
  • Standing orders governing telephonic and video appearance procedures
  • Standing orders addressing the form and content of confirmation orders in Chapter 13 cases
  • Standing orders adopting form documents (chapter 13 plan, model orders) for use across the district
  • General orders implementing changes to the Bankruptcy Code or Federal Rules at the district level

The complete list of standing orders is maintained on the court's website. Counsel should consult the current list before deviating from any default federal procedure, because a standing order may have modified the default in ways that affect notice periods, form requirements, or hearing-calendar placement.

Practical Impact for Counsel

Three features of W.D. Mo. practice most often surprise attorneys whose primary experience is in other districts:

Withdrawal cannot be unilateral. The LBR 2019-1 (formerly 2091-1) requirement that withdrawal be by court order after notice to the client, other counsel of record, the trustee, and the United States Trustee, with successor counsel required except for good cause, is stricter than the practice in some districts that permit substitution of counsel by stipulation. Counsel intending to terminate a representation should plan for the motion-and-order timeline and identify successor counsel early.

Section 327 employment is firm-level. An employment order entered under 11 U.S.C. § 327 runs to the firm named in the application, not to any individual attorney within the firm. Departure of the originally-named lead attorney does not, without more, terminate the firm's authority to render compensable services. This is a feature of federal bankruptcy law rather than W.D. Mo. local rule, but the district enforces the distinction consistently.

Negative-notice scope is broader than default federal practice. The court's standing orders authorize negative-notice treatment for a broader range of motions than counsel may expect. The 21-day default notice period under FRBP 9014 applies where no shorter or longer period is fixed by standing order; counsel should verify the applicable notice period from the current standing-order list rather than assuming the federal default.
