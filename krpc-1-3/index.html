KRPC 1.3 / MRPC 4-1.3: Diligence

Reasonable diligence, promptness, and the duty to carry matters through to completion.

Verbatim text of KRPC 1.3 (Kansas)

A lawyer shall act with reasonable diligence and promptness in representing a client. Kan. Sup. Ct. R. 226, KRPC 1.3.

Missouri Rule 4-1.3

Missouri Rule 4-1.3 is substantively identical to KRPC 1.3 and tracks ABA Model Rule 1.3. The rule itself is a single sentence; the operative content is supplied by the Comment, which Kansas and Missouri have largely adopted.

"Reasonable diligence"

Diligence is the application of attention and effort to the client's matter at a level commensurate with what the matter requires. Comment 1 to Model Rule 1.3 explains that a lawyer should pursue a matter on behalf of a client despite opposition, obstruction, or personal inconvenience, and must take whatever lawful and ethical measures are required to vindicate the client's cause. The standard is "reasonable," not maximal; the rule does not require heroic effort, but it does require effort proportionate to the matter.

Comment 1 also recognizes the lawyer's autonomy in the choice of means. A lawyer is not bound to press for every advantage that might be realized for a client. The lawyer has professional discretion in determining the means by which a matter should be pursued. But the discretion is bounded by the lawyer's duty of competent and zealous representation; "discretion" does not encompass inaction on the matter.

"Promptness"

Promptness is the temporal dimension of diligence. Comment 3 emphasizes that perhaps no professional shortcoming is more widely resented than procrastination. Even when a client's interests are not affected in substance, unreasonable delay can cause the client needless anxiety and undermine confidence in the lawyer's trustworthiness. The duty of promptness applies to:

  • Returning client communications within a reasonable time
  • Filing documents required by deadlines (statutory, rule-based, or court-ordered)
  • Taking action on opportunities that have a time-limited window
  • Pursuing the matter to its conclusion rather than allowing it to stall

Carrying the matter through to completion

Comment 4 establishes that unless the relationship is terminated as provided in Rule 1.16, a lawyer should carry through to conclusion all matters undertaken for a client. If a lawyer's employment is limited to a specific matter, the relationship terminates when the matter has been resolved. If a lawyer has served a client over a substantial period in a variety of matters, the client sometimes may assume that the lawyer will continue to serve on a continuing basis unless the lawyer gives notice of withdrawal.

Ambiguity about whether the representation continues is itself a problem. Comment 4 advises that doubt about whether a client-lawyer relationship still exists should be clarified by the lawyer, preferably in writing, so that the client will not mistakenly suppose the lawyer is looking after the client's affairs when the lawyer has ceased to do so. Where a representation has effectively ended without formal closure, the duty of diligence interacts with the duty of communication under Rule 1.4 and the termination duties under Rule 1.16.

Workload management - Comment 2

Comment 2 addresses the lawyer's professional discretion in managing workload. A lawyer's workload must be controlled so that each matter can be handled competently. A lawyer who takes on more work than the lawyer can competently and diligently perform - whether through underestimation of the work required or through accepting too many matters - has not satisfied the rule by performing diligently on some matters and neglecting others. The duty of diligence runs to each client.

The interaction with Rule 1.1 (Competence)

Rules 1.1 and 1.3 are conceptually distinct but operationally paired. Competence concerns the quality of the lawyer's work; diligence concerns its timing and consistency. A lawyer can be competent but not diligent (capable but inattentive). A lawyer can be diligent but not competent (attentive but unprepared). Both rules must be satisfied for representation to meet the basic professional standard.

Common procedural posture

Missed deadline

A statutory deadline, court-ordered deadline, or rule-based deadline passes without action by the lawyer. Unless the lawyer can establish that the inaction was the product of a reasonable strategic decision communicated to and approved by the client under Rule 1.4, the missed deadline is a diligence violation. Depending on the consequence to the client, the violation may also generate exposure under malpractice principles independent of the disciplinary rules.

Stalled matter with no client communication

A matter sits without action for an extended period and the client is not advised of the status. This implicates both Rule 1.3 (failure to pursue the matter with reasonable diligence) and Rule 1.4 (failure to keep the client reasonably informed). Where the inaction has caused the client to lose substantive rights, the diligence violation is the underlying conduct and the communication failure compounds it.

Diligence in the bankruptcy context

Bankruptcy practice is characterized by short deadlines (proof-of-claim bar dates, objection-to-discharge deadlines, plan-confirmation timelines) and intersecting jurisdictional triggers (automatic stay, plan effective dates). Diligence in this context routinely requires both calendar discipline and active attention to docket events. A lawyer who relies on the client to identify deadlines, or who treats incoming docket notifications as background noise, is at risk under Rule 1.3 even before any substantive harm results.

Relationship to other rules

  • Rule 1.1 (Competence) - The qualitative companion; the two rules are typically analyzed together.
  • Rule 1.4 (Communication) - Diligence in the matter usually requires communication with the client; failures of one frequently track failures of the other.
  • Rule 1.16 (Withdrawal) - When the lawyer is unable to satisfy diligence obligations because of workload, the answer is not silent inaction but withdrawal subject to the termination duties of Rule 1.16(d).
  • Rule 3.2 (Expediting Litigation) - The litigation analog; a lawyer must make reasonable efforts to expedite litigation consistent with the interests of the client.

Practical impact

Diligence is the most-cited duty in lawyer-discipline matters because its violation is often visible from a docket or calendar without requiring analysis of substance. Two patterns recur:

  • Procrastination as professional misconduct. The rule treats delay not as a soft failure but as an enumerated professional duty violation. The Comment's reference to procrastination as a shortcoming that is "widely resented" is a direct signal that delay is a disciplinable matter.
  • Workload is the lawyer's problem. Overcommitment is not a defense to diligence violations. The lawyer must control the workload to meet diligence obligations to every client; the alternative is to decline matters under Rule 1.16(a) or to withdraw if commitments cannot be met.
