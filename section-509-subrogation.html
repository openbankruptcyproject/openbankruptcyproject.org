11 U.S.C. Section 509 - Subrogation of Co-Debtors and Sureties

How co-debtors, sureties, and guarantors who pay a creditor step into that creditor's shoes against the debtor's estate - and the statutory exclusions and subordination rules that frequently swallow the rule.

What Is Section 509?

Section 509 codifies the equitable doctrine of subrogation as it applies in bankruptcy. When a third party - typically a co-debtor, surety, guarantor, or co-maker - pays a creditor whose claim is also owed by the debtor, the third party "steps into the shoes" of the paid creditor against the debtor's estate, to the extent of the payment.

Subrogation is not the same as contribution. A contribution claim is a debt the co-debtor independently holds against the principal debtor for paying more than its proportional share. A subrogation claim, by contrast, is the creditor's claim itself, transferred to the paying co-debtor. The distinction matters because subrogation may carry liens, priorities, or guarantees the contribution claim would not.

Official citation: 11 U.S.C. § 509

The General Rule: Section 509(a)

Section 509(a) provides: "Except as provided in subsection (b) or (c) of this section, an entity that is liable with the debtor on, or that has secured, a claim of a creditor against the debtor, and that pays such claim, is subrogated to the rights of such creditor to the extent of such payment." Three elements must be satisfied:

  • Co-liability or secured guarantor status: The third party must be liable with the debtor on the claim or must have secured the creditor's claim against the debtor (for example, by pledging collateral as a guarantor).
  • Actual payment to the creditor: Subrogation runs only to the extent of the payment actually made. Partial payment yields proportional subrogation.
  • Same claim: The subrogee acquires the rights of the paid creditor, no more and no less, including any security interest, priority, or contract terms attached to that claim.

Statutory Exclusions: Section 509(b)

Section 509(b) significantly narrows the general rule. Subrogation is unavailable where:

  • 509(b)(1)(A): The co-debtor's claim for reimbursement or contribution is allowed under Section 502;
  • 509(b)(1)(B): Such claim has been disallowed other than under Section 502(e); or
  • 509(b)(1)(C): Such claim has been subordinated under Section 510.

In addition, Section 509(b)(2) bars subrogation when the third party received consideration from the debtor for the obligation. The classic application is a paid guarantor: if the surety received a fee from the debtor in exchange for guaranteeing the obligation, the surety cannot turn around and claim subrogation against the debtor's estate. The premise is that the surety has already been compensated for the risk.

Subordination: Section 509(c)

Section 509(c) ranks claims when both the creditor and the subrogated co-debtor hold claims arising from the same debt: "The court shall subordinate to the claim of a creditor and for the benefit of such creditor an allowed claim, by way of subrogation under this section, or for reimbursement or contribution, of an entity that is liable with the debtor on, or that has secured, such creditor's claim, until such creditor's claim is paid in full, either through payments under this title or otherwise."

The practical effect: the original creditor recovers in full before any co-debtor recovers anything on a subrogation, reimbursement, or contribution theory. This prevents the co-debtor from competing with the creditor for the same dollar of distribution and reflects the equitable principle that the creditor's substantive position must be made whole first.

Interaction with Section 502(e)

Section 502(e)(1) requires the court to disallow a claim "for reimbursement or contribution of an entity that is liable with the debtor on or has secured the claim of a creditor" to the extent the claim is contingent at the time of allowance or the creditor's claim is itself disallowed. The result is a one-way ratchet: until the co-debtor actually pays, its contingent reimbursement claim is disallowed under 502(e)(1)(B), and the co-debtor must instead rely on Section 509 subrogation after it pays. This avoids double counting between the creditor's direct claim and the co-debtor's derivative claim.

Common Procedural Postures

Section 509 issues typically surface in the following postures:

  • Guarantor of a secured loan: A personal guarantor of a corporate debtor's bank loan pays the bank, then asserts the bank's secured claim against the corporate estate by subrogation;
  • Co-maker on commercial paper: Two business partners co-sign a note; one files bankruptcy, the other pays the holder, and the non-filing co-maker asserts a subrogation claim against the filing partner's estate;
  • Insurance carrier paying a covered claim: An insurer that pays a third-party claim against the insured-debtor may claim subrogation rights against the estate (subject to the 509(b)(2) consideration exclusion if premiums were paid pre-petition);
  • Surety on a performance or payment bond: A construction surety completes a bonded project, then asserts subrogation to the obligee's contract rights and any retainage held by the obligee.

Equitable Subrogation Beyond Section 509

Courts have recognized that Section 509 does not displace common-law equitable subrogation entirely; rather, it provides a statutory floor and structure. Where Section 509(b) bars statutory subrogation, the co-debtor may still in some cases assert equitable subrogation under non-bankruptcy state law, but such claims are vulnerable to subordination under Section 510, including equitable subordination under Section 510(c). The Supreme Court has long recognized subrogation as a flexible equitable doctrine, and bankruptcy courts apply traditional equitable considerations when evaluating non-statutory subrogation claims.
