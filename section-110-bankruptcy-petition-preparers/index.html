11 U.S.C. Section 110 - Bankruptcy Petition Preparer Regulation

A deep-dive guide to the federal regulation of non-attorney bankruptcy petition preparers: definitional scope, disclosure mandates, prohibited acts, fee disgorgement, injunctive relief, and statutory fines.

What Is Section 110?

Section 110 of the Bankruptcy Code is the comprehensive federal regulatory regime that governs non-attorney bankruptcy petition preparers (BPPs). Enacted in 1994 and substantially expanded by BAPCPA in 2005, the statute is designed to address documented abuses by typing services and document-preparation businesses that historically charged consumer debtors significant fees while providing no legal advice and frequently producing defective petitions.

Section 110 occupies a distinctive regulatory niche: it does not authorize non-attorneys to provide legal services, and it does not preempt state unauthorized-practice-of-law (UPL) statutes. Rather, it imposes a federal disclosure-and-enforcement scheme on persons who already lawfully prepare bankruptcy documents under state law (typically by limiting their activity to typing services and scrivener work).

Official citation: 11 U.S.C. § 110

Who Is a Bankruptcy Petition Preparer: Section 110(a)

Section 110(a)(1) defines a "bankruptcy petition preparer" as a person, other than an attorney for the debtor or an employee of such attorney under the direct supervision of such attorney, who prepares for compensation a document for filing. Section 110(a)(2) defines "document for filing" as a petition or any other document prepared for filing by a debtor in a United States bankruptcy court or a United States district court in connection with a case under this title.

The definition is broad. It captures typing services, paralegal services held out to the public, online form-fill businesses, and any person who prepares bankruptcy documents for a fee outside the attorney-employee relationship. Conversely, an attorney who prepares documents for the attorney's own client, and that attorney's supervised employees, are categorically outside Section 110's reach because they are governed by state attorney-licensure rules and the Bankruptcy Code's attorney-discipline provisions (Sections 329 and 526-528).

Disclosure Requirements: Section 110(b)-(c)

Section 110(b)(1) requires the BPP to sign the document for filing and print on the document the BPP's name and address. Section 110(b)(2) requires the BPP to provide the debtor, before the BPP prepares any document for filing or accepts any fee from the debtor, a written notice on a court-prescribed form that informs the debtor in simple language that the BPP is not an attorney, may not practice law, may not give legal advice, and that lists examples of legal advice that the BPP may not give.

Section 110(c) requires the BPP to place an identifying number on each document for filing. Under the current statute, this is the Social Security number of the individual responsible for preparing the document or, in the case of a non-individual BPP, the Social Security number of the principal officer responsible.

The disclosure regime is meant to address the central historical abuse: BPPs holding themselves out, expressly or implicitly, as legal-advice providers. By requiring a signed, court-form notice up front, Section 110 forces a clear delineation between scrivener service (permitted) and legal advice (prohibited).

Prohibited Acts: Section 110(e)-(g)

Section 110(e)(1) bars a BPP from executing any document on behalf of a debtor. Section 110(e)(2) prohibits the BPP from offering a potential bankruptcy debtor any legal advice, including advice on:

  • Whether to file a petition or under which chapter;
  • Whether the debtor's debts will be discharged;
  • Whether the debtor will be able to retain a home, car, or other property after filing;
  • Concerning the tax consequences of a case;
  • Concerning the dischargeability of tax claims;
  • Whether the debtor may or should promise to repay debts;
  • Concerning how to characterize the nature of the debtor's interests in property or the debtor's debts;
  • Concerning bankruptcy procedures and rights.

Section 110(f) prohibits the BPP from using the word "legal" or any similar term in advertisements. Section 110(g) prohibits the BPP from receiving from or on behalf of the debtor any payment for court fees.

These prohibitions are calibrated to keep BPPs strictly in the scrivener role. Federal courts uniformly hold that case-strategy advice, document-content advice, and exemption-selection advice all fall on the legal-advice side of the line and are forbidden.

Fee Disgorgement: Section 110(h)

Section 110(h)(2) requires the BPP to file a declaration disclosing any fee received from or on behalf of the debtor within 12 months immediately before filing and any unpaid fee charged to the debtor. Section 110(h)(3) authorizes the court, on motion or sua sponte, to disallow and order the immediate turnover to the trustee of any fee found to be in excess of the value of any services rendered by the BPP during the 12-month period immediately preceding the date of the petition.

Some districts have adopted local rules establishing presumptive maximum BPP fees (commonly in the $150 to $200 range). A fee exceeding the presumptive cap is subject to disgorgement absent specific proof of higher reasonable value. Because Section 110 limits BPPs to clerical and scrivener tasks, the upper bound on "value of services rendered" is necessarily modest.

Injunctive Relief: Section 110(j)

Section 110(j) authorizes the U.S. Trustee, the debtor, or any party in interest to bring a civil action to enjoin a BPP from engaging in conduct that violates Section 110 or any other federal or state law. On a showing of repeated or significantly harmful violations, the court may enjoin the BPP from acting as a BPP in that district, in any district, or for a specified period of time.

Injunctive practice under Section 110(j) is one of the principal mechanisms by which abusive BPPs are removed from the system. U.S. Trustee-initiated enforcement is common in districts with active consumer-protection programs, and nationwide injunctions are not unusual in cases of significant interstate conduct.

Fines: Section 110(l) and Treble Damages: Section 110(i)

Section 110(l)(1) authorizes a fine of not more than $500 for each failure to comply with Section 110(b)-(h). Section 110(l)(2) triples the fine to a maximum of $1,500 per violation where the BPP fails to disclose, defrauds, or willfully engages in conduct in violation of Section 110.

Section 110(i)(1) authorizes the court, on motion of the debtor, trustee, U.S. Trustee, or creditor, to certify violations and order the BPP to pay to the debtor the greater of (A) $2,000 or (B) twice the amount paid by the debtor to the BPP, plus reasonable attorney fees, where the BPP commits any fraudulent, unfair, or deceptive act, or fails to comply with disclosure requirements, and the case is dismissed or converted under the Code because of that violation.

These remedies stack: a single course of conduct can produce disgorgement under (h), per-violation fines under (l), injunctive relief under (j), and statutory damages under (i), with attorney fees layered on top.

Interplay with State UPL Law

Section 110(k) preserves state remedies: nothing in Section 110 shall be construed to permit activities that are otherwise prohibited by law, including rules and laws that prohibit the unauthorized practice of law. A BPP who provides legal advice in violation of Section 110(e) almost always simultaneously violates state UPL statutes, which carry their own civil and criminal penalties. State bar UPL committees and federal bankruptcy courts often coordinate enforcement.
