Federal Rule of Bankruptcy Procedure 1007

Rule 1007: Schedules and Statement of Financial Affairs

The lists, schedules, and Statement of Financial Affairs the debtor must file, the filing deadlines, and the continuing duty of completeness.

What Rule 1007 requires

Federal Rule of Bankruptcy Procedure 1007 specifies the documents a debtor must file with the bankruptcy petition, the deadlines for filing them, the consequences of late or missing filings, and the relationship between the initial filings and any subsequent amendment under Rule 1009. The rule is the procedural backbone of every consumer and business bankruptcy: it converts the debtor's snapshot disclosure obligation into a structured set of standardized filings that creditors, the trustee, the United States Trustee, and the court rely on as the factual baseline of the case.

The Rule 1007 filing package

For a voluntary case, the debtor must file the following documents either with the petition or within the 14-day extension period provided by Rule 1007(c):

  • List of creditors (the "creditor matrix") containing the name and address of each entity included on the schedules
  • Schedules of assets and liabilities (Official Form 106 series, schedules A/B through J)
  • Schedule of current income and current expenditures (Schedules I and J)
  • Statement of Financial Affairs (Official Form 107 for individuals; Official Form 207 for non-individuals)
  • Statement of Intention regarding retained collateral and leased property (Chapter 7 individuals only - Official Form 108)
  • Statement of Current Monthly Income and means-test calculation (Chapter 7: Forms 122A-1 / 122A-2; Chapter 13: Forms 122C-1 / 122C-2)
  • Copies of payment advices from any employer received within 60 days before the petition date (11 U.S.C. Section 521(a)(1)(B)(iv))
  • Statement of Social Security Number (Official Form 121, filed under seal and not part of the public record)

Chapter 11 cases additionally require an equity holder list under Rule 1007(a)(1) and, for non-individual debtors, the schedule of executory contracts and unexpired leases (Schedule G of Form 206).

The Official Form 106 schedule series

Schedules A through J of Official Form 106 (the individual debtor's schedules) are the structured asset, liability, income, and expense disclosure framework that supersedes the older single-form schedules. Each schedule addresses a discrete category of disclosure:

ScheduleCategoryKey disclosure
A/BReal and personal propertyEvery interest in property the debtor owns, including contingent, future, and joint interests
CProperty claimed as exemptEach exemption claim with statutory citation and value claimed exempt
DSecured claimsEvery creditor holding a security interest, the collateral, and the amount of the claim
E/FUnsecured claims (priority and non-priority)Priority claims (taxes, domestic support, wages) and general unsecured claims
GExecutory contracts and unexpired leasesEvery contract or lease under which performance remains due on both sides
HCodebtorsEvery person or entity also liable on any debt of the debtor
IIncomeThe debtor's (and any non-filing spouse's) current monthly income, source by source
JExpensesThe debtor's projected monthly expenses, line by line

The 2015 forms revision restructured the schedules to require granular property identification (each automobile by VIN, each financial account by institution and last four digits) rather than the older free-text disclosure format. The change increased the evidentiary precision of the schedules at the cost of more onerous preparation; the completeness duty applies to every required field.

The Statement of Financial Affairs (Official Form 107)

The Statement of Financial Affairs - the "SOFA" - is the narrative companion to the schedules. Where the schedules capture the debtor's position as of the petition date, the SOFA captures pre-petition transactions and circumstances that may bear on the administration of the estate. The form requires disclosure of:

  • Income from employment or operation of a business for the current year and the two prior calendar years
  • Income other than from employment or operation of a business for the same periods
  • Payments to creditors aggregating more than the statutory threshold within 90 days before the petition (the preference window)
  • Payments to insiders within one year before the petition
  • Lawsuits, garnishments, repossessions, foreclosures, and assignments for the benefit of creditors within one year
  • Gifts and charitable contributions aggregating more than the statutory threshold within two years
  • Losses from fire, theft, casualty, or gambling within one year
  • Payments to attorneys, accountants, debt counselors, and bankruptcy petition preparers within one year
  • Other property transferred outside the ordinary course within two years (or ten years if to a self-settled trust)
  • Closed financial accounts, safe deposit boxes, and prior addresses within three years
  • Business operations within four years (if applicable)

The SOFA is signed under penalty of perjury. A material misstatement or omission may constitute a false oath under 18 U.S.C. Section 152(2), grounds for denial of discharge under Section 727(a)(4), and grounds for revocation of a discharge already granted under Section 727(d)(1).

Filing deadlines under Rule 1007(c)

Rule 1007(c) sets the filing deadlines:

  • In a voluntary case, the schedules, statements, and other documents required by Rule 1007(b) are due with the petition or within 14 days after the order for relief
  • In an involuntary case, the documents are due within 14 days after entry of the order for relief
  • For good cause shown, on motion, the court may extend the time on notice to the United States Trustee and any committee, trustee, or other party as the court directs

Failure to timely file the Rule 1007 documents is grounds for dismissal of the case under 11 U.S.C. Section 707(a)(3) (Chapter 7) or Section 1307(c) (Chapter 13) for unreasonable delay prejudicial to creditors. Many districts have implemented automatic dismissal procedures: if the schedules are not filed within the 14-day window, a deficiency notice issues and the case is dismissed without further hearing if the deficiency is not cured.

Amendment under Rule 1009

Rule 1009(a) provides that a voluntary petition, list, schedule, or statement may be amended by the debtor as a matter of course at any time before the case is closed. No motion or court order is required to file an amendment. The amended document supersedes the prior version for purposes of the case record. The debtor's right to amend is, however, subject to two important limitations:

  • Notice to affected parties. Rule 1009(a) requires that the debtor give notice of the amendment to the trustee and to any entity affected. An amendment adding a creditor not on the original matrix that is not noticed to that creditor does not afford the creditor the discharge protection that the original schedules would have - the creditor's claim may be nondischargeable under 11 U.S.C. Section 523(a)(3).
  • Bad-faith limitation. Courts have routinely held that the right to amend is not absolute: an amendment filed in bad faith, or one that would prejudice creditors who have relied on the original schedules, may be disallowed. The leading authority is the Tenth Circuit's decision in In re Talbot, which articulates a multi-factor test for evaluating bad-faith amendments.

The continuing duty of completeness

The debtor's disclosure duty does not end when the schedules and SOFA are filed. Under 11 U.S.C. Section 521(a)(1) and the case law construing it, the debtor has a continuing duty to amend the schedules and SOFA whenever the debtor becomes aware that the original filings were incomplete or inaccurate. The duty applies whether the omission was deliberate, negligent, or innocent at the time of the original filing. Common omissions that trigger the duty to amend include:

  • A pending personal-injury or employment-discrimination claim not disclosed on Schedule A/B item 33
  • An inheritance interest, life-insurance beneficiary designation, or trust interest the debtor later identifies
  • A pre-petition transfer to an insider not disclosed on SOFA Question 4 or 7
  • A bank account or financial-asset interest not disclosed on Schedule A/B items 16-23

Failure to amend after the debtor becomes aware of the omission is independently grounds for denial of discharge under Section 727(a)(4)(A) (false oath) and may convert what would have been a forgivable omission into a deliberate concealment.

Common procedural posture

Late-discovered cause of action

After the petition date, the debtor learns of a pre-petition cause of action (for example, an employment claim that accrued before filing but the debtor did not appreciate as a "claim"). The debtor must amend Schedule A/B to add the claim and Schedule C to assert any applicable exemption. Failure to schedule the claim before the case closes generally precludes the debtor from prosecuting the claim post-discharge under judicial-estoppel principles.

Preference payment not disclosed on SOFA

The debtor's review of pre-petition bank records reveals a payment to a relative within one year before filing that was not disclosed on SOFA Question 4 (payments to insiders). The debtor must amend the SOFA promptly. The trustee may then evaluate whether to pursue an avoidance action under 11 U.S.C. Section 547 or 548; voluntary disclosure significantly mitigates the inference of bad faith.

The schedules are signed under penalty of perjury. Every line on the Official Form 106 schedules and the Official Form 107 SOFA carries the same evidentiary weight as a sworn affidavit. The unsigned, undated, or improperly verified version of the form is not a compliant filing; courts have stricken schedules that omit the verification page.
