Federal Rule of Bankruptcy Procedure 7012

Rule 7012: Motion to Dismiss in Adversary Proceedings

FRCP 12(b)(1)/(2)/(3)/(6) incorporation, the Stern v. Marshall jurisdictional motion framework, abstention-grounds motions, and the responsive-pleading choice between answer and motion.

The structure of Rule 7012

Federal Rule of Bankruptcy Procedure 7012 governs the responsive pleading in an adversary proceeding. The rule has two operative subdivisions. Rule 7012(a) sets the time within which a defendant must serve an answer (30 days after issuance of the summons, with extensions for service on the United States and its agencies). Rule 7012(b) incorporates Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 12(b) through (i) and adds the bankruptcy-specific Stern-v.-Marshall consent requirement: "A responsive pleading shall admit or deny an allegation that the proceeding is core or non-core. If the response is that the proceeding is non-core, it shall include a statement that the party does or does not consent to entry of final orders or judgment by the bankruptcy judge."

The incorporation of FRCP 12 is comprehensive. Every motion available under the civil-procedure rule - lack of subject-matter jurisdiction (12(b)(1)), lack of personal jurisdiction (12(b)(2)), improper venue (12(b)(3)), insufficient process (12(b)(4)), insufficient service of process (12(b)(5)), failure to state a claim (12(b)(6)), and failure to join an indispensable party (12(b)(7)) - is available in a bankruptcy adversary proceeding.

The FRCP 12(b)(1) motion: subject-matter jurisdiction

The 12(b)(1) motion is the standard mechanism for raising the threshold question whether the bankruptcy court has subject-matter jurisdiction over the claims pled in the complaint. Bankruptcy-court subject-matter jurisdiction is conferred by 28 U.S.C. Section 1334, which gives the district court original and exclusive jurisdiction over Title 11 cases and original (but not exclusive) jurisdiction over civil proceedings "arising under" Title 11, "arising in" a Title 11 case, or "related to" a Title 11 case.

The "arising under," "arising in," and "related to" distinctions are jurisdictionally consequential. "Arising under" proceedings invoke substantive rights created by Title 11 (avoidance actions, exemption disputes, dischargeability proceedings). "Arising in" proceedings are those that could not exist outside a bankruptcy case (motions to assume executory contracts, plan confirmation). "Related to" proceedings have a more attenuated connection - the test, as articulated in Pacor, Inc. v. Higgins, 743 F.2d 984 (3d Cir. 1984) and adopted in most circuits, is whether the outcome of the proceeding could conceivably have any effect on the estate being administered in bankruptcy.

A 12(b)(1) motion may challenge the jurisdictional predicate at any of these levels. The challenge is non-waivable - subject-matter jurisdiction may be raised at any time, including for the first time on appeal.

The Stern v. Marshall jurisdictional framework

The Supreme Court's decision in Stern v. Marshall, 564 U.S. 462 (2011), introduced a constitutional layer to bankruptcy-court adjudicatory authority that operates separately from the statutory jurisdictional grant. Stern holds that even where a proceeding is statutorily "core" under 28 U.S.C. Section 157(b)(2), the bankruptcy court lacks constitutional authority under Article III to enter a final judgment on certain state-law counterclaims if the counterclaim is not necessarily resolved in the process of ruling on the creditor's proof of claim.

The practical operation of Stern is that the responsive-pleading determination required by Rule 7012(b) carries constitutional weight. A defendant in an adversary proceeding must affirmatively address whether the proceeding is "core" or "non-core" - and, if non-core, whether the defendant consents to entry of final orders by the bankruptcy judge. The Supreme Court's decision in Wellness International Network, Ltd. v. Sharif, 575 U.S. 665 (2015), confirmed that a litigant may consent to bankruptcy-court adjudication of a Stern claim, but the consent must be knowing and voluntary. Silence on consent is interpreted under the case law to be inconsistent with adjudication.

Stern v. Marshall is a constitutional, not statutory, issue. The Rule 7012(b) consent statement is not a procedural courtesy; it is the mechanism by which the defendant either accepts or preserves objection to bankruptcy-court entry of final judgment. A defendant who fails to address consent in the answer risks being held to have waived the constitutional objection.

The FRCP 12(b)(6) motion: failure to state a claim

The 12(b)(6) motion challenges the sufficiency of the complaint to state a claim upon which relief can be granted. The motion is governed by the plausibility standard articulated in Bell Atlantic Corp. v. Twombly, 550 U.S. 544 (2007), and Ashcroft v. Iqbal, 556 U.S. 662 (2009): the complaint must contain factual allegations that, accepted as true, state a claim that is plausible on its face. Conclusory allegations and bare recitations of the elements of a cause of action are insufficient.

In the bankruptcy context, 12(b)(6) motions are most commonly directed at avoidance-action complaints (preference, fraudulent-transfer, equitable-subordination), at non-dischargeability complaints under Section 523, and at adversary complaints seeking declarations of property rights. Each context applies the plausibility standard against the substantive elements of the specific claim. A preference complaint, for example, must allege facts that plausibly support each element of 11 U.S.C. Section 547(b); a non-dischargeability complaint under Section 523(a)(2)(A) must plead with the particularity required by FRCP 9(b) (incorporated by Rule 7009) the facts constituting the alleged fraud.

The 12(b)(2) and 12(b)(3) motions: personal jurisdiction and venue

Personal jurisdiction in bankruptcy adversary proceedings benefits from the nationwide service provision of Rule 7004(d), which authorizes service of summons anywhere in the United States. Nationwide service generally satisfies the personal-jurisdiction inquiry against U.S. defendants, subject to the constitutional minimum-contacts analysis required by the Due Process Clause.

Venue is governed by 28 U.S.C. Section 1409. The general rule is that a proceeding arising in or related to a bankruptcy case may be commenced in the district where the case is pending. Section 1409(b) provides a "small-dollar" carve-out: trustee suits to recover a money judgment of less than approximately $1,500 (or to recover a consumer debt of less than approximately $23,000, with periodic dollar-amount adjustments) must be brought in the district where the defendant resides.

Abstention as a basis for dismissal

Although abstention is not expressly listed in FRCP 12(b), bankruptcy courts routinely entertain abstention motions in the same procedural posture as a motion to dismiss. The statutory authority is 28 U.S.C. Section 1334(c), which provides two abstention regimes:

  • Mandatory abstention under Section 1334(c)(2) - applies if the proceeding is based on a state-law claim, is "related to" a bankruptcy case but not "arising under" or "arising in" the case, an action is pending in a state forum of appropriate jurisdiction, and the action can be timely adjudicated. All four conditions must be satisfied.
  • Permissive abstention under Section 1334(c)(1) - applies "in the interest of justice, or in the interest of comity with State courts or respect for State law." The multi-factor analysis varies by circuit but typically considers the effect on bankruptcy administration, the extent of state-law issues, the relative expertise of the forum, and the prejudice to non-debtor parties.

A motion invoking abstention is typically framed as a motion to abstain and dismiss (or, in some circuits, to abstain and remand if the proceeding was removed). The procedural mechanics align with the 12(b) motion practice.

The answer-versus-motion choice

FRCP 12(b) permits the assertion of the listed defenses either in the responsive pleading (the answer) or by motion before the responsive pleading. The choice is consequential. A defense raised by 12(b) motion must be decided before the answer is due; the time to answer is suspended pending disposition. A defense raised in the answer must be litigated on a more compressed schedule, typically without the focused pre-discovery treatment that a 12(b) motion permits.

The 12(b)(6) "failure to state a claim" defense and the 12(b)(1) "lack of subject-matter jurisdiction" defense are typically raised by motion when the defendant believes the issue can be resolved on the face of the pleadings. Personal-jurisdiction and venue defenses are usually raised by motion because they are waived under Rule 12(h)(1) if not raised in the first responsive pleading or motion.

Common procedural posture

Stern-implicated counterclaim

A trustee files an adversary proceeding seeking to recover a fraudulent transfer. The defendant files a counterclaim asserting state-law tort claims unrelated to the bankruptcy. The defendant files a Rule 7012(b) answer expressly declining consent to entry of final judgment on the counterclaim. The bankruptcy court must then enter proposed findings and conclusions for de novo review by the district court rather than a final judgment.

Mandatory abstention motion

A debtor files an adversary proceeding asserting state-law breach-of-contract claims against a non-debtor defendant. The defendant moves to dismiss under Rule 7012(b) on mandatory-abstention grounds, identifying a pending state-court action between the parties. The bankruptcy court evaluates the four Section 1334(c)(2) conditions; if satisfied, it abstains and dismisses the proceeding.

Practical impact

Rule 7012 is the procedural gateway between the filing of an adversary complaint and the substantive litigation of the dispute. Beyond the standard FRCP 12 incorporation, the bankruptcy-specific Stern-consent requirement places constitutional weight on a step that civil-procedure practitioners are accustomed to treating as routine. The responsive pleading is no longer a perfunctory document; it is the instrument by which the defendant either accepts bankruptcy-court adjudicative authority or preserves the right to Article III review.

The core/non-core determination is the structural pivot. Where the proceeding is core and the court has constitutional authority under Stern, the bankruptcy court enters final judgment subject to ordinary appellate review. Where the proceeding is non-core, or core but Stern-implicated, the bankruptcy court enters proposed findings for de novo review by the district court. The Rule 7012(b) consent statement is the procedural mechanism by which this choice is made.
