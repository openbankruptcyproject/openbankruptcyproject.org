Federal Criminal Statutes

Bankruptcy Crimes

Chapter 9 of Title 18 of the United States Code criminalizes a range of conduct that defrauds creditors, obstructs the bankruptcy process, or undermines the integrity of court records. These statutes are enforced by United States Attorneys' Offices, often on referral from the United States Trustee Program, the bankruptcy court, or a private party with knowledge of the offense. The five guides below explain the principal Title 18 provisions used in bankruptcy-related prosecutions.

About the federal bankruptcy-crimes regime

Federal bankruptcy crimes share a few common features: each requires proof of a specific state of mind (typically knowing or fraudulent), each carries a five-year maximum sentence (twenty years for section 1519), and each operates independently of any civil sanction the bankruptcy court may impose. A debtor, attorney, custodian, or third party can be prosecuted under these statutes whether or not the bankruptcy case itself produced a civil recovery for creditors.

The most-charged bankruptcy crime is 18 U.S.C. section 152, which contains nine subsections covering concealment of assets, false oaths, false claims, and bribery of officers of the estate. Section 157, added in 1994, broadens the scope of prosecutable bankruptcy fraud by reaching schemes to defraud accomplished through the filing of a bankruptcy case or any document in connection with one. Section 1519, enacted as part of the Sarbanes-Oxley Act in 2002, is not a bankruptcy-specific statute but applies broadly to obstruction of any matter within federal-agency jurisdiction - which includes the United States Trustee Program.

Each deep-dive below explains the statutory elements, the typical fact patterns prosecuted under the section, the available defenses, and the sentencing exposure under the United States Sentencing Guidelines.

Statute-by-statute explainers

  • 18 U.S.C. Section 152 Concealment of AssetsThe nine-subsection workhorse of bankruptcy prosecutions: concealment of property from the trustee, false statements under oath, false claims, fee splitting, and bribery of bankruptcy officers.
  • 18 U.S.C. Section 153 Trustee EmbezzlementEmbezzlement of estate property by a trustee, custodian, marshal, or other officer of the court, with a five-year maximum sentence per count.
  • 18 U.S.C. Section 156 Knowing DisregardBankruptcy-petition preparer offenses: knowing disregard of the bankruptcy law or rule resulting in dismissal of the case or denial of discharge.
  • 18 U.S.C. Section 157 Bankruptcy FraudSchemes or artifices to defraud accomplished through the filing of a petition or any document in a bankruptcy case, the broadest of the bankruptcy-fraud statutes.
  • 18 U.S.C. Section 1519 Anti-Shredding StatuteSarbanes-Oxley provision criminalizing alteration, destruction, or falsification of records in any matter within the jurisdiction of a federal department or agency, including the United States Trustee Program. Twenty-year maximum.
Referral note: Suspected bankruptcy crimes are typically referred under 18 U.S.C. section 3057, which directs judges and trustees to report apparent violations to the local United States Attorney. Private parties may make the same referral directly to the U.S. Attorney's Office for the district in which the bankruptcy case is pending.
