18 U.S.C. Section 156 - Knowing Disregard of Bankruptcy Law or Rule

Doctrinal overview of the misdemeanor offense applicable to bankruptcy petition preparers who knowingly disregard the Bankruptcy Code or the Federal Rules of Bankruptcy Procedure. One-year maximum; complements the civil-enforcement framework of 11 U.S.C. Section 110.

Home › Bankruptcy Crimes › Section 156

What Is Section 156?

Section 156 of Title 18 is a misdemeanor offense added by the Bankruptcy Reform Act of 1994 as a companion to the new civil regulatory scheme for non-attorney bankruptcy petition preparers (BPPs) in 11 U.S.C. § 110. Section 156 criminalizes a bankruptcy petition preparer's knowing disregard of the Bankruptcy Code or Bankruptcy Rules where that disregard causes a case or related proceeding to be dismissed.

The statute is the only criminal provision in Title 18 directed specifically at bankruptcy petition preparers as a regulated class. It pairs with the broader civil-penalty and injunction framework of 11 U.S.C. § 110 to form a two-track BPP enforcement system: civil fines and disgorgement for routine violations, and criminal prosecution for the most serious cases.

Official citation: 18 U.S.C. § 156 (knowing disregard of bankruptcy law or rule).

Statutory Text and Elements

Section 156(a) defines "bankruptcy petition preparer" by cross-reference to 11 U.S.C. § 110, and defines "document for filing" to mean a petition or any other document prepared for filing in a case under Title 11 in connection with a case or proceeding by a person other than the debtor.

Section 156(b) provides: "If a bankruptcy case or related proceeding is dismissed because of a knowing attempt by a bankruptcy petition preparer in any manner to disregard the requirements of title 11, United States Code, or the Federal Rules of Bankruptcy Procedure, the bankruptcy petition preparer shall be fined under this title, imprisoned not more than 1 year, or both."

The elements the government must prove are:

  1. The defendant is a bankruptcy petition preparer as defined in 11 U.S.C. § 110(a);
  2. The defendant knowingly attempted, in any manner, to disregard the requirements of Title 11 or the Federal Rules of Bankruptcy Procedure;
  3. A bankruptcy case or related proceeding was dismissed because of that knowing disregard.

Defining the Bankruptcy Petition Preparer

11 U.S.C. § 110(a)(1) defines a "bankruptcy petition preparer" as a person, other than an attorney for the debtor or an employee of such attorney under the direct supervision of such attorney, who prepares for compensation a document for filing. The statute deliberately excludes attorneys and their supervised employees, who are governed by Rule 9011 and the rules of professional conduct rather than by the BPP regime.

The Supreme Court in Lamie v. United States Trustee, 540 U.S. 526 (2004), and a substantial body of subsequent bankruptcy-court decisions have construed the term narrowly to exclude paralegals working directly under attorney supervision, but broadly to include "typing services," document-preparation businesses, online petition-preparation services, and others who prepare bankruptcy documents for compensation outside the attorney-supervision relationship.

The Dismissal-Causation Element

A distinctive feature of Section 156 is that the offense is not complete until a case or related proceeding has been dismissed because of the preparer's knowing disregard. The causation requirement gives the dismissal order itself an effective gatekeeping function: a Section 156 prosecution generally awaits, and is built upon, a bankruptcy-court dismissal order that identifies BPP misconduct as a cause of dismissal.

This structural feature explains why Section 156 prosecutions are relatively rare compared to civil enforcement under 11 U.S.C. § 110. Most BPP misconduct can be addressed through civil fines and disgorgement under Section 110 without ever producing a dismissal; the criminal track is reserved for the comparatively narrow set of cases where the disregard is both knowing and case-fatal.

Interplay with the Civil Regime of 11 U.S.C. § 110

11 U.S.C. § 110 imposes a layered set of civil requirements on bankruptcy petition preparers, including:

  • Signing every document prepared for filing and printing the preparer's name and address on it (§ 110(b)(1));
  • Furnishing the debtor with a copy of every document the moment it is signed (§ 110(d));
  • Not collecting court fees (§ 110(g));
  • Filing a declaration of compensation under penalty of perjury (§ 110(h));
  • Not using the word "legal" or any similar term in advertising (§ 110(f));
  • Not offering legal advice (§ 110(e)(2)).

Civil enforcement is handled by the bankruptcy court on motion of the United States Trustee, a chapter 7 trustee, or a debtor. Civil remedies include statutory fines (typically up to $500 per violation, with treble damages in some categories), disgorgement of fees, and permanent injunctions barring the preparer from acting as a BPP under § 110(j).

Section 156 supplies the criminal escalation pathway for the same regulatory subject. A preparer who has been the subject of repeated civil enforcement under § 110, who ignores court orders restricting practice, who structures business to evade BPP disclosure rules, or whose disregard is so substantial that it produces a dismissal, becomes a candidate for Section 156 prosecution. The United States Trustee Program publishes annual statistics on BPP civil enforcement, including injunction orders and disgorgement totals, in its Report to Congress.

Mens Rea: "Knowing"

Section 156 requires that the disregard have been "knowing." The statute does not require the heightened "fraudulent" intent that Sections 152 and 153 require. The lower mens rea reflects the regulatory character of Section 156 and the consumer-protection purpose of the BPP regime: the offense targets reckless or willful disregard of regulatory requirements, not specific intent to defraud.

The lower mens rea is one of the reasons the offense is a misdemeanor with only a one-year maximum. The fraudulent-intent felonies of Sections 152 and 153 are available where the conduct rises to that level; Section 156 occupies the regulatory-compliance space below.

Penalties and Sentencing

A Section 156 violation is punishable by a fine under Title 18, imprisonment for not more than 1 year, or both. Because the maximum is 1 year, the offense is a Class A misdemeanor under 18 U.S.C. § 3559(a)(6). Sentencing typically proceeds under U.S.S.G. § 2J1.7 or the residual fraud guideline at § 2B1.1, depending on the nature of the underlying disregard and the loss amount.

Conviction triggers an automatic injunction-eligibility predicate under 11 U.S.C. § 110(j) and supports debarment from future BPP activity. Restitution under 18 U.S.C. § 3663A is available where the disregard caused identifiable financial loss to the debtor or to other identifiable victims.

Related Provisions

  • 18 U.S.C. § 157 - Bankruptcy fraud (which may apply where a preparer's conduct rises to the level of a scheme).

Historical Note

Sections 110 and 156 were enacted together in the Bankruptcy Reform Act of 1994 to respond to concerns that non-attorney petition-preparation businesses were proliferating, often misleading consumer debtors as to the scope of the services they were providing and the legal advice the preparers were qualified to give. Congress declined to authorize licensing of non-attorney preparers but imposed disclosure and conduct rules backed by civil fines and the misdemeanor escalation of Section 156. The framework has been amended periodically since 1994 to update fine amounts and clarify scope, most notably in the Bankruptcy Abuse Prevention and Consumer Protection Act of 2005.
