18 U.S.C. Section 153 - Embezzlement Against Estate

Doctrinal overview of the federal embezzlement statute that reaches bankruptcy trustees, custodians, attorneys, agents, and other officers entrusted with estate property. Five-year maximum, with scope extending to debtor-in-possession personnel.

Home › Bankruptcy Crimes › Section 153

What Is Section 153?

Section 153 of Title 18 criminalizes the knowing and fraudulent appropriation, embezzlement, spending, transfer, or concealment of any property or recorded information of a bankruptcy estate by a defined class of fiduciaries. Unlike Section 152, which reaches conduct by debtors, creditors, and others external to the estate's administration, Section 153 is specifically directed at the persons charged with administering the estate.

The statute occupies a focused niche in the federal bankruptcy-crimes regime: it criminalizes the misuse of estate property by the very officers entrusted with its preservation. As a doctrinal matter, it parallels other federal embezzlement statutes that protect federally administered property funds (such as 18 U.S.C. § 656 covering bank embezzlement and 18 U.S.C. § 666 covering programs receiving federal funds), but is purpose-built for the bankruptcy context.

Official citation: 18 U.S.C. § 153 (embezzlement against estate).

Statutory Text and Elements

Section 153(a) provides: "A person described in subsection (b) who knowingly and fraudulently appropriates to the person's own use, embezzles, spends, or transfers any property or secretes or destroys any document belonging to the estate of a debtor shall be fined under this title, imprisoned not more than 5 years, or both."

The elements the government must prove beyond a reasonable doubt are:

  1. The defendant is a person described in subsection (b) (the scope provision);
  2. The defendant knowingly and fraudulently engaged in one of the enumerated acts (appropriation, embezzlement, spending, transfer, secreting, or destruction);
  3. The object of the act was property or a document belonging to the estate of a debtor.

Scope: Who Is Covered Under Section 153(b)

Section 153(b) defines the class of persons subject to the statute. The covered persons include:

  • A trustee, custodian, marshal, attorney, or other officer of the court;
  • An agent, employee, or other person engaged by such an officer to perform a service with respect to the estate.

The reference to "attorney" covers counsel for trustees and counsel for the estate generally. Where the trustee retains counsel, paraprofessionals, accountants, brokers, appraisers, or other professionals under 11 U.S.C. § 327, those professionals' employees who handle estate property fall within Section 153(b)'s "agent, employee, or other person engaged by such an officer" language.

Debtor-in-Possession Personnel

In a chapter 11 case, the debtor-in-possession exercises the rights, powers, and duties of a trustee under 11 U.S.C. § 1107. Courts have recognized that officers, directors, and managing employees of a debtor-in-possession who handle estate property fall within the trustee-like fiduciary scope of Section 153(b), although the case law on this point is comparatively thin and varies by circuit.

Where a debtor proceeds under Subchapter V of chapter 11, the debtor remains in possession under 11 U.S.C. § 1184, and the Subchapter V trustee under 11 U.S.C. § 1183 takes on a more limited supervisory role. The Subchapter V trustee, like any trustee, is plainly within Section 153(b)'s scope.

Mens Rea

Section 153 requires that the conduct have been undertaken "knowingly and fraudulently," the same formulation that appears in Section 152. The defendant must have been aware that the property belonged to the estate and acted with intent to deceive or defraud. Negligent commingling, inadvertent overpayments, or mistaken classifications of property are not the criminal embezzlement contemplated by Section 153.

Specific intent is typically inferred from circumstantial evidence including the size of the diversion, the absence of contemporaneous documentation, false explanations offered to the court or United States Trustee, structuring of payments to evade detection, and any pattern of similar conduct across multiple cases.

Penalties and Restitution

A Section 153 violation is punishable by a fine under Title 18, imprisonment for not more than 5 years, or both. Restitution to the estate (and ultimately to defrauded creditors) is mandatory under 18 U.S.C. § 3663A for fraud offenses with identifiable victims.

For trustees and other officers, conviction routinely triggers removal under 11 U.S.C. § 324, disbarment from the panel of private trustees, disgorgement of fees, and state-bar discipline (including disbarment). For professionals retained under 11 U.S.C. § 327, conviction supports denial of compensation under 11 U.S.C. § 328(c) and disqualification from future appointments.

Trustees in the United States Trustee panel-trustee program must maintain a blanket bond under 11 U.S.C. § 322 and Bankruptcy Rule 2010. A Section 153 conviction typically triggers a claim against that bond, and bond surety carriers may pursue subrogation against the convicted trustee personally.

Relationship to Civil Surcharge and Removal Remedies

Criminal liability under Section 153 is distinct from civil remedies available in the bankruptcy court. The bankruptcy court may surcharge a defaulting trustee under 11 U.S.C. § 704 (chapter 7) or analogous provisions for other chapters, remove a trustee under 11 U.S.C. § 324 for cause, deny or disgorge fees under 11 U.S.C. § 326 and § 328(c), and refer matters for criminal investigation under 18 U.S.C. § 3057 (which directs bankruptcy judges who have reasonable grounds to believe a violation has occurred to report it to the appropriate United States Attorney).

The civil and criminal tracks may proceed in parallel. A trustee surcharged or removed in the bankruptcy court is not thereby insulated from criminal prosecution, and a criminal conviction is admissible in subsequent civil proceedings under the doctrine of issue preclusion as to the elements necessarily decided by the conviction.

Statute of Limitations and Procedural Posture

Section 153 prosecutions are governed by the general federal criminal limitations period of 5 years under 18 U.S.C. § 3282. Unlike Section 152(1) concealment-of-assets prosecutions, Section 153 does not benefit from the discharge-deferral rule of 18 U.S.C. § 3284, because the conduct addressed by Section 153 typically occurs in the course of administration rather than at filing.

The five-year period generally runs from the date of the embezzling act, although where the conduct constitutes a continuing offense (e.g., a continuous course of conduct in which estate funds are systematically diverted), the limitations clock may not begin until the last act in the course of conduct.

Historical Note

Section 153 originated in Section 29(b) of the Bankruptcy Act of 1898, which criminalized a more compact set of acts by trustees and other officers. The 1948 codification of Title 18 moved the provision to its present location. The 1994 Bankruptcy Reform Act updated Section 153 to align its scope with the modern Title 11 fiduciary structure, including the addition of United States Trustees to the persons-protected language and the expansion of the predicate-act list to cover modern forms of misappropriation. The structural unity of the offense - knowing, fraudulent misuse of estate property by a fiduciary - has remained constant since 1898.
