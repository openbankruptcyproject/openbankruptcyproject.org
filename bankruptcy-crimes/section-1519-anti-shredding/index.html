18 U.S.C. Section 1519 - Destruction, Alteration, or Falsification of Records in Federal Investigations and Bankruptcy

Doctrinal overview of the Sarbanes-Oxley anti-shredding statute: a twenty-year maximum, an "in contemplation of" reach that does not require a pending investigation, and a comparison with the bankruptcy-specific records offenses in 18 U.S.C. Section 152(8) and (9).

What Is Section 1519?

Section 1519 of Title 18 is the anti-shredding statute enacted as Section 802 of the Sarbanes-Oxley Act of 2002 in the wake of the Enron-Andersen document-destruction scandal. The statute creates a broad obstruction-of-justice offense for the knowing destruction, alteration, or falsification of records with intent to impede, obstruct, or influence a federal investigation, including investigations of cases under Title 11.

Although enacted as a securities-fraud-response statute, Section 1519's text expressly extends to bankruptcy. The statute is materially broader and more severe than the bankruptcy-specific records offenses in 18 U.S.C. § 152(8) and (9), and is increasingly an option of choice for prosecutors addressing document destruction tied to bankruptcy cases.

Official citation: 18 U.S.C. § 1519 (destruction, alteration, or falsification of records in federal investigations and bankruptcy).

Statutory Text and Elements

Section 1519 provides in full: "Whoever knowingly alters, destroys, mutilates, conceals, covers up, falsifies, or makes a false entry in any record, document, or tangible object with the intent to impede, obstruct, or influence the investigation or proper administration of any matter within the jurisdiction of any department or agency of the United States or any case filed under title 11, or in relation to or contemplation of any such matter or case, shall be fined under this title, imprisoned not more than 20 years, or both."

The elements the government must prove beyond a reasonable doubt are:

  1. The defendant knowingly altered, destroyed, mutilated, concealed, covered up, falsified, or made a false entry in;
  2. Any record, document, or tangible object;
  3. With the intent to impede, obstruct, or influence;
  4. The investigation or proper administration of any matter within the jurisdiction of a U.S. department or agency, or any case filed under Title 11;
  5. Or in relation to or contemplation of any such matter or case.

Breadth: "Any Record, Document, or Tangible Object"

Section 1519 reaches "any record, document, or tangible object." The phrase has been the subject of significant Supreme Court attention. In Yates v. United States, 574 U.S. 528 (2015), a plurality of the Court construed "tangible object" narrowly, holding that the statute's noscitur a sociis context confines "tangible object" to objects used to record or preserve information (e.g., a hard drive) and not, in the case at hand, undersized fish that had been thrown overboard. The plurality's narrowing construction is significant for non-bankruptcy applications but does not constrict the statute's reach as applied to documentary destruction in bankruptcy cases, which falls squarely within the statute's core.

The "In Contemplation" Clause

The most consequential feature of Section 1519 in the bankruptcy context is the "in relation to or contemplation of" clause. Section 1519, unlike many obstruction statutes, does not require that an investigation or proceeding be pending at the time of the obstructive act. It is sufficient that the destruction or alteration occur in "contemplation" of a matter within federal jurisdiction or a case under Title 11.

This reach distinguishes Section 1519 from older obstruction statutes such as 18 U.S.C. § 1503 and § 1505, both of which require a pending judicial proceeding or pending agency proceeding at the time of the obstructive conduct. The Supreme Court in Arthur Andersen LLP v. United States, 544 U.S. 696 (2005), reversed a Section 1512(b) obstruction conviction on the ground that the jury instructions failed to require a sufficient nexus to a federal proceeding; Section 1519 was deliberately drafted to avoid that limitation by extending criminal liability to obstructive conduct undertaken in contemplation of a future investigation or proceeding.

For bankruptcy applications, the practical significance is that document destruction undertaken before a bankruptcy petition is filed, but in contemplation of an anticipated Title 11 case, falls within Section 1519. Section 152(8) of Title 18, by contrast, also reaches conduct "in contemplation" of a Title 11 filing, but Section 152(8) requires fraudulent intent and carries a five-year maximum, whereas Section 1519 requires only intent to obstruct and carries a twenty-year maximum.

Mens Rea

Section 1519 requires that the defendant act "knowingly" (with respect to the act of destruction, alteration, etc.) and "with the intent to impede, obstruct, or influence" the relevant matter. The statute does not require the more demanding "corruptly" mens rea that appears in 18 U.S.C. § 1512(c)(2), and it does not require the "fraudulent" intent that appears in 18 U.S.C. § 152(8). The defendant need not have known of a specific pending investigation; it is sufficient that the defendant acted in contemplation of an investigation or matter within federal jurisdiction.

Several circuits have held that Section 1519's mens rea is satisfied where the defendant subjectively believed that an investigation or proceeding was likely, even if no investigation was in fact then under way. The doctrine traces directly to the "in contemplation" clause discussed above.

Comparison with 18 U.S.C. § 152(8) and (9)

The bankruptcy-crimes regime in 18 U.S.C. § 152 includes two paragraphs that address records-related conduct, both of which overlap with Section 1519:

  • § 152(8) punishes the knowing and fraudulent concealment, destruction, mutilation, falsification, or making of false entries in recorded information relating to a debtor's property or financial affairs, after the filing of a Title 11 case or in contemplation thereof. Maximum: 5 years.
  • § 152(9) punishes the knowing and fraudulent withholding of recorded information from a trustee or other officer of the court entitled to its possession, after the filing of a Title 11 case. Maximum: 5 years.

The differences from Section 1519 are:

  1. Sentence. Section 1519 carries a 20-year maximum; Section 152(8) and (9) each carry a 5-year maximum. The disparity reflects Section 1519's broader obstruction-of-justice posture compared to Section 152's bankruptcy-specific framework.
  2. Mens rea. Section 1519 requires only intent to obstruct; Section 152(8) and (9) require fraudulent intent. The lower scienter under Section 1519 substantially eases the government's proof burden.
  3. Scope of records. Section 1519 reaches "any record, document, or tangible object," not limited to records relating to the debtor's property or financial affairs.
  4. Temporal reach. Both Section 1519 and Section 152(8) extend to conduct undertaken in contemplation of a Title 11 case. Section 152(9), by contrast, applies only after filing.

The two regimes are not mutually exclusive. Conduct that violates Section 152(8) or (9) frequently also violates Section 1519, and an indictment may charge both. Charging decisions reflect the comparative strengths of each statute on the particular facts and the prosecutorial strategy in the case at hand.

Penalties and Sentencing

Section 1519 carries a statutory maximum of 20 years' imprisonment, a fine under Title 18, or both. It is the most severe penalty available among the records-related federal criminal statutes. Sentencing typically proceeds under U.S.S.G. § 2J1.2 (obstruction of justice), with a base offense level of 14 and enhancements for substantial interference with the administration of justice, extensive scope, or causing or threatening physical injury.

Restitution under 18 U.S.C. § 3663A is available where the obstructive conduct caused identifiable financial loss to identifiable victims. Although Section 1519 prosecutions historically arose primarily in financial-fraud and environmental-investigation contexts, the bankruptcy clause has been invoked with growing frequency over the past decade.

Statute of Limitations

The general 5-year federal criminal limitations period of 18 U.S.C. § 3282 applies to Section 1519. Unlike Section 152(1) concealment-of-assets prosecutions, Section 1519 does not benefit from the discharge-deferral rule of 18 U.S.C. § 3284. The limitations period generally runs from the date of the obstructive act, although where the conduct is continuing, the period may run from the last act in the continuous course.

Historical Note

Section 1519 was enacted as Section 802 of the Sarbanes-Oxley Act of 2002. The drafting was motivated by the document-destruction conduct of Arthur Andersen LLP during the SEC investigation of Enron Corporation, and by the perception that existing obstruction statutes (most prominently 18 U.S.C. § 1503 and § 1505) had been read by the courts to require too narrow a temporal nexus to a pending proceeding. Section 1519's drafters expressly addressed bankruptcy by including "any case filed under title 11" alongside federal-agency matters in the protected-matter clause, recognizing that the same accounting and document-destruction conduct that had concerned Congress in the securities context arose with at least equal frequency in major bankruptcy cases. The Supreme Court's narrowing construction of "tangible object" in Yates and its restrictive reading of Section 1512(c)(2) in Fischer have not impaired Section 1519's core application to documentary destruction in bankruptcy investigations.
