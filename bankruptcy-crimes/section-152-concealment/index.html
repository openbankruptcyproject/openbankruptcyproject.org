18 U.S.C. Section 152 - Concealment of Assets; False Oaths and Claims; Bribery

Doctrinal overview of the principal federal bankruptcy-crimes statute: nine numbered offenses, fraudulent-intent mens rea, five-year maximum, and the framework that organizes most bankruptcy-fraud prosecutions in the United States.

Home › Bankruptcy Crimes › Section 152

What Is Section 152?

Section 152 of Title 18 is the central bankruptcy-crimes statute. It enumerates nine distinct offenses, each addressing a particular form of fraudulent conduct directed at the bankruptcy system. Although the section is organized as a single statute, courts and prosecutors treat each numbered subsection as a separate offense with its own elements, and an indictment routinely charges multiple subsections as separate counts arising from the same factual transaction.

Section 152 has its origins in the 1898 Bankruptcy Act and was carried into the modern federal criminal code with successive revisions in 1948, 1978, 1994, and 2005. The current structure reflects the Bankruptcy Reform Act of 1994, which broke the prior consolidated bankruptcy-fraud provision into the nine-paragraph form that appears today.

Official citation: 18 U.S.C. § 152 (concealment of assets; false oaths and claims; bribery).

The Nine Numbered Offenses

Section 152 enumerates the following nine offenses, each punishable by a fine, imprisonment for not more than 5 years, or both:

  1. § 152(1) - Concealment of assets. Knowingly and fraudulently concealing from a custodian, trustee, marshal, or other court officer charged with the control or custody of property, or from creditors or the United States Trustee, any property belonging to the estate of a debtor.
  2. § 152(2) - False oath or account. Knowingly and fraudulently making a false oath or account in or in relation to any case under Title 11.
  3. § 152(3) - False declaration, certificate, verification, or statement. Knowingly and fraudulently making a false declaration, certificate, verification, or statement under penalty of perjury as permitted under 28 U.S.C. § 1746 in or in relation to any case under Title 11.
  4. § 152(4) - False claim. Knowingly and fraudulently presenting any false claim for proof against the estate of a debtor, or using any such claim in any case under Title 11, in a personal capacity or as or through an agent, proxy, or attorney.
  5. § 152(5) - Receipt of property to defeat the Code. Knowingly and fraudulently receiving any material amount of property from a debtor after the filing of a case under Title 11, with intent to defeat the provisions of Title 11.
  6. § 152(6) - Extortion or bribery to act or refrain from acting. Knowingly and fraudulently giving, offering, receiving, or attempting to obtain any money or property, remuneration, compensation, reward, advantage, or promise thereof for acting or forbearing to act in any case under Title 11.
  7. § 152(7) - Fraudulent transfer or concealment in contemplation of bankruptcy. Knowingly and fraudulently transferring or concealing any of the debtor's property or the property of any other person, in contemplation of a case under Title 11 by or against the debtor, or with intent to defeat the provisions of Title 11.
  8. § 152(8) - Destruction or falsification of records. After the filing of a case under Title 11 or in contemplation thereof, knowingly and fraudulently concealing, destroying, mutilating, falsifying, or making a false entry in any recorded information (including books, documents, records, and papers) relating to the property or financial affairs of a debtor.
  9. § 152(9) - Withholding of recorded information from a trustee or officer. After the filing of a case under Title 11, knowingly and fraudulently withholding from a custodian, trustee, marshal, or other officer of the court or a United States Trustee entitled to its possession any recorded information (including books, documents, records, and papers) relating to the property or financial affairs of a debtor.

Mens Rea: "Knowingly and Fraudulently"

Every Section 152 offense requires that the conduct have been undertaken "knowingly and fraudulently." This two-part scienter element distinguishes Section 152 from mere negligent misstatements or inadvertent omissions, neither of which suffices for criminal liability.

The Tenth Circuit and other courts have generally construed "knowingly" to require awareness of the act and its general character, and "fraudulently" to require intent to deceive a court officer, the United States Trustee, or creditors. Mistakes of fact, good-faith disclosures later shown to be incorrect, and amended schedules filed to correct prior omissions are common defense vehicles; an honest, even if careless, omission does not satisfy the fraudulent-intent prong.

Because intent is rarely susceptible to direct proof, the government commonly establishes fraudulent intent through circumstantial evidence including pattern of conduct, magnitude of the omission, timing relative to the petition, false explanations, and inconsistencies between schedules and contemporaneous business records.

Penalties and Sentencing Framework

Each Section 152 offense carries a statutory maximum of 5 years' imprisonment, a fine under Title 18 (currently $250,000 for individuals and $500,000 for organizations under 18 U.S.C. § 3571), or both. Restitution under the Mandatory Victims Restitution Act of 1996 (18 U.S.C. § 3663A) is required where there is an identifiable victim and the offense involves fraud or deceit.

Sentencing in the federal system is guided by the United States Sentencing Guidelines. Bankruptcy-fraud offenses are most commonly sentenced under U.S.S.G. § 2B1.1 (theft, property destruction, and fraud), with a base offense level of 6 or 7 and a loss-table enhancement under § 2B1.1(b)(1). Additional enhancements may apply for sophisticated means, abuse of position of trust, obstruction of justice, or violation of a judicial order.

Convictions are routinely accompanied by collateral consequences including bar discipline, loss of professional license, and (for non-citizens) immigration consequences as a crime involving moral turpitude or an aggravated felony where the loss exceeds the statutory threshold.

Statute of Limitations

The general federal criminal limitations period of 5 years under 18 U.S.C. § 3282 applies to Section 152 offenses. The 1994 Bankruptcy Reform Act, however, added 18 U.S.C. § 3284, which provides that for the offense of concealment of assets, the limitations period runs from the date of the discharge or denial of discharge of the debtor, not from the date of the concealing act itself.

The practical effect of Section 3284 is to substantially extend the period during which a Section 152(1) concealment prosecution may be brought, particularly in chapter 7 cases where discharge typically issues only months after the petition, and in chapter 11 and 13 cases where discharge may not enter until years after the filing.

Prosecutorial Framework

Section 152 prosecutions are typically initiated by referral from the United States Trustee Program. Each United States Trustee field office maintains a criminal-enforcement unit that screens potential matters, prepares a referral package documenting the suspected offense, and forwards the package to the local United States Attorney's Office. The referral package customarily includes copies of relevant schedules, statements of financial affairs, 341-meeting transcripts, and supporting documentary evidence.

Other referral sources include private chapter 7 and chapter 11 trustees, creditors who develop evidence of fraud during the case, the Federal Bureau of Investigation, and the Internal Revenue Service Criminal Investigation Division where the conduct overlaps with tax offenses. The Executive Office for United States Trustees publishes annual statistics on criminal-referral volumes and outcomes in its Report to Congress, available at justice.gov/ust.

Once referred, a Section 152 matter proceeds through ordinary federal grand-jury investigation. Charging decisions are committed to the discretion of the United States Attorney's Office, in coordination with the Fraud Section of the Department of Justice Criminal Division for matters with multi-district scope or that fit within national enforcement priorities.

Related Provisions

Section 152 operates in the Chapter 9 ("Bankruptcy") cluster of Title 18, which also includes:

  • 18 U.S.C. § 153 - Embezzlement against estate by a trustee, custodian, marshal, or other officer.
  • 18 U.S.C. § 154 - Adverse interest and conduct of officers.
  • 18 U.S.C. § 155 - Fee agreements in cases under Title 11 and receiverships.
  • 18 U.S.C. § 156 - Knowing disregard of the bankruptcy law or rule by a bankruptcy petition preparer.
  • 18 U.S.C. § 157 - Bankruptcy fraud (scheme to defraud by means of a bankruptcy case).
  • 18 U.S.C. § 158 - Designation of United States attorneys and FBI agents to address abusive reaffirmations of debt and materially fraudulent statements.
  • 18 U.S.C. § 1519 - Destruction, alteration, or falsification of records in federal investigations (Sarbanes-Oxley anti-shredding statute).

Conduct that violates Section 152 frequently also implicates 18 U.S.C. § 157 (which treats the bankruptcy case itself as a vehicle for a broader scheme) and, where document destruction is involved, 18 U.S.C. § 1519 (which carries a 20-year maximum and a broader scienter formulation tied to obstruction of any federal investigation).

Historical Note

The first federal bankruptcy-crimes provisions appeared in the Bankruptcy Act of 1898 and were consolidated into Title 18 of the United States Code in 1948 as part of Reviser's Note revisions. The 1978 Bankruptcy Reform Act created the modern Bankruptcy Code in Title 11, but the criminal provisions remained in Title 18, where they continue to reside. The 1994 amendments expanded coverage to include conduct directed at United States Trustees (previously, the statute spoke only of court officers and creditors) and broke the prior unitary fraud provision into the nine-paragraph framework that organizes prosecutions today.
