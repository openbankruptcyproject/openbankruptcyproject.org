18 U.S.C. Section 157 - Bankruptcy Fraud

Doctrinal overview of the federal bankruptcy-fraud scheme statute: three sub-offenses, the "devised or intending to devise a scheme" framework, modeled on the mail and wire fraud statutes, and distinguished from the single-act offenses of Section 152.

Home › Bankruptcy Crimes › Section 157

What Is Section 157?

Section 157 of Title 18 is the bankruptcy-fraud-scheme statute. Added by Section 312 of the Violent Crime Control and Law Enforcement Act of 1994, it imports the mail-fraud and wire-fraud architecture ("devised or intending to devise a scheme or artifice to defraud") into the bankruptcy context. Where Section 152 punishes specific discrete acts (false oaths, concealment, false claims), Section 157 punishes the use of the bankruptcy system as a vehicle for a broader fraudulent scheme.

Section 157 is a relatively recent addition to the bankruptcy-crimes regime, but it has become a workhorse statute for prosecutions involving recurring or systemic bankruptcy abuse. Common Section 157 patterns include rent-skimming schemes built around serial filings of fractional-interest deed-transfer petitions, identity-theft bankruptcy filings used to extend automatic-stay protection over property the filer does not own, and bust-out schemes in which a debtor accumulates credit and then files to discharge it.

Official citation: 18 U.S.C. § 157 (bankruptcy fraud).

Statutory Text and the Three Sub-Offenses

Section 157 provides: "A person who, having devised or intending to devise a scheme or artifice to defraud and for the purpose of executing or concealing such a scheme or artifice or attempting to do so —

  1. files a petition under Title 11, including a fraudulent involuntary petition under section 303 of such title;
  2. files a document in a proceeding under Title 11; or
  3. makes a false or fraudulent representation, claim, or promise concerning or in relation to a proceeding under Title 11, at any time before or after the filing of the petition, or in relation to a proceeding falsely asserted to be pending under such title,

shall be fined under this title, imprisoned not more than 5 years, or both."

The three sub-offenses share the same predicate scheme element but differ in the conduct that triggers liability: filing a petition (§ 157(1)), filing a document in a proceeding (§ 157(2)), or making a representation, claim, or promise (§ 157(3)). The drafters borrowed the "devised or intending to devise a scheme or artifice to defraud" framework directly from 18 U.S.C. § 1341 (mail fraud) and § 1343 (wire fraud), and federal courts construe Section 157's scheme element by reference to mail-fraud and wire-fraud case law.

The Scheme Element

Section 157's distinguishing element is the scheme. Under mail-fraud and wire-fraud doctrine imported into Section 157, a "scheme to defraud" need not be elaborate but must involve a plan or pattern that contemplates fraudulent conduct, not merely an isolated false statement. The scheme need not have succeeded; an attempt to execute or conceal the scheme satisfies the statute.

The scheme requirement explains why Section 157 prosecutions tend to involve facts with a broader temporal or transactional sweep than Section 152 prosecutions. A single false answer on Schedule B is the paradigmatic Section 152(2) or (3) offense; a coordinated multi-petition rent-skimming pattern, an organized identity-theft bankruptcy ring, or a serial-filing scheme calculated to stall foreclosure proceedings is the paradigmatic Section 157 offense.

Distinction from Section 152

Although Sections 152 and 157 frequently appear in the same indictment, the two statutes target distinct conduct:

  • Section 152 punishes specific enumerated acts (false oaths, concealment of assets, false claims, document destruction, etc.) regardless of whether they are part of a broader scheme. Each of the nine paragraphs is a stand-alone offense.
  • Section 157 punishes the use of the bankruptcy system as a vehicle for a scheme. The scheme element is essential; without it, conduct may still violate Section 152 but does not reach Section 157.

Both statutes carry a five-year maximum, and conduct that violates both may be charged as separate counts under Blockburger v. United States, 284 U.S. 299 (1932), so long as the elements differ. The Sentencing Guidelines treat both as fraud offenses under U.S.S.G. § 2B1.1 and apply the loss table similarly, but the existence of a Section 157 scheme commonly supports a sophisticated-means enhancement under § 2B1.1(b)(10).

Mens Rea

Section 157 requires that the defendant have "devised or intend[ed] to devise a scheme or artifice to defraud" and have engaged in the predicate filing or representation "for the purpose of executing or concealing such a scheme or artifice." The specific-intent requirement parallels mail-fraud and wire-fraud doctrine: the government must prove that the defendant intended to defraud, that the scheme was material, and that the defendant acted with knowledge of its fraudulent character.

Because the petition or document filing is itself the actus reus, the government need not prove that the scheme caused identifiable loss to a victim. The offense is complete when the fraudulent filing or representation is made for the purpose of executing or concealing the scheme, even if the bankruptcy court ultimately detects and prevents the fraud.

Common Section 157 Fact Patterns

The Department of Justice and the Executive Office for United States Trustees have over time identified several recurring fact patterns that drive Section 157 enforcement:

  • Serial filings to stall foreclosure. A debtor (or an associate) files repeated petitions, sometimes under different names or different chapters, to invoke the automatic stay of 11 U.S.C. § 362 and delay a scheduled foreclosure sale, with no genuine intention to reorganize.
  • Fractional-interest deed transfers. A homeowner facing foreclosure transfers a fractional interest in the property to a third party who then files a bankruptcy petition, invoking the stay over property the petitioner does not meaningfully own.
  • Identity-theft filings. A petition is filed in the name of a person whose identity has been stolen, often as part of a broader credit-fraud or rent-skimming scheme.
  • Bust-out cases. A debtor systematically incurs credit-card and other unsecured debt with no intention of repayment, then files chapter 7 to discharge the debt.
  • Concealed-asset schemes. A debtor coordinates with associates to transfer or hide assets in advance of filing, then files schedules omitting the assets and representations consistent with the concealment scheme.

The first three fact patterns frequently produce dual indictments under Section 157 and the mail- and wire-fraud statutes (Sections 1341 and 1343), depending on the means used to execute the scheme.

Penalties and Sentencing

Section 157 carries a statutory maximum of 5 years' imprisonment per count, a fine under Title 18, or both. Restitution under 18 U.S.C. § 3663A is mandatory where there is an identifiable victim. Sentencing typically proceeds under U.S.S.G. § 2B1.1 with loss-table enhancements, often supplemented by enhancements for sophisticated means (§ 2B1.1(b)(10)), more than ten victims or substantial financial hardship (§ 2B1.1(b)(2)), and abuse of position of trust (§ 3B1.3) where the defendant is a fiduciary.

Conviction of a Section 157 scheme offense often produces meaningfully higher sentencing exposure than a Section 152 single-act conviction on comparable underlying facts, because the loss table aggregates losses across the scheme and sophisticated-means and victim-count enhancements apply more readily to scheme conduct.

Statute of Limitations

Section 157 prosecutions are governed by the general federal criminal limitations period of 5 years under 18 U.S.C. § 3282. Unlike Section 152(1) concealment-of-assets prosecutions, Section 157 does not benefit from the discharge-deferral rule of 18 U.S.C. § 3284. The limitations period generally runs from the date of the predicate filing or representation, although where the scheme is continuing the period may run from the last act in furtherance of the scheme.

Historical Note

Section 157 was enacted in 1994 in response to a perception among prosecutors that Section 152's single-act framework was poorly suited to the systemic and recurring forms of bankruptcy abuse that had become prominent in the 1980s and early 1990s, particularly serial-filing and identity-theft fact patterns. The drafters deliberately modeled the new statute on the mail-fraud and wire-fraud statutes to give prosecutors the same scheme-based architectural framework that had proved effective in those contexts. Subsequent enforcement practice has confirmed that Section 157 occupies a distinct prosecutorial niche from Section 152, with the two statutes commonly charged together when both single-act offenses and a broader scheme are present.
