If you already received a bankruptcy discharge, you must usually wait before a new discharge is available. The waiting period runs from the filing date of your prior case to the filing date of your new case: 2 years (prior Chapter 13 to new Chapter 13), 4 years (prior Chapter 7, 11, or 12 to new Chapter 13), 6 years (prior Chapter 13 to new Chapter 7, with a 100 percent or 70 percent payment exception), or 8 years (prior Chapter 7 or 11 to new Chapter 7). The Four Successive-Discharge Bars (the 2-4-6-8 Rule)

Federal law limits how soon you can receive a second bankruptcy discharge after receiving a first one. There are four separate timing bars. Each one is measured from the filing date of the prior case to the filing date of the new case, unless noted. The shorthand is the 2-4-6-8 rule.

Prior Chapter 7 or 11 to a new Chapter 7: the 8-year bar

Under 11 U.S.C. 727(a)(8), you cannot receive a Chapter 7 discharge if you already received a discharge in a Chapter 7 or Chapter 11 case that was filed within 8 years before the new Chapter 7 is filed. The 8-year clock runs from the prior filing date to the new filing date. See our guide to Section 727 and the Chapter 7 discharge.

Prior Chapter 13 to a new Chapter 7: the 6-year bar with a payment exception

Under 11 U.S.C. 727(a)(9), if your prior discharge was in a Chapter 13 (or Chapter 12) case filed within 6 years before the new Chapter 7, the new Chapter 7 discharge is generally barred. There is an important exception: the 6-year bar does not apply if payments under the prior plan totaled at least 100 percent of the allowed unsecured claims, or at least 70 percent of those claims under a plan that was proposed in good faith and was your best effort. If either condition is met, a Chapter 7 discharge may be available even inside the 6-year window.

Exception in plain terms: A prior Chapter 13 that paid creditors in full (100 percent), or paid 70 percent or more under a good-faith best-effort plan, can let you obtain a new Chapter 7 discharge before 6 years have passed. Whether your prior plan qualifies is a fact question that turns on what the prior plan actually paid.

Prior Chapter 7, 11, or 12 to a new Chapter 13: the 4-year bar

Under 11 U.S.C. 1328(f)(1), you cannot receive a Chapter 13 discharge if you received a discharge in a Chapter 7, Chapter 11, or Chapter 12 case filed within 4 years before the new Chapter 13. Again, the clock runs filing date to filing date.

Prior Chapter 13 to a new Chapter 13: the 2-year bar

Under 11 U.S.C. 1328(f)(2), you cannot receive a Chapter 13 discharge if you received a prior Chapter 13 discharge in a case filed within 2 years before the new one. Because most Chapter 13 plans run 3 to 5 years, this 2-year bar rarely blocks a later Chapter 13 discharge in practice.

Two Things People Get Wrong

The clock runs from the filing date, not the discharge date. Every one of these bars uses the phrase a case filed under. If your prior Chapter 7 was filed on March 1, 2019, the 8-year bar lifts on March 1, 2027, even if your discharge was not entered until several months after you filed.

These are discharge bars, not filing bars. You can still file a new case during the waiting period. Filing triggers the automatic stay, which can stop a foreclosure, garnishment, or repossession, and a Chapter 13 plan can cure arrears over time. The only thing the timing bar takes away is the discharge of personal liability at the end of the new case. Separate filing limits, such as Section 109(g) after certain dismissals, can also apply.

Dismissed without a discharge? These four bars apply only when you actually received a discharge in the prior case. If the prior case was dismissed without a discharge, the 2-4-6-8 bars do not apply. Other rules may still affect a quick refiling.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the 2-4-6-8 rule in bankruptcy?

The 2-4-6-8 rule is shorthand for the four successive-discharge timing bars. A prior Chapter 13 to a new Chapter 13 is a 2-year bar (Section 1328(f)(2)). A prior Chapter 7, 11, or 12 to a new Chapter 13 is a 4-year bar (Section 1328(f)(1)). A prior Chapter 13 to a new Chapter 7 is a 6-year bar with a payment exception (Section 727(a)(9)). A prior Chapter 7 or 11 to a new Chapter 7 is an 8-year bar (Section 727(a)(8)). Every period is measured from the filing date of the prior case to the filing date of the new case.

Does the clock run from the filing date or the discharge date?

It runs from the filing date of the prior case to the filing date of the new case, not from the discharge date. Each statute uses the phrase a case filed under. So if a prior Chapter 7 was filed on March 1, 2019, the 8-year bar lifts on March 1, 2027, regardless of when the discharge was actually entered.

Can I still file bankruptcy during the bar period?

Yes. These are discharge bars, not filing bars. You may file a new case during the bar period and still receive the automatic stay, stop a foreclosure, and treat debts through a plan. You simply will not receive a discharge of your personal liability at the end if the timing bar applies. Other filing limits, such as Section 109(g) after certain dismissals, are separate.

What is the exception to the 6-year Chapter 13 to Chapter 7 bar?

Under Section 727(a)(9), the 6-year bar after a prior Chapter 13 does not apply if payments under that prior plan totaled at least 100 percent of the allowed unsecured claims, or at least 70 percent of those claims under a plan that was proposed in good faith and was your best effort. If either condition is met, a new Chapter 7 discharge may be available even inside the 6-year window.

Is the relevant date the same in every district?

The statutory periods are federal and uniform, but how a court treats edge cases (such as conversions between chapters, refiled cases, or the precise day-count) can vary. The controlling date in your situation can depend on local rules and case law. Always confirm the exact date with a licensed bankruptcy attorney before relying on it.
