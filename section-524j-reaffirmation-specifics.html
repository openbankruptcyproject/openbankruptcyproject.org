11 U.S.C. Section 524(j) - Mortgage Statements and Reaffirmation Specifics

The narrow BAPCPA-era safe harbor permitting home-mortgage holders to send ordinary periodic statements and accept voluntary payments after discharge - and what falls outside the safe harbor.

What Is Section 524(j)?

Section 524(j) is a narrow safe harbor inside the discharge-injunction structure of Section 524. It permits a holder of a claim secured by the debtor's principal residence to communicate with the discharged debtor about the mortgage and accept voluntary payments, without violating the Section 524(a)(2) discharge injunction, provided certain conditions are met. The provision was added to address the practical reality that many Chapter 7 debtors choose to retain their home and continue making payments without formally reaffirming the mortgage.

Outside of 524(j), the discharge injunction prohibits any "act, to collect, recover or offset any such debt as a personal liability of the debtor." Sending account statements, late notices, or arrearage demands to a discharged debtor would ordinarily be presumptively coercive and sanctionable. Section 524(j) carves out a defined zone where such ordinary servicing communications are permitted.

Official citation: 11 U.S.C. § 524(j)

The Three Statutory Conditions

Section 524(j) requires all three of the following:

  • (j)(1): The creditor holds a security interest in the principal residence of the debtor;
  • (j)(2): The creditor (or its assignee) seeks "periodic payments associated with a valid security interest" in lieu of pursuit of in rem relief to enforce the lien; and
  • (j)(3): The creditor's communication is "in the ordinary course of business between the creditor and the debtor" and is not an attempt to collect the debt as a personal liability of the debtor.

If any of these conditions is missing, the communication falls outside the safe harbor and is governed by the general 524(a)(2) injunction.

What the Safe Harbor Permits

Under Section 524(j), a home-mortgage holder may, after discharge, send:

  • Periodic billing statements showing current payment amount, escrow figures, payment due date, and outstanding principal balance;
  • Notices of escrow adjustments, including annual escrow analyses required by RESPA;
  • Year-end mortgage-interest statements (IRS Form 1098);
  • Insurance and tax notices showing flood insurance, hazard insurance, and property tax obligations;
  • Routine servicing communications such as transfer-of-servicing notices, change-of-address notifications, and customer-service correspondence.

The creditor may also accept voluntary payments from the discharged debtor and apply them to the loan account. The CFPB has issued guidance and amended Regulation X periodic-statement rules to align with the Section 524(j) framework, recognizing that most discharged debtors who retain their home expect and benefit from continued statements.

What the Safe Harbor Does Not Permit

Section 524(j) does not protect:

  • Demand letters, collection calls, or threats of personal-liability collection;
  • Late notices or default notices that imply personal liability beyond the in rem remedy;
  • Suit, judgment, or wage-garnishment efforts seeking personal recovery on the discharged debt;
  • Negative credit reporting that misrepresents the debt as a personal obligation (the underlying personal liability is discharged; only the in rem lien survives);
  • Statements that omit required bankruptcy-discharge disclosures where Regulation X / Regulation Z requires them.

A creditor that strays beyond ordinary-course servicing into collection-coercive conduct loses 524(j) protection and exposes itself to contempt sanctions under Taggart v. Lorenzen, 587 U.S. 554 (2019), which establishes that a contempt sanction for violating a discharge injunction requires "no fair ground of doubt" that the conduct was unlawful.

"Ride-Through" and the Practical Reality

Although the Code formally requires either reaffirmation or redemption to retain personal liability on a secured debt, courts have long recognized a "ride-through" or "fourth option": continue to make payments without reaffirming, and the creditor's lien survives but the personal liability is discharged. Section 524(j) effectively codifies the servicing dimension of ride-through for mortgages, allowing the loan to continue functioning operationally even though personal liability has been wiped out. BAPCPA's 2005 amendments tightened ride-through for personal property (see Section 521(a)(2) and (a)(6)), but the home-mortgage ride-through remains common practice.

Reaffirmation Distinguished: Section 524(c) and (d)

Section 524(c) provides the procedure for an enforceable reaffirmation agreement: written agreement, attorney certification (or, if pro se, court approval after a hearing), disclosures required under 524(k), and timely filing. A properly reaffirmed debt remains personally enforceable after discharge. Section 524(j) is fundamentally different: it does not create or revive any personal liability. The discharged debt remains discharged; 524(j) simply permits routine post-discharge servicing communications about the surviving lien.

The distinction matters in default scenarios: if the debtor reaffirmed, the creditor may sue for any deficiency after foreclosure; if the debtor merely rode through with 524(j) servicing, the creditor is limited to foreclosure proceeds and cannot pursue a deficiency judgment.

Common Procedural Postures

Section 524(j) issues typically arise in the following contexts:

  • Post-discharge contempt motions where a homeowner alleges the mortgage servicer's statements crossed into collection coercion;
  • Class-action discharge-injunction litigation against national servicers over statement formatting and disclosure language;
  • CFPB or state-AG enforcement actions under the FDCPA or state UDAP statutes targeting servicer communications with discharged debtors;
  • Loss-mitigation disputes where servicer post-discharge documentation requirements implicate ordinary-course-servicing analysis;
  • Credit-reporting disputes about how discharged mortgage debt should be coded with the consumer reporting agencies.
