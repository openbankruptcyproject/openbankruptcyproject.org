Federal Rule of Bankruptcy Procedure 9019

Rule 9019: Compromise and Settlement

Court approval of compromises of estate claims, the Iridium fair-and-equitable test, and the TMT Trailer factors.

Verbatim text of Rule 9019(a)

On motion by the trustee and after notice and a hearing, the court may approve a compromise or settlement. Notice shall be given to creditors, the United States trustee, the debtor, and indenture trustees as provided in Rule 2002 and to any other entity as the court may direct. Fed. R. Bankr. P. 9019(a).

The structure of Rule 9019

Federal Rule of Bankruptcy Procedure 9019 governs court approval of compromises and settlements of claims by and against the bankruptcy estate. The rule has three subdivisions:

  • 9019(a) requires court approval of any compromise or settlement after notice and a hearing.
  • 9019(b) permits the court, after notice and a hearing, to authorize the trustee to compromise and settle controversies of a particular class without further hearing or notice.
  • 9019(c) authorizes the court to require parties to participate in a settlement conference or other alternative dispute resolution proceeding.

Rule 9019(a) applies to virtually every consensual resolution of a contested matter, adversary proceeding, or claim dispute in which the estate has an interest. Settlements presented for approval typically include resolutions of avoidance actions, insurance coverage disputes, lien priority controversies, fraudulent transfer claims, breach-of-contract claims held by the estate, and contested proofs of claim.

The TMT Trailer Ferry standard

The Supreme Court's decision in Protective Committee for Independent Stockholders of TMT Trailer Ferry, Inc. v. Anderson, 390 U.S. 414 (1968), establishes the foundational rule that a bankruptcy court may not approve a compromise of estate claims unless the settlement is "fair and equitable." The Court emphasized that the bankruptcy judge must apprise himself or herself of the relevant facts and law so that an informed and intelligent judgment can be reached. A perfunctory approval, or approval based solely on the recommendation of the trustee, is insufficient.

The TMT Trailer Ferry standard requires the court to consider:

  • The probability of success in the underlying litigation
  • The complexity, expense, and likely duration of the litigation
  • The difficulties to be encountered in collecting any judgment obtained
  • The paramount interest of the creditors and a proper deference to their reasonable views

These four factors are the canonical TMT Trailer factors, applied in some formulation by every circuit. Courts have elaborated on the standard with additional considerations including the extent to which the settlement is the product of arm's-length bargaining, whether the settlement is supported by competent counsel, and whether the settlement is in the public interest.

The Iridium fair-and-equitable refinement

The Second Circuit's decision in In re Iridium Operating LLC, 478 F.3d 452 (2d Cir. 2007), refined the application of the fair-and-equitable test in the specific context of settlements that distribute estate assets in a manner inconsistent with the absolute priority rule. The Iridium court held that, in evaluating whether a proposed settlement of avoidance claims may bypass the statutory priority scheme of the Bankruptcy Code, the bankruptcy court must consider:

  • Whether the settlement complies with the priority rules of the Bankruptcy Code
  • If not, whether a sufficient reason exists to deviate from those rules

Iridium signals a strong preference for settlements that distribute consideration in priority order. A settlement that pays unsecured creditors out of consideration that would otherwise reach administrative claimants, secured creditors, or priority claimants requires articulated justification. The Iridium standard is most often invoked in Chapter 11 cases where the proposed settlement is part of a plan or a structured dismissal.

Notice under Rule 2002(a)(3)

Rule 9019(a) requires notice "as provided in Rule 2002." Under Rule 2002(a)(3), the clerk or other person directed by the court shall give the debtor, the trustee, all creditors, and indenture trustees at least 21 days' notice by mail of the hearing on approval of a compromise or settlement, unless the court orders otherwise.

The notice must include:

  • The date, time, and place of the hearing
  • A summary of the proposed compromise or settlement sufficient to permit informed evaluation
  • The deadline for filing objections, if any is set
  • Identification of the claim or controversy being compromised
  • The consideration to be received by the estate, if any, and the consideration to be released or paid by the estate

A compromise approved without the notice required by Rule 2002(a)(3) is vulnerable to collateral attack and may be set aside if the failure to give notice prejudiced a party in interest. The 21-day notice requirement may be shortened by the court for cause, but the cause must be apparent on the record.

The Rule 9019(a) motion

The motion to approve a compromise or settlement typically includes:

  • A description of the underlying claim or controversy
  • A summary of the proposed compromise, including the consideration to be received by and paid by the estate
  • An analysis of each TMT Trailer factor applied to the specific controversy
  • A statement of the relationship of the settlement to the priority scheme of the Bankruptcy Code, where Iridium applies
  • Identification of the parties whose consent has been obtained and the parties from whom objections are anticipated
  • The proposed form of order

The settlement agreement itself is typically attached as an exhibit. A settlement that includes mutual releases, confidentiality provisions, or non-disparagement clauses must disclose those terms in the motion; the court is unlikely to approve undisclosed settlement terms.

Objections and the hearing

Any party in interest may object to a proposed compromise. The most common objectors are:

  • Unsecured creditors who believe the settlement undercompensates the estate for valuable claims
  • Secured creditors whose collateral is implicated by the settlement
  • The United States Trustee, where the settlement raises questions about administrative-claim treatment, fee structure, or compliance with the priority scheme
  • Other holders of estate claims that may be affected by the proposed release

The objector bears the burden of identifying specific deficiencies in the settlement under the TMT Trailer factors. A general objection that "the estate could do better" without identifying the basis for that belief is unlikely to succeed. The proponent of the settlement carries the ultimate burden of demonstrating that the compromise is fair and equitable and in the best interests of the estate.

Common settlement contexts

Avoidance action settlements

The trustee compromises a preference or fraudulent transfer action against a creditor for a percentage of the alleged transfer amount. The court evaluates the probability of success on the merits of the avoidance theory, the defendant's defenses (ordinary course, new value, contemporaneous exchange), the cost of trial, and collection risk.

Insurance coverage settlements

The estate settles with an insurer over coverage of a pre-petition mass-tort or environmental claim. The settlement typically includes a buyback of the policy. Notice must reach claimants whose recoveries depend on the policy proceeds; courts often require supplemental notice to such claimants under Rule 9019(a)'s "any other entity as the court may direct" clause.

Rule 9019(b) class compromises

Rule 9019(b) permits the court to authorize the trustee to settle controversies of a defined class - typically small-dollar preference claims, repetitive contract claims, or routine clean-up matters - without separate motion practice for each. The order authorizing class compromise establishes parameters (dollar thresholds, factual criteria, reporting requirements) within which the trustee may settle without further court approval. The trustee files periodic reports identifying the settlements made under the class authority.

Rule 9019(c) settlement conferences

Rule 9019(c) authorizes the court to direct the parties to participate in a settlement conference or other alternative dispute resolution proceeding. Many districts have implemented mediation programs by local rule that operate within the Rule 9019(c) framework. See the companion page on court-annexed bankruptcy mediation.

The "fair and equitable" standard requires informed judgment, not deference. The bankruptcy court must apprise itself of the facts and law; mere reliance on the trustee's recommendation is insufficient under TMT Trailer Ferry. The proponent of the settlement should expect the court to engage substantively with the TMT Trailer factors and, where applicable, the Iridium priority analysis.
