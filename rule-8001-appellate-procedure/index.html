Federal Rule of Bankruptcy Procedure 8001

Rule 8001: Appellate Procedure Framework

The Part VIII appellate framework, the interlocutory vs. final order distinction under 28 USC Section 158(a)(1)/(2)/(3), and the bankruptcy court to district court / BAP appellate routing.

The structure of Part VIII

Part VIII of the Federal Rules of Bankruptcy Procedure - comprising Rules 8001 through 8028 - governs the procedure for appeals from a bankruptcy court. Part VIII was substantially rewritten effective December 1, 2014, to model its structure on the Federal Rules of Appellate Procedure and to recognize the increasing use of electronic filing in bankruptcy appellate practice.

Rule 8001 is the framing rule. It introduces the scope of Part VIII, identifies the appellate courts that may hear bankruptcy appeals (the district court and the bankruptcy appellate panel), and ties Part VIII to the statutory appellate authority in 28 U.S.C. Section 158. The remaining rules in Part VIII govern specific procedural elements: Rule 8002 (time for appeal), Rule 8003 (notice of appeal), Rule 8004 (leave to appeal interlocutory orders), Rule 8005 (election to district court), and so on through the briefing, record, and disposition rules.

The statutory framework: 28 U.S.C. Section 158

Section 158(a) authorizes the district court to hear appeals from a bankruptcy judge:

  • Section 158(a)(1) - from final judgments, orders, and decrees
  • Section 158(a)(2) - from interlocutory orders and decrees issued under Section 1121(d) (extending or reducing the exclusivity periods in Chapter 11)
  • Section 158(a)(3) - with leave of the court, from other interlocutory orders and decrees

Section 158(b) authorizes the establishment of bankruptcy appellate panels in each circuit, comprising bankruptcy judges from the districts within the circuit, to hear appeals that would otherwise be heard by the district court. Section 158(c) governs the appellate jurisdiction of the bankruptcy appellate panel and provides the election mechanism by which a party may opt for district-court review rather than panel review.

Section 158(d) governs appeals to the courts of appeals from the district court or the bankruptcy appellate panel. Section 158(d)(2), added by the 2005 BAPCPA amendments, creates the direct-appeal mechanism by which the bankruptcy court, the district court, or the bankruptcy appellate panel may certify an order for direct appeal to the court of appeals, bypassing the intermediate appellate court.

Interlocutory versus final orders

The interlocutory/final distinction is the most consequential jurisdictional fork in the appellate framework. A final order is appealable as of right under Section 158(a)(1); an interlocutory order is appealable only with leave under Section 158(a)(3).

The general rule of finality in federal civil practice - that an order is final when it ends the litigation on the merits and leaves nothing for the court to do but execute the judgment - applies in bankruptcy with substantial modification. Bankruptcy cases involve numerous discrete proceedings (claim objections, dischargeability complaints, sale motions, plan confirmation), each of which may be functionally "final" with respect to a particular controversy even though the larger case continues. The Supreme Court's decision in Bullard v. Blue Hills Bank, 575 U.S. 496 (2015), addressed the finality question in the context of plan confirmation: the denial of plan confirmation, in itself, does not constitute a final order if the debtor remains free to propose an amended plan. Confirmation of a plan, by contrast, is a final, appealable order.

The case law has settled on a flexible standard for bankruptcy finality. The order disposes of a discrete dispute within the larger bankruptcy case; the order resolves the rights of one or more parties; the order is sufficiently distinct that delayed appellate review would impose meaningful prejudice. A sale order, a stay-relief order, a confirmation order, an order granting or denying discharge, and a dischargeability order are typically final. An order denying confirmation (following Bullard), an order denying summary judgment, and an order on a discovery motion are typically interlocutory.

Leave to appeal interlocutory orders under Rule 8004

Rule 8004 governs the procedure for seeking leave to appeal an interlocutory order under Section 158(a)(3). A party seeking leave must file a notice of appeal accompanied by a motion for leave to appeal. The motion must state the facts necessary to understand the question presented, identify the question itself, address the relief sought, and explain why an appeal is warranted under the standard the district court applies.

Most circuits apply the standard articulated in 28 U.S.C. Section 1292(b) (the analogous interlocutory-appeal provision in the district-court context) by analogy: a controlling question of law, substantial ground for difference of opinion, and an immediate appeal that would materially advance the ultimate termination of the litigation. The standard is exacting; leave is granted sparingly.

District court versus bankruptcy appellate panel

Section 158 creates two parallel intermediate appellate forums for bankruptcy appeals. Where a circuit has established a bankruptcy appellate panel under Section 158(b), the default is panel review unless a party elects district-court review under Section 158(c)(1). The election must be made under Rule 8005 within the time fixed by the rule (typically at the time of filing the notice of appeal or within 30 days after entry of the order being appealed).

The bankruptcy appellate panel is composed of bankruptcy judges from the districts within the circuit, selected on a rotating basis. The panel typically sits in three-judge configurations. The choice between district-court review and panel review carries practical consequences. The bankruptcy appellate panel is composed of judges with subject-matter expertise in bankruptcy law and tends to dispose of appeals more rapidly. District courts hear bankruptcy appeals along with their general civil and criminal docket and typically lack the subject-matter specialization the panel provides, but generate decisions that are sometimes given greater weight by parties evaluating future positions.

Not every circuit has a bankruptcy appellate panel. The First, Sixth, Eighth, Ninth, and Tenth Circuits have established panels; the others rely on district-court review as the default. Within a circuit that has a panel, the election mechanism preserves the parties' choice.

Direct appeal under Section 158(d)(2)

Section 158(d)(2) authorizes direct appeal to the court of appeals - bypassing the district court or the bankruptcy appellate panel - if the bankruptcy court, the district court, or the bankruptcy appellate panel, or all parties jointly, certify that one of three conditions is satisfied:

  • The order involves a question of law as to which there is no controlling decision of the court of appeals or of the Supreme Court, or involves a matter of public importance
  • The order involves a question of law requiring resolution of conflicting decisions
  • An immediate appeal from the order may materially advance the progress of the case or proceeding in which the appeal is taken

Even with certification, the court of appeals may decline to authorize the direct appeal. Section 158(d)(2)(A) makes the direct appeal subject to the court of appeals' authorization.

The time-for-appeal regime under Rule 8002

Rule 8002 sets the time for filing the notice of appeal. The general rule is 14 days after entry of the judgment, order, or decree being appealed. The 14-day window is jurisdictional in nature - a late notice of appeal deprives the appellate court of authority to entertain the appeal except where the rule itself provides for an extension.

Rule 8002(b) provides that the time for appeal is suspended by the filing of any of several specified motions, including motions for new trial, motions to alter or amend judgment under Rule 9023 (incorporating FRCP 59), and motions for relief from judgment under Rule 9024 (incorporating FRCP 60) if filed within the appeal period. The suspension runs from the filing of the motion until entry of the order disposing of the last such motion outstanding.

Rule 8002(d) authorizes the bankruptcy court to extend the time for appeal upon a showing of excusable neglect. The extension may not exceed 21 days from the original deadline, and the motion must be filed within the original 14-day window or within the 21-day extension period. The case law applies the Pioneer equitable-factors framework to the excusable-neglect inquiry.

Common procedural posture

Final-order appeal from sale order

A bankruptcy court enters a sale order approving the sale of estate property under Section 363. A party in interest believes the order is improper and files a notice of appeal within 14 days under Rule 8002. The sale is a final order; the appeal proceeds as of right under Section 158(a)(1). Unless the appellant obtains a stay pending appeal under Rule 8007, the Section 363(m) good-faith finding will likely render the appeal moot upon closing.

Leave-required appeal from denial of summary judgment

The bankruptcy court denies a motion for summary judgment in a dischargeability adversary proceeding. The denial is interlocutory - it does not dispose of the dispute. A party seeking immediate appellate review must file a notice of appeal accompanied by a motion for leave to appeal under Rule 8004, demonstrating the Section 1292(b) factors. Leave is granted sparingly.

Practical impact

Rule 8001 and the broader Part VIII framework operate as the formal interface between the trial-level bankruptcy court and the appellate system. The most common errors at this interface are timing errors (missed 14-day deadlines) and characterization errors (treating an interlocutory order as final or vice versa). Both errors are typically fatal to appellate review: a missed deadline forecloses appeal entirely, and an attempted appeal of an interlocutory order without leave is subject to dismissal.

Finality in bankruptcy is functional, not formal. The question is not whether the bankruptcy case has ended, but whether the specific order disposes of a discrete dispute within the case. A sale order, a confirmation order, a discharge order, and an order in an adversary proceeding can each be final even though the larger bankruptcy case continues. The Bullard v. Blue Hills Bank framework distinguishes orders that conclusively resolve from orders that leave room for further litigation.
