PACER Access for Journalists and Researchers

Registration, the per-page fee structure, the $30 quarterly waiver threshold, Misc. Fee Schedule Section 9 fee-waiver applications, the ECF-versus-PACER distinction, and the RECAP Archive bypass for already-fetched documents.

What PACER is and what it costs

PACER (Public Access to Court Electronic Records) is the case-management interface through which the federal judiciary exposes docket sheets and filed documents to the public. It is operated by the Administrative Office of the United States Courts. Access requires a free registered account, but most search and retrieval operations carry a per-page charge. The charge is the principal barrier to systematic newsroom or academic use of federal court records, and the rules around it deserve careful attention.

The published fee schedule, the Electronic Public Access Fee Schedule set by the Judicial Conference, charges $0.10 per page for documents retrieved through PACER, capped at $3.00 (30 pages) per document for most filings. Search results (the list of dockets matching a query) are charged at the same per-page rate, with no cap on the search-result page count. Audio recordings of court hearings are charged at $2.40 per recording. Name searches and the case-locator interface are billed by results page returned.

The quarterly waiver threshold

The fee schedule contains an automatic waiver provision: a user whose total accrued charges in a calendar quarter do not exceed $30 owes nothing for that quarter. The threshold operates at the account level; charges below $30 in a quarter are forgiven, and charges above $30 are billed in full from the first dollar. The practical effect for an occasional user is free access up to roughly 300 pages of document retrieval per quarter. Heavy users blow through the threshold quickly and are billed monthly.

The quarterly windows are calendar quarters (January through March, April through June, July through September, October through December). Charges reset at the start of each quarter; an account that accrued $29 in March and $2 in April owes nothing for either quarter, but an account that accrued $31 in March owes the full $31.

Fee waivers under Misc. Fee Schedule Section 9

Item 9 of the Electronic Public Access Fee Schedule authorizes any court to grant a fee exemption for "indigents, bankruptcy case trustees, individual researchers associated with educational institutions, courts, section 501(c)(3) not-for-profit organizations, court appointed pro bono attorneys, and pro bono ADR neutrals." The exemption is per-court, not system-wide; an exemption granted by the District of Delaware does not cover queries in the Northern District of California, and an exemption granted by the District of Delaware bankruptcy court does not cover queries in the Delaware district court.

The application is by motion to the court, supported by a declaration explaining the requestor's affiliation, the planned research use, the duration sought, and the limits the requestor will observe (the schedule requires that exempted users not redistribute the accessed material commercially and that they delete it when their research concludes). Courts grant or deny by order. The chambers practice varies; some districts route the motion to the Clerk for administrative handling, others to a designated judge. A search of the court's local rules and standing orders usually identifies the proper procedure.

Practical note for journalists. Journalist status by itself does not appear in the categorical list of eligible recipients under Section 9. A journalist seeking a fee exemption typically needs to qualify through one of the named categories - most commonly by affiliation with an educational institution, a 501(c)(3) news organization, or a research project sponsored by such an institution. Some courts have granted exemptions to journalists in their discretion outside the enumerated categories, but the practice is not uniform.

ECF versus PACER

The case-management system used by federal courts is CM/ECF (Case Management / Electronic Case Files). ECF is the filing interface used by attorneys to submit documents; PACER is the read-only interface that exposes ECF contents to the public. Most courts share the underlying database, so PACER displays what ECF has docketed in close to real time. A handful of older or specialized dockets are not exposed through PACER and require a clerk-of-court request for paper copies.

Each district court and each bankruptcy court operates its own ECF/PACER instance. A user with a PACER account may access any court's instance using the same credentials; the per-page charges from each court roll up to the same quarterly billing statement. A single search through the PACER Case Locator queries the federal-judiciary-wide index and returns hits across districts.

The RECAP Archive bypass

The RECAP Archive, operated by the Free Law Project, is a free-public mirror of PACER documents that have been retrieved at least once through the RECAP browser extension. The mirror operates at courtlistener.com/recap. Because the federal courts hold their records as public works not subject to copyright, the Free Law Project's redistribution is lawful, and the mirror grows organically as RECAP users retrieve documents from PACER.

For any document already in the RECAP Archive, retrieval is free. The Archive's coverage is uneven: heavily-litigated cases and cases of public interest tend to be densely mirrored; obscure consumer bankruptcy dockets often are not. A journalist working a story should always check RECAP first; the per-document savings add up quickly when reviewing a 500-document docket, and the Archive often includes hearing transcripts and exhibits not easily found through PACER search.

The RECAP browser extension, available for Firefox and Chrome, automates the contribution: when an authenticated user retrieves a document through PACER with the extension running, the document is silently uploaded to the Archive in addition to being downloaded to the user's machine. Newsrooms that routinely query PACER should install RECAP on shared browsers as a public service.

Search-versus-fetch fee structure

PACER bills separately for search operations (queries that produce a list of matching dockets) and fetch operations (retrieval of a specific document or docket sheet). A single broad search that returns ten pages of results costs $1.00 even before any document is retrieved. A narrow search that returns one page of results costs $0.10. Drafting search queries that are precise enough to return short result lists is a meaningful cost-management practice.

Within a docket, the docket sheet itself is billable per page (capped at $3.00 per docket sheet retrieval). Each individual document is then billed separately, capped at $3.00 per document (with the exception that transcripts and certain audio recordings have their own fee structures). A long docket with many short filings can produce a substantial cumulative bill even when no single document is expensive.

Account types and access levels

PACER offers three account types: (1) a basic individual account, suitable for ad-hoc research; (2) a filer account, required for attorneys filing documents in ECF; and (3) an aggregated billing account for organizations whose multiple users should be billed to a single firm or institutional account. Most journalists and researchers want a basic individual account. Registration is at pacer.uscourts.gov and requires a credit card on file for billing.

Bankruptcy-specific access notes

Bankruptcy court PACER instances expose the schedules, statement of financial affairs, claims register, plan, and disclosure statement (in Chapter 11 cases) for any case that has been filed. Sealed documents - typically tax returns, certain medical records, and documents subject to a protective order - are listed on the docket but not retrievable through PACER; a non-party seeking access to a sealed document must move the court for unsealing.

Bankruptcy courts also operate a separate claims register, accessible through the same PACER interface, that lists every proof of claim filed and the supporting documentation attached to each. The claims register is often the most efficient way to identify a debtor's lender, landlord, and trade-creditor relationships in a single retrieval.

Bulk data access

PACER does not offer bulk download or API access to its raw case data. Researchers requiring a corpus of dockets or filings ordinarily build it through one of three paths: (1) the RECAP Archive's CourtListener REST API, which exposes Archive contents for free; (2) the Administrative Office's "Statistical Tables for the Federal Judiciary," which publish aggregate statistics by court and case type; or (3) commercial wrappers (Westlaw Dockets, Bloomberg Law Docket Search) that crawl PACER under fee-shifting commercial arrangements and resell access to subscribers.

The friction is the point and the friction can be navigated. The PACER fee structure was designed to recoup the cost of running the public-access system, not to deter use. The waiver provisions, the RECAP Archive, and the per-court Section 9 fee exemptions together render most research-quality access either free or close to free for users who know how to navigate them.
