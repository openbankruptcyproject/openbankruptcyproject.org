Citation Discipline in Bankruptcy Research

Bluebook Rule 14.2 statutory citation format, the precedential hierarchy of Bankruptcy Appellate Panel versus district court versus circuit court decisions, Cornell LII versus the official statute version, the In re case-name caption convention, and pin-cite practice.

Why citation discipline matters

Bankruptcy practice draws on a Title 11 statutory text that has been amended at least twelve times since enactment, a set of Federal Rules of Bankruptcy Procedure independent of (and partially divergent from) the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure, official forms updated on an irregular schedule, a sprawling case law that includes a unique Bankruptcy Appellate Panel layer in five circuits, and a complex relationship to non-bankruptcy state law incorporated by reference. A citation written without attention to this terrain misdirects the reader and, in adversarial briefing, invites Rule 11 or Rule 9011 challenge.

The Bluebook (currently in its 21st edition) is the dominant citation manual; the ALWD Guide to Legal Citation and individual local court rules supply supplementary conventions. This page concerns the citation practices most often required for bankruptcy research, with cross-references to the primary authorities.

Statutory citation under Bluebook Rule 14.2

Bluebook Rule 14.2 (and the closely related Rule 12 for the United States Code) governs statutory citation. The canonical form for the Bankruptcy Code is: 11 U.S.C. Section 1125(b) (2018). The components are: (1) the title number ("11"); (2) the code abbreviation ("U.S.C." for the official United States Code, "U.S.C.A." for West's annotated edition, "U.S.C.S." for LexisNexis); (3) the section symbol and section number; (4) any subsection or paragraph designation; and (5) the year of the code edition in parentheses.

Under current Bluebook practice for citation to the official U.S.C., the year cited is the year of the codified edition in print at the time of writing, not the year the section was enacted or last amended. Title 11 was reprinted in the 2018 edition of the U.S.C.; subsequent amendments appear in U.S.C. supplements. A precise citation distinguishes the main volume year from the supplement year when relying on a post-2018 amendment: 11 U.S.C. Section 1190 (2018 & Supp. III 2021).

In practice, many federal courts permit and even prefer the simplified form without the year parenthetical for the Bankruptcy Code, on the theory that the current text is readily identifiable. Local rules vary; consult the local rules of the relevant court before relying on a simplified form in a brief.

Subsection designation conventions

The Bankruptcy Code is heavily nested. A typical section may run subsection-paragraph-subparagraph-clause-subclause, designated (a)(1)(A)(i)(I). The full nesting must be reproduced when citing a subordinate provision. Section 727(a)(2)(A) (a particular ground for denial of a Chapter 7 discharge) is not the same provision as Section 727(a)(2) (the general fraudulent-transfer denial category) or Section 727(a) (the entire list of grounds). Pin-cite precision matters because amendments often add or remove subordinate clauses without renumbering the parent.

The section symbol (Section) is typed as the Unicode character on platforms that render it cleanly; on platforms that do not, the spelled-out word "Section" is the conventional substitute. The Bluebook (Rule 8) permits use of the symbol in textual sentences as well as in citations.

Federal Rules of Bankruptcy Procedure

The Federal Rules of Bankruptcy Procedure are cited as Fed. R. Bankr. P. [rule number] in formal text. In informal contexts the abbreviation FRBP is widely used. The current edition is the result of a 2024 amendment cycle. Rules promulgated for adversary proceedings (the 7000-series) parallel the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure; the Bankruptcy Rule and the corresponding Civil Rule should both be cited where briefing in an adversary proceeding turns on the relationship.

Case-name citation: the In re convention

Bankruptcy cases are typically captioned In re [Debtor Name] in main-case proceedings. Adversary proceedings are typically captioned [Plaintiff] v. [Defendant] (In re [Debtor]). The Bluebook (Rule 10.2.1(b)) governs the case-name conventions in both formats.

Under Rule 10.2.1(b), the "In re" prefix is italicized as part of the case name. The debtor's name follows in italics. The full caption in an adversary proceeding includes the parenthetical bankruptcy-case caption: Smith v. Jones (In re Acme Corp.), 123 F.3d 456, 458 (9th Cir. 2010). The parenthetical is necessary because the reported decision lives within an adversary proceeding that arises from a particular main bankruptcy case; the parenthetical fixes that relationship.

Short-form citation to a previously cited case uses the shortened name without "In re" (Acme, 123 F.3d at 461) or, for adversary proceedings, the lead party name (Smith, 123 F.3d at 461). Consistency within a single document is the operative discipline.

Precedential weight: BAP versus district versus circuit

The federal-court hierarchy in bankruptcy matters runs: bankruptcy court (trial level, with original jurisdiction over Title 11 cases), then an appellate layer (either the district court or, in five circuits, the Bankruptcy Appellate Panel), then the circuit court of appeals, then the Supreme Court. The choice between district-court and BAP review is governed by 28 U.S.C. Section 158 and the procedural elections of the appellant.

The First, Sixth, Eighth, Ninth, and Tenth Circuits maintain Bankruptcy Appellate Panels (BAPs) under 28 U.S.C. Section 158(b). In those circuits, the BAP is an authorized appellate forum unless any party timely elects district-court review; if any party elects, the appeal is heard in district court instead. The remaining circuits have not established BAPs, and bankruptcy appeals run to the district court.

BAP decisions are persuasive but not binding on bankruptcy courts within the BAP's circuit; this is the holding of Bank of Maui v. Estate Analysis, Inc., 904 F.2d 470 (9th Cir. 1990), and subsequent decisions in the other BAP circuits. The treatment of BAP decisions as persuasive rather than binding is one of the consistent rules across all five BAP circuits; the Ninth Circuit BAP itself has acknowledged that "[the BAP's] decisions are not binding on [trial-level] bankruptcy courts."

District-court decisions on appeal are binding only on the parties to that appeal; they are not stare decisis binding on other bankruptcy courts within the district. Circuit-court decisions are binding on every bankruptcy court within the circuit. The Supreme Court is binding nationally.

The practical citation hierarchy: cite Supreme Court precedent first where applicable; cite circuit precedent next; cite district-court and BAP precedent for persuasive value only, with a signal acknowledging non-binding status when relying on them in a court within the relevant circuit.

Reporter and database conventions

The principal bankruptcy reporter for trial-level and appellate-panel decisions is West's Bankruptcy Reporter, abbreviated B.R.. Circuit court bankruptcy decisions are reported in the Federal Reporter series (F.3d, F.4th). District-court bankruptcy appellate decisions appear in Federal Supplement (F. Supp. 3d) or in the Bankruptcy Reporter. Unreported decisions are cited to the Westlaw or Lexis database with the citation manual's prescribed format.

For decisions available on a publicly accessible database, the Bluebook now permits citation to that database (Rule 18.3) as a parallel cite. The Free Law Project's CourtListener exposes a substantial archive of bankruptcy decisions and supplies persistent URLs suitable for parallel citation in informal contexts.

Cornell LII versus the official statute version

Cornell Law School's Legal Information Institute (LII) hosts a free public copy of the United States Code, current through the most recent enactment. The LII text is derived from the Office of the Law Revision Counsel's official compilation and is generally accurate; nevertheless it is not the official statute. Formal briefing should cite the U.S.C. itself; informal research and online linking can rely on LII for accessibility.

The Office of the Law Revision Counsel maintains the official U.S. Code online edition, which is the source of truth for the codified text. Where the LII text and the OLRC text differ - usually because LII has not yet absorbed a recent amendment - the OLRC text controls. A researcher relying on LII for a recent amendment should cross-check against OLRC.

Bankruptcy provisions are sometimes enacted as freestanding statutes outside Title 11 (for example, the Bankruptcy Abuse Prevention and Consumer Protection Act of 2005, which amended Title 11 but also contained freestanding provisions). Citation to freestanding provisions runs to the Statutes at Large (Pub. L. No. 109-8, 119 Stat. 23) rather than to the U.S. Code.

Pin-cite practice

A pin-cite is a reference to the specific page (in a printed reporter) or paragraph (in an online reporter) supporting a proposition. The general Bluebook rule (Rule 3.2) requires a pin-cite whenever the cited authority is being relied on for a specific holding or quotation. In bankruptcy briefing, the convention is rigorous: a general citation to a 70-page Chapter 11 opinion for a single proposition is not sufficient; the page or paragraph supporting that proposition must be identified.

The pin-cite format is the first page of the opinion, followed by a comma, followed by the pin-cite page: 123 F.3d 456, 461 (9th Cir. 2010). Multiple pin-cites are separated by commas. A pin-cite to a footnote uses the format 123 F.3d at 461 n.5.

When to pin-cite versus general citation

General citation (no pin-cite) is appropriate when the proposition is supported by the holding of the entire decision and no particular passage is being relied on; pin-citation is required when a particular passage supports the proposition or a quotation is taken from it. The distinction is not always clean. A useful rule: if the reader cannot locate the supporting passage in 30 seconds without the pin-cite, the pin-cite was required.

Quotations require pin-cites in every system. A quotation without a pin-cite is a Rule 9011 problem; if the quotation is unverifiable, the citation itself may be inaccurate, and the proposition supported by the quotation may collapse on verification.

Citation discipline is verification discipline. Every citation in a bankruptcy brief is a representation to the court that the cited authority exists, says what the brief claims it says, and stands for the proposition for which it is cited. The discipline is not stylistic; it is the documentary foundation of the argument.
