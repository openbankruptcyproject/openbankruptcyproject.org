KRPC / MRPC 1.16(d) Entire-File Production Standard at Termination of Representation

First published 2026-05-22/Version 0.1.0/CC-BY-4.0/status: draft

Abstract

At the termination of a lawyer-client relationship, the lawyer owes the client an affirmative duty to take steps reasonably practicable to protect the client's interests, including surrender of papers and property to which the client is entitled. In Kansas, this duty is codified at Kansas Rule of Professional Conduct 1.16(d); in Missouri, at Missouri Supreme Court Rule 4-1.16(d). Both rules adopt the substantive text of ABA Model Rule of Professional Conduct 1.16(d). The prevailing interpretation in both jurisdictions, consistent with the majority view across state-bar opinions, is that the lawyer must produce the entire client file, not a curated subset of it.

The duty triggers at termination of representation, regardless of which party initiated the termination and regardless of whether fees remain outstanding. Common firm-side defenses (retainer-balance withholding, work-product privilege, partial production of "what the client needs") fail under the entire-file standard. The file belongs to the client; the firm's role is custodial, not proprietary.

Rule Text: Kansas and Missouri

Kansas Rule of Professional Conduct 1.16(d)

Kansas Supreme Court Rule 226, adopting the Kansas Rules of Professional Conduct, includes KRPC 1.16(d):

"Upon termination of representation, a lawyer shall take steps to the extent reasonably practicable to protect a client's interests, such as giving reasonable notice to the client, allowing time for employment of other counsel, surrendering papers and property to which the client is entitled and refunding any advance payment of fee or expense that has not been earned or incurred. The lawyer may retain papers relating to the client to the extent permitted by other law."

The final sentence's "as permitted by other law" clause is narrow. In Kansas, the only "other law" that materially authorizes retention is the common-law retaining lien for unpaid fees, and even there the prevailing ethics-opinion view is that the retaining lien may not be exercised in a manner that prejudices the client's substantial interests (see KBA Ethics Op. 91-13 and subsequent guidance).

Missouri Supreme Court Rule 4-1.16(d)

The Missouri Rules of Professional Conduct, promulgated as Missouri Supreme Court Rule 4, include Rule 4-1.16(d) in substantively identical terms:

"Upon termination of representation, a lawyer shall take steps to the extent reasonably practicable to protect a client's interests, such as giving reasonable notice to the client, allowing time for employment of other counsel, surrendering papers and property to which the client is entitled, and refunding any advance payment of fee or expense that has not been earned or incurred. The lawyer may retain papers relating to the client to the extent permitted by other law."

Missouri's interpretation of the rule, articulated in Missouri Bar Informal Opinion 940084 and reaffirmed in subsequent informal opinions, embraces the entire-file standard: the client is entitled to "all papers and property that the lawyer has prepared or received in the course of the representation, regardless of whether the client has paid in full for the work."

ABA Model Rule 1.16(d)

The ABA Model Rule, on which both state rules are based, reads identically in operative substance. ABA Formal Opinion 471 (July 1, 2015), titled "Ethical Obligations of Lawyer to Surrender Papers and Property to which Former Client is Entitled," is the leading ABA interpretation and adopts the entire-file standard as the default rule.

The Entire-File Standard

The "entire file" interpretation holds that the client is entitled to all papers and property that the lawyer prepared or received in the course of representation, with limited exceptions. The standard contrasts with the older "end-product" view, under which the lawyer was entitled to retain internal work product, attorney notes, drafts, and similar materials, producing only the final filed documents and originals of client property.

The entire-file standard prevails in the substantial majority of state jurisdictions, including Kansas and Missouri. Representative authorities include:

  • ABA Formal Opinion 471 (2015) (entire-file standard is the default; end-product standard adopted only in a minority of jurisdictions)
  • Restatement (Third) of the Law Governing Lawyers § 46 cmt. c (2000) (client entitled to documents created by lawyer in course of representation)
  • Kansas Bar Association Ethics Opinion 91-13 (lawyer's retaining lien limited; client file generally must be produced)
  • Missouri Bar Informal Opinion 940084 (entire-file production at termination)
  • Sage Realty Corp. v. Proskauer Rose Goetz & Mendelsohn, L.L.P., 91 N.Y.2d 30 (1997) (leading state-court decision articulating the entire-file standard)

The standard's substantive content is straightforward: at termination, the firm produces the file. The file includes everything the firm prepared or received in the matter, subject to narrow exceptions discussed below. The firm cannot select what to produce; the client (or successor counsel) decides what is useful.

What Constitutes the Client File

The client file under the entire-file standard includes the following categories of material:

  • Pleadings and filings. All complaints, answers, motions, briefs, and related papers filed in any court or administrative tribunal in the matter, in both filed-version and final-draft form.
  • Discovery materials. All written discovery (interrogatories, requests for production, requests for admission) propounded by or served on the firm, together with responses, document productions, and privilege logs.
  • Correspondence. All correspondence between the firm and the client, between the firm and opposing counsel, between the firm and the court or court personnel, and between the firm and third parties acting in the matter. Email and other electronic correspondence are included.
  • Attorney work product. Internal memoranda, legal research, factual analyses, draft pleadings, draft briefs, and similar materials prepared by the firm in the course of representation. Under the entire-file standard, work product is part of the client file even though it was generated by the firm internally.
  • Notes and records of client communications. Notes of meetings with the client, telephone-call records, contemporaneous file memoranda documenting client communications.
  • Materials received from the client. Original documents provided by the client, copies of records produced to the firm, exhibits prepared by or for the client.
  • Materials received from third parties. Documents received from opposing counsel in discovery; documents subpoenaed from third parties; expert reports; investigator reports.
  • Electronic file materials. Electronic documents stored in the firm's case-management system, including drafts saved but not finalized, version histories, and metadata to the extent reasonably accessible.
  • Billing-related materials. Detailed time records and billing statements, to the extent the client requests them in connection with assessing the reasonableness of fees.

The file is not limited to materials the firm finds useful or convenient. The standard is custodial: the firm holds the file for the client. At termination, the file returns to the client.

Narrow Exceptions

The entire-file standard recognizes a small number of narrow exceptions. The exceptions are jurisdictionally variable and are construed narrowly against the firm.

  • Materials the firm is legally prohibited from disclosing. A protective order in unrelated litigation, a sealed grand-jury subpoena, or analogous legal restrictions may prevent disclosure of specific items. The firm must inventory withheld items and document the basis.
  • Materials whose disclosure would violate a third party's confidentiality interests. Materials received subject to a confidentiality agreement with a third party may be subject to withholding obligations. The firm typically must seek the third party's consent or seek court guidance.
  • Internal firm-administration materials unrelated to the representation. Conflict-checking records, internal staffing memoranda, and similar materials that are not "about" the representation are typically not part of the client file. Internal memoranda discussing strategy in the representation, however, are part of the file.

Materials prepared in anticipation of a malpractice claim by the client (so-called "self-protective work product") are subject to specific treatment in some jurisdictions. The ABA's view in Formal Opinion 471 is that such materials may be retained only to the extent they are not "of any importance to" the client's interests; the burden is on the firm to justify retention.

The retaining-lien exception, where recognized, is narrow. The lien permits temporary retention of file materials for the purpose of securing unpaid fees but generally cannot be exercised in a manner that prejudices the client's substantial interests. In bankruptcy practice, courts have been particularly hostile to retaining-lien arguments because the debtor's need for the file typically intersects with statutory deadlines and the duty of cooperation.

When the Duty Triggers

The duty under Rule 1.16(d) triggers at termination of representation. Termination occurs in any of the following circumstances:

  • Client discharge. The client terminates the relationship. The duty triggers immediately upon receipt of the client's instruction.
  • Mandatory withdrawal under Rule 1.16(a). The lawyer is required to withdraw because continued representation will violate the rules of professional conduct, the lawyer's physical or mental condition materially impairs the representation, or the lawyer is discharged.
  • Permissive withdrawal under Rule 1.16(b). The lawyer withdraws under one of the permissive-withdrawal grounds (with court permission if required). The duty triggers upon effective withdrawal.
  • Completion of the matter. The representation ends because the engagement's scope has been completed.

The duty does not turn on which party initiated termination, on the cause of termination, or on whether the firm believes the client has been unreasonable. The duty is the firm's; the client's reasonableness is not an element. The client need not have paid all fees, need not have signed a release, need not have engaged successor counsel.

The duty is not a one-time event satisfied by a single transmittal. If additional materials are identified after the initial production, the firm has a continuing duty to produce them. Successor counsel typically issues follow-up requests as the representation develops; the firm is obligated to respond to those requests as if they were part of the original production demand.

Common Firm-Side Defenses and Why They Fail

Retainer fee balance

The most common firm-side defense is the assertion that outstanding fees justify retention of the file. The defense fails under the entire-file standard except in narrow retaining-lien circumstances, and even there, the retention may not prejudice the client's substantial interests. In bankruptcy practice, the debtor's pending statutory obligations almost always satisfy the substantial-prejudice threshold.

ABA Formal Opinion 471 expressly addresses this: "An advance of unearned fees does not justify withholding the file." Comments to Model Rule 1.16(d) reinforce that fee disputes are not a basis for withholding file materials whose absence will prejudice the client.

Work-product privilege

The work-product doctrine protects attorney work product from discovery by adversaries; it does not protect attorney work product from disclosure to the client. The client is the holder of the privilege, not the firm; the privilege does not run from the firm to the client. The firm cannot invoke work product to withhold materials from the entity whose interests the work product was developed to advance.

Partial production "of what the client needs"

Some firms argue that they have satisfied Rule 1.16(d) by producing the materials the firm judges the client requires. This argument inverts the standard. The client decides what is useful; the firm produces the entire file and lets the client (or successor counsel) make that judgment. A firm-curated subset is not compliance; it is a constructive denial of access to materials the client owns.

"The materials are available on the docket"

In litigation matters, the firm sometimes argues that publicly-filed pleadings need not be produced because they are available on PACER or the analogous state-court docket. This argument fails for two reasons. First, the client's file includes drafts, internal annotations, and related work product not appearing on the docket. Second, the burden of compilation should not be shifted to the client; the file as the firm maintained it is what is owed.

Confidentiality of internal firm communications

Internal firm communications about the matter (lawyer-to-lawyer discussion, conflict assessments, strategy discussions) are typically part of the client file. The firm cannot invoke its own internal-process interest in confidentiality against the client. To the extent specific items raise unrelated firm-administration questions, the firm bears the burden of justifying withholding on an item-by-item basis.

Cross-Jurisdictional Consistency

The entire-file standard is the majority rule across state-bar interpretations. Jurisdictions adopting the standard include (with representative authorities):

  • California (Cal. Rule of Prof. Conduct 1.16(e); Sage Realty reasoning applied)
  • New York (Sage Realty Corp. v. Proskauer Rose, 91 N.Y.2d 30 (1997))
  • Illinois (Ill. Sup. Ct. R. Prof. Conduct 1.16(d); ISBA Op. 94-13)
  • Texas (Tex. Disciplinary R. Prof. Conduct 1.15(d); Texas Center for Legal Ethics Op. 627)
  • Kansas (KRPC 1.16(d); KBA Ethics Op. 91-13)
  • Missouri (MRPC 4-1.16(d); MoBar Informal Op. 940084)
  • Florida (Fla. Bar Rule 4-1.16(d); ABA Formal Op. 471 followed)

A minority of jurisdictions continue to apply the older "end-product" standard, under which the firm retains internal work product and produces only filed documents and originals of client property. Even within end-product jurisdictions, the trend is toward expanded production, and ABA Formal Opinion 471 has accelerated the convergence on the entire-file rule.

The Restatement (Third) of the Law Governing Lawyers § 46(2) summarizes the modern majority view: "On request, a lawyer must allow a client or former client to inspect and copy any document possessed by the lawyer relating to the representation, unless substantial grounds exist to refuse." The Restatement's comments make clear that substantial grounds for refusal are narrowly defined and do not include unpaid fees, internal firm preferences, or work-product convenience.
