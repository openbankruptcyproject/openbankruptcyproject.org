Rule 2016(b) Post-Confirmation Fee Disclosure Framework

First published 2026-05-22/Version 0.1.0/CC-BY-4.0/status: draft

Abstract

Federal Rule of Bankruptcy Procedure 2016(b) imposes a continuing disclosure obligation on every attorney representing a debtor in a bankruptcy case. The initial disclosure is made on Official Form B2030 (Disclosure of Compensation of Attorney for Debtor) within fourteen days of the order for relief, or any time thereafter as the court directs. The obligation does not terminate at confirmation; any subsequent payment or agreement to pay must be disclosed by supplemental statement filed within fourteen days of the change. This continuing obligation interacts with 11 U.S.C. § 329 and with local-rule treatment of post-confirmation services to define the boundary between what may be billed without further court action and what requires a noticed application.

The post-confirmation interval is the period in which compliance failures concentrate. The initial Form B2030 is typically filed contemporaneously with the petition; the supplemental obligation activates each time the compensation arrangement changes. In Chapter 11 and Subchapter V cases, the confirmed plan typically defines the scope of routine post-confirmation services that may be billed without further application; services outside that scope require a fee application reviewed under § 330.

Rule 2016(b): The Continuing Disclosure Obligation

Federal Rule of Bankruptcy Procedure 2016(b) provides, in operative part:

"Every attorney for a debtor, whether or not the attorney applies for compensation, shall file and transmit to the United States trustee within 14 days after the order for relief, or at another time as the court may direct, the statement required by § 329 of the Code including whether the attorney has shared or agreed to share the compensation with any other entity. The statement shall include the particulars of any such sharing or agreement to share by the attorney, but the details of any agreement for the sharing of the compensation with a member or regular associate of the attorney's law firm shall not be required. A supplemental statement shall be filed and transmitted to the United States trustee within 14 days after any payment or agreement not previously disclosed."

Three operative obligations follow. First, every attorney for the debtor must file, even if no fee application is contemplated. Second, the initial statement covers compensation received and fee-sharing arrangements as of the date of filing. Third, the supplemental-statement clause is open-ended; it triggers whenever a payment or agreement is made that was not previously disclosed, and it applies for the entire duration of the case, including post-confirmation.

The supplemental-disclosure obligation is independent of any obligation to seek court approval of fees. The disclosure obligation is informational; the approval obligation, where it applies under § 330 or applicable local rules, is substantive. An attorney may have a duty to disclose a post-confirmation payment under Rule 2016(b) and a separate duty to seek approval of the underlying services under § 330 or a confirmed plan's provisions.

Form B2030: What Must Be Disclosed and How

Official Form B2030 (Disclosure of Compensation of Attorney for Debtor) is the standardized form prescribed by the Judicial Conference for the Rule 2016(b) disclosure. The form requires:

  • The agreed compensation for legal services to be rendered, broken into amounts received prior to filing and amounts to be paid post-petition
  • The source of the compensation (debtor, third party, or other identified source)
  • An itemized scope of services included in the disclosed compensation
  • An itemized scope of services NOT included (services that, if rendered, will be billed separately)
  • Any fee-sharing arrangement with persons outside the attorney's firm
  • Certification under penalty of perjury signed by the disclosing attorney

The "services not included" section is the operative carve-out for post-confirmation work. Standard local-rule practice in many districts specifies routine post-confirmation services (such as filing the final report, responding to the trustee's final inquiries, attending the discharge hearing) that fall within the original retainer. Services outside the carve-out require either a fee application or a supplemental disclosure of a new arrangement.

The form is signed by the attorney, not by the firm. Where a firm represents the debtor, the disclosing attorney's signature binds the firm to the substance of the disclosure, but firm-level fee arrangements (rate changes, billing of additional attorneys not named in the original disclosure) trigger the supplemental obligation.

Local-Rule Carve-Outs for Routine Post-Confirmation Services

Most bankruptcy districts have adopted local rules or general orders that define a narrow category of routine post-confirmation services that may be billed without a separate fee application, provided the original Form B2030 disclosure contemplates them. The pattern, often codified at a local-rule number such as 2016-1(E), reads substantively along these lines:

"Routine post-confirmation services not requiring a separate application for compensation include: (1) filing the final report and account; (2) attending the discharge hearing; (3) preparing and filing certifications of plan completion; (4) responding to trustee inquiries regarding plan administration. Services beyond this scope require a Motion for Approval of Fees filed in accordance with this rule."

Within the carve-out, the firm may bill the disclosed retainer amount or a per-service flat fee specified in the original disclosure, without filing a fee application or seeking court approval. Services beyond the carve-out require an itemized fee application under § 330 or, in some districts, a notice-and-opportunity motion served on the United States Trustee and parties in interest.

The exact list of services within the carve-out varies by district. Some districts include modification motions of a routine technical character; others exclude any post-confirmation litigation entirely. The Western District of Missouri, the District of Kansas, and the Eastern and Western Districts of Missouri all maintain district-specific carve-out lists that should be consulted directly. The carve-out is typically narrower than firms assume.

The Supplemental-Disclosure Trigger

The supplemental-statement obligation under Rule 2016(b) triggers in any of the following circumstances:

  • A new payment from the debtor or any third party that was not contemplated in the original disclosure
  • A new fee-sharing arrangement
  • A change in the firm's hourly rates applicable to the case (where the disclosure was made on an hourly basis)
  • The assignment of new attorneys to the case whose rates differ from those originally disclosed
  • A modification of the scope of services originally disclosed (whether expansion or contraction)
  • Receipt of payment from a non-debtor third party for services rendered to the debtor

The supplemental statement must be filed within fourteen days of the change and transmitted to the United States Trustee. Failure to file the supplemental statement is a discrete violation of Rule 2016(b), independent of any underlying issue with the substantive entitlement to the payment.

The supplemental-disclosure obligation continues post-confirmation. A confirmed plan does not terminate the case; the case remains open until final decree under 11 U.S.C. § 350 and Federal Rule of Bankruptcy Procedure 3022. Any change in the compensation arrangement during the post-confirmation administration period triggers the supplemental obligation. This applies with particular force when the firm continues to render services (modifications, claim objections, asset sales, adversary proceedings, dismissal motions) that were not contemplated in the original Form B2030.

Interplay With 11 U.S.C. Section 329

Section 329(a) requires disclosure of any compensation paid or agreed to be paid by or on behalf of the debtor for services rendered in contemplation of or in connection with the case. Rule 2016(b) implements § 329(a). Section 329(b) authorizes the court to review the reasonableness of any compensation and to order the return of any payment "to the extent excessive":

"If such compensation exceeds the reasonable value of any such services, the court may cancel any such agreement, or order the return of any such payment, to the extent excessive, to—(1) the estate, if the property transferred—(A) would have been property of the estate; or (B) was to be paid by or on behalf of the debtor under a plan under chapter 11, 12, or 13 of this title; or (2) the entity that made such payment."

The disclosure obligation under Rule 2016(b) feeds the review power under § 329(b). The court can review only what it knows about; the supplemental-disclosure obligation ensures that the court and the United States Trustee receive the information necessary to exercise the review power throughout the life of the case.

Section 329(b) review is not time-limited. A motion under § 329(b) may be filed at any time during the case, by the United States Trustee, any party in interest, or sua sponte by the court. Post-confirmation supplemental disclosures (or failures to make them) are within scope. The remedies include disgorgement, fee reduction, and cancellation of fee-sharing agreements; in cases involving misrepresentation, the court may order disgorgement of the full amount paid, not merely the amount in excess of reasonable value.

The interaction with the § 330 fee-approval process is structural. Section 330 governs prospective allowance of compensation to professionals employed under § 327; § 329 governs retrospective review of compensation paid by or on behalf of the debtor. The two operate on different procedural tracks and apply different standards, but both depend on the Rule 2016(b) disclosure to function.

Consequences of Disclosure Failures

The case law on Rule 2016(b) disclosure failures is uniform on one point: the disclosure obligation is mandatory and self-executing, and good faith does not excuse non-compliance. Representative authorities include:

  • In re Whitley, 737 F.3d 980, 988–89 (5th Cir. 2013) (failure to disclose post-petition fee arrangement supports disgorgement under § 329(b))
  • In re Kisseberth, 273 F.3d 714, 720 (6th Cir. 2001) (failure to file Rule 2016(b) statement is itself a basis for disgorgement)
  • In re Saturley, 131 B.R. 509 (Bankr. D. Me. 1991) (continuing duty to amend; supplemental disclosure obligation is open-ended)
  • In re Park-Helena Corp., 63 F.3d 877 (9th Cir. 1995) (incomplete disclosure justifies denial or reduction of compensation regardless of the actual reasonableness of the underlying services)

The practical consequences of post-confirmation disclosure failures can include (i) denial or reduction of the undisclosed compensation under § 330 if a fee application is later filed; (ii) disgorgement of amounts already received under § 329(b); (iii) sanctions under § 105(a) or Federal Rule of Bankruptcy Procedure 9011; (iv) referral to the United States Trustee for further review under 28 U.S.C. § 586(a)(3); and (v) reporting obligations of opposing counsel and the United States Trustee under MRPC 8.3 if the conduct rises to the level of a substantial question as to honesty, trustworthiness, or fitness.

Practical Mechanics: When Must the Firm File?

The operative question for a firm representing a debtor in a confirmed Chapter 11, Chapter 13, or Subchapter V case is: when must a supplemental statement be filed, and when must a fee application be filed?

Supplemental statement under Rule 2016(b). Required within fourteen days whenever any payment or agreement is made that was not previously disclosed. This includes any post-confirmation arrangement under which the firm will continue to bill the debtor for services. The supplemental statement is a disclosure obligation; it does not by itself authorize the firm to bill.

Fee application under Section 330. Required for any post-confirmation services that fall outside the local-rule carve-out for routine services. The application must comply with the form and content requirements of Rule 2016(a) and any local-rule supplement (such as the requirement that detailed time records be attached and that the application include a project-by-project itemization). The application is noticed to the United States Trustee, the debtor, and other parties in interest, with an opportunity for hearing.

Routine services within the carve-out. No supplemental statement required if the services are within the scope of the original Form B2030 disclosure. No fee application required if the local rule expressly carves out the service. The firm bills from the original retainer or as specified in the original disclosure.

The mechanism of choice depends on the specific service and the original disclosure's scope. A firm that planned for a narrow scope at the petition stage and now finds itself rendering substantial post-confirmation services has both a supplemental-disclosure obligation and a fee-application obligation. A firm that bills post-confirmation services without filing either is in violation of Rule 2016(b) and potentially of § 330; the underlying payment is subject to § 329(b) review and disgorgement.
