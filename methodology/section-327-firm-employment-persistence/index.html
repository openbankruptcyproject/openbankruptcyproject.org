Section 327 Employment of Professional Persons — How Firm Authorization Persists After Individual Attorney Movement

First published 2026-05-22/Version 0.1.0/CC-BY-4.0/status: draft

Abstract

11 U.S.C. § 327 authorizes the trustee (or, in Chapter 11 and Subchapter V, the debtor in possession under § 1107(a)) to employ professional persons to assist in administering the estate. The authorization runs to the named professional — ordinarily a firm — not to any individual attorney within the firm. The distinction is consequential when an individual attorney within an employed firm departs, withdraws an appearance, or becomes unavailable: the firm's § 327 authorization continues by default, and termination requires a separate procedural step under Federal Rule of Bankruptcy Procedure 2014 or by motion to modify or vacate the employment order.

The persistence of firm-level authorization across individual-attorney movement is a feature of the statutory architecture, not a drafting accident. Bankruptcy estates require continuity of professional representation; the trustee should not lose authority to compensate the firm merely because the firm's lead attorney has departed. The continuing duty of disinterestedness and the continuing supervisory duty under Model Rule of Professional Conduct 5.1 follow the firm, not the individual.

Statutory Text and Structure

11 U.S.C. § 327(a) reads:

"Except as otherwise provided in this section, the trustee, with the court's approval, may employ one or more attorneys, accountants, appraisers, auctioneers, or other professional persons, that do not hold or represent an interest adverse to the estate, and that are disinterested persons, to represent or assist the trustee in carrying out the trustee's duties under this title."

Section 327(b) authorizes employment of professionals previously regularly employed by the debtor "if such professional person is not an attorney." Section 327(c) addresses creditor representation. Section 327(d) authorizes the court to specify the terms of employment. Section 327(e) creates a narrower track for employment of the debtor's prior attorney "for a specified special purpose, other than to represent the trustee in conducting the case," subject to disinterestedness with respect to the specific matter.

Section 1107(a) gives the debtor in possession all rights, powers, and duties of a trustee in a Chapter 11 case, including the § 327 employment power. The same provision applies in Subchapter V cases by incorporation through 11 U.S.C. § 1184. In a Chapter 13 case, the standing trustee's authority is more limited, but the debtor's attorney is typically engaged through a separate disclosure and compensation framework rather than a § 327 employment order.

The disinterestedness requirement is defined at 11 U.S.C. § 101(14): an entity that is not a creditor, equity security holder, or insider; is not an officer or director of the debtor; does not have an interest materially adverse to the estate; and meets other specified criteria. The disinterestedness requirement applies at the time of employment and continues throughout the engagement.

The Firm as the Named Professional Person

Employment under § 327 is typically requested by an Application to Employ filed under Federal Rule of Bankruptcy Procedure 2014(a). The application names the professional to be employed, recites the disinterestedness requirements, and attaches a verified statement of the professional disclosing connections to the debtor, creditors, and other parties in interest.

Rule 2014(a) prescribes the content of the application:

"An order approving the employment of attorneys, accountants, appraisers, auctioneers, agents, or other professionals pursuant to § 327, § 1103, or § 1114 of the Code shall be made only on application of the trustee or committee. The application shall be filed and a copy of the application shall be transmitted by the applicant to the United States trustee. The application shall state the specific facts showing the necessity for the employment, the name of the person to be employed, the reasons for the selection, the professional services to be rendered, any proposed arrangement for compensation, and, to the best of the applicant's knowledge, all of the person's connections with the debtor, creditors, any other party in interest, their respective attorneys and accountants, the United States trustee, or any person employed in the office of the United States trustee."

The application typically names the firm as the "person to be employed" and identifies individual attorneys within the firm whose services are anticipated. The order entered on the application authorizes the firm to render services and to seek compensation under § 330. The order's named professional is the firm; the listed individual attorneys are descriptive of the staffing model but do not themselves become § 327-employed apart from the firm.

Where the application contemplates that a specific individual attorney's expertise is the basis for engagement (sometimes called the "lead attorney" framing), some courts will note in the employment order that the engagement is premised on that attorney's involvement. Even in such cases, the order runs to the firm, and the firm's compensation rights persist until the order is modified or vacated, even if the lead attorney departs.

The Continuing Disinterestedness Duty

The disinterestedness requirement under § 327(a) is not satisfied once at the inception of the engagement. It is a continuing duty. The professional must remain disinterested throughout the engagement; if circumstances change such that a previously disclosed connection ripens into a disqualifying adverse interest, the professional has an affirmative duty to disclose the change and may be required to withdraw or to limit the scope of services.

The continuing duty was articulated in Rome v. Braunstein, 19 F.3d 54, 59 (1st Cir. 1994):

"The disclosure requirements of Rule 2014 are not discretionary. An attorney who fails to disclose timely and completely his connections with the debtor, creditors, and other parties in interest is held to have failed to satisfy his or her duty of disclosure under Rule 2014, even if the failure resulted from negligence rather than from bad faith."

Subsequent decisions have applied the duty broadly. In re EWC, Inc., 138 B.R. 276, 280 (Bankr. W.D. Okla. 1992), held that the duty to update Rule 2014 disclosures continues until the case is closed; In re Granite Partners, L.P., 219 B.R. 22, 35 (Bankr. S.D.N.Y. 1998), reinforced that the firm bears the continuing duty regardless of which individual within the firm originally executed the disclosure.

The continuing duty creates a structural obligation that survives individual-attorney movement. When an individual attorney within an employed firm departs (whether through firm exit, death, or removal from the case), the firm must assess whether the departure or any related circumstance triggers a new disclosure. The firm cannot deflect responsibility to the departed individual; the disclosure duty belongs to the firm.

The Supreme Court's decision in In re Pierce, 809 F.2d 1356 (9th Cir. 1987), and the Tenth Circuit's analysis in In re Interwest Business Equipment, Inc., 23 F.3d 311 (10th Cir. 1994), both treat the disclosure obligation as firm-level: the firm is the § 327-employed entity, and the firm bears the duty.

What Happens When One Attorney Departs

Three scenarios capture the recurring patterns when an individual attorney within an employed firm departs the case.

Scenario A: Lead attorney departs the firm; remaining firm continues representation. The departing attorney's individual appearance is withdrawn under the local rules governing withdrawal of appearance. The firm's § 327 employment continues unchanged. Remaining attorneys within the firm continue to render services. The firm has a continuing duty to update the Rule 2014 disclosure if the departure surfaces any previously undisclosed connection or any new adverse interest. No new § 327 application is required for the firm to continue billing; the original employment order remains operative.

Scenario B: Lead attorney departs the firm and continues representing the debtor under a new firm. Two parallel procedural events occur. First, the original firm's § 327 employment continues until terminated by motion; the firm may file a motion to modify or vacate the employment order if it no longer wishes to be employed. Second, the new firm (now engaging the attorney) must file its own Rule 2014 application if it intends to bill the estate. The estate cannot be billed by two firms for the same services; the parties must clarify which firm holds the § 327 authorization for the engagement going forward.

Scenario C: Lead attorney departs the firm; no successor firm has been engaged; the firm wishes to withdraw entirely. The firm must file a motion to withdraw under the local rule governing attorney withdrawal AND must seek modification or vacatur of the § 327 employment order. In a corporate case, the § 327 employment cannot be terminated until successor counsel is engaged and a new employment order entered, because the estate must have authorized representation. The firm remains the § 327-employed professional in the interim and continues to bear the disinterestedness, communication, and competence duties.

How Firm Employment Is Terminated

Termination of a § 327 employment order requires court action. The Bankruptcy Code does not provide a self-executing termination mechanism; the order continues in force until modified or vacated. Procedurally, termination is accomplished by one of the following:

  • Motion to modify or vacate the employment order. The employed firm, the United States Trustee, or any party in interest may file a motion seeking modification or vacatur. The motion is noticed for hearing under the local rules. The court considers whether the firm remains disinterested, whether circumstances justify termination, and (in a corporate case) whether successor counsel has been engaged.
  • Motion to disqualify. Where the basis for termination is a newly-arisen disqualifying conflict, the United States Trustee or a party in interest may file a motion to disqualify the firm under § 327(a). Disqualification is a form of termination that imposes additional consequences, including potential disgorgement of fees paid during the period of disqualifying conflict under § 328(c).
  • Conversion or dismissal of the case. Conversion under § 1112 or dismissal terminates the employment by terminating the underlying case. In a Chapter 7 conversion, the appointed Chapter 7 trustee will engage independent counsel; the previously-employed firm's authority terminates with respect to the converted case.
  • Closing of the case under § 350. Final decree closes the case and terminates all professional employment authorizations as a function of the case's closure.

An individual attorney's withdrawal of appearance does not terminate the firm's § 327 employment. Departure of the lead attorney does not terminate the firm's § 327 employment. The death or disability of any individual attorney does not terminate the firm's § 327 employment. Only a court order modifying or vacating the employment order, or one of the case-terminating events listed above, terminates the firm's authority.

Compensation Consequences of the Firm-Level Architecture

Under § 330(a), only entities employed under § 327, § 1103, or § 1114 may be paid from the estate for professional services rendered in the case. The corollary is twofold.

First, services rendered by attorneys within an § 327-employed firm are compensable from the estate, regardless of which individual attorney rendered the services, provided the services fall within the scope of the employment order. A firm that continues to render services after the departure of its lead attorney may continue to seek compensation; the question is whether the services are reasonable and necessary under the § 330 standard, not whether the lead attorney is still on the case.

Second, services rendered by an attorney whose firm has been disemployed are not compensable from the estate, even if the attorney remains the docket-listed counsel of record. The attorney may continue to act on the docket (and bears continuing professional duties under the rules of professional conduct), but the estate is not the source of compensation. The attorney must look to the debtor personally or to a third-party source, and the compensation arrangement must be disclosed under Rule 2016(b).

This creates a structural distinction between the docket appearance and the § 327 employment that is easy to lose sight of. The docket appearance authorizes the attorney to act and to receive service; the § 327 employment authorizes the firm to be paid from the estate. The two records track different attributes of the engagement and are terminated by different procedural mechanisms.
