Federal Rule of Bankruptcy Procedure 9011 Sanctions Practice

First published 2026-05-22/Version 0.1.0/CC-BY-4.0/status: draft

Abstract

Federal Rule of Bankruptcy Procedure 9011 is the bankruptcy-court counterpart to Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 11. It governs the certifications that an attorney or unrepresented party makes by presenting a petition, pleading, written motion, or other paper to the bankruptcy court. Rule 9011(b) imposes four affirmative certifications regarding the paper's purpose, legal warrant, factual basis, and evidentiary support; Rule 9011(c) supplies the sanctions procedure, including the 21-day safe-harbor mechanism for motion-initiated sanctions and the court's sua sponte authority.

The rule's standard is objective: an attorney's subjective good faith does not insulate a paper from sanctions if a reasonable inquiry would have disclosed the defect. The available sanctions range from monetary penalties payable to the court to fee-shifting awards payable to the opposing party. Rule 9011 is a routine feature of bankruptcy practice and is engaged with particular frequency in connection with petition accuracy, schedule completeness, automatic-stay-violation disputes, and proofs of claim.

Rule Text and Structure

Rule 9011(a) — Signature

Rule 9011(a) requires every petition, pleading, written motion, or other paper to be signed by at least one attorney of record in the attorney's individual name, or, if the party is unrepresented, by the party. The signature requirement is the trigger for the substantive certifications under subdivision (b). An unsigned paper must be stricken unless the omission is corrected promptly after being called to the attorney's or party's attention.

Rule 9011(b) — Representations to the Court

Rule 9011(b) provides:

"By presenting to the court (whether by signing, filing, submitting, or later advocating) a petition, pleading, written motion, or other paper, an attorney or unrepresented party is certifying that to the best of the person's knowledge, information, and belief, formed after an inquiry reasonable under the circumstances, —
(1) it is not being presented for any improper purpose, such as to harass or to cause unnecessary delay or needless increase in the cost of litigation;
(2) the claims, defenses, and other legal contentions therein are warranted by existing law or by a nonfrivolous argument for the extension, modification, or reversal of existing law or the establishment of new law;
(3) the allegations and other factual contentions have evidentiary support or, if specifically so identified, are likely to have evidentiary support after a reasonable opportunity for further investigation or discovery; and
(4) the denials of factual contentions are warranted on the evidence or, if specifically so identified, are reasonably based on a lack of information or belief."

The four certifications attach not only at the moment of signing but also at the moment of "later advocating" the paper. An attorney who learns, after filing, that a previously-presented factual contention lacks evidentiary support has a duty to refrain from continued advocacy of that contention. Rule 9011(b)'s "later advocating" clause is the rule's continuing-duty hook.

Rule 9011(c) — Sanctions

Rule 9011(c) provides:

"If, after notice and a reasonable opportunity to respond, the court determines that subdivision (b) has been violated, the court may, subject to the conditions stated below, impose an appropriate sanction upon the attorneys, law firms, or parties that have violated subdivision (b) or are responsible for the violation."

The two operative subdivisions of (c) describe the procedures by which sanctions may be sought: (c)(1)(A) governs motions by another party (with the safe-harbor mechanism), and (c)(1)(B) governs the court's own initiative (sua sponte).

Rule 9011(d) — Inapplicability to Discovery

Rule 9011(d) excludes from the rule's scope discovery requests, responses, objections, and motions covered by Rules 7026 through 7037. Discovery conduct is governed by the discovery rules themselves, which contain their own sanctions framework.

Parallel to Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 11

Rule 9011 was patterned on FRCP 11 and was amended in 1997 to incorporate the 1993 amendments to FRCP 11. The two rules use substantively identical operative text. Differences between the rules are limited and primarily reflect the bankruptcy context.

  • Scope of papers covered. Rule 9011 expressly covers "petitions," reflecting the centrality of the petition in bankruptcy practice. FRCP 11 does not include "petition" as a separately enumerated category because civil litigation typically begins with a complaint.
  • Verified papers exception. Rule 9011 does not contain a verified-pleadings exception comparable to one feature of FRCP 11 practice in some districts. Bankruptcy filings frequently require verification under penalty of perjury (the petition, the schedules, the statement of financial affairs); the Rule 9011 certification operates in parallel with the verification requirement but is not displaced by it.
  • Inapplicability to Rule 2014 disclosures. The interplay between Rule 9011 and Rule 2014 disclosure obligations regarding professional connections is layered: the Rule 2014 disclosure is itself subject to Rule 9011, but inadequate Rule 2014 disclosure is more typically addressed through § 327 disqualification and § 330 fee denial than through Rule 9011 sanctions.

The substantive identity of the certification standards means that the substantial body of FRCP 11 case law informs Rule 9011 practice. The Supreme Court's leading Rule 11 decisions, including Cooter & Gell v. Hartmarx Corp., 496 U.S. 384 (1990), and Pavelic & LeFlore v. Marvel Entertainment Group, 493 U.S. 120 (1989), are routinely cited in Rule 9011 proceedings.

The "Objectively Reasonable" Inquiry Standard

Rule 9011(b)'s certifications are made "to the best of the person's knowledge, information, and belief, formed after an inquiry reasonable under the circumstances." The standard is objective: the question is what a reasonable attorney in the circumstances would have known or believed after a reasonable inquiry, not what the signing attorney subjectively believed.

The Supreme Court articulated the objective standard for the analogous FRCP 11 inquiry in Business Guides, Inc. v. Chromatic Communications Enterprises, Inc., 498 U.S. 533 (1991), holding that "Rule 11 imposes on any party who signs a pleading, motion, or other paper an affirmative duty to conduct a reasonable inquiry into the facts and the law before filing, and that the applicable standard is one of reasonableness under the circumstances." Subjective good faith is not a defense; objective unreasonableness establishes the violation.

The "reasonableness under the circumstances" qualifier is fact-sensitive. Relevant circumstances include the time available before the filing deadline; the complexity of the matter; the resources reasonably available to investigate; the extent to which the attorney was relying on a client's representations; and the consequences of error. A late-filed emergency motion is held to a different reasonable-inquiry standard than a well-resourced reply brief filed months after the underlying matter was joined.

For factual contentions, reasonable inquiry typically requires the attorney to obtain support for the contention from primary sources: client interviews, document review, witness statements, expert reports. Reliance solely on adversary representations or on a single client statement, where readily-available verification was not pursued, is a recurring basis for finding the inquiry inadequate. For legal contentions, reasonable inquiry requires consultation of the controlling statutes, rules, and case law of the relevant jurisdiction; a contention contradicted by uniformly-applied controlling precedent will not pass reasonable-inquiry review absent a specifically-identified non-frivolous argument for change in the law.

The 21-Day Safe Harbor and Motion Procedure

Rule 9011(c)(1)(A) governs motions by another party. The procedure has three sequential steps:

  1. Service of the motion. The motion for sanctions must be served on the offending attorney or party but not filed with the court. The motion must describe the specific conduct alleged to violate Rule 9011(b).
  2. The 21-day safe harbor. The offending attorney or party has 21 days from service in which to withdraw or appropriately correct the challenged paper, claim, defense, or contention. If the challenge is withdrawn within the safe-harbor period, no motion for sanctions may be filed.
  3. Filing after expiration. If the offending paper is not withdrawn or corrected within 21 days, the moving party may then file the motion with the court. The court determines the motion on the merits, with notice and opportunity for response.

The safe-harbor mechanism is a strict procedural prerequisite. A motion filed without the 21-day pre-service period must be denied on procedural grounds, regardless of the merits of the underlying Rule 9011 challenge. The Supreme Court has reinforced the strictness of the safe-harbor requirement in FRCP 11 practice; the bankruptcy courts apply the same standard to Rule 9011.

The safe-harbor mechanism does not apply to sanctions imposed sua sponte by the court. The court may, on its own initiative under Rule 9011(c)(1)(B), enter an order directing an attorney or party to show cause why specified conduct has not violated subdivision (b). The court may then impose sanctions after notice and an opportunity to be heard; the 21-day safe harbor is not available because the procedural framework runs through the court rather than through opposing-party motion.

Sua Sponte Sanctions Procedure

Rule 9011(c)(1)(B) authorizes the court to enter sanctions on its own initiative. The procedure requires the court to enter an order describing the specific conduct that appears to violate subdivision (b) and directing the attorney, law firm, or party to show cause why the conduct has not violated subdivision (b).

The sua sponte procedure is functionally available throughout the case. A court that observes, in the course of routine docket review, a pattern of papers that appear not to satisfy the reasonable-inquiry standard may issue a show-cause order at any time. The court is not dependent on a party's motion and not bound by the safe-harbor mechanism that conditions party-initiated sanctions.

Two sua-sponte triggers are common in bankruptcy practice:

  • Petition and schedule deficiencies. Where the petition, schedules, or statement of financial affairs contains errors or omissions that pattern-suggest inadequate pre-filing inquiry, the court may issue a show-cause order regardless of any creditor complaint.
  • Repeated contention rejected on the merits. Where a contention has been rejected by the court on the merits and the attorney continues to advocate the same contention in subsequent papers, the "later advocating" prong of Rule 9011(b) supplies the basis for sua sponte review.

Available Sanctions

Rule 9011(c)(2) describes the available sanctions. The sanctions imposed must be "limited to what is sufficient to deter repetition of such conduct or comparable conduct by others similarly situated." The available sanctions include:

  • Monetary penalty payable to the court. A fine or other monetary penalty payable to the court treasury. This is the default form of sanction for sua-sponte-initiated proceedings and is typically the only form of sanction available where the violation involved a non-economic harm.
  • Fee-shifting payable to the moving party. An award to the moving party of some or all of the reasonable attorney fees and other expenses incurred as a direct result of the violation. This form of sanction is more typical in motion-initiated proceedings under Rule 9011(c)(1)(A) and requires a causation showing.
  • Directives of a nonmonetary nature. The rule contemplates nonmonetary sanctions such as orders striking the offending paper, requiring corrective filings, requiring continuing-legal-education attendance, or other equitable measures tailored to the violation.

Rule 9011(c)(2) imposes two specific limits. First, sanctions against a represented party may not be imposed for violations of subdivision (b)(2) (the legal-warranty certification); only the attorney may be sanctioned for an unwarranted legal contention. Second, sanctions for violations of subdivisions (b)(1), (b)(3), and (b)(4) may be imposed on the represented party where the party participated in the violation.

The court must include in any sanctions order a description of the conduct found to violate the rule and an explanation of the basis for the sanction. The explanation requirement creates an appellate record and ensures the sanctions decision is reviewable.

Firm vs. Individual Liability

Rule 9011(c) authorizes sanctions against "the attorneys, law firms, or parties that have violated subdivision (b) or are responsible for the violation." The plural reach permits sanction of both the individual signing attorney and the firm. The 1993 amendments to FRCP 11, paralleled in Rule 9011, made firm liability presumptive for the conduct of partners and associates acting within the scope of their employment.

The Supreme Court's decision in Pavelic & LeFlore v. Marvel Entertainment Group, 493 U.S. 120 (1989), held under the pre-1993 version of FRCP 11 that the law firm could not be sanctioned. The 1993 amendments overruled this aspect of Pavelic; firm liability is now expressly available in both rules. The firm's liability is independent of the individual attorney's liability and may be imposed in addition to, or instead of, individual sanctions.

In bankruptcy practice, firm liability commonly attaches where the conduct reflects an inadequate firm-wide procedure (such as routine filing of facially-deficient petitions in a high-volume practice) or where supervisory attorneys ratified the offending conduct. Firm liability runs parallel to MRPC 5.1 supervisory liability under the state-bar rules.

Strategic Considerations: Bringing and Defending Rule 9011 Motions

Bringing a Rule 9011 motion

The decision to bring a Rule 9011 motion involves several strategic considerations:

  • Safe-harbor compliance. The 21-day safe-harbor requirement is non-negotiable. A motion that fails to comply will be denied on procedural grounds. The pre-filing safe-harbor letter should describe the alleged violation with sufficient specificity to make withdrawal or correction practicable.
  • Cost-benefit of fee-shifting. The fee-shifting sanction must be limited to fees incurred "as a direct result of the violation," and the moving party bears the burden of documenting the causal connection. Where the underlying litigation would have been costly even without the violation, the fee-shifting recovery may be modest.
  • Tone and proportionality. Bankruptcy courts disfavor Rule 9011 motions used as litigation tactics. A motion that is itself disproportionate to the underlying violation may invite Rule 9011 review of the moving party. Targeting clear, demonstrable violations conserves credibility.
  • Alternative remedies. Many Rule 9011-eligible disputes can be addressed more efficiently through other mechanisms: a motion to strike, a motion in limine, a motion for summary judgment, or a request for an evidentiary hearing. Rule 9011 should be reserved for conduct that survives or recurs after these less-formal mechanisms have been pursued.

Defending a Rule 9011 motion

  • Use the safe harbor. The 21-day safe-harbor period is the principal defensive opportunity. Withdrawal or correction of the challenged paper within the period defeats the motion entirely. A defensible posture is to assess the challenge on its merits during the safe-harbor period and, where the assessment confirms the violation, to take corrective action rather than risk an adverse ruling.
  • Document the reasonable inquiry. The objective-reasonableness standard turns on what a reasonable attorney would have done under the circumstances. Documentation of the pre-filing inquiry — research memoranda, client interviews, fact-investigation notes — supports the reasonableness defense.
  • Address the specific conduct. A defense that addresses the motion's characterization of the conduct generically, without engaging the specific certifications alleged to have been violated, will not satisfy the court. Each challenged certification must be addressed individually.
  • Avoid escalation. Defending a Rule 9011 motion by counter-attacking the moving party is rarely productive. The court evaluates the defendant's conduct, not the moving party's motives; counter-attack tends to confirm an unfavorable impression.

Common Rule 9011 Violations in Bankruptcy Practice

Recurring factual patterns generate the bulk of Rule 9011 practice in bankruptcy court:

  • Petition and schedule deficiencies. Filing of petitions or schedules with material inaccuracies that would have been corrected by a reasonable client interview or document review.
  • Strategic dismissal-and-refiling. Serial filings to invoke and re-invoke the automatic stay without genuine reorganization intent.
  • Proof-of-claim defects. Filing of proofs of claim that lack supporting documentation, that misstate the claim amount, or that fail to disclose the basis for the claim.
  • Untenable plan provisions. Filing of plans containing provisions facially contrary to controlling Supreme Court or circuit precedent.
  • Motion practice without factual basis. Motions for stay relief, for conversion, or for dismissal that rest on factual contentions unsupported by the record.
  • Post-rejection re-advocacy. Continuing advocacy of contentions previously rejected by the court without acknowledgment of the prior ruling and without identifying a basis for reconsideration.

Each pattern is independently actionable. The court's tolerance for repeated incidents within a single case, or across multiple cases by the same attorney or firm, is generally low, and aggregate patterns may be cited to support a finding that conduct in any single case satisfies the objective-reasonableness test for violation.
