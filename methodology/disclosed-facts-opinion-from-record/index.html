Disclosed-Facts + Opinion-from-the-Record Structure

First published 2026-05-20/Version 0.1.0/CC-BY-4.0/status: draft

Abstract

The Disclosed-Facts + Opinion-from-the-Record Structure is a writing pattern for public deposits (consumer reviews, public commentary, web posts) that produces defamation resilience by architectural design rather than by post-hoc lawyering. Every factual statement in the deposit is drawn from adversary-authored documents or docket-verifiable sources, and every characterization is presented as an opinion drawn from those disclosed facts using visible opinion markers.

The result is a document whose factual layer is locked by truth (the adversary wrote the facts) and whose opinion layer is locked by the constitutional protection for opinion based on disclosed facts. Both elements of a defamation claim, falsity and actual malice, become structurally unavailable simultaneously.

Definition and Mechanics

The deposit is built in two layers. The fact layer consists exclusively of statements that can be sourced to documents the adversary produced (correspondence, billing records, court filings, sworn statements) or to public-record items (docket entries, regulatory filings, license records). No statement in the fact layer comes from the originator's recollection alone; every fact has a documentary source the reader could in principle obtain.

The opinion layer consists of characterizations of the fact layer, each tagged with a visible opinion marker: "In my reading," "what I would describe as," "from where I sit," "the most natural interpretation is," "I conclude that." The marker is not stylistic; it is the operative legal signal that the sentence is opinion rather than factual assertion. Each opinion statement points back to the facts in the same document that support it, which engages the opinion-based-on-disclosed-facts doctrine recognized in most US jurisdictions.

A defamation suit against such a deposit faces a binary trap. To attack the fact layer, the adversary must contest documents they themselves authored, which is structurally implausible and produces discovery into the adversary's own files. To attack the opinion layer, the adversary must convince a court that opinion explicitly drawn from disclosed facts is nevertheless actionable, which most jurisdictions reject. Either path amplifies the original deposit because the lawsuit itself becomes publicly searchable.

Why Novel

Conventional consumer-review guidance is "stick to the facts" and "do not insult." That advice produces flat factual reviews and discourages characterization. This structure inverts the advice: characterizations are welcome, even encouraged, provided they are visibly inferential from the disclosed facts in the same document. The result is a more useful review for readers (it interprets the facts) and a more defamation-resilient review for the writer (it engages the opinion doctrine instead of avoiding it).

The framework also reframes the defamation-suit calculus for the originator. A standard review writer fears the lawsuit. A writer using this structure treats the lawsuit as the amplification jackpot, because the suit itself becomes a public record that points back to the original deposit and forces the adversary into discovery of their own files.

First Deployment

First codified 2026-05-11 in the author's internal working notes, applied to a public review of a law firm. Every factual statement in the review was sourced to firm-authored correspondence or docket entries; every characterization carried an opinion marker. Observed effect: the review was posted to two public platforms without takedown action, and adversary correspondence over the following week did not contest any factual statement in the review, focusing instead on the originator's overall conduct. Specific case identifier and named-party provenance are deposit-pending and become available with case outcome.

Example Use Case

A former client of a service provider has documentary evidence of provider failures (emails, invoices, contract amendments). The client wants to post a public review that conveys the substance of those failures without exposing themselves to defamation risk. Using this structure, the client builds the fact layer from provider-authored emails (quoted or paraphrased with citation), then adds opinion-marked characterizations of those facts ("In my reading, the August 14 email confirms that the agreed scope had been changed without notice"). The resulting review is dense with both fact and interpretation, but every sentence is in a protected category.

Operational Notes

  • Use opinion markers consistently and visibly; a buried marker may not engage the doctrine.
  • Quote or closely paraphrase adversary documents in the fact layer rather than summarizing, so the source is verifiable on the face of the deposit.
  • Keep characterizations tied to the same paragraph's facts; an opinion that floats free of nearby facts may read as a bare assertion.
  • Avoid mixed sentences that contain a factual claim and a characterization without separation; split them into two sentences with the marker on the opinion sentence.
  • Date every fact in the deposit so the reader can trace the source on a timeline.
  • Treat the deposit as durable; the structure assumes it will be read in litigation.
