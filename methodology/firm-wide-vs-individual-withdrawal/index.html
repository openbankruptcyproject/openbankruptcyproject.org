Firm-Wide vs. Individual Attorney Withdrawal in U.S. Bankruptcy Court

First published 2026-05-22/Version 0.1.0/CC-BY-4.0/status: draft

Abstract

Withdrawal of counsel in United States bankruptcy practice is not a single procedural event. Two distinct mechanisms are in play whenever a law firm represents a debtor or other party in interest: the entry and withdrawal of an individual attorney's appearance, governed by the local rules of the relevant bankruptcy court; and the court-approved employment of the firm itself as a professional person under 11 U.S.C. § 327, which is governed by Federal Rule of Bankruptcy Procedure 2014 and is terminated only by an order modifying or vacating the employment order. The two operate on different records, have different prerequisites, and produce different downstream consequences when one is performed without the other.

The distinction matters most acutely in corporate Chapter 11 and Subchapter V cases, where the debtor cannot proceed pro se and where a firm departure without successor counsel can stall the estate. It also matters for supervisory liability under the Model Rules of Professional Conduct, for compensation review under § 329, and for the integrity of the docket as a public record of who is authorized to act for the estate at any given moment.

The Two Records: Appearance vs. Employment

Every bankruptcy case maintains two parallel records of who acts for whom. The first is the entry of appearance. An individual attorney enters an appearance by filing a notice or by signing a pleading on behalf of a party; once entered, the attorney is "counsel of record" and receives Bankruptcy Noticing Center service. The appearance is an individual event, attached to a single licensed attorney's name and bar number.

The second is the employment of the professional person. Under 11 U.S.C. § 327(a), the trustee (or debtor in possession exercising trustee powers under § 1107(a)) "may employ one or more attorneys, accountants, appraisers, auctioneers, or other professional persons, that do not hold or represent an interest adverse to the estate, and that are disinterested persons, to represent or assist the trustee in carrying out the trustee's duties under this title." Section 327(e) permits employment of "an attorney that has represented the debtor" for a specified special purpose. In either case, employment requires court approval on an application filed under Federal Rule of Bankruptcy Procedure 2014(a), which prescribes the form and disclosures required.

The application for employment names the firm and identifies each attorney within the firm expected to render services. The order approving employment runs to the firm. Termination of the order is a separate procedural event from the withdrawal of any one attorney's appearance.

Withdrawal Under Local Rules: Western District of Missouri and Kansas Examples

The procedural prerequisites for an attorney's withdrawal of appearance are set by the local rules of the bankruptcy court in which the case is pending. Two representative examples illustrate the structure that prevails across most districts.

W.D. Mo. Bankr. L.R. 2019-1 (formerly 2091-1)

The Western District of Missouri renumbered its withdrawal rule from L.R. 2091-1 to L.R. 2019-1 (Attorneys – Withdrawal and Substitution) in the Local Rules adopted January 5, 2026; citations to 2091-1 in earlier filings refer to the same rule. The rule's operative subsection (A) provides:

"An attorney of record may withdraw only by Court permission, by an order entered after service of notice of withdrawal on the client, other counsel of record, the trustee, if any, and the United States Trustee. An order will not be entered until new counsel enters an appearance, except for good cause."

Two features control. First, withdrawal requires Court permission, on notice served on the client, other counsel of record, the trustee (if any), and the United States Trustee, not a unilateral notice. Second, the order will not be entered absent successor counsel's appearance, except for good cause. The "except for good cause" clause supplies the only escape from the successor-counsel prerequisite; it is narrowly applied and is most commonly invoked in individual consumer cases where the debtor will proceed pro se.

D. Kan. Bankr. L.R. 9010.1

The District of Kansas Bankruptcy Court's Local Rule 9010.1 imposes a parallel structure. Counsel of record may not withdraw without leave of court, granted on motion served on the client and parties in interest. The rule similarly conditions withdrawal in cases where the client is a corporation or other artificial entity on the entry of successor counsel, because a corporation cannot litigate without counsel in the federal courts.

Other districts follow comparable patterns. The recurring features across the federal bankruptcy local-rule landscape are: (i) leave of court required, (ii) motion served on the client and on appearing parties, and (iii) some form of successor-counsel prerequisite for corporate or other non-natural-person clients.

The Two-Step Mechanism for Firm Withdrawal

Firm withdrawal under § 327 is procedurally distinct from individual attorney withdrawal and typically requires a two-step mechanism when no successor firm has yet been engaged.

Step One: Limited Entry of Appearance. An attorney unaffiliated with the employed firm enters a Limited Entry of Appearance solely for the purpose of filing a motion seeking the firm's withdrawal. The Limited Entry is scoped on its face to the withdrawal motion and does not constitute general representation of the debtor. This step is necessary in corporate cases because the debtor entity cannot file the motion itself; the corporation cannot proceed pro se in federal court, and the existing firm has a conflict in filing its own withdrawal motion against the interests of its client.

Step Two: The Firm Withdrawal Motion. The Limited-Entry attorney then files a motion seeking leave for the firm to withdraw from its § 327-approved employment. The motion describes the basis for withdrawal, identifies the proposed successor firm (or recites the absence of successor counsel and the good-cause justification for entry without it), and addresses fee disposition and file-transfer obligations under Federal Rule of Bankruptcy Procedure 2016 and the applicable state Rule of Professional Conduct 1.16(d).

The court will set the motion for hearing and consider any objection from the United States Trustee, the official committee (if any), and other parties in interest. In a corporate Chapter 11 or Subchapter V case, the United States Trustee will routinely insist on successor counsel's entry before the court grants withdrawal, because the alternative is conversion to Chapter 7 or dismissal.

The Corporate-Debtor Pro Se Bar

The successor-counsel prerequisite is not merely a local-rule artifact. It is anchored in the federal common-law rule that an artificial entity (corporation, LLC, partnership) cannot appear pro se in federal court. The rule was articulated by the Supreme Court in Rowland v. California Men's Colony, 506 U.S. 194, 201–02 (1993), which held that 28 U.S.C. § 1654 authorizes natural persons to litigate in person but does not extend that right to artificial entities. The rule is uniformly applied across the federal circuits and is reflected in bankruptcy practice through the requirement that corporate debtors maintain counsel of record at all times.

The practical consequence is that a firm representing a corporate debtor cannot effectively withdraw, regardless of how meritorious the firm's reasons for withdrawal, until successor counsel enters. Even a granted motion to withdraw will typically be conditioned on substitution. The firm is therefore in a constructive trust posture from the moment it forms an intent to withdraw until the moment substitution occurs: it owes ongoing duties of competence, communication, and loyalty under MRPC 1.1, 1.4, and 1.7 throughout the interval.

The bar applies in Chapter 11, Chapter 12, Subchapter V, and Chapter 7 corporate cases. It does not apply to individual debtors, who may proceed pro se if they elect to do so, subject to the court's general supervisory authority and to the inadvisability of pro se complex bankruptcy practice as a substantive matter.

Supervisory Implications Under MRPC 5.1 and Analog State Rules

When individual attorneys within an employed firm withdraw their appearances but the firm's § 327 employment order remains in force, supervisory liability attaches to the firm's remaining partners under Model Rule of Professional Conduct 5.1. MRPC 5.1(a) imposes on partners and supervisory attorneys a duty to "make reasonable efforts to ensure that the firm has in effect measures giving reasonable assurance that all lawyers in the firm conform to the Rules of Professional Conduct." MRPC 5.1(c) imposes individual responsibility where a supervisory attorney "orders or, with knowledge of the specific conduct, ratifies the conduct involved" or fails to take reasonable remedial action.

The Kansas Rules of Professional Conduct adopt MRPC 5.1 substantively at KRPC 5.1. The Missouri Rules of Professional Conduct adopt the same substantive standard at Missouri Supreme Court Rule 4-5.1. Both rules trigger when the firm remains the employed professional person of record but the individual attorneys responsible for client communication and ongoing representation withdraw or become unavailable. The remaining partners cannot deflect responsibility to a departed individual attorney because the firm's § 327 authorization is the operative employment relationship, not any one attorney's appearance.

Comment [2] to MRPC 5.1 emphasizes that the supervisory duty extends to "the ethical atmosphere of a firm." A pattern in which individual attorneys quietly withdraw appearances while the firm maintains § 327 employment (and continues to bill the estate) without disclosing the staffing change to the United States Trustee or to the client raises distinct supervisory questions independent of any single attorney's conduct.

Docket-Listed Counsel of Record vs. Section 327 Authorization

A common point of confusion is the assumption that the docket header listing of "attorneys for debtor" is coextensive with the firm's § 327 employment authorization. It is not.

The docket header is a clerk-maintained ledger of attorneys who have entered appearances on behalf of each party. It is updated when new appearances are entered or when withdrawals are granted. The header may show that one attorney has been terminated from the docket while the firm's § 327 employment order remains in force; conversely, an attorney may continue to appear on the docket header after the firm's employment has been terminated by court order, until the docket is administratively updated.

The operative record of who is authorized to render compensable professional services to the estate is the § 327 employment order, as modified or vacated by subsequent orders. The application for employment under Bankruptcy Rule 2014 typically names individual attorneys within the firm whose services are expected, but the authorization runs to the firm. Departure of an individual attorney does not, without more, terminate the firm's authority; nor does an individual attorney's continued docket listing, without § 327 employment, authorize the attorney to bill the estate.

This distinction has direct compensation consequences. Under § 330, only entities employed under § 327 may be paid from the estate. Services rendered by an attorney whose appearance is on the docket but whose firm has been disemployed are not compensable from estate funds absent a separate § 327 application and order. Conversely, services rendered by a firm that remains § 327-employed are compensable even if the original lead attorney within the firm has departed.

Practical Mechanics: When the Mechanisms Diverge

The mechanisms diverge most visibly in three recurring scenarios.

Scenario 1: Lead attorney withdraws; firm remains employed. A firm represents a corporate debtor under a § 327 employment order naming several attorneys. The lead attorney withdraws her individual appearance and is terminated from the docket. The firm files no withdrawal motion. The firm's § 327 employment continues. The remaining named attorneys within the firm retain authority to render compensable services. Supervisory duty under MRPC 5.1 attaches to the firm's senior partners with respect to the continuing representation. The firm's duty of communication under MRPC 1.4 continues; the firm must notify the client of the staffing change.

Scenario 2: Firm files withdrawal motion; no successor counsel. The firm wishes to withdraw entirely but no successor firm has been engaged. In a corporate case, the firm's options are limited: it may file the motion and request entry conditioned on substitution; it may invoke the local-rule "good cause" exception, which is rarely granted in corporate cases; or it may seek conversion to Chapter 7, in which a Chapter 7 trustee will engage independent counsel. The firm continues to owe duties of competence, communication, and loyalty until substitution.

Scenario 3: Individual attorney departs the firm; remains on the case under new firm. An individual attorney leaves the originally-employed firm and joins a new firm. If the attorney continues to represent the debtor, a new § 327 application must be filed for the new firm; the attorney's individual appearance must be updated; and the original firm's § 327 employment must be addressed (either continued for limited purposes or terminated by motion). The original firm is not automatically disemployed merely because the lead attorney has departed.
