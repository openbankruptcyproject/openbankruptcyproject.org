KRPC 5.3 / MRPC 4-5.3 Supervision of Non-Lawyer Assistants and UPL Boundaries

First published 2026-05-22/Version 0.1.0/CC-BY-4.0/status: draft

Abstract

The supervision of non-lawyer assistants in a law firm is governed by two layers of authority. The first is the state-bar rule of professional conduct — in Kansas, KRPC 5.3; in Missouri, Missouri Supreme Court Rule 4-5.3 — which imposes supervisory responsibility on partners, lawyers with managerial authority, and the lawyer directly supervising a non-lawyer. The second is the state's unauthorized-practice-of-law statute — K.S.A. 7-104 in Kansas; RSMo § 484.020 in Missouri — which makes the unauthorized practice of law itself a separate offense subject to civil and criminal penalty.

The two regimes interact. A lawyer's failure to supervise a non-lawyer can produce conduct that constitutes UPL by the non-lawyer; the same failure can constitute a Rule 5.3 violation by the lawyer; and in egregious circumstances the lawyer's affirmative facilitation can amount to assisting in UPL under Rule 5.5. The 5.3(c) ratification-by-silence provision, parallel to the analogous provision in Rule 5.1, creates individual lawyer responsibility for non-lawyer conduct that the lawyer knew of and failed to remediate.

Rule Text: Kansas and Missouri

Kansas Rule of Professional Conduct 5.3

KRPC 5.3 provides:

"With respect to a nonlawyer employed or retained by or associated with a lawyer:
(a) a partner, and a lawyer who individually or together with other lawyers possesses comparable managerial authority in a law firm shall make reasonable efforts to ensure that the firm has in effect measures giving reasonable assurance that the person's conduct is compatible with the professional obligations of the lawyer;
(b) a lawyer having direct supervisory authority over the nonlawyer shall make reasonable efforts to ensure that the person's conduct is compatible with the professional obligations of the lawyer; and
(c) a lawyer shall be responsible for conduct of such a person that would be a violation of the rules of professional conduct if engaged in by a lawyer if:
(1) the lawyer orders or, with the knowledge of the specific conduct, ratifies the conduct involved; or
(2) the lawyer is a partner or has comparable managerial authority in the law firm in which the person is employed, or has direct supervisory authority over the person, and knows of the conduct at a time when its consequences can be avoided or mitigated but fails to take reasonable remedial action."

Missouri Supreme Court Rule 4-5.3

MRPC 4-5.3 is substantively identical, adopting the same three-subdivision structure and the same operative text. Both rules are derived from ABA Model Rule of Professional Conduct 5.3 with minor non-substantive variation.

The Three Tiers of Responsibility

Rule 5.3 establishes three distinct categories of responsibility, attaching to different categories of lawyer:

Subdivision (a) — Firm-level systems responsibility

Partners and lawyers with comparable managerial authority have a duty to ensure the firm has in effect "measures giving reasonable assurance" of non-lawyer compliance. The obligation is systemic: it concerns the firm's procedures, training, supervision practices, and disciplinary mechanisms. The duty attaches independently of any specific incident; failure to maintain reasonable systems is itself a violation, even where no specific non-lawyer misconduct has occurred.

Subdivision (b) — Direct supervisory responsibility

A lawyer with direct supervisory authority over a non-lawyer has a duty to make reasonable efforts to ensure that particular individual's conduct is compatible with the lawyer's professional obligations. The obligation runs to the specific lawyer-non-lawyer relationship and concerns the supervising lawyer's day-to-day oversight of the particular non-lawyer's work.

Subdivision (c) — Individual responsibility for non-lawyer conduct

A lawyer becomes personally responsible for a non-lawyer's conduct that would be a violation of the rules of professional conduct if engaged in by a lawyer, in either of two circumstances:

  • (c)(1) Ordering or ratifying. The lawyer orders the conduct, or, with knowledge of the specific conduct, ratifies the conduct involved.
  • (c)(2) Knowledge with failure to remediate. The lawyer is a partner, has comparable managerial authority, or has direct supervisory authority over the non-lawyer, and knows of the conduct at a time when its consequences can be avoided or mitigated, but fails to take reasonable remedial action.

The two subdivision-(c) routes are independent. The lawyer need not have ordered the conduct; knowledge plus failure to remediate is sufficient. The remediation obligation triggers when knowledge is acquired, not earlier; but once acquired, the obligation is actionable for failures to act.

Ratification by Silence Under 5.3(c)

Subdivision (c)(1)'s "ratifies" language is the operative ratification-by-silence trigger. Comment [1] to the Model Rule explains that ratification "may occur by a course of conduct as well as by express approval." A lawyer who learns of a non-lawyer's improper conduct, takes no remedial action, and continues to receive the benefit of the non-lawyer's work product effectively ratifies the conduct.

The ratification standard is parallel to the analogous standard in Rule 5.1(c) governing supervisory responsibility for the conduct of subordinate lawyers. The two rules operate in tandem: a supervisory lawyer's silence in the face of known misconduct triggers individual responsibility regardless of whether the actor is a non-lawyer (Rule 5.3) or a lawyer (Rule 5.1).

The "knowledge" element under (c)(2) is the substantive lever. Knowledge can be actual or constructive. A pattern of misconduct that produces visible artifacts (defective filings, client complaints, billing entries reflecting non-attorney legal work) supplies constructive knowledge to lawyers who reviewed the artifacts, regardless of whether the lawyer recognized the misconduct at the time. The Restatement (Third) of the Law Governing Lawyers § 11 articulates the constructive-knowledge standard in supervisory liability terms applicable to both Rule 5.1 and Rule 5.3.

The Unauthorized Practice of Law Framework

The unauthorized practice of law (UPL) is regulated separately from the rules of professional conduct. UPL statutes are state-law restrictions, typically codified in the practice-of-law title of the state code, and enforced through civil action by the state bar or attorney general and, in some states, by criminal prosecution.

Kansas UPL — K.S.A. 7-104

K.S.A. 7-104 provides that no person shall practice law in Kansas except a licensed attorney. The Kansas Supreme Court has plenary authority over the practice of law and has articulated the substantive definition of "practice of law" through a series of decisions including State ex rel. Stephan v. Williams, 246 Kan. 681 (1990), and Martin v. Walton, 368 P.2d 866 (Kan. 1962).

Missouri UPL — RSMo § 484.020

RSMo § 484.020 provides that no person shall engage in the practice of law in Missouri unless duly admitted to the bar. The Missouri Supreme Court regulates the practice of law under Mo. Const. art. V, § 5 and has articulated the substantive definition through decisions including Hulse v. Criger, 247 S.W.2d 855 (Mo. banc 1952), and subsequent Supreme Court rule adoptions.

The substantive definition of "practice of law"

Both states define the practice of law through a multi-element approach. The recurring elements include:

  • Giving legal advice to a specific person concerning that person's specific legal rights or obligations;
  • Drafting or selecting legal instruments by which legal rights are secured;
  • Representing a person in negotiations affecting legal rights;
  • Appearing as counsel before a court, administrative tribunal, or other adjudicative body;
  • Holding oneself out as authorized to perform the foregoing functions.

The definition is functional, not formal. A non-lawyer who performs any of the enumerated functions for another person, for compensation or otherwise, may be engaged in UPL even where the non-lawyer does not use a title (such as "attorney" or "lawyer") that would constitute holding-out.

The "holding out" element

"Holding out" is independently sufficient to constitute UPL in both jurisdictions. A non-lawyer who represents to the public that he or she is qualified to perform legal services — through business cards, signage, advertising, signature lines on correspondence, or oral representation — is engaged in UPL even if no substantive legal service has been performed. The holding-out prong is the easiest to prove and is a frequent enforcement target.

Firm-Side UPL Exposure: Rule 5.5 and Rule 5.3 Interplay

The firm-side concerns are governed by two rules of professional conduct operating in tandem with the UPL statute.

Rule 5.5(a) — Direct lawyer prohibition

Rule 5.5(a) provides that a lawyer shall not "practice law in a jurisdiction in violation of the regulation of the legal profession in that jurisdiction, or assist another in doing so." The "assist another" clause is the operative cross-reference: a lawyer who facilitates the unauthorized practice of law by a non-lawyer commits an independent Rule 5.5(a) violation regardless of whether the lawyer personally engaged in UPL.

Rule 5.3(c) — Individual responsibility

Where a non-lawyer in the lawyer's firm or under the lawyer's supervision engages in UPL, the lawyer's individual responsibility under Rule 5.3(c) attaches if the lawyer ordered the conduct, ratified the conduct with knowledge of it, or knew of the conduct in a supervisory capacity and failed to take remedial action. The UPL constitutes "conduct ... that would be a violation of the rules of professional conduct if engaged in by a lawyer" for purposes of subdivision (c) because Rule 5.5(a) makes UPL a Rule violation when performed by a lawyer.

Rule 5.3(a) — Systems responsibility

Firm-level managerial lawyers have an independent duty under subdivision (a) to maintain firm systems that prevent UPL. A firm that lacks staff training on the UPL boundary, lacks supervision protocols for non-lawyer client communications, and lacks review procedures for non-lawyer work product is exposed to subdivision-(a) liability even before any specific incident.

Common Non-Lawyer Staff Conduct That Crosses the UPL Line

Several recurring patterns of non-lawyer conduct constitute or trend toward UPL. Each pattern is independently actionable and is a recurring focus of state-bar UPL enforcement and Rule 5.3 disciplinary proceedings.

  • Direct client legal advice. A non-lawyer who responds to a client question by providing legal analysis, statutory interpretation, or guidance on how the client should proceed has crossed the line. Routing the question to the supervising lawyer and conveying the lawyer's answer is permissible; supplying the answer directly is UPL.
  • Independent strategy or settlement recommendations. A non-lawyer who recommends a litigation strategy, a settlement amount, or a negotiating position to a client without lawyer involvement engages in UPL. Conveying a lawyer-developed recommendation is permissible.
  • Drafting of legal documents for client signature without lawyer review. A non-lawyer who drafts a pleading, contract, or other legal instrument and provides it to the client for execution without lawyer review has engaged in document-preparation UPL. Drafting under lawyer supervision, with lawyer review before client delivery, is permissible.
  • Solicitation of legal-services engagements. A non-lawyer who solicits prospective clients to engage the firm for legal services, particularly with representations regarding the firm's expertise or the likelihood of a particular outcome, may engage in UPL through the holding-out prong and may simultaneously trigger Rule 7 (advertising and solicitation) violations.
  • Signature-block representation of legal authority. A non-lawyer who signs correspondence to the client, opposing counsel, or third parties in a manner that implies legal authority (signature block reading "On behalf of [Firm]" without paralegal-title clarification, signature characterizing the person as "Bankruptcy Specialist," and similar formulations) trends toward holding-out UPL.
  • Appearance at court hearings or proceedings. A non-lawyer who attends a court hearing or administrative proceeding and speaks on behalf of the client — even briefly — engages in UPL. Filing documents on the lawyer's behalf, transmitting written communications, and similar ministerial functions are permissible; substantive courtroom advocacy is not.
  • Representations at consumer-debtor meetings. A non-lawyer who represents a client at a § 341 meeting of creditors, an administrative interview, or a similar adjudicative or quasi-adjudicative proceeding engages in UPL. The relevant standard is whether the person is performing functions reserved to attorneys; speaking on behalf of the client in the proceeding is such a function.
  • Public marketing of legal expertise. A non-lawyer who participates in firm marketing (social-media posts, public-facing communications, advertising appearances) in a manner that holds the person out as qualified to provide legal services engages in holding-out UPL.

Firm-Side Remediation Obligations

When a Rule 5.3 supervisory or UPL incident is identified within a firm, the supervisory lawyers' obligations include both immediate remedial action and structural correction.

Immediate remediation

Subdivision (c)(2) makes the failure to take reasonable remedial action a basis for individual responsibility. "Reasonable remedial action" includes:

  • Stopping the offending conduct immediately;
  • Withdrawing or correcting any specific client communications that exceeded the non-lawyer's permissible scope;
  • Notifying the affected client of the staffing-and-scope issue and of any consequence that may have followed;
  • Documenting the incident and the remedial response;
  • Where the conduct may have caused legal harm to the client, evaluating whether KRPC / MRPC 1.4 (communication) requires disclosure to the client of the firm's own conduct.

Structural correction

Subdivision (a) requires firm systems to provide reasonable assurance of compliance. After an incident, the systems-review obligation typically includes:

  • Review of supervision protocols for the category of non-lawyer involved (paralegal, assistant, support staff);
  • Review of training materials on the UPL boundary;
  • Review of work-product review procedures (who reviews what, on what timeline);
  • Review of client-communication protocols (who may communicate with clients in writing, by phone, in person);
  • Review of the firm's marketing and public-communication practices to ensure non-lawyers are not represented as holding lawyer-equivalent qualifications.

Reporting obligations

Rule 8.3(a) requires a lawyer who knows that another lawyer has committed a violation of the Rules of Professional Conduct that raises a substantial question as to the other lawyer's honesty, trustworthiness, or fitness to practice law to inform the appropriate professional authority. Where the underlying incident involves a supervisory lawyer's known failure to supervise (subdivision (c)(2) violation) and the conduct rises to substantial-question level, Rule 8.3 reporting may be triggered.

The Rule 8.3 obligation is distinct from the Rule 5.3 obligation. A lawyer with knowledge of a colleague's Rule 5.3 violation may have both a Rule 5.3 remediation obligation (if the lawyer has supervisory authority over the offending non-lawyer) and a Rule 8.3 reporting obligation (if the colleague's failure to supervise rises to substantial-question level).

Enforcement Mechanics

State-bar disciplinary proceedings

Rule 5.3 violations are enforced through the state-bar disciplinary process. In Kansas, the process runs through the Office of the Disciplinary Administrator and the Kansas Supreme Court. In Missouri, the process runs through the Office of Chief Disciplinary Counsel and the Missouri Supreme Court. Sanctions range from informal admonition to disbarment, with the severity calibrated to the seriousness of the conduct, the lawyer's disciplinary history, and the harm caused.

UPL civil enforcement

State UPL statutes typically authorize civil injunction and civil penalty actions brought by the state attorney general, the state-bar UPL committee, or in some states by private parties. The remedies include injunctive relief, civil penalties, restitution to victims, and (in some jurisdictions) attorney fees.

UPL criminal enforcement

K.S.A. 7-104 establishes UPL in Kansas as a misdemeanor punishable by fine. RSMo § 484.020 establishes UPL in Missouri as a misdemeanor, with enhanced penalties for repeated or aggravated conduct. Criminal enforcement is uncommon but available and signals the public-policy seriousness with which both jurisdictions treat UPL.

Federal court UPL implications

In a federal court matter, UPL by a non-lawyer participating in the matter may trigger additional federal-court responses, including disqualification of any signed pleadings, fee disgorgement, and (in egregious cases) referral to state authorities. Federal courts apply the UPL standard of the state in which the court sits for purposes of evaluating whether conduct constitutes UPL.
