Document Production Methodology in Bankruptcy Practice

Bates-stamping practice, the native-format versus PDF production debate, ESI protocols under FRCP 26(f) imported through FRBP 7026, redaction practice under FRBP 9037, the organization-by-folder versus organization-by-date conventions, and chain-of-custody documentation.

The scope of document production in bankruptcy

Document production in bankruptcy practice arises in three principal contexts: (1) adversary proceedings, where the Federal Rules of Bankruptcy Procedure import the discovery provisions of the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure through the 7000-series rules; (2) contested matters, where FRBP 9014 makes the discovery framework selectively available; and (3) client-file production obligations following withdrawal or termination of counsel, governed by state-bar rules of professional conduct rather than by federal discovery rules. The same physical productions move through these channels with different procedural framings.

The starting authorities are FRBP 7026 (importing FRCP 26 into adversary proceedings), FRBP 7034 (importing FRCP 34's request-for-production framework), FRBP 9014 (selective application in contested matters), and FRBP 9037 (privacy-redaction requirements applicable to any document filed with the court).

Bates-stamping practice

Bates-stamping (also Bates-numbering) is the assignment of a unique sequential identifier to each page of a produced document set. The convention takes its name from the Bates Manufacturing Company's nineteenth-century hand stamp; the modern implementation is software-applied. A standard Bates number has three components: (1) a producing-party prefix (typically a two-to-four-letter abbreviation identifying the producer); (2) a separator (a hyphen or period); and (3) a zero-padded sequence number (typically seven digits, supporting up to 9,999,999 unique pages per series).

A typical Bates number: ABC-0001234. The page identified by this number is the 1,234th page in the production from the party whose prefix is ABC. The producing party maintains an internal Bates log that maps each Bates range to the underlying source document - the email message ID, the file path, the custodian, the original filename. The log is not produced with the document set but is retained for chain-of-custody and re-production purposes.

Bates numbers serve three operational functions. First, they let any party reference a specific page across all subsequent litigation events ("the loan agreement at ABC-0023411 through ABC-0023417"). Second, they prevent ambiguity when the same document is produced by multiple parties under different prefixes. Third, they create an audit trail that supports redaction-management workflows: a redaction log can identify exactly which pages were redacted and why.

Native-format versus PDF production

The native-format-versus-PDF production debate is the principal technical dispute in modern document production. Native format means the producing party hands over the file in its original format - .docx for Word documents, .xlsx for spreadsheets, .pst for Outlook mail stores, .eml for individual messages. PDF format means each file has been converted to a Portable Document Format static image, typically with text-recognition (OCR) applied where the source was scanned paper.

Each format has costs. Native files preserve metadata (author, modification history, embedded comments, hidden columns in spreadsheets) but are difficult to Bates-stamp and difficult to redact safely (redaction marks applied to a layer above the underlying data can be removed without destroying the underlying content). PDF files are easy to Bates-stamp and amenable to flattened redaction (the redacted text is permanently obliterated, not merely covered), but they lose the dynamic features and embedded metadata of the original.

Modern practice converges on a hybrid: image-format production with native files supplied for a subset of document types. The default production form is single-page or multi-page TIFF or PDF, with Bates stamps applied. Spreadsheets, audio recordings, video recordings, and certain CAD or design files are produced in native format because conversion to image destroys their usable content. Email messages are produced as PDF with the original message ID and routing headers preserved in the load file metadata.

Load files and metadata

A load file is a structured text file that accompanies a production and indexes the documents within it. The two principal load-file formats are the Concordance .DAT format (a pipe-delimited or tab-delimited text file) and the EDRM XML format (an XML-based schema developed by the Electronic Discovery Reference Model project). Each load file row corresponds to a single produced document and carries fields for the Bates range, the document type, the custodian, the date of the document, the date of the email message (where applicable), the subject line (for email), the participants (from / to / cc / bcc for email), and any other metadata fields specified by the production protocol.

The load file enables the receiving party to ingest the production into a review platform (Relativity, Everlaw, Reveal, DISCO) and to filter, search, and tag the documents. A production without a load file (a "flat" production of PDFs with no accompanying index) is technically lawful in many cases but is materially less useful and may trigger meet-and-confer disputes over adequacy.

ESI protocols under FRCP 26(f)

FRCP 26(f) requires parties to confer early in litigation about, among other things, "any issues about disclosure or discovery of electronically stored information." In bankruptcy adversary proceedings, FRBP 7026 imports this requirement. The product of the conference is typically a written ESI Protocol (Electronically Stored Information Protocol) that the parties exchange and sometimes submit to the court for entry as a scheduling-order provision.

A typical ESI Protocol addresses: (1) the universe of preserved data and the form of preservation; (2) the custodians whose data will be searched; (3) the date range of the search; (4) the search terms to be applied (often negotiated keyword lists with Boolean operators); (5) the format of production (image-plus-native hybrid is the modern default); (6) the metadata fields to be produced for each document type; (7) the deduplication methodology (custodian-level versus collection-wide); (8) the email-threading methodology (whether parent emails alone are produced where all child replies are duplicative); (9) the privilege-log format; (10) the redaction protocol; and (11) the procedures for clawback of inadvertently produced privileged material under Rule 502 of the Federal Rules of Evidence.

Redaction practice under FRBP 9037

FRBP 9037 imposes privacy-redaction requirements on documents filed with the bankruptcy court. The rule requires that the following information be redacted from filings: (1) Social Security numbers (only the last four digits may appear); (2) taxpayer-identification numbers; (3) dates of birth (only the year may appear); (4) names of minors (only the initials may appear); and (5) financial account numbers (only the last four digits may appear). The redaction obligation runs to the filer; the clerk does not redact retroactively.

Redaction technique matters as much as redaction substance. A redaction that obscures text visually but leaves the underlying text recoverable from the file metadata or layer structure is a failed redaction. The recommended technique is to flatten the PDF after applying redaction marks using a tool that destroys the underlying text content within the redacted region (Adobe Acrobat's "Apply Redactions" command, the open-source pdftk or qpdf with redaction plugins, or specialized e-discovery tools). A test of redaction adequacy is to OCR the redacted region; if any text is recoverable, the redaction is incomplete.

A failed redaction under FRBP 9037 can trigger sanctions, a motion to seal the filing pending re-redaction, and (where the unredacted information was sensitive) potential claims for damages by the persons identified. The rule applies to documents filed with the court; documents produced in discovery but not filed are not subject to FRBP 9037 unless the parties' ESI Protocol incorporates equivalent protections.

Organization conventions

Two principal conventions govern the organization of produced documents within a production set: organization by folder (documents grouped into directories that mirror the source filesystem or repository structure) and organization by date (documents sorted chronologically without regard to source location). A third convention, organization by custodian then date, combines elements of both.

FRCP 34(b)(2)(E) permits production in the order in which records were kept in the usual course of business or organized to correspond to the categories in the request. The first option (usual course of business) typically maps to folder-based organization; the second (correspondence to request categories) typically maps to date-based or topic-based organization. The producing party chooses; the receiving party may move to compel re-organization only if the chosen organization renders the production "not reasonably usable."

In practice, productions made through a load-file-supported workflow are often agnostic to the underlying folder organization because the review platform allows the receiving party to re-sort by any indexed metadata field. The organization-of-files-on-the-delivery-media question matters most for flat productions without load files, where the receiving party is dependent on the folder structure to find what is in the production.

Chain-of-custody documentation

Chain-of-custody documentation tracks the handling of a document from collection through production. A complete chain-of-custody record identifies, for each custody handoff: (1) the date and time of transfer; (2) the persons or entities transferring and receiving; (3) the medium of transfer (encrypted drive, secure file-transfer protocol, in-person delivery); (4) the integrity verification (MD5 or SHA-256 hash values compared at both ends); and (5) the recipient's acknowledgment of receipt.

Chain-of-custody documentation supports two distinct litigation purposes: (1) it satisfies the foundational requirements for admission of the documents at trial under Federal Rule of Evidence 901 (authentication), particularly when the documents include digital records whose integrity is contested; and (2) it permits the producing party to demonstrate that no alteration occurred between the source system and the production, defeating claims of spoliation under Rule 37(e). A production made without contemporaneous chain-of-custody documentation can usually be authenticated later by other means, but the friction is real and the cost of reconstruction substantial.

Privilege logs

Documents withheld from production on the basis of attorney-client privilege or attorney work-product doctrine are recorded in a privilege log produced concurrently with the responsive document set. FRCP 26(b)(5) (imported through FRBP 7026) requires that the privilege-log entry describe the document with sufficient particularity to enable the receiving party to assess the privilege claim without disclosing privileged content. A typical entry includes: (1) document date; (2) document type (email, memo, draft); (3) author; (4) recipients (including any cc or bcc); (5) general subject matter; (6) the privilege asserted; and (7) the Bates range that would have been assigned had the document been produced.

Document production methodology is the architecture of evidence. The format choices made at production govern what the receiving party can do with the material - what it can search, what it can authenticate, what it can use at trial. Methodology disputes are not technical disputes; they are disputes about the usable scope of the discovery record.
