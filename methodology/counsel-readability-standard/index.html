Counsel-Readability Standard

First published 2026-05-20/Version 0.1.0/CC-BY-4.0/status: draft

Abstract

The Counsel-Readability Standard requires that every artifact produced in a pro-se accountability arc (memory file, internal draft, public deposit, demand letter, pattern catalog, exhibit index) function simultaneously as a four-lens roadmap for new counsel. The four lenses are: (1) case-summary briefing for fast onboarding, (2) fee-economics signal showing where the money is, (3) derisk-of-engagement signal demonstrating that the work product is documentary-grade, (4) deliverables-remaining specification listing what counsel does first. The binding constraint in pro-se accountability work is not document quality; it is substitute-counsel onboarding cost. The standard treats every artifact as a recruitment instrument.

Definition and Mechanics

Each lens addresses a different question a substitute attorney asks within the first thirty minutes of reviewing a potential engagement.

Lens 1 (case-summary): Can I understand the procedural posture and the factual core in fifteen minutes? Artifacts must lead with a one-paragraph summary, a chronology, and pointers to the canonical identifiers. The reader should not need to assemble the case from scattered files.

Lens 2 (fee-economics): Is there enough money here to justify my time? Artifacts must surface the fee-economics signals: § 329(b) disgorgement size, malpractice contingency exposure, federal-criminal referral participation potential (qui tam analogues, civil forfeiture overlap), insurance carrier reservation-of-rights posture. A demand letter that documents $2,550 in challengeable fees plus a documented withdrawal-breach pattern reads as a tractable fee economy.

Lens 3 (derisk-of-engagement): Is this client going to embarrass me? Artifacts must demonstrate documentary discipline (no hyperbole, citations to record, clean exhibit chains, no demonstrably false claims). Channel-already-in-motion is the strongest derisk signal; it shows that institutional intake has already accepted the work.

Lens 4 (deliverables-remaining): What do I do first? Artifacts must include an itemized list of the next moves counsel would make (file Application to Withdraw response, file § 324 motion for trustee removal, file § 329(b) motion for disgorgement, draft criminal referral for federal channel). The list should be sortable by urgency and by independence.

Each artifact passes the standard if, read by a competent attorney with no prior context, the response is "yes, tractable, I see the path." Each artifact fails if the response is any of "I don't understand the posture," "I don't see the money," "this reads as a grudge," or "I don't know where to start."

Why Novel

Legal-tech literature on AI-assisted legal writing focuses on document quality (cite-checking, hallucination prevention, formatting). Pro-se litigation literature focuses on procedural correctness. Neither treats artifacts as recruitment instruments. The Counsel-Readability Standard makes the recruitment function explicit and operationalizes it through the four-lens test, which is applied before any artifact is treated as complete. The closest analogue is plaintiffs'-firm intake-package practice (the package a client brings to a contingency firm), but those packages are usually assembled by the firm itself; here the pro-se complainant assembles them in advance.

First Deployment

First codified 2026-05-20, in the author's internal working notes. The feedback file made explicit a pattern that had been emerging across the prior thirty days of drafts: artifacts that passed the four-lens test were the ones that produced traction with intake clerks, opposing counsel responses, and informal counsel feedback; artifacts that failed produced silence. Observed effect was that retroactive application of the standard improved a backlog of seven drafts before any of them were sent. Specific case identifier and named-party provenance are deposit-pending and become available with case outcome.

Example Use Case

Consider a pattern catalog documenting nine instances of opposing-counsel misconduct. Under prior drafting practice, the catalog was organized chronologically with citations at the end of each entry. Under the standard, the catalog is reorganized: a one-paragraph executive summary at the top (Lens 1), a fee-and-disgorgement column added to each row (Lens 2), an "evidence type" column added to show whether each row is record-citable or testimony-based (Lens 3), and an "action recommended" column for each row tied to a procedural vehicle (Lens 4). The reorganized catalog reads to incoming counsel as a case-in-a-box, not as a complaint.

Operational Notes

  • Apply the four-lens test before any artifact is treated as complete. The test is fast (under five minutes per artifact).
  • Lead every artifact with a one-paragraph executive summary; never make incoming counsel assemble the summary themselves.
  • Include a "next moves" section at the end of every long-form artifact, even if the next moves are obvious to the author. Onboarding counsel will not find them obvious.
  • Surface fee economics in concrete dollar figures wherever possible. Vague references to disgorgement potential do not satisfy Lens 2.
  • The standard applies retroactively. Existing artifacts that fail the test should be reworked before they are shown to incoming counsel.
  • Counsel-gated drafts (see UPL Guardrail) are held in reserve, but they must still pass the standard so that they read cleanly when handed over.
