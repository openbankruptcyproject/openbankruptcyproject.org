11 U.S.C. Section 329(b) Court Review of Attorney Fees Framework

First published 2026-05-22/Version 0.1.0/CC-BY-4.0/status: draft

Abstract

Section 329 of the Bankruptcy Code regulates compensation paid to or agreed to be paid to an attorney for services rendered in contemplation of or in connection with a bankruptcy case. Subsection (a) requires the attorney to file with the court a written statement disclosing the compensation paid or agreed and the source of that compensation; subsection (b) authorizes the court, on its own initiative or on motion, to cancel any such agreement or order return of any such payment "to the extent excessive."

The disclosure obligation under § 329(a) and the review power under § 329(b) are independent of the employment-and-compensation regime that governs estate-paid professionals under §§ 327 and 330. They reach any attorney compensated in connection with the case, regardless of whether the attorney has been employed by the estate. The reasonable-value standard, the sua sponte review authority, and the recovery procedure together create a court-supervised backstop on debtor-attorney compensation that operates throughout the case and survives confirmation in most chapters.

Statutory Text and Structure

Section 329(a) — Disclosure

11 U.S.C. § 329(a) provides:

"Any attorney representing a debtor in a case under this title, or in connection with such a case, whether or not such attorney applies for compensation under this title, shall file with the court a statement of the compensation paid or agreed to be paid, if such payment or agreement was made after one year before the date of the filing of the petition, for services rendered or to be rendered in contemplation of or in connection with the case by such attorney, and the source of such compensation."

The disclosure obligation reaches three categories of compensation: amounts already paid, amounts agreed to be paid, and the source of payment. The one-year look-back captures pre-petition compensation arrangements that the attorney would otherwise have no statutory obligation to disclose to the bankruptcy court. The phrase "whether or not such attorney applies for compensation under this title" makes clear that disclosure is required even when the attorney will not seek estate funds — the disclosure regime is broader than the employment regime.

Section 329(b) — Court Review

11 U.S.C. § 329(b) provides:

"If such compensation exceeds the reasonable value of any such services, the court may cancel any such agreement, or order the return of any such payment, to the extent excessive, to (1) the estate, if the property transferred — (A) would have been property of the estate; or (B) was to be paid by or on behalf of the debtor under a plan under chapter 11, 12, or 13 of this title; or (2) the entity that made such payment."

The review power has three operative features. First, the court may act "on its own motion" (the statutory text does not require a party-in-interest motion). Second, the standard is "reasonable value" — the court is not deferring to the attorney-client agreement. Third, the remedy is graduated: the court may cancel the agreement prospectively, order return of payments retrospectively, or both, to the extent the compensation exceeds reasonable value.

The recovery direction depends on the source of payment. Where the property would have been property of the estate or was to be paid under a Chapter 11, 12, or 13 plan, the recovery flows to the estate. Where the payment came from a non-estate source (typically a friend or family member who paid the retainer), the recovery flows to that third party. This bifurcation prevents the attorney from using a non-estate payment source to evade the reasonable-value backstop.

Connection to Federal Rule of Bankruptcy Procedure 2016

The procedural vehicle for § 329(a) disclosure is Federal Rule of Bankruptcy Procedure 2016(b), which requires the attorney to file a "Disclosure of Compensation of Attorney for Debtor" within 14 days after the order for relief, and a supplemental statement within 14 days of any payment or agreement not previously disclosed. The standard form is Official Form 2030 (formerly Form B 203).

Rule 2016(b)'s supplemental-statement requirement is the disclosure mechanism that runs throughout the case. Any payment received, any agreement modified, any fee arrangement entered into after the initial disclosure triggers a fresh disclosure obligation. The duty is continuing; it is not satisfied by the initial filing alone. The failure to file required supplemental disclosures is itself a basis for § 329(b) review and for denial or disgorgement of fees under separate authority.

The interplay is important. Rule 2016(b) supplies the disclosure mechanism; § 329(a) supplies the underlying statutory duty; § 329(b) supplies the court's review and recovery authority. A failure at the disclosure layer (Rule 2016(b) / § 329(a)) is independently actionable even if the underlying compensation is later determined to be reasonable, because the disclosure failure deprives the court and parties in interest of the information needed to evaluate reasonableness in real time.

The "Reasonable Value" Standard

Section 329(b) does not define "reasonable value." Courts have developed the standard through case-law application, drawing on the analogous "reasonable compensation" inquiry under § 330(a) and on general legal-fee jurisprudence.

The lodestar method

The dominant analytical framework is the lodestar method, derived from federal fee-shifting jurisprudence and applied in bankruptcy fee review since the early 1980s. The lodestar is the product of (i) the reasonable number of hours expended on the matter and (ii) a reasonable hourly rate. The Supreme Court adopted the lodestar approach for fee-shifting statutes in Hensley v. Eckerhart, 461 U.S. 424 (1983), and reinforced it in Perdue v. Kenny A. ex rel. Winn, 559 U.S. 542 (2010).

In the bankruptcy context, the Supreme Court endorsed the lodestar as the starting point for § 330 fee review in Mississippi Valley Electric Co. v. Brown and confirmed the analytical framework in subsequent appellate decisions. The lodestar applies equally to § 329(b) reasonable-value analysis, though § 329(b) is not formally restricted to the lodestar.

The Johnson factors

Courts supplement the lodestar with the twelve "Johnson factors" articulated in Johnson v. Georgia Highway Express, Inc., 488 F.2d 714 (5th Cir. 1974). The factors include the time and labor required, the novelty and difficulty of the questions, the skill requisite to perform the legal service properly, the preclusion of other employment, the customary fee, whether the fee is fixed or contingent, time limitations, the amount involved and results obtained, the experience of the attorney, the undesirability of the case, the nature and length of the professional relationship, and awards in similar cases.

The Johnson factors operate as a check on the lodestar calculation. A lodestar may be adjusted upward in cases of exceptional results or downward where billed hours are duplicative, excessive, or insufficiently documented.

Fee enhancement

Fee enhancement above the lodestar is rarely granted. Perdue v. Kenny A. established that enhancement is permitted only in "rare and exceptional" cases where the lodestar does not adequately account for the quality of representation. In bankruptcy practice, enhancement is uncommon; the lodestar dominates.

Market rate

The "reasonable hourly rate" component of the lodestar is tied to the prevailing market rate for similar legal services in the relevant geographic market. Courts examine local-market data, comparable attorney rates, and the attorney's own customary billing rate in non-bankruptcy matters as evidence of the appropriate rate. Where an attorney's bankruptcy rate substantially exceeds the local market, the court may reduce the rate to a market-conforming level.

Sua Sponte Review Authority

Section 329(b) explicitly authorizes the court to act on its own initiative. This sua sponte authority is a structural feature: the court is not dependent on a party in interest to surface a fee issue. The United States Trustee, while a frequent moving party, is not a prerequisite to court action.

The sua sponte authority extends through three discrete review opportunities:

  • Initial review. Upon the filing of the Rule 2016(b) disclosure, the court may scrutinize the disclosed compensation against the apparent complexity of the case. Many courts conduct a routine examination at this stage and request additional information where the disclosed amount appears facially excessive.
  • Fee-application review. Where the attorney has also been employed under § 327 and applies for compensation under § 330, the court reviews the application against both standards. The two analyses run in parallel; a fee may pass § 330 review (reasonable for services performed for the estate) but fail § 329(b) review (excessive in relation to the value provided to the debtor in connection with the case).
  • End-of-case or post-confirmation review. The court retains § 329(b) authority after confirmation in most chapters. A pattern of post-confirmation billing, undisclosed supplemental fee agreements, or fee arrangements that emerge during plan administration may trigger sua sponte review months or years after confirmation.

The court's sua sponte authority is reinforced by the statutory structure: the disclosure obligation under § 329(a) gives the court the information needed to exercise the review, and the absence of any party-motion prerequisite in § 329(b) makes clear that the court need not wait for an objection.

Recovery Procedure

The remedies available under § 329(b) are flexible. The court may cancel an unperformed agreement prospectively; order return of payments already received; reduce a fee application before payment; or combine these remedies as the facts require.

Cancellation

Where the attorney holds an agreement for compensation that has not yet been fully performed, the court may cancel the agreement to the extent excessive. The effect is to relieve the debtor (or third-party payor) of further payment obligations under the agreement. The attorney may still receive the reasonable value of services already rendered.

Return of payment

Where compensation has already been paid, the court may order return of the excessive portion. The return obligation runs to the estate where the payment came from estate funds or was to be paid under a Chapter 11, 12, or 13 plan, and to the third-party payor where the payment came from a non-estate source. The return is ordered as a disgorgement and is enforceable as a judgment of the bankruptcy court.

Procedural mechanics

The court typically issues an order to show cause directing the attorney to explain why the compensation should not be canceled or returned. The attorney is entitled to notice and an opportunity to be heard. The court conducts an evidentiary hearing where contested factual issues require it, and issues findings of fact and conclusions of law supporting the § 329(b) determination.

The attorney bears the burden of demonstrating that the compensation is reasonable. This burden-allocation is significant: the statute creates an inquiry the attorney must satisfy, not a presumption the court must overcome. Inadequate documentation of time spent, vague task descriptions, lack of supporting fee records, and similar evidentiary failures count against the attorney.

Interplay with Sections 327 and 330

Sections 327, 329, and 330 operate as a coordinated regime, but each addresses a distinct concern.

Section 327 governs the employment of professional persons by the trustee (or by the debtor in possession exercising trustee powers under § 1107(a)). It establishes who may be paid from the estate.

Section 329 governs disclosure and review of any attorney compensation paid in connection with the case, whether from estate or non-estate sources. It does not turn on employment under § 327; it reaches debtor's counsel even where counsel is paid privately.

Section 330 governs the compensation that may be awarded from the estate to professionals employed under § 327. It establishes the reasonableness standard for estate-paid compensation.

The same fee arrangement may be subject to all three sections simultaneously. Where debtor's counsel is employed under § 327 and seeks compensation under § 330, the § 329 disclosure obligation runs in parallel and the § 329(b) review power remains available. A fee that survives § 330 review may still be subject to § 329(b) reduction; the standards overlap substantially but are not identical, and the § 329(b) inquiry includes pre-petition compensation that § 330 does not reach.

In Chapter 13 practice, where debtor's counsel is typically not "employed" under § 327 (because the debtor in Chapter 13 is not exercising trustee powers in the same manner as a Chapter 11 DIP), § 329 is the primary fee-review mechanism. Many districts have adopted "no-look" or "presumptive-reasonable" fees in Chapter 13 cases, but the court retains § 329(b) authority to depart from the presumptive amount where the facts warrant.

Common Factual Postures That Trigger Section 329(b) Review

Courts routinely invoke § 329(b) review in response to recurring factual patterns:

  • Undisclosed pre-petition payments. A payment received within the one-year § 329(a) look-back period that does not appear on the Rule 2016(b) statement triggers disclosure-failure and reasonable-value scrutiny in parallel.
  • Undisclosed supplemental fee agreements. A fee agreement modified after the initial disclosure but not supplementally disclosed under Rule 2016(b) triggers the same dual scrutiny.
  • Block-billed or vague time entries. Time entries that describe services in generic terms ("work on case," "review file," "telephone conference") without specifying the substance of the work make reasonable-value review impossible and tend to result in fee reduction.
  • Rate increases without disclosure. An hourly-rate increase that is not disclosed in a supplemental Rule 2016(b) statement violates the disclosure obligation and tends to draw scrutiny on the underlying reasonableness as well.
  • Fee concentration on single dates or matters. A disproportionate billing on a single day, in a single task category, or for "pro forma" services without identifiable deliverables draws attention as a possible padding or overbilling pattern.
  • Post-confirmation billing without disclosure. Continued billing after plan confirmation, particularly for services that should have been completed before confirmation, triggers both disclosure and reasonableness review.
  • Fee arrangements involving third-party payors. Where compensation flows from a non-debtor source (typically a friend or family member), the source must be disclosed and the recovery direction under § 329(b) follows the source.

Standard of Review on Appeal

A bankruptcy court's § 329(b) determination is reviewed for abuse of discretion. The deferential standard reflects the trial court's superior position to evaluate the reasonableness of services in the context of the specific case. Findings of fact supporting the determination are reviewed for clear error; legal conclusions (including the choice of analytical framework) are reviewed de novo.

The Supreme Court has emphasized in fee-shifting jurisprudence that "a request for attorney's fees should not result in a second major litigation." Hensley v. Eckerhart, 461 U.S. at 437. Bankruptcy courts apply this principle in § 329(b) review, conducting fee analysis on a documentary record where possible and reserving evidentiary hearings for genuinely contested issues.
