11 U.S.C. Section 327(a) vs. Section 1107(a) — The DIP Attorney Distinction

First published 2026-05-22/Version 0.1.0/CC-BY-4.0/status: draft

Abstract

The employment of debtor's counsel in a Chapter 11 case is governed by the interplay between two sections of the Bankruptcy Code. Section 327(a) authorizes the trustee to employ professional persons; section 1107(a) confers on the debtor in possession the rights, powers, and duties of a trustee, subject to specified limitations. The combined effect is that the debtor in possession's selection and employment of counsel must satisfy the same statutory requirements that govern a trustee's selection and employment of counsel, modified by a single statutory accommodation that softens the disinterestedness inquiry as applied to pre-petition counsel.

The framework operates with full force in conventional Chapter 11 cases and, with minor structural variation, in Subchapter V cases under Chapter 11. The disinterestedness inquiry, the duty of disclosure of professional connections under Federal Rule of Bankruptcy Procedure 2014, and the requirement of court approval of employment all attach to debtor's counsel notwithstanding the absence of a separately-appointed trustee.

Statutory Architecture

Section 327(a)

Section 327(a) provides:

"Except as otherwise provided in this section, the trustee, with the court's approval, may employ one or more attorneys, accountants, appraisers, auctioneers, or other professional persons, that do not hold or represent an interest adverse to the estate, and that are disinterested persons, to represent or assist the trustee in carrying out the trustee's duties under this title."

The three operative elements are: (1) court approval; (2) no interest adverse to the estate; and (3) disinterestedness. Each element is an independent inquiry, and the absence of any one disqualifies the proposed employment.

Section 1107(a)

Section 1107(a) provides:

"Subject to any limitations on a trustee serving in a case under this chapter, and to such limitations or conditions as the court prescribes, a debtor in possession shall have all the rights, other than the right to compensation under section 330 of this title, and powers, and shall perform all the functions and duties, except the duties specified in sections 1106(a)(2), (3), and (4) of this title, of a trustee serving in a case under this chapter."

The DIP "shall have all the rights ... and powers" of a trustee. The DIP's employment of professional persons is one such power; the DIP exercises § 327(a) when employing counsel. The DIP is not entitled to compensation under § 330 (which is reserved for trustees as compensation for trustee services), but the professional employed by the DIP is compensated under § 330 in the same manner as a professional employed by a trustee.

Section 1107(b) — The Pre-Petition Counsel Accommodation

Section 1107(b) supplies the statutory accommodation that allows continued employment of pre-petition counsel:

"Notwithstanding section 327(a) of this title, a person is not disqualified for employment under section 327 of this title by a debtor in possession solely because of such person's employment by or representation of the debtor before the commencement of the case."

The accommodation is narrow. It addresses one specific potential disqualifier — the fact of pre-petition representation — and provides that this fact, standing alone, is not disqualifying. The other § 327(a) elements (no adverse interest, disinterestedness) and the disclosure obligations under Rule 2014 remain fully applicable.

Without § 1107(b), a strict reading of "disinterested person" (defined at § 101(14) to exclude any person who "is or was, within two years before the date of the filing of the petition, a director, officer, or employee of the debtor" or who "has an interest materially adverse to the interest of the estate" by reason of any direct or indirect relationship to the debtor) would disqualify essentially all pre-petition attorneys. Section 1107(b) prevents this outcome and preserves the substantive continuity that pre-petition counsel typically provides.

The Disinterestedness Requirement

Statutory definition

"Disinterested person" is defined at 11 U.S.C. § 101(14):

"The term 'disinterested person' means a person that — (A) is not a creditor, an equity security holder, or an insider; (B) is not and was not, within 2 years before the date of the filing of the petition, a director, officer, or employee of the debtor; and (C) does not have an interest materially adverse to the interest of the estate or of any class of creditors or equity security holders, by reason of any direct or indirect relationship to, connection with, or interest in, the debtor, or for any other reason."

The three prongs operate cumulatively. A person is disinterested only if all three prongs are satisfied.

Application to DIP counsel

As applied to DIP counsel, the disinterestedness inquiry typically focuses on prong (C) — whether the proposed counsel has a materially adverse interest. Several recurring questions arise:

  • Pre-petition fee balance. Counsel who is a creditor for unpaid pre-petition fees runs into prong (A) (creditor status) and may have a materially adverse interest under prong (C). The common practice is to waive the pre-petition fee balance, or to write down the balance below a threshold the court will accept, to remove the disqualification.
  • Representation of insiders. Where counsel has represented the debtor's principals or other insiders in unrelated matters, the representation may give rise to a materially-adverse-interest question if the insider's interests are likely to diverge from the estate's during the case (a common occurrence in single-asset or closely-held entity bankruptcies).
  • Pre-petition transactions. Where counsel structured pre-petition transactions that may be subject to avoidance action in the bankruptcy, the conflict between counsel's interest in defending those transactions and the estate's interest in maximizing avoidance recoveries is materially adverse.
  • Inter-creditor relationships. Where counsel represents or has represented major creditors of the debtor in other matters, the relationship must be disclosed and evaluated; the court determines whether the relationship rises to material adversity.

The "no actual conflict, no appearance of conflict" gloss

Some courts apply a stricter "no appearance of conflict" overlay on the disinterestedness analysis. The Supreme Court has not endorsed an appearance-of-conflict standard in the § 327 context, but several circuits apply a sliding-scale approach in which closer relationships warrant heightened scrutiny. The Second Circuit's approach in In re Project Orange Associates, LLC, 431 B.R. 363 (Bankr. S.D.N.Y. 2010), illustrates the strict end of the spectrum.

Rule 2014 Disclosure of Connections

Federal Rule of Bankruptcy Procedure 2014(a) governs the application for employment. The rule requires the application to "state the specific facts showing the necessity for the employment, the name of the person to be employed, the reasons for the selection, the professional services to be rendered, any proposed arrangement for compensation, and, to the best of the applicant's knowledge, all of the person's connections with the debtor, creditors, any other party in interest, their respective attorneys and accountants, the United States trustee, or any person employed in the office of the United States trustee."

The disclosure obligation is broad and is independent of the substantive disinterestedness inquiry. The applicant must disclose all connections; the court evaluates whether the disclosed connections amount to a disqualifying interest. The Rule 2014 obligation is continuing: the applicant must supplement the disclosure when previously-unknown connections come to light.

Failure to disclose Rule 2014 connections is a recurring basis for § 327 disqualification and § 330 fee denial, often with greater consequence than the underlying connection itself would have warranted. The Third Circuit's decision in In re BH&P, Inc., 949 F.2d 1300 (3d Cir. 1991), is the leading appellate authority on the consequences of inadequate Rule 2014 disclosure; the court characterized the disclosure obligation as "of the utmost importance" and treated even unintentional non-disclosure as a basis for sanction.

Subchapter V Application

Subchapter V of Chapter 11, enacted by the Small Business Reorganization Act of 2019 and codified at 11 U.S.C. §§ 1181-1195, modifies the standard Chapter 11 architecture for small-business debtors. The Sub V framework retains the § 327 / § 1107(a) interplay with two structural variations.

The Sub V trustee role

A Sub V trustee is appointed in every Sub V case under § 1183. The Sub V trustee's duties are limited and oversight-focused rather than operational. Section 1183(b) enumerates the Sub V trustee's duties, which include monitoring the debtor's operations, facilitating consensual plan confirmation, and appearing at major hearings. The Sub V trustee does not displace the DIP from operational control; § 1184 expressly provides that the debtor "shall remain in possession of all property of the estate" except in narrow circumstances of removal under § 1185.

The Sub V trustee employs the trustee's own counsel (or proceeds without separate counsel where the case is straightforward), separate from the DIP's counsel. The two professional groups operate in parallel: the DIP's counsel under § 1107(a) / § 327(a) represents the debtor and the estate operationally; the Sub V trustee's counsel (where engaged) represents the trustee in the trustee's oversight role.

DIP counsel employment in Sub V

The DIP's employment of counsel in Sub V proceeds under the same § 327(a) / § 1107(a) / § 1107(b) framework as conventional Chapter 11. The disinterestedness requirement, the no-adverse-interest requirement, and the Rule 2014 disclosure obligation all apply. The § 1107(b) pre-petition counsel accommodation is available.

The structural variation is the additional oversight perspective. The Sub V trustee may object to the DIP's employment application or may raise concerns about the disclosed connections in the trustee's monitoring role. The U.S. Trustee continues to play the principal review role, but the Sub V trustee supplies an additional check.

Conflicts Inherited from Pre-Petition Representation

The § 1107(b) accommodation does not erase pre-petition conflicts; it only prevents the fact of pre-petition representation from itself being disqualifying. Where the pre-petition representation generated specific conflicts that survive into the bankruptcy, those conflicts may still disqualify the firm.

Common patterns of pre-petition conflict that may carry forward:

  • Joint representation of debtor and insider. A firm that represented both the debtor entity and an insider in transactional or other matters may face a materially-adverse-interest question if avoidance actions or other estate claims against the insider become live.
  • Counseling on transactions subject to avoidance. A firm that structured pre-petition transfers that are now potential preferential or fraudulent transfers faces a divided-loyalty problem: the firm's professional interest in defending its work product diverges from the estate's interest in pursuing the avoidance claim.
  • Holding security interests in debtor property. Pre-petition arrangements under which the firm holds security for fees may create a creditor relationship that survives into the bankruptcy and triggers prong (A) of the disinterestedness analysis.
  • Concurrent representation of a creditor in other matters. A firm that represents a major creditor in unrelated matters may face a materially-adverse-interest question where the creditor's interests in the bankruptcy are likely to diverge from the estate's.

The firm's obligation under Rule 2014 is to disclose all such connections and let the court make the disqualification determination. A firm that cures the conflict (for example, by withdrawing from the non-debtor representation) may then be eligible for § 327 employment; a firm that withholds the disclosure entirely faces independent sanction for the disclosure failure.

Change of Counsel After Filing

A DIP may change counsel during the case. The mechanics depend on whether the original firm's employment is being terminated or whether new counsel is being added.

Adding co-counsel

A separate § 327 application is filed for the additional firm. The application must disclose all connections, address disinterestedness and adverse interest, and identify the services to be rendered. The court evaluates the addition against the same standards applied to the original employment. Coordination between original counsel and added counsel is typically described in the application to demonstrate non-duplicative scope.

Substituting counsel

A two-step process is typical: (i) an application for § 327 employment of the new firm; and (ii) a motion or stipulation modifying the original employment order to release the original firm. The original firm's fee-and-expense final application is typically filed contemporaneously, and the new firm's employment is approved with effective date coordinated to avoid a gap in § 327 coverage.

The original firm's compensation for services rendered through the date of substitution is determined under § 330. The new firm's compensation begins as of the effective date of its own employment order. Services rendered in the gap between the original firm's termination and the new firm's authorization are not compensable from the estate absent retroactive approval, which is granted only in extraordinary circumstances under the standards articulated in In re Indep. Eng'g Co., 197 F.3d 13 (1st Cir. 1999), and similar appellate authority.

Conflict-driven substitution

Where the substitution is driven by a conflict that has emerged during the case, the original firm typically files a motion to withdraw simultaneously with the new firm's employment application. The withdrawal motion describes the conflict in terms sufficient to demonstrate the necessity of withdrawal without breaching attorney-client privilege; the court grants the withdrawal upon the new firm's appearance and confirms the new firm's § 327 employment.

Parallels to Trustee Counsel Employment

The § 327(a) framework applies identically to counsel employed by a Chapter 7 trustee or a Chapter 11 trustee (where one is appointed under § 1104). The differences from DIP counsel employment are practical rather than statutory.

  • No § 1107(b) accommodation. A Chapter 7 trustee or appointed Chapter 11 trustee is not a debtor in possession; § 1107(b) does not apply. Pre-petition counsel who has represented the debtor in the period before filing cannot generally be employed by the trustee (though § 327(e) permits special-purpose employment in narrow circumstances).
  • Independent selection. The trustee selects counsel without the practical constraint of continuity with pre-petition representation. The selection criteria emphasize independence, experience with the trustee's role, and capacity to pursue avoidance actions and other estate claims without conflict.
  • Reduced conflict-inheritance risk. Because trustee counsel is selected after appointment of the trustee, the firm typically has no pre-petition exposure to the debtor or its insiders, and the disinterestedness inquiry is straightforward.

In Sub V, the Sub V trustee's counsel (where engaged) follows the trustee-counsel pattern: independent selection, no § 1107(b) accommodation, reduced inheritance risk. The DIP's counsel follows the DIP-counsel pattern.

Continuing Obligations of DIP Counsel

Employment under § 327 carries a continuing set of obligations independent of any single representation event:

  • Disclosure supplementation. The Rule 2014 disclosure must be supplemented whenever new connections come to light. The duty does not end with the initial application.
  • Disinterestedness monitoring. Material changes in the firm's relationships during the case (acquisition of debtor securities, retention by a creditor in unrelated matters, departure of attorneys to firms with conflicting representations) must be evaluated for § 327 implications.
  • Compensation under § 330. Compensation requires fee applications conforming to Rule 2016 and the § 330 reasonableness standards. The application must connect time entries to services performed for the estate (not for individual principals, related entities, or unrelated matters).
  • Fiduciary loyalty to the estate. The DIP's counsel owes fiduciary duties to the estate as the client. Where the interests of the debtor entity (and its principals) diverge from the estate's, counsel must privilege the estate's interests. This is the bedrock of debtor-counsel ethics in bankruptcy and is the substantive content of the disinterestedness requirement as it operates through the case.
