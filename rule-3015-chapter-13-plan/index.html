Federal Rule of Bankruptcy Procedure 3015

Rule 3015: Chapter 13 Plan Filing and Modification

Filing the Chapter 13 plan, Official Form 113 national plan and local-form alternatives, pre-confirmation and post-confirmation modification, and confirmation timing under 11 U.S.C. Section 1324.

What Rule 3015 does

Federal Rule of Bankruptcy Procedure 3015 governs the filing, service, modification, and confirmation procedure for plans under Chapter 12 (subdivisions (a) and (b)) and Chapter 13 (subdivisions (b) through (h)). For Chapter 13 cases, Rule 3015 specifies the filing deadline for the plan, the official form on which the plan must be presented, the procedure for pre-confirmation modification, the procedure for post-confirmation modification under 11 U.S.C. Section 1329, and the service requirements that ensure creditors receive the plan with sufficient time to evaluate and respond.

Filing deadline - Rule 3015(b)

Rule 3015(b) provides:

The debtor may file a chapter 13 plan with the petition. If a plan is not filed with the petition, it shall be filed within 14 days thereafter, and such time may not be further extended except for cause shown and on notice as the court may direct. Fed. R. Bankr. P. 3015(b).

The 14-day deadline is procedural; failure to file the plan within the deadline is grounds for dismissal under 11 U.S.C. Section 1307(c)(1) for unreasonable delay prejudicial to creditors. Many districts have implemented automatic dismissal procedures that mirror the Rule 1007 schedules-default procedure: the clerk issues a deficiency notice when the plan is not timely filed and the case is dismissed if the deficiency is not cured within a stated period.

Official Form 113 - the national Chapter 13 plan

Effective December 1, 2017, the Judicial Conference adopted Official Form 113 as a national, uniform Chapter 13 plan form. Form 113 is a structured plan that addresses the principal categories of plan treatment in a standardized format:

  • Part 1 - Notice to interested parties (the "warning section" identifying nonstandard provisions and limit-on-creditor-objection deadlines)
  • Part 2 - Plan payments and length, including monthly payment amount, payment source (employer payroll deduction order or direct payment), and plan length (36-60 months depending on above-median or below-median status)
  • Part 3 - Treatment of secured claims (cure-and-maintain on home mortgages, lien strip on wholly underwater junior mortgages, valuation and modification of non-home secured claims under Section 506(a), and treatment of 910-day auto claims)
  • Part 4 - Treatment of priority claims (domestic-support obligations, tax claims, and other Section 507 priorities)
  • Part 5 - Treatment of nonpriority unsecured claims and the proposed dividend
  • Part 6 - Executory contracts and unexpired leases (assumption or rejection)
  • Part 7 - Vesting of estate property (at confirmation or at discharge)
  • Part 8 - Nonstandard plan provisions (must be itemized in Part 1 and set forth in Part 8 to be effective)

Local-form variation under Rule 3015.1

Rule 3015.1, also effective December 1, 2017, permits districts to opt out of Official Form 113 and adopt a local form upon certification by the district's judges that the local form complies with the rule's substantive requirements. Rule 3015.1 requires that any local form:

  • Have its title and content in substantial conformity with Official Form 113
  • Adequately address the same matters as Official Form 113
  • Include a section for nonstandard plan provisions that mirrors Form 113 Part 8
  • Be approved by the district's judges and adopted by local rule following the public-comment procedure required for local rules

Approximately half the federal judicial districts use Official Form 113; the remaining districts use locally adopted plans certified under Rule 3015.1. The Administrative Office of the U.S. Courts publishes a current list of districts using local forms. A practitioner appearing in an unfamiliar district must determine at the outset which plan form applies.

Service and notice of the plan

Rule 3015(d) requires the debtor or the proponent of a plan to give service to creditors and other parties in interest. The plan must be served on the trustee and all creditors not later than the date the plan is filed. The notice of confirmation hearing required by Section 1324 (sent by the clerk or the proponent depending on local practice) must include the proposed plan or a summary sufficient to apprise creditors of the proposed treatment.

Confirmation hearing under Section 1324

11 U.S.C. Section 1324(b) provides that the confirmation hearing "may be held not earlier than 20 days and not later than 45 days after the date of the meeting of creditors under section 341(a), unless the court determines that it would be in the best interests of the creditors and the estate to hold such hearing at an earlier date." The statutory timing window converts the Chapter 13 case into a comparatively fast-moving proceeding: in most districts, the confirmation hearing follows the 341 meeting by 30-45 days, and the trustee's confirmation recommendation is filed shortly after the meeting.

At the confirmation hearing, the court determines whether the plan satisfies the confirmation requirements of Section 1325(a), which include:

  • The plan complies with the provisions of Chapter 13 and other applicable provisions of the Code
  • The required fees have been paid
  • The plan is proposed in good faith
  • The plan satisfies the "best-interests-of-creditors" test (unsecured creditors receive at least what they would receive in a hypothetical Chapter 7)
  • The plan is feasible (the debtor can make the payments and comply with the plan)
  • Secured claims are treated as provided in Section 1325(a)(5) (surrender, cure-and-maintain, or pay in full with interest under the Till v. SCS Credit Corp. rate methodology)
  • The debtor has filed all required tax returns under Section 1308

Pre-confirmation modification - Rule 3015(c)

Before confirmation, the debtor may modify the plan as a matter of right under 11 U.S.C. Section 1323. Rule 3015(c) requires that a modified plan be filed and served on the trustee and any other entity affected by the modification. The court will not confirm the original plan if a modified plan has been filed; the modified plan supersedes the original.

Pre-confirmation modification is common: the debtor may modify in response to trustee objections, creditor objections, valuation determinations, or amended proofs of claim that materially change the plan's feasibility or distribution structure. The modified plan typically follows the same Official Form 113 (or local-form equivalent) and goes through a renewed service process.

Post-confirmation modification - Rule 3015(g) and (h)

After confirmation, the plan may be modified under 11 U.S.C. Section 1329 to (1) increase or decrease the amount of payments on claims of a particular class, (2) extend or reduce the time for payments, (3) alter the amount of distribution to a creditor whose claim is provided for by the plan, or (4) reduce the amount of payments required by the plan to the extent of payments made by the debtor's employer.

Rule 3015(g) specifies the procedure for the debtor's, trustee's, or unsecured-claim-holder's motion to modify the plan after confirmation. The motion must be filed, served on the trustee and any other entity affected, and set for hearing on 21 days' notice. Rule 3015(h) provides a streamlined procedure when the modification is unopposed and the proposed modification has no material adverse effect on the debtor or creditors.

Post-confirmation modification is the principal vehicle for accommodating changed circumstances during the plan term: job loss, medical emergency, vehicle loss, divorce, or any other event that changes the debtor's ability to pay or the case's feasibility. The Supreme Court's decision in Hamilton v. Lanning, 560 U.S. 505 (2010), addressing the projected-disposable-income test for plan confirmation, also informs the analysis of post-confirmation modification when the debtor's actual income diverges from the projected income calculated at confirmation.

Common procedural posture

Mortgage modification mid-plan

The debtor obtains a loan modification on the home mortgage after confirmation. The debtor files a Rule 3015(g) post-confirmation modification motion to conform the plan to the modified mortgage payment and to discontinue conduit payments (if the plan provided for them). The motion is served on the trustee and the mortgage servicer with 21 days' notice and is granted on negative notice if no objection is filed.

Plan defeasibility for income drop

The debtor loses employment in plan month 28 and finds new employment at lower pay. The debtor files a Section 1329 motion to reduce the monthly plan payment. The trustee evaluates the reduced payment against the projected-disposable-income test and the best-interests-of-creditors test as of the modification effective date and either consents or contests. If contested, the court holds an evidentiary hearing on feasibility under the modified terms.

Nonstandard plan provisions must be in Part 8 to be effective. Official Form 113 Part 1 contains a notice that nonstandard provisions buried elsewhere in the plan are not effective. A plan that purports to alter standard treatment (e.g., a vesting provision different from the form's default, a non-cure default-cure structure, a special interest rate on a secured claim) must place the nonstandard provision in Part 8 and identify it in Part 1. The structural rule prevents creditor surprise from buried nonstandard terms.
