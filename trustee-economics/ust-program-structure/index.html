United States Trustee Program - Structure, Funding, and Oversight

Plain-English guide to the USTP’s status as a DOJ component, its 21 regional offices, panel and standing trustee appointments, funding through the US Trustee System Fund under 28 U.S.C. § 589a, and the statutory oversight functions under 28 U.S.C. § 586.

What Is the United States Trustee Program?

The United States Trustee Program (USTP) is a component of the U.S. Department of Justice charged with the federal supervision of the administration of bankruptcy cases under Chapters 7, 11, 12, 13, and 15 of the Bankruptcy Code. The Program is the executive-branch arm of bankruptcy administration. Bankruptcy judges decide cases; the USTP supervises the trustees, professionals, and debtors who execute those decisions and ensures that the system functions in accordance with the Bankruptcy Code and Federal Rules of Bankruptcy Procedure.

The USTP was created in pilot form by the Bankruptcy Reform Act of 1978 and expanded nationwide (with the exception of Alabama and North Carolina) by the Bankruptcy Judges, United States Trustees, and Family Farmer Bankruptcy Act of 1986. Alabama and North Carolina districts are instead served by Bankruptcy Administrators, who are appointed by the judiciary and perform parallel functions outside the executive branch.

Official citations: 28 U.S.C. §§ 581-589a; 11 U.S.C. § 307

DOJ Component Status

The USTP sits within the Department of Justice. It is headed by the Director of the Executive Office for United States Trustees (EOUST), appointed by the Attorney General. The Director supervises the regional United States Trustees and sets nationwide policy. The placement matters: as an executive-branch agency, the USTP can appear in bankruptcy cases and be heard on any issue under 11 U.S.C. § 307, but it does not adjudicate. Adjudication remains with the Article I bankruptcy courts and, on appeal, with the Article III district courts, bankruptcy appellate panels, and courts of appeals.

The structural distinction is important for understanding the USTP’s role. The USTP is a watchdog and gatekeeper. It objects, moves, examines, audits, refers, and reports. It does not decide. When the USTP and a debtor disagree, the bankruptcy court resolves the dispute on the merits.

21 Regions and Field Office Structure

The country (excluding Alabama and North Carolina) is divided into 21 USTP regions. Each region is headed by a United States Trustee, appointed by the Attorney General under 28 U.S.C. § 581 for a five-year term, with possible reappointment. Each region contains one or more field offices, and each field office is operated by Assistant United States Trustees who report to the regional U.S. Trustee. Field offices are staffed by trial attorneys, bankruptcy analysts, paralegals, and administrative personnel.

The regional structure tracks judicial districts rather than circuits. A single region may include multiple federal judicial districts; conversely, especially in large states, multiple regions may share state boundaries. Each judicial district’s bankruptcy cases are assigned to one specific regional U.S. Trustee’s jurisdiction.

Panel of Chapter 7 Trustees

Under 28 U.S.C. § 586(a)(1), the United States Trustee for each region "shall establish, maintain, and supervise a panel of private trustees that are eligible and available to serve as trustees in cases under chapter 7 of title 11." When a Chapter 7 case is filed, an interim trustee from the panel is appointed under 11 U.S.C. § 701, and absent election of a successor by creditors at the meeting of creditors, that interim trustee becomes the trustee for the case under Section 702.

Panel-trustee selection is administrative. The regional U.S. Trustee publishes application criteria and reviews applicants for qualifications including legal or accounting background, integrity, and operational capacity. Panel trustees are not federal employees; they are private individuals (typically attorneys, occasionally accountants) who accept appointments case-by-case. The USTP exercises ongoing supervision: it reviews Trustee’s Final Reports, audits no-asset reports, and can remove a panel trustee for cause.

Compensation of panel trustees follows the dual framework discussed at Section 326: a fixed amount (currently $60) out of the filing fee for each case under 28 U.S.C. § 330(b)(1), plus the sliding-scale percentage cap under 11 U.S.C. § 326(a) for asset cases.

Standing Chapter 12 and Chapter 13 Trustees

Under 28 U.S.C. § 586(b), the United States Trustee may appoint one or more individuals to serve as standing trustees for Chapter 12 and Chapter 13 cases in the region. Unlike panel trustees who serve case-by-case, a standing trustee operates an ongoing office that handles all (or a designated geographic share) of the Chapter 12 or Chapter 13 caseload in a district.

Standing trustees are quasi-public officials. They collect plan payments, disburse to creditors, monitor debtor compliance, and bring motions to dismiss or modify when warranted. The standing trustee’s office is funded by a percentage of plan payments (set by the U.S. Trustee under 28 U.S.C. § 586(e), capped at 10% for Chapter 13 and 10% or the actual maximum allowed in Chapter 12 for the relevant district), which covers staff, office overhead, and the trustee’s own compensation.

The standing trustee model achieves administrative economies of scale that the case-by-case panel model cannot: a single office handles thousands of repayment plans simultaneously, with standardized processes, payment portals, and creditor-disbursement infrastructure. The cost is borne by the debtor population through the percentage fee, which functions as a built-in administrative tax on plan payments.

Subchapter V Trustee Appointment

Subchapter V trustees, authorized by the Small Business Reorganization Act of 2019 and codified at 11 U.S.C. § 1183, are appointed by the United States Trustee under 28 U.S.C. § 586(a)(7). Each region maintains a roster of qualified individuals available for appointment. Unlike Chapter 7 panel trustees, Subchapter V trustees are not selected by automated rotation; the regional U.S. Trustee or Assistant U.S. Trustee designates a specific trustee for each Subchapter V case, exercising discretion based on case complexity, trustee availability, and any relevant subject-matter background.

Subchapter V trustees serve a facilitator role focused on consensual plan formation, monitoring debtor performance, and reporting to the court. They are compensated under Section 330, subject to court approval of fee applications, and are not subject to the Section 326(a) sliding-scale cap. The compensation framework reflects the trustee’s narrower role compared to a Chapter 11 trustee appointed under Section 1104.

Oversight Functions Under 28 U.S.C. § 586

Section 586(a) enumerates the United States Trustee’s statutory duties. These include:

  • Establishing, maintaining, and supervising the Chapter 7 panel;
  • Serving as and supervising standing Chapter 12 and Chapter 13 trustees;
  • Supervising the administration of cases and trustees in all chapters by monitoring applications for compensation and reimbursement, monitoring plans and disclosure statements, and monitoring creditors’ committees;
  • Taking action to ensure that all reports, schedules, and fees required to be filed under the Bankruptcy Code are properly and timely filed;
  • Monitoring the progress of cases and taking action to prevent undue delay;
  • Monitoring the appointment and performance of professionals employed by trustees and debtors-in-possession;
  • Notifying the U.S. Attorney of matters that relate to the occurrence of any action which may constitute a crime under the laws of the United States and assisting the U.S. Attorney in carrying out prosecutions; and
  • Establishing, maintaining, and supervising a list of Subchapter V trustees.

The criminal-referral function is enumerated by statute and is one of the USTP’s significant accountability tools. Section 586(a)(3)(F) operationalizes the U.S. Trustee’s parallel obligation under 18 U.S.C. § 3057, which requires any judge, receiver, or trustee to report apparent violations of bankruptcy criminal statutes to the appropriate U.S. Attorney.

Funding: The US Trustee System Fund (28 U.S.C. § 589a)

The USTP is funded almost entirely through user fees rather than through general DOJ appropriations. 28 U.S.C. § 589a establishes the United States Trustee System Fund in the U.S. Treasury, into which specified bankruptcy filing fees and Chapter 11 quarterly fees are deposited. The Fund supports the operation of the USTP, including salaries, office expenses, training, and information technology.

The principal revenue source historically has been the Chapter 11 quarterly fee assessed against debtors-in-possession under 28 U.S.C. § 1930(a)(6). Quarterly fees scale with the debtor’s disbursements during the quarter, producing a funding stream that grows with the size and activity of the Chapter 11 caseload. Smaller filing-fee allocations from Chapter 7, 12, and 13 cases supplement the Chapter 11 stream.

The user-fee funding model has policy consequences. Because Chapter 11 quarterly fees are the dominant revenue source, the USTP’s budget is sensitive to fluctuations in large-business bankruptcy filings. Congress has periodically adjusted the Chapter 11 quarterly fee schedule to balance USTP funding against debtor cost burdens, and the Supreme Court’s decision in Siegel v. Fitzgerald, 596 U.S. 464 (2022), addressed constitutional uniformity questions arising from differential fee treatment between USTP and Bankruptcy Administrator districts.

Bankruptcy Administrators in Alabama and North Carolina

Alabama and North Carolina remain outside the USTP. Bankruptcy cases in those states are administered by Bankruptcy Administrators appointed by and reporting to the judiciary rather than the executive branch. The Bankruptcy Administrator program is funded through judicial-branch appropriations rather than user fees. The functional duties largely parallel those of regional U.S. Trustees, and many policies (including the Chapter 7 panel structure and standing Chapter 13 trustee system) are mirrored.

The dual system has produced occasional constitutional and policy frictions, most prominently the fee-uniformity question addressed in Siegel v. Fitzgerald. Subsequent congressional action equalized the Chapter 11 quarterly fee schedule between USTP and Bankruptcy Administrator districts.

The Section 307 Standing Provision

11 U.S.C. § 307 provides that "[t]he United States trustee may raise and may appear and be heard on any issue in any case or proceeding under this title but may not file a plan pursuant to section 1121(c) of this title." This is the USTP’s litigation authority. The U.S. Trustee can object to plan confirmation, professional employment, fee applications, claim allowances, discharge, and virtually any other matter without needing to establish party-in-interest standing under any other provision. The carve-out for filing a Chapter 11 plan preserves the debtor’s and creditors’ primary role in plan formation.
