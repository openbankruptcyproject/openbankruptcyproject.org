11 U.S.C. Sections 327 and 328 - Employment of Estate Professionals

Plain-English guide to the gatekeeping provisions that govern who a bankruptcy estate can hire: disinterestedness, no adverse interest, the 327(c) creditor-representation safe harbor, the 327(e) limited-purpose exception, and 328 alternative compensation terms with the reasonableness reservation.

What Is Section 327?

Section 327 is the front door for any professional who wants to be paid by a bankruptcy estate. Before a trustee, debtor-in-possession, or official committee can hire an attorney, accountant, auctioneer, appraiser, or other professional and pay them from the estate, the engagement must be approved under Section 327. Without Section 327 approval, Section 330 compensation is unavailable: the work may be excellent, the rates reasonable, the result favorable, and the court still cannot order payment from estate funds.

The gatekeeping function exists because every dollar paid to a professional reduces the pool available for creditors. Congress structured Section 327 to ensure that the professionals taking those dollars have no conflicting loyalties that would skew their judgment away from the estate’s interests.

Official citations: 11 U.S.C. §§ 327, 328

The Two-Part Test of Section 327(a)

Section 327(a) authorizes a trustee, with the court’s approval, to "employ one or more attorneys, accountants, appraisers, auctioneers, or other professional persons" provided the professional:

  1. Does not hold or represent an interest adverse to the estate; and
  2. Is a disinterested person as defined in Section 101(14).

The two requirements operate independently. A professional who is "disinterested" within the technical definition can still hold an adverse interest, and vice versa. Both must be satisfied. The "adverse interest" inquiry is functional: does the professional hold any economic interest that would tend to lessen the value of the estate or create a predisposition under conditions of conflict?

"Disinterested person" is defined in Section 101(14). The core requirement is that the person is not a creditor, equity security holder, or insider; is not and was not within two years before the petition a director, officer, or employee of the debtor; and does not have an interest materially adverse to the estate by reason of any direct or indirect relationship to, connection with, or interest in the debtor.

Disclosure Obligations

Federal Rule of Bankruptcy Procedure 2014 requires the proposed professional to file a verified statement disclosing all of the proposed professional’s connections with the debtor, creditors, any other party in interest, their respective attorneys and accountants, the United States trustee, or any person employed in the office of the United States trustee.

The disclosure obligation is comprehensive and ongoing. A professional who later discovers an undisclosed connection must supplement promptly. Failure to disclose is independently sanctionable even if the undisclosed relationship would not have disqualified the professional from employment. Courts routinely deny or disgorge fees for incomplete disclosures, treating Rule 2014 as a strict information-forcing rule.

Section 327(c): The Creditor-Representation Exception

Section 327(c) carves out a limited exception to the disinterestedness requirement. A person is not disqualified for employment under Section 327 "solely because of such person’s employment by or representation of a creditor, unless there is objection by another creditor or the United States trustee, in which case the court shall disapprove such employment if there is an actual conflict of interest."

The provision recognizes that in commercial cases, the universe of qualified professionals is finite, and many of them represent some creditor somewhere. A blanket prohibition on creditor-side connections would shrink the talent pool dramatically. Section 327(c) accordingly treats creditor representation as presumptively allowable, but creates a fast-track objection process: any creditor or the U.S. Trustee can force the court to evaluate whether the representation creates an "actual" (not merely potential) conflict.

Section 327(e): Limited-Purpose Employment of Debtor’s Pre-Petition Counsel

Section 327(e) is a narrower exception that allows the trustee, with court approval, to employ an attorney "for a specified special purpose, other than to represent the trustee in conducting the case," if such employment is in the best interest of the estate, and "if such attorney does not represent or hold any interest adverse to the debtor or to the estate with respect to the matter on which such attorney is to be employed."

The provision is commonly used to continue employment of the debtor’s pre-petition counsel for a discrete, non-bankruptcy task (for example, defending an ongoing patent-infringement suit or completing a real-estate closing). The key distinctions from Section 327(a) are: (1) the employment is for a specified special purpose, not general representation of the estate; (2) the disinterestedness requirement is relaxed; and (3) the no-adverse-interest test is matter-specific rather than estate-wide.

Section 327(e) cannot be used to employ general bankruptcy counsel for the estate. The Fifth Circuit and other courts have emphasized that the "specified special purpose" language is a meaningful limitation; counsel who would be conducting the bankruptcy case itself cannot be employed under 327(e) and must qualify under 327(a) with full disinterestedness.

Section 328 Alternative Compensation Terms

Section 328(a) allows the trustee or debtor-in-possession, "with the court’s approval, [to] employ or authorize the employment of a professional person under section 327 or 1103 of this title, as the case may be, on any reasonable terms and conditions of employment, including on a retainer, on an hourly basis, on a fixed or percentage fee basis, or on a contingent fee basis." This is the textual hook for investment-banker success fees, contingent fee arrangements for litigation counsel, and similar non-lodestar compensation structures.

The critical feature of Section 328 is what comes next: the court may allow compensation "different from the compensation provided under such terms and conditions after the conclusion of such employment, if such terms and conditions prove to have been improvident in light of developments not capable of being anticipated at the time of the fixing of such terms and conditions." This reservation is narrow; it preserves the court’s ability to adjust only if circumstances were genuinely unforeseeable at the time of approval.

A retention application that invokes Section 328 must make the invocation explicit. The Fifth Circuit’s line of cases addressing this point holds that a professional employed under a vague order that does not clearly invoke Section 328 will be evaluated under Section 330’s ordinary reasonableness standard, even if the engagement letter purported to set fixed fees. Practitioners seeking Section 328’s deferential review should ensure the retention order recites Section 328 expressly and identifies the specific terms being approved.

Section 328(c): Denial of Compensation for Adverse Interest

Section 328(c) provides an enforcement teeth: "Except as provided in section 327(c), 327(e), or 1107(b) of this title, the court may deny allowance of compensation for services and reimbursement of expenses of a professional person employed under section 327 or 1103 of this title if, at any time during such professional person’s employment under section 327 or 1103 of this title, such professional person is not a disinterested person, or represents or holds an interest adverse to the interest of the estate with respect to the matter on which such professional person is employed."

The provision authorizes both retroactive denial and disgorgement when an adverse interest or loss of disinterestedness emerges during the engagement. The "at any time" language captures both initial defects and post-employment conflicts. Even a professional approved under Section 327 in good faith faces a fee-denial risk if a disqualifying interest later comes to light.

Nunc Pro Tunc Employment

Professionals who begin work before obtaining a Section 327 employment order historically sought nunc pro tunc (retroactive) approval. The Supreme Court’s 2020 decision in Roman Catholic Archdiocese of San Juan v. Acevedo Feliciano sharply curtailed nunc pro tunc orders generally, holding that federal courts lack equitable authority to alter the historical record. Bankruptcy courts now generally require strict, contemporaneous compliance with Section 327, although some districts have adopted procedures allowing approval of pre-application work upon a showing of extraordinary circumstances.
