11 U.S.C. Section 330 - Allowance of Compensation

Plain-English guide to the authorizing provision for trustee and professional compensation in bankruptcy: lodestar methodology, the Johnson factors, the benefit-to-the-estate requirement, and the Supreme Court’s fee-defense rule under Baker Botts v. ASARCO.

What Is Section 330?

Section 330 is the authorizing provision for compensation in bankruptcy cases. Where Section 326 sets the ceiling on trustee compensation, Section 330 sets the framework for awarding compensation in the first instance to trustees, examiners, Subchapter V trustees, and professionals employed under Section 327 or Section 1103. Every dollar paid from a bankruptcy estate to a trustee, attorney, accountant, financial advisor, investment banker, or other professional traces its authority to Section 330.

The statute mandates a "reasonable compensation for actual, necessary services" plus "reimbursement for actual, necessary expenses." Reasonableness is the workhorse standard, and the statute supplies a non-exhaustive list of factors a court must consider. Sections 330(a)(3) and 330(a)(4) supply both the affirmative criteria and the categories of work that are not compensable.

Official citation: 11 U.S.C. § 330

The Reasonableness Factors: Section 330(a)(3)

Section 330(a)(3) directs the bankruptcy court, in determining the amount of reasonable compensation, to consider the nature, extent, and value of the services, taking into account all relevant factors, including:

  • The time spent on such services;
  • The rates charged for such services;
  • Whether the services were necessary to the administration of, or beneficial at the time at which the service was rendered toward the completion of, a case under this title;
  • Whether the services were performed within a reasonable amount of time commensurate with the complexity, importance, and nature of the problem, issue, or task addressed;
  • With respect to a professional person, whether the person is board certified or otherwise has demonstrated skill and experience in the bankruptcy field; and
  • Whether the compensation is reasonable based on the customary compensation charged by comparably skilled practitioners in cases other than cases under this title.

The final factor (comparable-rate analysis) is the textual hook for importing market-rate evidence. A bankruptcy professional cannot ordinarily bill the estate at a premium over what comparably skilled non-bankruptcy practitioners charge for similar work in the same market.

The Lodestar Methodology

Although Section 330 does not by its terms require a lodestar, virtually every circuit applies lodestar analysis as the default starting point: reasonable hours multiplied by a reasonable hourly rate equals the presumptive fee. The lodestar may then be adjusted upward or downward based on case-specific factors.

Lodestar analysis requires contemporaneous, detailed time records. Block-billing (lumping multiple tasks into a single time entry) is disfavored and often reduced. Vague descriptions ("work on case," "review file") are routinely cut. Many districts have local rules or U.S. Trustee guidelines specifying minimum descriptive detail; the U.S. Trustee Program publishes Appendix A and Appendix B guidelines for fee applications in Chapter 11 cases of various sizes.

The Third Circuit’s decision in In re Busy Beaver Building Centers, Inc., 19 F.3d 833 (3d Cir. 1994), is a frequently-cited articulation of the bankruptcy court’s independent duty to review fee applications regardless of whether any party objects. The court emphasized that the bankruptcy judge sits in a quasi-fiduciary posture for the estate, must inspect fee applications for unreasonable entries on its own initiative, and cannot rubber-stamp an unopposed application. The Busy Beaver framework remains the leading articulation of judicial fee-review duty.

The Johnson Factors

The twelve factors articulated in Johnson v. Georgia Highway Express, Inc., 488 F.2d 714 (5th Cir. 1974), predate the current Bankruptcy Code but supply the canonical reasonableness vocabulary that bankruptcy courts continue to use. The Johnson factors are:

  1. The time and labor required;
  2. The novelty and difficulty of the questions;
  3. The skill requisite to perform the legal service properly;
  4. The preclusion of other employment due to acceptance of the case;
  5. The customary fee;
  6. Whether the fee is fixed or contingent;
  7. Time limitations imposed by the client or circumstances;
  8. The amount involved and the results obtained;
  9. The experience, reputation, and ability of the attorneys;
  10. The "undesirability" of the case;
  11. The nature and length of the professional relationship with the client; and
  12. Awards in similar cases.

The Supreme Court’s general fee-shifting jurisprudence (notably Hensley v. Eckerhart and Perdue v. Kenny A.) has constrained the Johnson factors’ role outside the bankruptcy context, generally folding them into the lodestar rather than treating them as independent multipliers. Bankruptcy courts use the Johnson vocabulary primarily to describe the reasonableness adjustments authorized by Section 330(a)(3), not as a separate test.

Benefit to the Estate: Section 330(a)(4)(A)

Section 330(a)(4)(A) excludes from compensation any services that were not "reasonably likely to benefit the debtor’s estate" or "necessary to the administration of the case." This is the textual anchor for the benefit-to-the-estate doctrine that bars fees for work that, judged at the time it was performed, could not reasonably have advanced the estate’s interests.

The doctrine is judged ex ante, not ex post. A professional who pursued a non-frivolous litigation strategy that ultimately failed is not automatically denied fees; the question is whether a reasonable professional would have undertaken the work at the time. Conversely, work that obviously could not benefit the estate (for example, briefing a moot issue, or duplicative work already done by another professional) is not compensable even if it succeeded in some derivative sense.

Section 330(a)(4)(A) also disallows "unnecessary duplication of services." When multiple professionals work on the same matter, the court will scrutinize whether each contribution added value beyond what the others provided. Conferences among multiple billing professionals are a common source of duplication reductions.

The Baker Botts v. ASARCO Fee-Defense Rule

One of the most consequential modern Supreme Court bankruptcy compensation decisions is Baker Botts L.L.P. v. ASARCO LLC, 576 U.S. 121 (2015). The question was whether time spent defending a fee application against objections is itself compensable from the estate under Section 330. The Court held it is not.

Justice Thomas, writing for the majority, applied the American Rule presumption that each party bears its own attorney’s fees absent specific and explicit statutory authorization. Section 330 authorizes compensation for "services rendered" to the estate. The Court reasoned that defending one’s own fee application is a service rendered to the professional, not to the estate, and the statute’s text does not displace the American Rule for this category of work.

The practical effect is that a professional whose fee application is challenged absorbs the cost of the defense. Engagement letters in large Chapter 11 cases sometimes attempt to contract around ASARCO by providing for indemnification, but Section 328 must be invoked at the employment stage with full disclosure for any such provision to be enforceable, and even then courts have been skeptical of fee-defense indemnification clauses post-ASARCO. The decision has materially altered fee-application risk allocation in contested cases.

Debtor’s Attorney Compensation: The Lamie Question

In Lamie v. United States Trustee, 540 U.S. 526 (2004), the Supreme Court held that Section 330(a)(1), as amended in 1994, does not authorize compensation from the estate for a debtor’s attorney in a Chapter 7 case who is not employed under Section 327. The Court applied a strict textual reading: the post-1994 statute lists trustees, examiners, and "professional person[s] employed under section 327 or 1103," and a Chapter 7 debtor’s attorney is not in that list.

The Lamie holding does not bar a Chapter 7 debtor’s attorney from being paid; it simply means the payment must come from the debtor, not from estate funds. In Chapter 13 cases, by contrast, debtor’s attorney fees are routinely paid through the plan because Section 330(a)(4)(B) expressly authorizes compensation to debtor’s counsel in individual Chapter 12 and Chapter 13 cases.
