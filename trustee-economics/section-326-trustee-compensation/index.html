11 U.S.C. Section 326 - Trustee Compensation Caps

Plain-English guide to the sliding-scale ceiling on bankruptcy trustee fees, the Pro Forma presumption for Chapter 7 trustees, and the disbursement-method jurisprudence that determines what counts toward the cap.

What Is Section 326?

Section 326 is the statutory ceiling on the compensation a bankruptcy trustee may receive for serving in that role. It does not authorize the compensation. The authorization comes from Section 330. Section 326 instead operates as an upper bound: regardless of what the trustee’s timesheets and lodestar calculation might otherwise support, the court may not award more than the sliding-scale percentages set out in subsection (a). For trustees serving in cases with significant disbursements, the cap rarely binds. For trustees in modest cases, the cap can be the operative number.

The cap exists because Congress chose to compensate trustees primarily out of money that flows through the estate to non-debtor parties. That structural choice creates a built-in alignment between trustee effort and creditor recovery: trustees are paid more when they marshal more money, less when there is little to marshal. It also creates the well-documented problem that no-asset Chapter 7 cases generate almost no compensation, which the statute addresses through a separate fixed-fee mechanism.

Official citation: 11 U.S.C. § 326

The Sliding-Scale Formula: Section 326(a)

In a case under Chapter 7 or Chapter 11 (other than a railroad reorganization), Section 326(a) caps trustee compensation at percentages of "all moneys disbursed or turned over in the case by the trustee to parties in interest, excluding the debtor, but including holders of secured claims." The current scale is:

Disbursement trancheMaximum percentage
First $5,00025%
$5,000.01 to $50,00010%
$50,000.01 to $1,000,0005%
Over $1,000,0003%

The percentages are marginal, not average: a trustee in a case with $200,000 of qualifying disbursements is capped at $1,250 (25% of first $5,000) + $4,500 (10% of next $45,000) + $7,500 (5% of next $150,000) = $13,250, not 5% of $200,000.

The carve-out for "the debtor" is doctrinally significant. Money returned to the debtor (for example, surplus in a rare solvent Chapter 7) is not part of the base. Money to "holders of secured claims" is explicitly included even though secured creditors are not unsecured-creditor distributees in the conventional sense. That inclusion was a Bankruptcy Reform Act amendment intended to recognize trustee work in liquidating collateral.

Disbursed or Turned Over: The In re Mohns Approach

The phrase "moneys disbursed or turned over" has produced substantial litigation about what counts toward the cap. Two competing approaches developed:

  • Source-of-funds approach. Only funds that physically passed through the trustee’s hands and were disbursed by the trustee count. A direct payoff of a secured lender by a third-party purchaser at closing, for example, would not count because the trustee never held those funds.
  • Disbursement-method approach. Funds count if the trustee’s administration was the operative mechanism by which a party in interest received payment, even if the funds did not literally flow through a trustee’s account.

The Seventh Circuit’s decision in In re Mohns, Inc., 55 F.3d 1268 (7th Cir. 1995), articulates the disbursement-method approach in the Chapter 11 context. The court held that a trustee’s commission base properly includes funds that the trustee caused to be paid to creditors at closing, even when the funds were wired directly from buyer to creditor and never sat in a trustee account. The court reasoned that the cap is meant to track the trustee’s administrative achievement, not the bookkeeping route taken by the dollars.

Other circuits have aligned with Mohns in result while articulating the test in slightly different terms. Practitioners should check the local circuit’s formulation. The practical effect under the disbursement-method approach is that trustees who structure sales producing direct payoffs at closing are not penalized in their compensation base.

Chapter 7 Trustees and the No-Asset Reality

Section 326(a) is the cap for Chapter 7 trustees handling asset cases. But the majority of Chapter 7 cases produce no nonexempt assets for distribution at all. In those cases, the cap is effectively zero. Congress therefore created a separate fixed compensation mechanism: under Section 330(b)(1), the Chapter 7 trustee receives a flat amount (currently $60) out of the filing fee for each case noticed, win or lose. This is the only compensation a no-asset trustee receives for that case.

The Pro Forma practice that has developed in many districts holds that when a trustee in an asset case files a Trustee’s Final Report seeking compensation at the statutory maximum, the bankruptcy court will ordinarily approve the request without an evidentiary hearing on the trustee’s hours, treating the cap as a presumptively reasonable fee. This Pro Forma presumption is judicially constructed, not statutory. It reflects the recognition that Chapter 7 trustees subsidize no-asset work with asset-case compensation, and that micro-managing hours in asset cases would defeat the cross-subsidy.

The Supreme Court’s decision in Lamie v. United States Trustee, 540 U.S. 526 (2004), addressed a separate but related compensation question (debtor’s attorney compensation in converted cases) and reinforced the principle that Section 330’s text controls, the bankruptcy compensation structure is heavily textual, and courts should not import equitable adjustments not grounded in the statute. Districts that have considered whether the Pro Forma presumption survives Lamie have generally concluded that it does, because the cap itself is a textual ceiling and applying it as a presumptive award is consistent with the statute.

Chapter 11 Trustee Compensation

A Chapter 11 trustee (appointed under Section 1104) is compensated under the same Section 326(a) sliding scale as a Chapter 7 trustee. Because Chapter 11 estates often involve very large disbursements, the cap rarely operates as a binding constraint in those cases. The operative number is usually the lodestar reasonableness analysis under Section 330.

The Chapter 11 trustee’s professional fees (counsel, financial advisors, accountants) are separately authorized under Section 330 and are not part of the trustee’s own cap. A debtor or creditor objecting to a Chapter 11 trustee’s fee application typically attacks the underlying hourly rates or the reasonableness of specific time entries under Section 330; the Section 326 cap is the outer bound that the parties rarely reach.

Chapter 12 Trustee Compensation: Section 326(b)

For a standing Chapter 12 trustee, Section 326(b) does not impose a percentage cap of its own. Instead, the standing trustee’s compensation is governed by the percentage fee set by the Attorney General under 28 U.S.C. § 586(e), capped at the higher of 5% or the amount specified for the relevant judicial district. The same framework controls standing Chapter 13 trustees.

The 28 U.S.C. § 586(e) framework treats standing trustees as quasi-public officials whose compensation is set administratively rather than case-by-case. Each standing trustee operates an office, hires staff, and bears overhead; the percentage fee on plan payments funds the operation. This is materially different from the case-by-case Section 326(a) cap that controls Chapter 7 and Chapter 11 trustees.

Chapter 13 Trustee Compensation

Standing Chapter 13 trustees collect a percentage of every plan payment received from the debtor. The percentage is set by the United States Trustee Program (or the Bankruptcy Administrator in Alabama and North Carolina) for the relevant district, subject to the 10% statutory ceiling in 28 U.S.C. § 586(e)(1)(B). The percentage funds the standing trustee’s entire operation, including disbursement to creditors, staff, and overhead.

Because the Chapter 13 standing trustee operates an ongoing office with predictable case flow, the percentage is calibrated annually based on projected receipts and approved expenses. The U.S. Trustee’s Office reviews each standing trustee’s annual budget and adjusts the percentage to fit. A debtor whose plan payment is $500 per month does not pay a separately-calculated trustee fee; the trustee’s percentage is built into the payment and disbursed to creditors after deduction.

Subchapter V Trustees

A Subchapter V trustee appointed under Section 1183 is compensated under Section 330 (subject to court approval) and is not subject to the Section 326(a) sliding-scale cap. The Subchapter V trustee’s role is intentionally narrower than a Chapter 11 trustee’s, focused on facilitating consensual plan formation, and the compensation framework reflects that narrower role. Subchapter V trustees typically bill at an hourly rate and submit fee applications for court approval.
