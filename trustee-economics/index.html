How the System Pays Itself

Trustee Economics

Trustees, estate professionals, and the United States Trustee Program are paid through interlocking statutory mechanisms that determine who can be retained, how compensation is calculated, and how the system is funded. These four deep-dives cover the trustee-compensation framework of section 326, the professional-fee framework of section 330, the section 327/328 employment retention regime, and the structural and budgetary architecture of the US Trustee Program.

About the compensation framework

The Bankruptcy Code carefully separates the right to be employed (sections 327 and 1103), the right to be compensated (section 330), the cap on trustee compensation (section 326), and the priority of those payments (sections 503 and 507). Each section has its own standard, its own procedure, and its own body of case law. A professional employed in violation of section 327 forfeits the right to compensation under section 330; a fee award that exceeds the section 326 cap is reversible; and an administrative claim must be allowed under section 503 before it can be paid under the section 507(a)(2) priority.

Section 330 codifies the lodestar method for fee awards: the reasonable hourly rate multiplied by the reasonable number of hours. The court must consider the section 330(a)(3) factors including the time spent, the rates charged, the necessity of the services, the results achieved, and whether the services were beneficial to the estate. The court has discretion to reduce fees that fail any of those tests. Detailed time records are required by Local Rules in most districts.

The US Trustee Program, established in the Bankruptcy Reform Act of 1978 and made permanent in 1986, is an executive-branch component of the Department of Justice charged with administrative oversight of the bankruptcy system. The Program is funded through quarterly fees paid by chapter 11 debtors under 28 U.S.C. section 1930(a)(6) - a fee schedule that scales with debtor disbursements and that has been the subject of significant constitutional litigation over the past decade.

Disclosure rule: Bankruptcy Rule 2014 requires every professional applying for employment under section 327 to file a verified statement disclosing all connections with the debtor, creditors, and other parties in interest. Incomplete disclosure can result in fee denial, disgorgement, or sanctions even years after the original retention.
