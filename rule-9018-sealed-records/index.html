Rule 9018: Sealed Records in Federal Bankruptcy Cases

The statutory anchor, the categories of sealable matter, and the access doctrines that limit when a court can close the file.

Sealing in bankruptcy is exceptional, not routine.

Federal bankruptcy filings are presumptively public. Rule 9018 and its statutory anchor at 11 U.S.C. Section 107 define narrow categories in which a court may seal, protect, or restrict access. Outside those categories the common-law right of access recognized in Nixon v. Warner Communications, Inc., 435 U.S. 589 (1978), and the First Amendment access doctrine, control.

The statutory and rule framework

Federal Rule of Bankruptcy Procedure 9018 is the procedural vehicle for sealing or protecting matter filed in a bankruptcy case. The rule itself does not define what may be sealed. It implements 11 U.S.C. Section 107, which establishes that papers filed in a case under the Bankruptcy Code and the dockets of a bankruptcy court are public records and open to examination by an entity at reasonable times without charge.

Section 107(b) and Section 107(c) carve out the exceptions. Subsection (b) authorizes the court to issue an order to protect an entity with respect to (1) a trade secret or confidential research, development, or commercial information, or (2) scandalous or defamatory matter contained in any paper filed in a case under the Code. Subsection (c) addresses personal-identification information that would create undue risk of identity theft or other unlawful injury if disclosed.

Rule 9018 supplies the procedure: on motion, or on its own initiative, with or without notice, the court may make any order permitted by Section 107.

The sealable categories under Section 107(b) and (c)

  1. Trade secret or confidential research, development, or commercial information. The category covers business information whose disclosure would cause competitive harm. Courts apply a definitional gate. Generic embarrassment about financial difficulty does not qualify. The Second Circuit's analysis in In re Orion Pictures Corp., 21 F.3d 24 (2d Cir. 1994), is the leading authority and reads Section 107(b)(1) as a directive: if the matter fits the category, the court is required to protect, without the discretionary balancing that governs sealing under the common law.
  2. Scandalous or defamatory matter. The category protects parties and non-parties from gratuitous reputational injury embedded in court filings. Courts distinguish material that is merely embarrassing or unfavorable from material whose only function is to defame. The leading framework derives from In re Gitto Global Corp., 422 F.3d 1 (1st Cir. 2005), which treats matter as scandalous if it would cause a reasonable person to alter their opinion of the affected party based on potentially untrue information.
  3. Personal-identification information creating undue risk of identity theft or other unlawful injury. Section 107(c) is the separate sealing pathway for identifiers that go beyond the redaction floor of Federal Rule of Bankruptcy Procedure 9037(a). The undue-risk standard requires a showing that goes beyond the routine privacy interest already covered by mandatory redaction.
  4. Means of access to the names of governmental matters. Section 107(b)(1) extends protection to confidential commercial information held by governmental units in narrow circumstances. The protection is rare in consumer cases and arises principally in complex Chapter 11 matters involving governmental contracts or regulatory information.

What sealing does and does not cover

Covers

Specific documents, specific exhibits, specific lines or paragraphs within a document. Courts often order sealing of an exhibit attached to a public motion while the motion itself remains on the docket with the exhibit replaced by a placeholder.

Does not cover

The fact of the filing, the existence of the case, the identity of the parties, the docket itself, or the general subject matter of a motion. Section 107(a) protects all of these as presumptively public.

The common-law right of access - Nixon v. Warner Communications

The Supreme Court in Nixon v. Warner Communications, Inc., 435 U.S. 589 (1978), recognized a federal common-law right of access to judicial records. The right is not absolute, and the Court declined to specify a single test, but it framed the inquiry as a sound-discretion balancing in which the trial court must weigh the relevant facts and circumstances of the particular case.

The circuits have since elaborated the framework. In bankruptcy, the common-law right operates alongside Section 107(a)'s express public-record declaration, which several courts read as a stronger statutory statement than the general common-law presumption. The practical consequence is that movants seeking to seal bear a heavy burden, and the court's reasoned analysis must appear on the record. See, for example, Father M v. Various Tort Claimants (In re Roman Catholic Archbishop of Portland in Or.), 661 F.3d 417 (9th Cir. 2011) (collecting authorities and holding that Section 107 is a clear statutory exception to the general presumption).

The First Amendment overlay - Globe Newspaper and the experience-and-logic test

Where the common-law right is grounded in the historical practice of public access to court records, the First Amendment right of access derives from Richmond Newspapers, Inc. v. Virginia, 448 U.S. 555 (1980), and was elaborated for press access in Globe Newspaper Co. v. Superior Court, 457 U.S. 596 (1982). The Court's analytical framework, often called the experience-and-logic test, asks (1) whether the place and process have historically been open to the press and the general public, and (2) whether public access plays a significant positive role in the functioning of the particular process in question.

Most circuits hold that the First Amendment access right applies to civil judicial proceedings, including bankruptcy, with respect to documents that play a meaningful role in the adjudicative process. Where the right applies, sealing requires a higher showing: the order must be supported by specific, on-the-record findings demonstrating that closure is essential to preserve higher values and is narrowly tailored to serve that interest.

Standard for issuing a Rule 9018 order

The court considering a motion to seal under Rule 9018 generally proceeds through a layered inquiry. First, does the matter fall within one of the Section 107(b) or 107(c) categories. Second, even within a covered category, is the proposed sealing the least restrictive means of protecting the interest. Third, has the movant carried the burden with specific evidence rather than conclusory assertions.

If the matter falls within Section 107(b), the Orion Pictures line of authority treats the protection as required rather than discretionary. The court still must verify the categorical fit. Conclusory assertions that information is a trade secret will not suffice; the movant typically must describe what the information is, why it qualifies as a trade secret or confidential commercial information, and what specific competitive harm would result from disclosure.

Procedure

  1. Motion or sua sponte. Rule 9018 permits the court to act on its own initiative, but in practice sealing arises by motion of a party or affected non-party.
  2. Notice. The rule allows the court to proceed with or without notice. In practice, motions affecting public access are routinely served on the United States Trustee and other parties in interest. Press intervention to challenge sealing is procedurally available and not uncommon in high-profile cases.
  3. Provisional sealing. Many courts permit provisional sealing while the motion is pending so that the matter does not enter the public record before the court rules.
  4. On-the-record findings. Where the First Amendment access right applies, the court must articulate specific findings supporting closure. Boilerplate is not sufficient.
  5. Narrow tailoring. Even when sealing is appropriate, the order should reach only the matter that satisfies the statutory category. Redaction of specific lines is preferred over wholesale sealing of an exhibit where the affected portion is severable.

Sealing is not a remedy for inadvertent Rule 9037(a) violations. When a personal identifier has been publicly filed in violation of the mandatory redaction floor, the appropriate remedy is a motion to redact under Rule 9037 paired with clerk notification and, where warranted, an auxiliary Section 107(c) order for the underlying record. Sealing an entire filing to suppress an identifier already on PACER does not cure prior disclosure and may obscure the procedural record needed for downstream remediation.

Press and public intervention

News organizations and members of the public have standing to challenge sealing orders. The procedural posture is typically a motion to intervene for the limited purpose of unsealing, followed by a motion to unseal that frames the challenge under both the common-law and First Amendment access doctrines. The intervenor need not be a party in interest in the bankruptcy case itself.

Courts evaluating intervention have generally favored access where the sealed material has been considered by the court in connection with a substantive ruling. The rationale is that documents that informed an adjudication should be available to the public so the basis for the ruling can be understood.
