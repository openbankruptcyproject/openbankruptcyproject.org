28 U.S.C. Section 1452 - Removal and Remand of Bankruptcy-Related Claims

Plain-English guide to bankruptcy removal: who may remove, where the claim goes, the discretionary equitable-remand standard, the non-appealability of remand orders, and the practical differences between bankruptcy removal under Section 1452 and ordinary removal under Section 1441.

What Is Section 1452?

Section 1452 is the bankruptcy-specific removal statute. It allows a party to a state-court action to remove the action to the federal district court for the district where the state action is pending, provided the action is one over which the district court has jurisdiction under 28 U.S.C. § 1334 - meaning it arises under, arises in, or is related to a Title 11 case. Removed actions become part of the federal docket and are typically referred to the bankruptcy court under the district's standing order of reference.

Section 1452 operates separately from the general removal statute, 28 U.S.C. § 1441. The two statutes differ in several important respects: who may remove, which claims are removable, where the case goes, and how remand operates. Understanding the differences is critical because a defective removal under one statute is not automatically curable under the other.

Official citation: 28 U.S.C. § 1452

Section 1452(a) - Removal Authority

Section 1452(a) provides: "A party may remove any claim or cause of action in a civil action other than a proceeding before the United States Tax Court and a civil action by a governmental unit to enforce such governmental unit's police or regulatory power, to the district court for the district where such civil action is pending, if such district court has jurisdiction of such claim or cause of action under section 1334 of this title."

Several features distinguish Section 1452(a) from ordinary removal:

  • "A party" may remove. Unlike Section 1441, which limits removal to defendants, Section 1452 permits any party to remove - including a plaintiff in some configurations. This is a meaningful expansion. A plaintiff who initially filed in state court and later becomes a debtor in a Title 11 case may want to consolidate the litigation in the bankruptcy court.
  • Claim-by-claim removal. Section 1452 permits removal of a "claim or cause of action" rather than the entire civil action. A party may remove only the bankruptcy-related claims and leave non-bankruptcy claims in state court, although in practice courts often remand or sever for case-management reasons.
  • Two statutory exclusions. Tax Court proceedings and police-or-regulatory-power actions by governmental units are categorically nonremovable. The police-power exclusion mirrors the automatic-stay exception in 11 U.S.C. § 362(b)(4) and reflects federalism concerns about federal interference with state regulatory enforcement.
  • Destination district. Removal is to the district court for the district where the state action is pending - not necessarily where the bankruptcy case is pending. If the state action is in one district and the bankruptcy case is in another, post-removal transfer under 28 U.S.C. § 1412 may be needed to consolidate.

Procedural Mechanics

The procedure for removal under Section 1452 is governed primarily by Federal Rule of Bankruptcy Procedure 9027. The notice of removal must be filed in the bankruptcy court for the district in which the state-court action is pending, and a copy must be promptly served on all parties and filed with the state court. The timing rules differ depending on when the claim becomes removable:

  • If the claim is removable when the state action is commenced, the notice must be filed within 90 days after the order for relief in the bankruptcy case;
  • If the claim becomes removable later (for example, by amendment or by later filing of the bankruptcy petition), the notice must be filed within 30 days after the event making the claim removable;
  • In Chapter 11 cases with an entered confirmation order, removal must occur before plan confirmation in most circumstances.

The timing structure differs materially from Section 1441 / Section 1446 removal, where the default window is 30 days from service or 30 days from the receipt of "other paper" rendering the case removable. Practitioners moving between general civil removal and bankruptcy removal must consult the applicable rule and confirm the operative deadline.

Section 1452(b) - Discretionary Equitable Remand

Section 1452(b) gives the receiving court broad discretion to remand: "The court to which such claim or cause of action is removed may remand such claim or cause of action on any equitable ground." This is a far broader standard than the limited remand authority under Section 1447(c) for ordinary removed cases, which permits remand only for lack of subject-matter jurisdiction or for procedural defects timely raised.

Courts addressing equitable-remand motions typically apply a multi-factor analysis that includes:

  • The effect on the efficient administration of the bankruptcy estate;
  • The extent to which state-law issues predominate over bankruptcy issues;
  • The difficulty or unsettled nature of the applicable state law;
  • The presence of related state-court litigation that argues for consolidation in the state forum;
  • The jurisdictional basis for retention (core, non-core but related, or arising under);
  • The feasibility of severing state claims from bankruptcy issues;
  • The burden on the bankruptcy court's docket;
  • The likelihood that removal involved forum-shopping;
  • The right to a jury trial; and
  • The prejudice to the involuntarily removed parties.

The factors overlap substantially with the permissive-abstention analysis under 28 U.S.C. § 1334(c)(1), and courts often analyze the two doctrines in parallel. The practical effect is that bankruptcy and district courts have considerable freedom to send state-law claims back to state court when the federal interest is attenuated.

Section 1452(b) - Non-Appealability of Remand

The second sentence of Section 1452(b) provides: "An order entered under this subsection remanding a claim or cause of action, or a decision to not remand, is not reviewable by appeal or otherwise by the court of appeals under section 158(d), 1291, or 1292 of this title or by the Supreme Court of the United States under section 1254 of this title." The non-appealability provision is sweeping. Unlike Section 1447(d), which preserves appellate review of certain remand grounds, Section 1452(b) shields the remand decision from ordinary appellate review.

The Supreme Court has interpreted analogous non-appealability provisions narrowly to preserve review of underlying jurisdictional questions where appropriate. But the core remand decision under Section 1452(b) - whether the equitable factors favor returning the case to state court - is effectively unreviewable. This gives the trial-level court enormous practical authority over the federal-state allocation of bankruptcy-related litigation.

Section 1452 Versus Section 1441 - Practical Differences

The two removal statutes can apply to the same dispute, and parties sometimes invoke both. The principal differences are:

  • Eligible removers. Section 1452 permits any party; Section 1441 limits removal to defendants.
  • Jurisdictional basis. Section 1452 requires Section 1334 jurisdiction (bankruptcy-related); Section 1441 requires diversity, federal question, or other original jurisdiction.
  • Scope. Section 1452 permits claim-by-claim removal; Section 1441 generally requires removal of the entire civil action.
  • Timing. Section 1452 follows Bankruptcy Rule 9027's tiered deadlines; Section 1441 follows the 30-day default in Section 1446.
  • Remand standard. Section 1452(b) authorizes remand on "any equitable ground"; Section 1447(c) is much more constrained.
  • Appellate review of remand. Section 1452(b) bars review categorically; Section 1447(d) preserves review for certain grounds.
  • Categorical exclusions. Section 1452 excludes Tax Court matters and police-power actions; Section 1441 has its own list of statutory exclusions.

Strategic Use of Section 1452

Removal under Section 1452 is most often invoked by debtors-in-possession and trustees seeking to consolidate state-court litigation in a single federal forum that is familiar with the estate's affairs. Creditors with significant exposure to a debtor sometimes remove to position their claims for resolution alongside the broader claim-allowance process. The equitable-remand standard means that aggressive removal often fails - courts routinely return predominantly state-law disputes to state court - but the removal itself produces a procedural pause and forces the opposing party to expend resources defending the federal forum.

The non-appealability of remand under Section 1452(b) makes the receiving court's discretion effectively final. Parties contemplating removal should evaluate not just the removability of the claim but the likelihood that the receiving court will exercise its broad equitable-remand authority to send it back.
