Jurisdictional Architecture

Title 28 Jurisdiction

The bankruptcy courts are creatures of statute, not of Article III. Their jurisdiction, the scope of their authority to enter final judgments, the avenues of appellate review, the venue rules for adversary proceedings, and the removal and remand mechanism for state-court actions all flow from a small cluster of provisions in Title 28 of the United States Code. These five guides walk through the jurisdictional architecture section by section.

About the Title 28 framework

The Bankruptcy Amendments and Federal Judgeship Act of 1984 (often called the Marathon fix, after the Supreme Court's decision in Northern Pipeline Construction Co. v. Marathon Pipe Line Co., 458 U.S. 50 (1982)) restructured the relationship between the Article III district courts and the non-Article III bankruptcy courts. Under that framework, 28 U.S.C. section 1334 vests bankruptcy jurisdiction in the district courts, which then refer all bankruptcy cases and proceedings to the bankruptcy judges of their district by standing order under 28 U.S.C. section 157(a). Section 157(b) authorizes bankruptcy judges to enter final orders in core proceedings, while section 157(c) restricts them to proposed findings in non-core proceedings that are merely related to a bankruptcy case.

This architecture has been the subject of repeated Supreme Court litigation. Stern v. Marshall, 564 U.S. 462 (2011), held that some statutorily core proceedings - specifically certain counterclaims under section 157(b)(2)(C) - could not constitutionally be finally adjudicated by a bankruptcy judge. Wellness International Network, Ltd. v. Sharif, 575 U.S. 665 (2015), held that the parties may consent to bankruptcy-court adjudication of Stern-affected matters.

The deep-dives below take each Title 28 provision in turn, explaining the reference mechanism (section 157), the appellate pathway (section 158), the underlying jurisdictional grant (section 1334), the venue rules for the case and for adversary proceedings (section 1409), and the unique bankruptcy removal and remand statute (section 1452).

Section-by-section deep-dives

  • 28 U.S.C. Section 157 - Bankruptcy Court JurisdictionThe reference of bankruptcy cases and proceedings from the district court to the bankruptcy judges, the core/non-core distinction, and the constitutional limits established by Stern v. Marshall.
  • 28 U.S.C. Section 158 - Appellate JurisdictionAppeals to the district court, the optional Bankruptcy Appellate Panel, the section 158(d)(2) direct certification to the court of appeals, and the interlocutory leave-to-appeal standard.
  • 28 U.S.C. Section 1334 - Federal Court JurisdictionThe original and exclusive jurisdiction over the bankruptcy case itself, jurisdiction over civil proceedings arising under, arising in, or related to the case, and the mandatory and permissive abstention provisions.
  • 28 U.S.C. Section 1409 - VenueThe home-court venue for proceedings against the estate, the small-dollar exception for preference and other claims against non-insiders, and the related venue-transfer mechanism of section 1412.
  • 28 U.S.C. Section 1452 - Removal and RemandThe bankruptcy-specific removal statute reaching any related-to claim or cause of action in a state-court civil action, and the equitable-grounds remand standard that displaces the ordinary 28 U.S.C. section 1447 framework.
Procedural note: Federal Rule of Bankruptcy Procedure 9027 governs the procedure for removal under 28 U.S.C. section 1452. The deadline for filing a notice of removal in a pending state-court action is, in general, the longer of 90 days after the order for relief or 30 days after the claim arose post-petition.
