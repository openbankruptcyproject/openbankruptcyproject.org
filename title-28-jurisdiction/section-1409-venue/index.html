28 U.S.C. Section 1409 - Venue of Bankruptcy Proceedings

Plain-English guide to where a proceeding arising under or related to a Title 11 case may be brought: the home-court default, the small-claims threshold protecting nonresident defendants, and special venue provisions for trustees and debtors-in-possession.

What Is Section 1409?

Section 1409 governs venue for "proceedings" arising under or related to a Title 11 case - the adversary proceedings and contested matters that are litigated within a bankruptcy case. It is the counterpart to 28 U.S.C. § 1408, which addresses venue for the bankruptcy case itself. The fundamental rule is the "home court" preference: most bankruptcy litigation is venued in the district where the bankruptcy case is pending, regardless of where the defendant resides or where the underlying transaction occurred.

The home-court rule serves the efficient administration of the estate. Trustees can pursue avoidance actions, turnover claims, and contract disputes in a single forum, before a judge familiar with the case, without the venue limitations that constrain ordinary civil litigation. But the rule has trade-offs: it concentrates defendants in distant fora, sometimes in cases where the defendant has no other connection to the district. Section 1409(b) addresses this through the small-claims threshold.

Official citation: 28 U.S.C. § 1409

Section 1409(a) - The Home-Court Rule

Section 1409(a) states the general rule: "Except as otherwise provided in subsections (b) and (d), a proceeding arising under title 11 or arising in or related to a case under title 11 may be commenced in the district court in which such case is pending." Three categories are reached: arising-under, arising-in, and related-to proceedings. The covered universe is coextensive with the jurisdictional grant of Section 1334(b).

"May" is the key word. Section 1409 is permissive rather than mandatory. A trustee may file an adversary proceeding in the home court even if the defendant has no contacts with the district; the trustee may also file in another district where venue would be proper under ordinary venue rules. In practice, the home court is the overwhelmingly preferred forum because of efficiency, judicial familiarity, and convenience to the trustee.

Section 1409(b) - The Small-Claims Threshold

Section 1409(b) carves out an important exception for nonresident defendants in small-claims cases. The trustee may not commence a proceeding "arising in or related to" a Title 11 case to recover (i) a money judgment of less than the statutory threshold for a consumer debt, (ii) a money judgment of less than the statutory threshold for a non-consumer debt against a non-insider, or (iii) a money judgment of less than the statutory threshold for a non-consumer debt - in the home court if the defendant is a nonresident.

The thresholds are periodically adjusted for inflation under 11 U.S.C. § 104. Below the applicable threshold, the trustee must sue in the district where the defendant resides or where venue would otherwise be proper. The carve-out is sometimes called the "anti-deadbeat-defendant" provision because, before its enactment, trustees routinely filed small-dollar preference actions in the home court against vendors and contractors across the country - a tactic that often produced default judgments simply because the cost of defending in a distant forum exceeded the amount in dispute.

The threshold applies only to "arising in or related to" proceedings; "arising under" proceedings (such as Bankruptcy-Code avoidance actions) are partially excluded from the carve-out in some courts' reading, though the statutory text and 2005 amendments have narrowed the trustee's home-court advantage in small-dollar preference and fraudulent-transfer claims as well.

Section 1409(c) - Personal-Injury and Wrongful-Death Claims

Section 1409(c) addresses an unusual but important category: a personal-injury or wrongful-death claim that the estate holds as a cause of action. Such a claim may be brought in the district court for the district where the bankruptcy case is pending, or in the district court for the district where the claim arose, "as determined by the applicable rules of venue." The provision recognizes that personal-injury actions implicate trial mechanics and witness availability that often counsel against shoehorning the case into a distant home court.

Section 1409(d) - Post-Petition Claims

Section 1409(d) provides that a proceeding arising under Title 11 or arising in or related to a Title 11 case based on a claim arising after the commencement of the case "from the operation of the business of the debtor" may be commenced only in a district court in which venue would be proper under 28 U.S.C. § 1391 (the general civil-venue statute) - that is, where the defendant resides, where a substantial part of the events occurred, or under the catchall in cases of foreign defendants.

The reason is fairness. Post-petition trade creditors and counterparties who deal with a debtor-in-possession should not be hauled into the home court for routine commercial disputes simply because their counterparty filed bankruptcy. Section 1409(d) restores ordinary civil-venue rules for the post-petition operations of the business.

Special Provisions for Trustees and Debtors-in-Possession

Section 1409(e) addresses claims by the trustee as the representative of the estate against claimants who have filed proofs of claim. Such claims may be commenced in the home court regardless of the amount and regardless of the claimant's residence. The rationale is the claims-allowance principle articulated in Langenkamp v. Culp, 498 U.S. 42 (1990), and its predecessors: a creditor who files a proof of claim subjects itself to the equitable jurisdiction of the bankruptcy court for the resolution of all claims by and against it.

Transfer of Venue and the Venue-of-Convenience Analysis

Where Section 1409 permits venue in the home court, defendants may still seek transfer under 28 U.S.C. § 1412, which authorizes transfer "in the interest of justice or for the convenience of the parties." The analysis tracks the traditional forum-non-conveniens factors: location of witnesses and evidence, the cost of obtaining attendance, the relative ease of access to sources of proof, and the public interest in resolving disputes locally.

Cases such as In re COIR Construction, Inc. illustrate the venue-of-convenience analysis in practice. Where the underlying transactions, the defendant's records, and most witnesses are located outside the home court, transfer is often appropriate even though the trustee's home-court designation is technically permissible. The party seeking transfer bears the burden, and courts generally afford some deference to the trustee's chosen forum, but a well-supported transfer motion can overcome the default home-court preference.

Interaction with Personal Jurisdiction

Venue and personal jurisdiction are distinct inquiries. Bankruptcy Rule 7004(d) authorizes nationwide service of process on parties to adversary proceedings, which solves most personal-jurisdiction questions through the minimum-contacts-with-the-United-States analysis. Venue under Section 1409 is the separate question of which district is the proper forum. A nonresident defendant may be subject to personal jurisdiction nationwide under Rule 7004(d) but still be entitled to a venue ruling in its favor under Section 1409(b) or a transfer under Section 1412.
