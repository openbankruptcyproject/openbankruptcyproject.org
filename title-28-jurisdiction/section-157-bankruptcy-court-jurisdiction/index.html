28 U.S.C. Section 157 - Bankruptcy Court Jurisdiction and Core Proceedings

Plain-English guide to the bankruptcy referral statute, the sixteen categories of core proceedings, non-core "related-to" matters, withdrawal of the reference, and how Stern v. Marshall reshaped what a bankruptcy judge can finally decide.

What Is Section 157?

Section 157 is the mechanical heart of the federal bankruptcy system. Title 11 cases and "civil proceedings arising under, arising in, or related to" Title 11 are vested in the district courts by 28 U.S.C. § 1334. Section 157 then authorizes the district courts to refer that business to the bankruptcy judges of their district. Every federal district has adopted a standing order of reference under Section 157(a), so in practice virtually every bankruptcy matter begins in the bankruptcy court rather than the district court.

Section 157 also draws the constitutional line. Because bankruptcy judges are Article I officers serving fourteen-year terms rather than Article III judges with life tenure, Congress had to construct a framework that respected the separation of powers identified by the Supreme Court in Northern Pipeline Construction Co. v. Marathon Pipe Line Co., 458 U.S. 50 (1982). The 1984 Bankruptcy Amendments and Federal Judgeship Act adopted the "core" versus "non-core" distinction in response.

Official citation: 28 U.S.C. § 157

Section 157(a) - The Standing Order of Reference

Section 157(a) authorizes each district court to provide that "any or all" cases under Title 11 and any or all proceedings arising under, in, or related to Title 11 "shall be referred to the bankruptcy judges for the district." Every district in the United States has entered a standing order of reference that automatically refers all bankruptcy business. The reference is administrative, not jurisdictional - the underlying jurisdiction is the district court's under Section 1334, and reference is the routing mechanism.

Section 157(b) - Core Proceedings: The Sixteen Categories

Under Section 157(b)(1), the bankruptcy judge "may hear and determine" core proceedings and enter "appropriate orders and judgments." Section 157(b)(2) provides a non-exhaustive enumeration of sixteen categories of core proceedings:

  1. Matters concerning the administration of the estate
  2. Allowance or disallowance of claims and exemptions
  3. Counterclaims by the estate against persons filing claims
  4. Orders in respect to obtaining credit
  5. Orders to turn over property of the estate
  6. Proceedings to determine, avoid, or recover preferences
  7. Motions to terminate, annul, or modify the automatic stay
  8. Proceedings to determine, avoid, or recover fraudulent conveyances
  9. Determinations as to the dischargeability of particular debts
  10. Objections to discharges
  11. Determinations of the validity, extent, or priority of liens
  12. Confirmations of plans
  13. Orders approving the use or lease of property
  14. Orders approving the sale of property other than ordinary-course
  15. Other proceedings affecting the liquidation of estate assets or the adjustment of the debtor-creditor or equity-security-holder relationship
  16. Recognition of foreign proceedings and other matters under chapter 15

The statutory list reaches almost the entirety of routine bankruptcy work. But the Supreme Court's decision in Stern v. Marshall, 564 U.S. 462 (2011), held that the statutory grant of "core" status is not coextensive with constitutional authority to enter final judgment.

Stern v. Marshall and the Constitutional Overlay

In Stern, the bankruptcy court entered judgment on a state-law tortious-interference counterclaim that was statutorily designated "core" under Section 157(b)(2)(C). The Supreme Court held that Congress could not constitutionally vest a non-Article III bankruptcy judge with authority to finally decide such a state-law claim that is independent of the federal bankruptcy proceeding and not necessarily resolved through the claims-allowance process. The result is a category of proceedings - sometimes called "Stern claims" - that are statutorily core but constitutionally non-core.

The Court in Wellness International Network, Ltd. v. Sharif, 575 U.S. 665 (2015), softened the practical impact by holding that the parties may consent (expressly or by implication) to final adjudication by a bankruptcy judge of a Stern claim. Most districts have implemented procedural rules requiring parties to elect or object in their pleadings.

The Granfinanciera, S.A. v. Nordberg, 492 U.S. 33 (1989), Seventh Amendment line is also pertinent: a defendant who has not filed a proof of claim retains a jury-trial right on a fraudulent-transfer action against it, which constrains the bankruptcy court's authority to enter final judgment without consent.

Section 157(c) - Non-Core "Related-To" Proceedings

Section 157(c)(1) addresses proceedings that are not core but are "otherwise related to a case under title 11." In a non-core proceeding, the bankruptcy judge submits proposed findings of fact and conclusions of law to the district court, which then enters final judgment "after considering the bankruptcy judge's proposed findings and conclusions and after reviewing de novo those matters to which any party has timely and specifically objected."

Section 157(c)(2) provides a consent override: with the consent of all parties, the district court may refer a non-core related-to proceeding to the bankruptcy judge for entry of final orders and judgments, subject to ordinary appellate review under 28 U.S.C. § 158. After Wellness International, this consent mechanism is the workhorse for resolving Stern-affected matters at the bankruptcy court level.

Section 157(d) - Withdrawal of the Reference

Section 157(d) authorizes the district court to withdraw a case or proceeding from the bankruptcy court. The statute creates two distinct paths:

Permissive Withdrawal

The district court may withdraw "in whole or in part, any case or proceeding referred under this section, on its own motion or on timely motion of any party, for cause shown." Courts evaluating "cause" typically weigh factors articulated by the Second Circuit in In re Orion Pictures Corp., 4 F.3d 1095 (2d Cir. 1993): whether the proceeding is core or non-core, judicial economy, uniformity of bankruptcy administration, reduction of forum shopping, conservation of debtor and creditor resources, expediency, and the presence of a jury demand.

Mandatory Withdrawal

The second sentence of Section 157(d) requires withdrawal "if the court determines that resolution of the proceeding requires consideration of both title 11 and other laws of the United States regulating organizations or activities affecting interstate commerce." Most circuits read this narrowly, requiring "substantial and material" consideration of non-bankruptcy federal law rather than routine application. The mandatory-withdrawal provision is most often invoked in cases implicating federal securities, environmental, communications, antitrust, and labor statutes.

Section 157(e) - Jury Trials by Consent

Section 157(e) provides that a bankruptcy judge may conduct a jury trial if specially designated by the district court and if "all the parties" have expressly consented. Without that consent and designation, jury proceedings must be conducted in the district court. The combined effect of Sections 157(d), 157(e), and the Granfinanciera jury right is that jury demands on non-consenting parties typically trigger withdrawal of the reference.

Practical Workflow

The day-to-day operation of Section 157 follows a predictable pattern. A bankruptcy petition is filed; the district's standing order automatically refers the case; the bankruptcy judge presides over all core proceedings and enters final judgments; non-core related-to proceedings either proceed by consent under Section 157(c)(2) or generate proposed findings under Section 157(c)(1); and a motion to withdraw the reference is filed only when a party identifies a substantial non-bankruptcy federal-law question, a constitutional limitation on bankruptcy-court authority, or a jury demand from a non-consenting party.

Even after Stern and Wellness International, the bankruptcy court remains the default federal forum for virtually all matters arising in bankruptcy. The architecture works because reference is automatic, withdrawal is the exception, and consent fills the constitutional gap left by Northern Pipeline.
