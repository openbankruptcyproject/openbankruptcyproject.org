28 U.S.C. Section 1334 - District Court Jurisdiction Over Bankruptcy

Plain-English guide to the original-jurisdiction grant for bankruptcy: exclusive jurisdiction over Title 11 cases, non-exclusive jurisdiction over arising-under, arising-in, and related-to proceedings, mandatory and permissive abstention, and in rem authority over estate property.

What Is Section 1334?

Section 1334 is the original-jurisdiction grant for the federal bankruptcy system. It vests jurisdiction in the United States district courts; 28 U.S.C. § 157 then provides the mechanism by which the district courts refer that jurisdiction to bankruptcy judges. Without Section 1334, there would be no federal subject-matter jurisdiction over bankruptcy at all, because the Constitution's Bankruptcy Clause (Article I, Section 8, Clause 4) authorizes Congress to create uniform bankruptcy laws but does not by itself confer jurisdiction.

The statute draws three categorical distinctions that drive jurisdictional analysis in every contested matter: (i) "cases under Title 11" (the bankruptcy case itself), (ii) "civil proceedings arising under, arising in, or related to" a Title 11 case, and (iii) property of the estate. Each receives a different jurisdictional treatment.

Official citation: 28 U.S.C. § 1334

Section 1334(a) - Exclusive Jurisdiction Over Title 11 Cases

Section 1334(a) provides that "the district courts shall have original and exclusive jurisdiction of all cases under title 11." The "case under Title 11" refers to the umbrella bankruptcy proceeding initiated by the filing of a petition. State courts have no jurisdiction to entertain a bankruptcy petition; only federal district courts (acting through their bankruptcy units) may administer the case itself.

The exclusivity is narrow but important. Many disputes that arise during a bankruptcy case are not themselves "cases under Title 11" - they are proceedings within the case - and those receive the non-exclusive treatment of Section 1334(b).

Section 1334(b) - Non-Exclusive Jurisdiction Over Civil Proceedings

Section 1334(b) gives the district courts "original but not exclusive jurisdiction of all civil proceedings arising under title 11, or arising in or related to cases under title 11." The three categories embedded in this single sentence drive the practical scope of bankruptcy jurisdiction:

Arising Under Title 11

These are proceedings whose cause of action is created by the Bankruptcy Code itself - for example, a trustee's avoidance action under Section 547 (preferences) or Section 548 (fraudulent transfers), a complaint objecting to discharge under Section 727, or a turnover action under Section 542.

Arising In a Title 11 Case

These are proceedings that, by their nature, can arise only in a bankruptcy case - administrative matters such as claim allowance, plan confirmation, sale approvals, and stay-relief motions. They are not created by the Bankruptcy Code in the cause-of-action sense, but they have no existence outside of bankruptcy.

Related To a Title 11 Case

The "related to" category is the broadest and most litigated. The leading test, articulated by the Third Circuit in Pacor, Inc. v. Higgins, 743 F.2d 984 (3d Cir. 1984), and adopted by the Supreme Court in Celotex Corp. v. Edwards, 514 U.S. 300 (1995), asks whether the outcome of the proceeding "could conceivably have any effect on the estate being administered in bankruptcy." Conceivability is a generous threshold, but it has limits. After confirmation of a plan, "related-to" jurisdiction generally contracts to matters affecting plan implementation, consistent with In re Resorts International, Inc., 372 F.3d 154 (3d Cir. 2004).

Section 1334(c) - Abstention

Even where Section 1334(b) jurisdiction exists, Section 1334(c) provides for abstention - the district court may, and sometimes must, decline to exercise its jurisdiction.

Section 1334(c)(1) - Permissive Abstention

The district court may abstain "in the interest of justice, or in the interest of comity with State courts or respect for State law." Permissive abstention is discretionary and is analyzed under a multi-factor balancing test that includes the effect on bankruptcy administration, the predominance of state-law issues, the difficulty or unsettled nature of the applicable law, the presence of a related state-court proceeding, the substance rather than form of an asserted "core" proceeding, the feasibility of severing state claims, the burden on the bankruptcy court's docket, the likelihood of forum shopping, and the right to a jury trial.

Section 1334(c)(2) - Mandatory Abstention

The district court "shall abstain" from hearing a non-core, related-to state-law claim if (i) the claim could not have been commenced in federal court absent bankruptcy jurisdiction, (ii) the action was commenced in state court, (iii) the action can be timely adjudicated in state court, and (iv) timely motion is made. Mandatory abstention applies only to "related-to" proceedings; it does not reach claims arising under or arising in Title 11. The mandatory-abstention requirement reflects Congress's intent that bankruptcy not become a vehicle for federalizing routine state-law disputes that merely happen to share a party with a bankruptcy estate.

Section 1334(d) - Immediate Effect of Abstention Orders

Section 1334(d) provides that any decision to abstain or not to abstain "is not reviewable by appeal or otherwise by the court of appeals" under Section 158(d), Section 1291, or Section 1292. The provision does not preclude review of related orders (such as remand orders or jurisdictional determinations) on appropriate grounds, but it does shield the core abstention decision from ordinary appellate review. Combined with the non-appealability of remand orders under 28 U.S.C. § 1452(b), the abstention regime gives bankruptcy and district courts substantial latitude to manage their dockets without prolonged appellate intervention.

Section 1334(e) - In Rem Jurisdiction Over Estate Property

Section 1334(e)(1) provides that the district court in which a Title 11 case is commenced has "exclusive jurisdiction" over "all of the property, wherever located, of the debtor as of the commencement of such case, and of property of the estate." This is the in rem foundation of bankruptcy. It explains why courts in other jurisdictions cannot adjudicate competing claims to estate property without leave of the bankruptcy court, and why the automatic stay under 11 U.S.C. § 362 is enforceable against actors in any jurisdiction.

Section 1334(e)(2), added in 2005, adds exclusive jurisdiction over "all claims or causes of action that involve construction of section 327 of title 11, or rules relating to disclosure requirements under section 327." This was Congress's response to debates over which forum could resolve professional-employment and disclosure disputes.

Constitutional Foundation and Practical Effect

Section 1334 implements the Bankruptcy Clause through the Article III district courts, then permits reference of routine matters to Article I bankruptcy judges under Section 157. The Supreme Court has consistently treated the underlying jurisdiction as residing in the district court. Northern Pipeline Construction Co. v. Marathon Pipe Line Co., 458 U.S. 50 (1982), invalidated the 1978 Code's broader grant of authority to bankruptcy judges; the 1984 amendments responded by retaining jurisdiction in the district courts under Section 1334 and routing it through Section 157 reference.

In practical operation, parties rarely litigate Section 1334 directly. The standing order of reference takes the case to the bankruptcy court; jurisdictional disputes typically appear as withdrawal-of-reference motions under Section 157(d), abstention motions under Section 1334(c), or remand motions under Section 1452(b). Each of those frames a Section 1334 question without naming it.
