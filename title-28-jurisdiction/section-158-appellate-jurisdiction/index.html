28 U.S.C. Section 158 - Appellate Jurisdiction in Bankruptcy

Plain-English guide to bankruptcy appeals: where to appeal, when to elect the Bankruptcy Appellate Panel, what counts as a final order, interlocutory review by leave, and direct certification to the court of appeals.

What Is Section 158?

Section 158 is the appellate roadmap for bankruptcy cases. While ordinary federal civil litigation has a single appellate path - district court to circuit court of appeals under 28 U.S.C. § 1291 - bankruptcy appeals follow a multi-track structure that gives parties a meaningful choice of first-level appellate forum, while also providing a fast path to the circuit court in cases of unusual importance.

The fundamental architecture: an order entered by the bankruptcy court is appealed first to the district court (or, in circuits that have established one, to a Bankruptcy Appellate Panel), and the second-tier appeal goes to the regional court of appeals. The 2005 BAPCPA amendments added Section 158(d)(2), a direct-certification mechanism that allows certain appeals to leap-frog the first appellate tier and go straight to the court of appeals.

Official citation: 28 U.S.C. § 158

Section 158(a) - District Court Appellate Jurisdiction

Section 158(a) gives the district courts jurisdiction to hear appeals from three categories of bankruptcy court orders:

  1. Final judgments, orders, and decrees - appealable as of right;
  2. Interlocutory orders and decrees issued under Section 1121(d) extending or reducing the exclusivity periods in Chapter 11 - appealable as of right;
  3. Other interlocutory orders and decrees - appealable only with leave of the district court.

The first category is the workhorse. Bankruptcy "finality" is judged more flexibly than in ordinary civil litigation because a bankruptcy case is a collection of many discrete controversies. An order resolving a single adversary proceeding, a contested matter, or even a discrete dispute within a larger contested matter can be final and immediately appealable. The Supreme Court confirmed this in Bullard v. Blue Hills Bank, 575 U.S. 496 (2015), which held that an order denying confirmation of a Chapter 13 plan is not itself final, but emphasized that bankruptcy finality is determined by reference to the discrete proceeding rather than the entire case.

Section 158(b) and (c) - The Bankruptcy Appellate Panel

Section 158(b) authorizes each circuit's judicial council to establish a Bankruptcy Appellate Panel (BAP) composed of bankruptcy judges from districts within the circuit, sitting in three-judge panels. BAPs currently operate in the First, Sixth, Eighth, Ninth, and Tenth Circuits. A BAP appeal proceeds with the same scope of review and the same standards as a district-court appeal.

Section 158(c) - The BAP Election

Where a BAP exists, the appellant has a one-time election under Section 158(c)(1). The default is BAP review unless the appellant timely elects to have the appeal heard by the district court. The election must be made by filing a statement with the bankruptcy clerk at the time the notice of appeal is filed (or within 30 days after service of an opposing appeal). Any other party to the appeal then has 30 days to opt the appeal back to the district court. The opt-out right ensures that no party is forced into BAP review against its will.

Strategic considerations drive the election. BAP panels are composed entirely of bankruptcy judges, which often translates to greater facility with the Bankruptcy Code and faster disposition. District judges hear bankruptcy appeals only occasionally and may bring a fresh perspective; in some districts the additional delay is meaningful. Both BAP and district-court decisions are reviewable in the court of appeals on equal terms.

Section 158(d)(1) - Court of Appeals Jurisdiction

Section 158(d)(1) gives the courts of appeals jurisdiction over appeals from "all final decisions, judgments, orders, and decrees entered" by district courts and BAPs in their appellate capacity. This is the second-tier review. Standard appellate-review principles apply: legal conclusions are reviewed de novo, factual findings for clear error, and discretionary rulings for abuse of discretion.

Section 158(d)(2) - Direct Certification to the Circuit

Section 158(d)(2), added by BAPCPA in 2005, allows the court of appeals to take immediate jurisdiction over an appeal from a bankruptcy court order - bypassing the district court or BAP entirely - if the order is certified for direct review. Certification may issue from:

  • The bankruptcy court, the district court, or the BAP that entered or is hearing the appeal of the order;
  • All the appellants and appellees acting jointly; or
  • A majority of the parties to the appeal acting together.

To support certification, the certifying authority must find that one of three statutory triggers is satisfied: (1) the order involves a question of law as to which there is no controlling decision of the Supreme Court or the relevant circuit, or involves a matter of public importance; (2) the order involves a question of law requiring resolution of conflicting decisions; or (3) immediate appeal "may materially advance the progress of the case or proceeding."

Even after certification, the court of appeals must accept the appeal. Acceptance is discretionary, and the courts of appeals have taken a measured approach, granting direct review in cases of genuine circuit-wide importance and declining in cases that are factbound or that would benefit from a first-tier appellate record.

Finality, Mootness, and Equitable Mootness

Bankruptcy appellate practice has developed a distinctive equitable-mootness doctrine. Where a Chapter 11 plan has been substantially consummated and the relief requested on appeal would unravel the consummated plan, the courts of appeals will often dismiss the appeal as equitably moot even though Article III mootness is not satisfied. The doctrine has been controversial - it is judge-made and turns on a multi-factor balancing test - but it remains influential in large-case Chapter 11 appellate practice. Critics argue it short-circuits review of legally significant confirmation orders; defenders argue it protects the reliance interests of plan participants. The doctrine is uniquely a creature of Section 158 appellate practice and has no direct analog in ordinary civil appellate law.

Procedural Timing

Federal Rule of Bankruptcy Procedure 8002 generally requires a notice of appeal to be filed within fourteen days of entry of the order being appealed - a significantly shorter window than the thirty-day default in ordinary civil litigation under Federal Rule of Appellate Procedure 4. Tolling motions and extensions of time are available but are governed by their own strict deadlines. The compressed timetable reflects the policy preference for prompt resolution of bankruptcy disputes.
