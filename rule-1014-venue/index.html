Federal Rule of Bankruptcy Procedure 1014

Rule 1014: Dismissal and Change of Venue

The 1014(a) discretionary transfer standard, mandatory action under 1014(b) when venue is improper, and the four-factor Commonwealth Oil framework.

Verbatim text of Rule 1014(a)

(1) Cases Filed in Proper District. If a petition is filed in the proper district, the court, on the timely motion of a party in interest or on its own motion, and after hearing on notice to the petitioners, the United States trustee, and other entities as directed by the court, may transfer the case to any other district if the court determines that the transfer is in the interest of justice or for the convenience of the parties.
(2) Cases Filed in Improper District. If a petition is filed in an improper district, the court, on the timely motion of a party in interest or on its own motion, and after hearing on notice to the petitioners, the United States trustee, and other entities as directed by the court, may dismiss the case or transfer it to any other district if the court determines that transfer is in the interest of justice or for the convenience of the parties. Fed. R. Bankr. P. 1014(a).

The structure of Rule 1014

Federal Rule of Bankruptcy Procedure 1014 implements the venue statutes for bankruptcy cases - 28 U.S.C. Section 1408 (proper venue for cases under title 11) and 28 U.S.C. Section 1412 (change of venue). The rule has two operative subdivisions:

  • 1014(a)(1) - Discretionary transfer when the case was filed in the proper district. The court may transfer "in the interest of justice or for the convenience of the parties."
  • 1014(a)(2) - When the case was filed in an improper district, the court may either dismiss the case or transfer it to a proper district.
  • 1014(b) - When petitions are filed against the same debtor or related debtors in different districts, the court in the district where the first petition was filed determines, on motion filed within the period prescribed by the court, the district in which the cases should proceed.

Proper venue under 28 U.S.C. Section 1408

Section 1408 establishes that a bankruptcy case may be commenced in any district in which, for the 180 days immediately preceding the commencement of the case (or for the longer portion of that 180-day period than in any other district), the debtor's:

  • Domicile,
  • Residence,
  • Principal place of business in the United States, or
  • Principal assets in the United States

were located. Section 1408(2) also permits filing in any district where there is a pending case under title 11 concerning an affiliate, general partner, or partnership of the debtor. The 180-day lookback prevents debtors from establishing token contacts with a preferred forum immediately before filing.

The Commonwealth Oil four-factor analysis

The leading framework for evaluating a discretionary transfer motion under Rule 1014(a)(1) is the four-factor analysis articulated in In re Commonwealth Oil Refining Co., 596 F.2d 1239 (5th Cir. 1979). Although decided under the predecessor statute, the framework remains the principal analytical tool used by courts evaluating bankruptcy venue transfer in the interest of justice or for the convenience of parties:

(1) Proximity of creditors of every kind to the court

The court considers where the creditors are physically located. A heavy concentration of creditors in a single district favors administration in that district. The presence of trade creditors, secured creditors, and significant unsecured creditors in the proposed transferee district is weighed against the convenience of the debtor's chosen forum.

(2) Proximity of the debtor to the court

Where is the debtor's headquarters, principal place of business, and management? Where are the debtor's books, records, and operating personnel? A debtor whose operational center is geographically remote from the chosen filing district is more readily transferred than one filing in its operational home.

(3) Proximity of witnesses necessary to administration of the estate

The court considers where the witnesses likely to be called in adversary proceedings, contested matters, and plan confirmation hearings reside. Witness convenience is one component of the broader convenience analysis but is given substantial weight when significant litigation is anticipated.

(4) Location of the assets

The physical location of the estate's principal assets is weighed. Real estate, operating facilities, inventory, and equipment that must be inspected, valued, marketed, or sold weigh in favor of the district where they are located, particularly in liquidating proceedings.

Some courts add a fifth factor - the economic and efficient administration of the estate - as a synthesizing consideration that overlays the four enumerated factors. The party moving for transfer bears the burden of demonstrating by a preponderance of the evidence that transfer is warranted.

Mandatory dismissal or transfer under Rule 1014(a)(2)

When a petition is filed in a district that is not a proper district under 28 U.S.C. Section 1408, the rule directs the court, on timely motion or sua sponte, to either dismiss the case or transfer it to a proper district. The court's choice between dismissal and transfer is governed by the same "interest of justice or convenience of the parties" standard that applies to discretionary transfer.

Some authorities read the rule as effectively requiring transfer rather than dismissal when transfer is feasible, on the theory that dismissal of an improperly venued case typically harms the estate (loss of the automatic stay, risk of preferential transfers becoming unrecoverable, dissipation of assets between dismissal and re-filing) without serving any countervailing interest. Other authorities preserve dismissal as a meaningful option, particularly where the improper venue reflects bad-faith forum shopping.

Timeliness of the venue objection

A motion to transfer or dismiss for improper venue must be "timely" under Rule 1014. Courts construe timeliness in light of when the moving party knew or should have known of the venue defect, the stage of the case at the time of the motion, and the prejudice to the estate from a late-stage transfer. A party that participates substantively in case administration for an extended period without raising venue may be deemed to have waived the objection.

Choice-of-venue concerns and forum shopping

Bankruptcy venue law has long been the subject of academic and policy debate concerning forum shopping. Large corporate debtors have, historically, exhibited a pronounced preference for a small number of districts - notably the District of Delaware and the Southern District of New York - based on perceptions of judicial expertise, favorable case law, and predictable administration. Section 1408(2) permits filing in any district where an affiliate case is pending, which has been used to establish venue in a preferred district by first filing a small affiliate case there.

Rule 1014(a) supplies the principal procedural check on forum shopping in cases that satisfy Section 1408 formally but raise convenience concerns. Courts considering Rule 1014(a)(1) transfer motions have, with varying frequency, transferred large cases away from chosen forums when the Commonwealth Oil factors weighed decisively in favor of a different district, particularly when an affiliate-venue mechanism was used to establish formal compliance with Section 1408.

Common procedural posture

Creditor motion to transfer

A creditor located in a district remote from the filing district moves under Rule 1014(a)(1) to transfer the case to the district where the debtor's operations, assets, and majority of creditors are located. The creditor presents evidence on each of the four Commonwealth Oil factors and argues that the debtor's chosen forum reflects forum shopping rather than operational convenience.

Sua sponte review of venue

On reviewing the petition and supporting documents, the court raises venue on its own motion under Rule 1014(a)(2), sets a hearing on notice to the United States trustee and parties in interest, and requires the debtor to demonstrate that venue is proper under one of the four Section 1408 bases.

Practical impact

A successful Rule 1014 transfer motion realigns the entire administrative architecture of a case: a different judge, a different United States trustee region, different local rules, and often a different bar of bankruptcy practitioners. Once a transfer order is entered, the case is administered as if it had originally been filed in the transferee district, and all prior orders remain in effect unless modified by the transferee court. The strategic stakes are high enough that contested venue motions in large cases often involve substantial evidentiary records and multiple days of hearing.

Venue is procedural, not jurisdictional. A bankruptcy court has subject-matter jurisdiction over title 11 cases regardless of venue, and venue objections that are not timely raised are waived. The remedy for improper venue is transfer or dismissal under Rule 1014, not the collateral attack on the validity of orders entered in the improperly venued case.
