Federal Rule of Bankruptcy Procedure 4008

Rule 4008: Discharge and Reaffirmation Hearing

The Rule 4008(a) filing requirement, the 4008(b) attorney certification, the 4008(c) undue-hardship affidavit, and the 60-day post-discharge default rule.

What reaffirmation does

A reaffirmation agreement under 11 U.S.C. Section 524(c) permits an individual debtor to agree, post-petition and before discharge, to remain personally liable on a debt that the discharge would otherwise extinguish. Reaffirmation is most commonly used in Chapter 7 to preserve secured-creditor relationships on vehicles, mortgages, and other collateral the debtor wishes to retain, by maintaining the personal-liability layer above the secured creditor's in rem rights against the collateral.

Because reaffirmation has the substantial consequence of preserving a personal-liability obligation that the bankruptcy would otherwise discharge, Section 524(c) and its implementing rule, Federal Rule of Bankruptcy Procedure 4008, impose a tight set of procedural and substantive requirements.

The substantive Section 524(c) framework

Before turning to the procedural rule, the substantive prerequisites of Section 524(c) must be understood. A reaffirmation agreement is enforceable only if:

  • (1) The agreement was made before the granting of the discharge under Section 727, Section 1141, Section 1228, or Section 1328
  • (2) The debtor received the disclosures described in Section 524(k) at or before the time the debtor signed the agreement
  • (3) The agreement was filed with the court
  • (4) If the debtor was represented by an attorney during the negotiation, the agreement was accompanied by a declaration or affidavit of the attorney stating that the agreement (i) represents a fully informed and voluntary agreement of the debtor, (ii) does not impose an undue hardship on the debtor or any dependent of the debtor, and (iii) the attorney has fully advised the debtor of the legal effect and consequences of the agreement and any default under the agreement
  • (5) The debtor did not, before the granting of the discharge, exercise the right to rescind the agreement
  • (6) For a debtor not represented by an attorney, the court approves the agreement after a hearing, finding that it does not impose an undue hardship and is in the best interest of the debtor (subject to certain consumer-debt exceptions)

Rule 4008(a) - filing the agreement

Rule 4008(a) requires that a reaffirmation agreement be filed no later than 60 days after the first date set for the meeting of creditors under Section 341(a). The court may, for cause, extend the time at any time before expiration of the period. The 60-day filing window aligns the reaffirmation deadline with the typical Chapter 7 discharge timeline, ensuring that reaffirmation agreements come before the court before the discharge enters.

The filed agreement must include the disclosure documents required by Section 524(k) and any required attorney declaration or affidavit. The agreement is submitted on Official Form 2400 (or its current numerical equivalent), which embeds the statutorily required disclosures and the structured presentation of debtor income, expenses, and any presumption of undue hardship.

Rule 4008(b) - attorney certification

When the debtor is represented by counsel during the reaffirmation negotiation, Rule 4008(b) requires the agreement to be accompanied by the attorney's declaration or affidavit certifying:

  • That the agreement represents a fully informed and voluntary agreement of the debtor
  • That the agreement does not impose an undue hardship on the debtor or any dependent of the debtor
  • That the attorney has fully advised the debtor of the legal effect and consequences of the agreement, including any default under the agreement

The attorney certification is a substantive professional-responsibility undertaking. The attorney who signs the certification is representing to the court that the attorney has, in fact, conducted the analysis required to make the three findings - not that the debtor wants to sign, not that the creditor has offered the agreement, but that the attorney has independently evaluated and concluded that the reaffirmation does not impose undue hardship on the debtor.

Rule 4008(c) - undue-hardship presumption and affidavit

Rule 4008(c) implements the Section 524(m) statutory framework governing the presumption of undue hardship. Where the debtor's monthly income on Schedule I (less monthly expenses on Schedule J) is less than the scheduled payment under the reaffirmation agreement, a presumption of undue hardship arises.

The presumption may be rebutted by a written statement, filed with the court, identifying additional sources of funds to make the agreed payments. The statement must:

  • Identify the additional sources of funds with reasonable specificity
  • Explain why the additional funds were not reflected on Schedules I or J
  • State that the additional funds are reasonably anticipated to be available throughout the term of the agreement

If the presumption arises and is not rebutted, the court must review the agreement and may decline to approve it. In the case of a represented debtor, the attorney's Rule 4008(b) certification - particularly the no-undue-hardship prong - is a separate evaluative checkpoint that does not depend on the mechanical calculation that triggers the Section 524(m) presumption.

Pro se debtors and the court hearing

When the debtor is not represented by counsel during the reaffirmation negotiation, Section 524(c)(6) and Rule 4008 require the court to hold a hearing and make findings before the agreement is enforceable. At the hearing, the court inquires of the debtor whether the reaffirmation is fully informed and voluntary, whether the debtor understands the legal effect, and whether the agreement imposes undue hardship. The court must approve the agreement on the record for the reaffirmation to be enforceable. The hearing is sometimes consolidated with the discharge hearing under Section 524(d).

Exceptions: For consumer debt secured by real property, the Section 524(c)(6) court-approval requirement does not apply. For other consumer debt of an unrepresented debtor, the court-approval requirement is the default.

The Section 524(d) discharge hearing

Section 524(d) authorizes the court to hold a discharge hearing for the purpose of informing the debtor of the granting of the discharge, the general effect of the discharge, the legal effect of any pending reaffirmation agreement, and the right to rescind such an agreement. The discharge hearing is a discretionary mechanism that the court may use, particularly in cases involving pro se reaffirmation negotiations. Rule 4008 governs the procedural posture of the hearing.

The right to rescind

Section 524(c)(4) permits the debtor to rescind a reaffirmation agreement at any time before discharge or within 60 days after the agreement is filed with the court, whichever is later. The rescission must be effective notice to the creditor of the debtor's intent to rescind. After rescission, the personal-liability discharge takes its ordinary effect, and the creditor's remaining rights are limited to its in rem rights against the collateral.

The 60-day post-discharge default rule

The interaction of the Rule 4008(a) filing window (60 days from the first Section 341 date) and the Section 524(c)(4) rescission window (60 days from filing) typically positions the reaffirmation decision before discharge. The discharge in a routine Chapter 7 case is granted shortly after the close of the rescission period, leaving the debtor a clean choice between reaffirmation (preservation of personal liability) and non-reaffirmation (personal liability discharged, in rem rights of the secured creditor preserved).

If the reaffirmation is not filed within the Rule 4008(a) deadline (and no extension is granted), the agreement is not enforceable under Section 524(c)(1). The debtor's personal liability on the debt is discharged. The secured creditor retains its in rem rights against the collateral and may pursue collateral recovery through nonbankruptcy remedies, subject to any "ride-through" practices recognized in the particular district.

Common procedural posture

Vehicle reaffirmation in Chapter 7

A Chapter 7 debtor wishes to retain a vehicle subject to a purchase-money security interest. The vehicle lender prepares a reaffirmation agreement on Official Form 2400 and transmits it to debtor's counsel. Counsel reviews the agreement, performs the Section 524(m) hardship analysis using Schedules I and J, makes the Rule 4008(b) certification, and files the agreement within the 60-day Rule 4008(a) window. The debtor may rescind within the 60-day rescission period; if no rescission, the personal-liability obligation survives discharge.

Pro se reaffirmation hearing

An unrepresented debtor files a reaffirmation agreement on Official Form 2400. Because no attorney certification can be made under Rule 4008(b), and because the debtor is not represented, the court schedules a hearing under Section 524(c)(6). At the hearing, the court inquires of the debtor, makes findings on the record regarding voluntariness, understanding, and absence of undue hardship, and either approves or declines to approve the agreement.

Practical impact

Reaffirmation is a tightly regulated procedural moment in the Chapter 7 case. The decision to reaffirm a debt that the bankruptcy would otherwise discharge is one of the few moments in the case where the debtor knowingly takes on a forward-looking liability. The procedural infrastructure of Rule 4008 - the 60-day filing deadline, the attorney certification, the undue-hardship affidavit, the rescission window, and (for pro se debtors) the court hearing - is designed to ensure that the decision is informed, voluntary, and not financially destructive.

Attorneys who routinely make Rule 4008(b) certifications carry substantial professional-responsibility exposure. The certification that the agreement "does not impose an undue hardship" is a substantive opinion grounded in the debtor's actual budget; an attorney who certifies in disregard of a debtor's evident inability to perform may be subject to sanctions under Rule 9011 and to disciplinary referral under the state rules of professional conduct.

Reaffirmation is the personal-liability decision; surrender and redemption are the in rem decisions. The debtor's choices for secured collateral in Chapter 7 are: (i) reaffirm (preserve personal liability + retain collateral), (ii) redeem under Section 722 (pay lump-sum value, discharge personal liability, retain collateral), (iii) surrender (discharge personal liability, surrender collateral to the secured creditor), or (iv) in some districts, ride-through (discharge personal liability, retain collateral, continue payments without contractual obligation). Rule 4008 governs only option (i).
