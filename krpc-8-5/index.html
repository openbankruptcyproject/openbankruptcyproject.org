Rule of Professional Conduct KRPC 8.5 / MRPC 4-8.5

KRPC 8.5 / MRPC 4-8.5: Disciplinary Authority and Choice of Law

Admission-state default, jurisdiction-of-conduct rule, predominant-effect test, and the reasonable-belief safe harbor.

Verbatim text of KRPC 8.5 (Kansas)

(a) Disciplinary Authority. A lawyer admitted to practice in this jurisdiction is subject to the disciplinary authority of this jurisdiction, regardless of where the lawyer's conduct occurs. A lawyer not admitted in this jurisdiction is also subject to the disciplinary authority of this jurisdiction if the lawyer provides or offers to provide any legal services in this jurisdiction. A lawyer may be subject to the disciplinary authority of both this jurisdiction and another jurisdiction for the same conduct.

(b) Choice of Law. In any exercise of the disciplinary authority of this jurisdiction, the rules of professional conduct to be applied shall be as follows:
(1) for conduct in connection with a matter pending before a tribunal, the rules of the jurisdiction in which the tribunal sits, unless the rules of the tribunal provide otherwise; and
(2) for any other conduct, the rules of the jurisdiction in which the lawyer's conduct occurred, or, if the predominant effect of the conduct is in a different jurisdiction, the rules of that jurisdiction shall be applied to the conduct. A lawyer shall not be subject to discipline if the lawyer's conduct conforms to the rules of a jurisdiction in which the lawyer reasonably believes the predominant effect of the lawyer's conduct will occur. Kan. Sup. Ct. R. 226, KRPC 8.5.

Missouri Rule 4-8.5

Missouri Rule 4-8.5 is substantively parallel and tracks ABA Model Rule 8.5. The rule does two things simultaneously: it identifies which jurisdictions may exercise disciplinary authority over a lawyer (subdivision (a)), and it specifies which jurisdiction's rules of professional conduct apply when that authority is exercised (subdivision (b)). The two questions are distinct and the rule addresses them in sequence.

The choice-of-law architecture 8.5 provides

The architecture has four moving parts, each of which performs a distinct function:

Admission-state default

A lawyer admitted in a jurisdiction is subject to that jurisdiction's disciplinary authority regardless of where the conduct occurs. Admission is itself a continuing submission to disciplinary jurisdiction.

Conduct-state extension

A lawyer not admitted in a jurisdiction is also subject to that jurisdiction's disciplinary authority if the lawyer provides or offers legal services in the jurisdiction. Activity, not admission, creates jurisdiction for the activity-based branch.

Predominant-effect test

For non-tribunal conduct, the rules of the jurisdiction where the conduct occurred apply - unless the predominant effect of the conduct is in a different jurisdiction, in which case the rules of the effect-jurisdiction apply. The test calibrates choice of law to substance.

Reasonable-belief safe harbor

A lawyer is not subject to discipline if conduct conforms to the rules of a jurisdiction where the lawyer reasonably believes the predominant effect will occur. The safe harbor protects good-faith analysis when the predominant-effect determination is uncertain.

Subdivision (a) - Disciplinary authority: admission-state default + conduct-state alternative

Admission-state default

The first sentence of 8.5(a) establishes the foundational rule: admission to a jurisdiction's bar creates disciplinary jurisdiction in that jurisdiction over all of the lawyer's professional conduct, regardless of where the conduct occurs. A lawyer admitted in Kansas remains subject to Kansas disciplinary authority for conduct in any state, in any federal court, in any administrative proceeding, and in any client-counseling activity anywhere in the world. The admission-state default is geographic-agnostic.

Conduct-state alternative

The second sentence extends disciplinary jurisdiction to lawyers who are not admitted but who provide or offer legal services within the jurisdiction. The provision recognizes that licensure boundaries are not the only meaningful jurisdictional touchstone; the act of practicing in a jurisdiction also creates a regulatory relationship. The interaction with Rule 5.5 on unauthorized practice is intentional: a lawyer engaging in cross-border practice is subject to both the admission state and the conduct state on the same conduct.

Dual jurisdiction

The third sentence acknowledges that the two branches can - and routinely do - overlap. A lawyer may be subject to discipline in both the admission jurisdiction and the conduct jurisdiction for the same conduct. The acknowledgement is important: a lawyer who anticipates that compliance with one jurisdiction's rules will suffice for all purposes has misread the architecture. Both jurisdictions retain authority.

Subdivision (b)(1) - Tribunal rule

For conduct in connection with a matter pending before a tribunal, the rules of the jurisdiction in which the tribunal sits apply - unless the rules of the tribunal provide otherwise. The clarity of this branch is intentional. A lawyer practicing before a court in Missouri is governed by Missouri's professional-conduct rules with respect to that conduct, even if the lawyer is admitted only in Kansas and physically located in Kansas while drafting the brief.

The "unless the rules of the tribunal provide otherwise" clause is important for federal courts. Federal district courts typically adopt the professional-conduct rules of the state in which the court sits, but some federal tribunals adopt different rules or supplement them with court-specific provisions. The lawyer should confirm the rules of the specific tribunal at the outset of the matter.

Subdivision (b)(2) - Non-tribunal conduct: the predominant-effect test

For all other conduct - transactional work, client counseling, negotiation, advice not connected to a pending tribunal matter - the choice-of-law analysis runs in two stages.

Stage one: conduct location

The default is the rules of the jurisdiction in which the lawyer's conduct occurred. For a lawyer drafting a contract in an office in Kansas, the conduct location is Kansas.

Stage two: predominant-effect override

If the predominant effect of the conduct is in a different jurisdiction, the rules of that jurisdiction apply instead. The override is the substantive heart of the choice-of-law branch. It recognizes that the conduct's location may be a thin connection compared to the location of the affected client, the affected transaction, the affected parties, and the affected market.

Comment [5] to the model rule explains the predominant-effect factors: where the lawyer's conduct will occur, where the affected parties are located, where the transaction is centered, and where the documents and assets are located. The factors are functional rather than mechanical.

The reasonable-belief safe harbor

The final sentence of 8.5(b)(2) supplies the safe harbor:

A lawyer shall not be subject to discipline if the lawyer's conduct conforms to the rules of a jurisdiction in which the lawyer reasonably believes the predominant effect of the lawyer's conduct will occur.

The safe harbor has four operative elements:

  • Reasonable belief - the lawyer must hold the belief, and the belief must be reasonable in light of the information available. A bare assertion is insufficient; a documented analysis is the practical floor.
  • A jurisdiction - the belief identifies a specific jurisdiction, not a vague sense that some other jurisdiction's rules might apply.
  • Predominant effect - the belief is about where the predominant effect will occur, evaluated under the functional factors Comment [5] identifies.
  • Conduct that conforms - the lawyer's conduct actually conforms to the rules of the reasonably-believed jurisdiction. The safe harbor protects the lawyer who calibrated practice to a reasonable choice-of-law analysis; it does not protect the lawyer whose conduct fails the chosen jurisdiction's rules anyway.

The safe harbor is structural. It permits the multi-jurisdiction practitioner to make a good-faith determination, conform conduct to the chosen jurisdiction's rules, and rely on the determination as a defense to discipline even if the choice turns out, in retrospect, to be debatable. The safe harbor's existence is what makes multi-jurisdiction practice tractable; without it, the lawyer would face residual exposure for any choice-of-law judgment that did not align with the disciplinary authority's later view.

The reasonable-belief safe harbor is the architectural feature that makes multi-jurisdiction practice workable. A lawyer who documents the choice-of-law analysis at the time of the conduct, identifies the predominant-effect jurisdiction, conforms conduct to the rules of that jurisdiction, and preserves the documentation has built the strongest available defense to disciplinary exposure on a debatable choice-of-law question.

Operating the architecture in practice

For a typical multi-jurisdiction matter, the architecture's application proceeds in three steps:

  1. Identify all jurisdictions with disciplinary authority over the lawyer for this conduct - the admission jurisdiction(s) under the first sentence of 8.5(a), and any conduct jurisdiction(s) under the second sentence.
  2. Determine which jurisdiction's rules apply under 8.5(b) - for tribunal matters, the tribunal's jurisdiction; for non-tribunal conduct, the conduct location, subject to the predominant-effect override.
  3. Conform conduct to the applicable rules and document the analysis - the documentation supports the reasonable-belief safe harbor if the choice-of-law determination is later challenged.

Relationship to Rule 5.5 (Unauthorized Practice)

Rule 8.5(a) and Rule 5.5 address overlapping but distinct questions. Rule 5.5 governs whether the lawyer may engage in the practice at all in a jurisdiction where the lawyer is not admitted. Rule 8.5(a) governs whether the jurisdiction's disciplinary authority reaches the lawyer's conduct. The two rules typically apply in tandem to cross-border practice. A lawyer authorized to act under one of the 5.5 safe harbors is still subject to disciplinary authority in the conduct jurisdiction under 8.5(a), and choice of law under 8.5(b) governs which jurisdiction's rules apply to the conduct.

Relationship to Rule 1.17 (Sale of Law Practice)

A cross-jurisdiction practice sale under Rule 1.17 requires attention to both the seller's and the buyer's admission jurisdictions, and to the predominant-effect jurisdiction for each transferred client matter. The choice-of-law analysis under 8.5 frames which jurisdiction's 1.17 conditions and notice requirements apply.

Practical impact

Rule 8.5 is the rule that organizes the modern multi-jurisdiction practice. The architecture - admission-state default + conduct-state extension + tribunal rule + predominant-effect test + reasonable-belief safe harbor - allows a lawyer admitted in one jurisdiction to practice ethically across many, while preserving the regulatory authority of every jurisdiction in which the lawyer's conduct touches down. The practical key for the multi-jurisdiction practitioner is the documented choice-of-law analysis: identifying applicable jurisdictions, choosing the controlling rules under 8.5(b), conforming conduct, and preserving the record that supports the reasonable-belief safe harbor.
