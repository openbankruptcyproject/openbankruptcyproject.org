Federal Rule of Bankruptcy Procedure 3007

Rule 3007: Objections to Claims

Procedure for objecting to a proof of claim, the 30-day notice requirement, contested matter status, omnibus objections, and the evidentiary burden-shift framework.

What Rule 3007 does

Federal Rule of Bankruptcy Procedure 3007 prescribes the procedure for objecting to a proof of claim filed in a bankruptcy case. The rule covers (1) the form, content, and service of a claim objection, (2) the 30-day notice period before a claim objection may be heard, (3) the conversion of a claim objection to a contested matter under Rule 9014, and (4) the use of omnibus objections to multiple claims under Rule 3007(d). The rule operates in tandem with 11 U.S.C. Section 502 (the substantive grounds for disallowance) and Rule 3001(f) (the prima facie validity presumption that the objection must overcome).

The basic objection procedure - Rule 3007(a)

Rule 3007(a) provides that an objection to a claim "shall be in writing and filed." A copy of the objection, with notice of the hearing on the objection, must be served on the claimant, the debtor or debtor in possession, and the trustee at least 30 days before the scheduled hearing.

The 30-day notice requirement is procedural. A claim objection set for hearing on less than 30 days' notice is procedurally defective and is normally continued by the court to a date satisfying the rule. Some districts have local rules that provide for a "negative-notice" or "no-response-required-if-no-objection" procedure: the objector serves the objection with notice that the court will consider the objection without hearing if no response is filed within a stated period; this local procedure is supplementary to and does not displace the Rule 3007 requirements.

Contested-matter status under Rule 9014

An objection to a claim is a contested matter governed by Rule 9014. Rule 9014 makes certain Part VII (adversary-proceeding) rules applicable to contested matters, including Rules 7026 through 7037 (discovery), Rule 7052 (findings of fact and conclusions of law), and Rule 7054 (judgment). Rule 9014(c) also gives the court discretion to apply other Part VII rules at any stage of the proceeding.

Treating a claim objection as a contested matter has practical consequences:

  • Discovery is available under Rules 7026-7037 (with court approval where the rules require it)
  • Motion practice (summary judgment, motions in limine) is available under the Part VII rules
  • The court enters findings of fact and conclusions of law on the record under Rule 7052
  • The court's order disallowing or allowing the claim is a final, appealable order

What the objection must contain

Although Rule 3007 does not prescribe the content of the objection itself, established practice requires that the objection:

  • Identify the claim being objected to by claimant name and claim number on the claims register
  • State the amount of the claim as filed and the amount, if any, the objector believes should be allowed
  • State the legal and factual grounds for the objection with specificity
  • Identify the statutory or rule-based basis for the objection (e.g., 11 U.S.C. Section 502(b)(1) - claim unenforceable under applicable nonbankruptcy law; Section 502(b)(2) - unmatured interest; Section 502(b)(4) - excessive insider compensation; etc.)
  • Be supported by exhibits, declarations, or other evidence sufficient to overcome the prima facie validity of the claim under Rule 3001(f)
  • Include a notice of hearing or, in negative-notice districts, the notice prescribed by local rule

A bare or conclusory objection that recites "lack of documentation" or "amount is disputed" without specifying the basis for the dispute may be insufficient to shift the evidentiary burden, even when the underlying claim is questionable. The objector's burden of going forward is satisfied only when the objection (and any supporting evidence) is sufficient to "equal the prima facie case" of the claim.

The evidentiary burden-shift framework

The burden-shift framework operating in claim-objection practice is well settled:

  1. Claimant's initial burden: The claimant files a proof of claim that complies with Rule 3001. If the claim is "executed and filed in accordance with these rules," Rule 3001(f) gives the claim "prima facie evidence of the validity and amount of the claim."
  2. Objector's burden of going forward: The objector must produce evidence sufficient to rebut the prima facie validity of the claim - evidence "equal in force" to the prima facie case. A bare denial does not suffice.
  3. Claimant's burden of persuasion: Once the objector rebuts the prima facie validity, the claimant carries the ultimate burden of proving the claim by a preponderance of the evidence. The claimant must produce the underlying documentation and any other evidence necessary to establish entitlement to the claim amount.

When a proof of claim does not comply with Rule 3001 (e.g., a credit-card claim lacking the Rule 3001(c)(2)(A) itemization, or a residential-mortgage claim lacking Form 410A), the claim is not entitled to prima facie validity. In that situation, the objector's burden of going forward is satisfied by pointing to the procedural deficiency, and the claimant must produce evidence supporting the claim from the outset.

Omnibus objections - Rule 3007(d)

Rule 3007(d) authorizes a single objection to more than one claim - an "omnibus objection" - but only on enumerated procedural grounds. The grounds permitted for an omnibus objection are limited:

  • Duplicate claims - the claims duplicate other claims
  • Amended or superseded claims - the claims have been amended by subsequently filed proofs of claim
  • Wrong claimant - the claims were not filed by the correct entity
  • Late-filed claims - the claims were not timely filed
  • Satisfied or released claims - the claims have been satisfied or released during the case
  • Incorrect classification - the claims were presented in a form that does not comply with applicable rules and the failure rendered the claim defective
  • Insufficient documentation - the claims are unsupported by the documentation required by Rule 3001
  • Amount disclosed on the schedules - the claims, as filed, are inconsistent with the amounts on the debtor's schedules

An omnibus objection asserting substantive (rather than procedural) grounds is procedurally defective; each substantive objection requires a separate filing. The rule also requires that an omnibus objection list the claims by claimant name and claim number, state the basis for the objection as to each claim, and be limited to no more than 100 claims unless the court orders otherwise.

Effect of a successful objection

If the court sustains the objection, the claim is disallowed (in whole or in part). The disallowance is binding for purposes of distribution and discharge: a disallowed claim receives no distribution from the estate, but a discharge under 11 U.S.C. Section 727 (Chapter 7) or Section 1328 (Chapter 13) generally extinguishes personal liability for the underlying debt to the same extent as an allowed claim, subject to Section 523 nondischargeability exceptions.

If the court overrules the objection, the claim is allowed in the amount stated (or in an amount the court determines after evidentiary hearing) and receives the distribution provided by the plan or trustee's final report.

Common procedural posture

Insufficient-documentation objection

The trustee objects to a credit-card debt-buyer claim that lacks the Rule 3001(c)(2)(A) itemization and any underlying account statement. The objection asserts the procedural ground (Rule 3001 noncompliance) and the substantive ground (insufficient evidence to establish the claim amount). The claimant fails to produce account-history evidence in response; the court disallows the claim under Section 502(b)(1) for failure to prove entitlement.

Omnibus objection to duplicate claims

The Chapter 13 trustee files an omnibus objection to 47 claims that duplicate other claims (commonly, a debt buyer filing a claim on a debt also asserted by the original creditor). The omnibus objection is properly limited to the Rule 3007(d) procedural ground of duplication, lists each duplicate by claimant and claim number, and is supported by a spreadsheet reconciling the duplicates. The court sustains the omnibus objection without individual hearings.

The 30-day service window is a hard procedural floor. A claim objection set for hearing on less than 30 days' notice to the claimant, the debtor, and the trustee is procedurally defective. Courts will not adjudicate the merits of an under-served objection; the matter will be continued to a date that satisfies Rule 3007(a). The 30-day window is the claimant's notice-and-opportunity-to-be-heard protection.
