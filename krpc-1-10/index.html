KRPC 1.10 / MRPC 4-1.10: Imputation of Conflicts of Interest

Screening protocols, lateral-mobility safe harbors, and the consent framework that preserves matters when lawyers change firms.

What Rule 1.10 affirmatively does

Rule 1.10 is the rule that defines the conflict-of-interest perimeter at the firm level. It identifies when one lawyer's conflict is imputed to the firm, but it does so within an architecture that includes a personal-interest carve-out, a screening safe harbor for lateral lawyers, a consent pathway for clients of the firm and prior firms, and a clean re-entry once a conflicted lawyer leaves. The rule lets firms grow through lateral hiring and lets lawyers move between firms without freezing the firms or stranding the clients.

Verbatim text of KRPC 1.10 (Kansas, abbreviated)

(a) While lawyers are associated in a firm, none of them shall knowingly represent a client when any one of them practicing alone would be prohibited from doing so by Rule 1.7 or 1.9, unless the prohibition is based on a personal interest of the prohibited lawyer and does not present a significant risk of materially limiting the representation of the client by the remaining lawyers in the firm.

(b) When a lawyer has terminated an association with a firm, the firm is not prohibited from thereafter representing a person with interests materially adverse to those of a client represented by the formerly associated lawyer and not currently represented by the firm, unless: (1) the matter is the same or substantially related to that in which the formerly associated lawyer represented the client; and (2) any lawyer remaining in the firm has information protected by Rules 1.6 and 1.9(c) that is material to the matter.

(c) A disqualification prescribed by this rule may be waived by the affected client under the conditions stated in Rule 1.7. Kan. Sup. Ct. R. 226, KRPC 1.10.

Missouri Rule 4-1.10

Missouri Rule 4-1.10 tracks ABA Model Rule 1.10 and adopts the same imputation default, the personal-interest carve-out, the post-departure rule, and the consent pathway. The screening framework for laterals has developed somewhat differently across jurisdictions; both Kansas and Missouri permit screening in specific contexts described below.

1.10(a) - The imputation default and the personal-interest carve-out

The default rule is firm-wide imputation: a conflict that would disqualify one lawyer under Rule 1.7 or 1.9 disqualifies all lawyers associated in the same firm. The default reflects the firm-as-unit conception of practice - clients engage the firm, confidences are shared within the firm, and the firm's incentives are unified.

The default has a built-in carve-out. If the prohibition is based on a personal interest of one lawyer - and that personal interest does not present a significant risk of materially limiting the representation by the remaining lawyers - the conflict is not imputed. A personal political belief, a personal financial entanglement that does not implicate the matter, or a personal social relationship that does not affect judgment are typical examples. The carve-out is structural: it keeps the firm available for matters that one lawyer cannot personally take.

1.10(b) - The clean re-entry rule after a lawyer leaves

When a lawyer leaves a firm, the firm is not categorically disqualified from accepting work adverse to that lawyer's former clients. Disqualification attaches only if two conditions both hold: (1) the matter is the same or substantially related to the matter in which the departed lawyer represented the client, and (2) any remaining lawyer in the firm has Rule 1.6 or 1.9(c) protected information material to the matter.

The architecture is enabling for the firm. When a lawyer moves on and takes the client matter with the lawyer, the firm reverts to a clean position with respect to that client unless the firm retains material confidential information through one of its remaining lawyers.

The screening safe harbor for laterals

The most operationally important development in Rule 1.10 is the screening safe harbor for lawyers moving from one firm to another. ABA Model Rule 1.10(a)(2) provides that imputation does not extend to a lateral lawyer's prior-firm conflicts if the disqualified lawyer is timely screened from any participation in the matter and apportioned no part of the fee, written notice is promptly given to the former client, and a certification of compliance is provided on the former client's request.

Kansas and Missouri have adopted versions of the screening safe harbor with conditions appropriate to each state. The effective elements of a screen are well-developed:

Elements of an effective screen

  • Timely imposition - at the moment of hire or earlier
  • Written prohibition of the lateral lawyer's access to files
  • Physical or electronic isolation from the matter
  • Apportionment of zero fee from the matter to the lateral lawyer
  • Firm-wide notice of the screen to all attorneys and staff

Documentation of the screen

  • Written screen memorandum signed by the lateral and by firm management
  • Written notice to the former client identifying the screened lawyer and the steps taken
  • Acknowledgement on the firm's conflict-check system that the matter is screened
  • Certification of compliance available on the former client's request

Screening enables lateral mobility. The screening safe harbor lets firms hire experienced lawyers without acquiring every conflict from every prior engagement. Lawyers can move between firms without abandoning their careers when they have touched matters at a prior firm.

1.10(c) - The consent pathway

Any disqualification under Rule 1.10 may be waived by the affected client under the same conditions as Rule 1.7. This is the second affirmative pathway - even where screening is unavailable or insufficient, the client whose interests the rule protects can consent. Informed consent confirmed in writing under 1.7(b)(4) reopens the matter to the firm.

Common procedural posture

Lateral arrival

A firm hires a lateral lawyer. The firm runs a conflict check that surfaces three matters where the lateral previously represented clients now adverse to the firm's existing clients. For each matter, the firm evaluates whether the lateral has material confidential information; if so, the firm imposes a written screen, gives written notice to the former clients, and certifies compliance. Where information is not material, no screen is needed. Where the lateral was the responsible attorney and retains substantial knowledge of the matter, the firm typically combines screening with client consent.

Lawyer departure

A lawyer leaves the firm and takes a client matter. The firm assesses whether any remaining lawyer in the firm has Rule 1.6 or 1.9(c) information material to that matter. If not, the firm is free under 1.10(b) to accept new work adverse to the former client. If so, the firm declines or seeks consent under 1.10(c).

Firm-management measures contemplated by 5.1(a)

Rule 5.1(a) requires firms to maintain measures that give reasonable assurance of compliance with the rules. For Rule 1.10, those measures typically include:

  • A conflict-checking system that captures every new engagement and every lateral arrival
  • Written protocols for the imposition of screens
  • Standard-form notice letters to former clients of laterals
  • Periodic audits of active screens
  • Training for partners and associates on identifying screening triggers

Continuing nature of the analysis

Imputation is not a one-time intake check. A matter that was conflict-free at the intake can become conflicted as new parties are added, as the firm hires new lawyers, or as existing engagements expand in scope. The conflict-checking system must run continuously to capture these changes.

Practical impact

Rule 1.10 is the rule that determines whether a firm can accept a matter or accept a lateral lawyer. Three takeaways recur:

  • The personal-interest carve-out is broader than firms sometimes realize. Many one-lawyer conflicts do not need to be imputed if they do not present a significant risk of materially limiting the representation by remaining lawyers.
  • Screening is a deliberate process. An effective screen is documented, timed at hire, and audited; an undocumented informal practice is not a screen.
  • Client consent reopens the matter. The 1.10(c) consent pathway is available even where the screening safe harbor is not.
