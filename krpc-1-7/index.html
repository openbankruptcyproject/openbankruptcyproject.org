KRPC 1.7 / MRPC 4-1.7: Conflict of Interest - Current Clients

The conditions under which concurrent representation may continue with informed consent in writing.

What Rule 1.7 affirmatively does

Rule 1.7 is the rule that tells a lawyer when, and on what terms, the lawyer may represent more than one client at the same time whose interests are not aligned. The rule identifies two patterns of concurrent conflict, then states four conditions that, when all met, permit the representation to go forward. The rule is enabling: it gives lawyers and clients a structured pathway to keep representation in place where it would otherwise have to end, and where the clients themselves prefer that it continue.

Verbatim text of KRPC 1.7 (Kansas)

(a) Except as provided in paragraph (b), a lawyer shall not represent a client if the representation involves a concurrent conflict of interest. A concurrent conflict of interest exists if:
(1) the representation of one client will be directly adverse to another client; or
(2) there is a significant risk that the representation of one or more clients will be materially limited by the lawyer's responsibilities to another client, a former client or a third person or by a personal interest of the lawyer.

(b) Notwithstanding the existence of a concurrent conflict of interest under paragraph (a), a lawyer may represent a client if:
(1) the lawyer reasonably believes that the lawyer will be able to provide competent and diligent representation to each affected client;
(2) the representation is not prohibited by law;
(3) the representation does not involve the assertion of a claim by one client against another client represented by the lawyer in the same litigation or other proceeding before a tribunal; and
(4) each affected client gives informed consent, confirmed in writing. Kan. Sup. Ct. R. 226, KRPC 1.7.

Missouri Rule 4-1.7

Missouri Rule 4-1.7 tracks ABA Model Rule 1.7 and adopts the same two-pattern definition and the same four-condition waiver framework. Both jurisdictions require that informed consent be confirmed in writing.

1.7(a) - The two patterns of concurrent conflict

Pattern one: direct adversity (1.7(a)(1))

Direct adversity exists when the lawyer would represent one current client in a matter against another current client - even an unrelated matter. Suing a current client in a matter unrelated to the work the firm is doing for that client is directly adverse. The rule treats direct adversity as a conflict because the lawyer's duty of loyalty to each client cannot be reconciled in the moment of representation against the other.

Pattern two: material limitation (1.7(a)(2))

Material limitation exists when there is a significant risk that the lawyer's representation of one client will be materially limited by the lawyer's responsibilities to another current client, a former client, a third person, or a personal interest. The material-limitation pattern is broader than direct adversity and reaches situations where the representation can proceed in form but the lawyer's judgment, advocacy, or candor would be measurably constrained by the competing responsibility or interest.

1.7(b) - The four conditions for representation to go forward

Subdivision (b) supplies the waiver framework. When all four conditions are satisfied, representation may proceed despite the concurrent conflict identified in (a).

Condition 1: Reasonable belief of competent representation

The lawyer must reasonably believe that the lawyer can provide competent and diligent representation to each affected client. This is an objective test - what a reasonable lawyer in the circumstances would believe. The condition is not satisfied by the lawyer's subjective optimism; it requires a basis in the structure of the engagement and the lawyer's capacity.

Condition 2: Not prohibited by law

The representation must not be prohibited by other law. Certain client combinations are foreclosed by statute or court rule regardless of consent - for example, some government-employment contexts and some banking-regulatory contexts.

Condition 3: No same-tribunal cross-claims

The representation must not involve one client asserting a claim against another client represented by the lawyer in the same litigation or other proceeding before a tribunal. This is a categorical prohibition: a lawyer may not represent both plaintiff and defendant in the same case even with consent.

Condition 4: Informed consent in writing

Each affected client must give informed consent, confirmed in writing. Informed consent requires the lawyer to communicate adequate information about the material risks of and reasonably available alternatives to the conflicted representation. Confirmation in writing closes the loop and supplies the documentary record.

The shape of effective informed consent

Informed consent that satisfies 1.7(b)(4) typically describes:

  • The identity of the other client whose interests are involved
  • The matter or matters in which the conflict arises
  • The material risks the conflict presents to the consenting client - including the risk of constrained advocacy and the risk of confidentiality limits
  • The reasonably available alternatives to the conflicted representation - including engagement of separate counsel
  • The implications for confidential information shared with the lawyer
  • The right of the client to revoke consent prospectively

Informed consent is a process, not a signature. The Comment to Model Rule 1.7 emphasizes that the lawyer's communication of material risks must be sufficient to permit the client to make an informed decision. A boilerplate consent form that does not describe the specific risks of the specific conflict does not satisfy the standard.

Advance conflict waivers

Subdivision (b)(4) accommodates advance waivers - consents given at the engagement's outset that cover conflicts that may arise later. The effectiveness of an advance waiver depends on the extent to which the client appreciated the material risks at the time of consent. A waiver from a sophisticated client represented by independent counsel that identifies the categories of conflicts in advance is more likely to be effective than a one-sentence waiver from an unsophisticated client.

Common procedural posture

Two clients in unrelated matters

A firm represents Client A in a real-estate transaction and is asked to represent Client B in an employment matter against Client A. The representation of B is directly adverse to A under 1.7(a)(1). Representation may go forward under 1.7(b) only if the firm reasonably believes it can serve both clients competently and diligently, and both clients give informed consent confirmed in writing.

Joint representation of co-parties

Two co-defendants ask one lawyer to represent both. The representation poses a material-limitation risk under 1.7(a)(2) because positions that benefit one defendant may disadvantage the other. The lawyer evaluates whether the co-defendants' interests are sufficiently aligned to permit competent representation of both. If so, joint representation can proceed with informed consent that addresses the specific risks - including loss of attorney-client privilege between the joint clients in any later dispute between them.

Continuing duty to monitor

A conflict analysis at intake is necessary but not sufficient. Rule 1.7 imposes a continuing duty: if circumstances change such that a new conflict arises or a previously waived conflict becomes more burdensome than the consent contemplated, the lawyer must re-evaluate and, if appropriate, seek fresh consent. The duty to monitor is structurally linked to the firm-management measures contemplated by Rule 5.1(a) - effective conflict screening is an ongoing function.

Practical impact

Rule 1.7 is the rule firms operationalize through conflict-checking systems. Two takeaways recur:

  • Early identification preserves options. A conflict identified at intake is often waivable; a conflict identified after substantive work has begun may not be.
  • Written consent is the durable record. The writing required by 1.7(b)(4) supplies the proof that the conflict was disclosed and consented to. Oral consent without written confirmation is not sufficient under the rule.
