11 U.S.C. Section 1111(b) Chapter 11 Secured Creditors

The Section 1111(b) Election

Nonrecourse claims treated as recourse, the two-thirds-in-amount and over-half-in-number class vote, and how the election reshapes plan treatment under Section 1129(b)(2)(A).

What Section 1111(b) does

11 U.S.C. Section 1111(b) performs two distinct functions that confuse many practitioners on first reading. The two provisions sit together because they both protect undersecured creditors in Chapter 11, but they operate independently.

Section 1111(b)(1)(A) converts nonrecourse claims into recourse claims by force of statute. Outside bankruptcy, a nonrecourse lender (typical of single-asset real estate finance) can look only to the collateral; the borrower has no personal liability for any deficiency. Inside a Chapter 11 case, Section 1111(b)(1)(A) erases that limitation. The lender's claim is "allowed... as if such holder had recourse against the debtor on account of such claim, whether or not such holder has such recourse," regardless of any contractual nonrecourse clause. This statutory conversion is automatic and requires no election.

Section 1111(b)(1)(B) and (b)(2) create the optional "1111(b) election." A class of secured creditors may elect to have its entire allowed claim treated as fully secured for plan purposes, even though the collateral is worth less than the debt. The election trades two valuable rights (the unsecured deficiency claim and the right to share in the unsecured-class distribution) for one valuable right (refusal of any present-value writedown of the principal balance).

The bifurcation problem that Section 1111(b) addresses

Section 506(a) ordinarily splits an undersecured claim into two: a secured claim equal to the value of the collateral, and an unsecured deficiency claim for the balance. A lender owed $10 million on real estate worth $6 million has a $6 million secured claim and a $4 million unsecured claim. Under a cramdown plan, the debtor can retain the property by paying the present value of the $6 million secured piece over time, while the $4 million unsecured deficiency receives whatever pennies-on-the-dollar treatment the unsecured class receives.

This bifurcation creates two problems for the lender. First, the lender loses ownership economics: if the collateral appreciates after confirmation, the lender does not share in the upside. Second, in single-asset real estate cases, the unsecured deficiency is often the largest single unsecured claim and frequently controls the vote of the unsecured class - but only if the lender retains a deficiency claim to vote.

The Section 1111(b)(2) election permits the lender to refuse the bifurcation. The class elects to have its entire allowed claim treated as secured. The lender gives up the deficiency claim and the right to vote in the unsecured class. In exchange, the plan cannot strip the principal balance below the face amount of the claim; the plan must propose payments whose aggregate face value equals the entire allowed claim and whose present value equals at least the value of the collateral.

The class-vote thresholds for the election

The election is made by a class, not by an individual creditor. Federal Rule of Bankruptcy Procedure 3014 governs the procedure. The class must affirmatively elect by the vote of creditors holding at least two-thirds in amount and more than one-half in number of the allowed claims of the class voting on the election. The thresholds mirror the acceptance thresholds of Section 1126(c) but apply to the election decision rather than to acceptance of the plan.

A class made up of a single creditor (the typical real-estate scenario) can therefore elect unilaterally, since a single creditor will always satisfy both the two-thirds and over-half-in-number tests. In multi-member classes - syndicated loans, mezzanine pools, mortgage-backed securitization trusts - the threshold becomes a coordination problem, and dissenting holders within a class are bound by the class election whether they would have preferred bifurcation or not.

The election deadline is set by the court but typically runs with the deadline to vote on the plan or to object to confirmation. Rule 3014 contemplates that the election may be made until the conclusion of the disclosure-statement hearing or such later time as the court fixes. Late elections are disfavored; the disclosure statement and plan are usually drafted with the election decision baked in.

Statutory carve-outs from the election right

Section 1111(b)(1)(B) carves out two situations in which the election is not available. First, the election is unavailable if the secured creditor's interest in the collateral is of "inconsequential value" - the de minimis threshold. The election would be meaningless in that scenario because the secured claim would round to zero. Second, the election is unavailable if the creditor has recourse against the debtor under applicable nonbankruptcy law and the property is to be sold under Section 363 or under the plan. The carve-out exists because a recourse creditor whose collateral is being sold can credit-bid under Section 363(k) and protect itself directly; it does not need the election as a substitute remedy.

The sale carve-out was central to the RadLAX Gateway Hotel, LLC v. Amalgamated Bank, 566 U.S. 639 (2012), credit-bid dispute. The Supreme Court held that a plan that sells secured property free and clear of liens under Section 1129(b)(2)(A)(ii) must permit the secured creditor to credit-bid; the debtor cannot use the "indubitable equivalent" sub-path of Section 1129(b)(2)(A)(iii) as a workaround to strip the credit-bid right. The Section 1111(b) election would not save a creditor in that posture because the sale carve-out withdraws the election in the first place.

Effect of the election on plan treatment

When a class elects under Section 1111(b)(2), Section 1129(b)(2)(A)(i) applies with a modified requirement. The plan must propose that the electing class:

  • retain its lien on the collateral securing the claim;
  • receive deferred cash payments with an aggregate face amount equal to the total allowed amount of the claim (the full $10 million in the running example); and
  • receive deferred cash payments with a present value, as of the effective date, of at least the value of the collateral (the $6 million in the running example).

The election thus locks in two separate floors: nominal face value equal to the entire claim, and discounted present value equal to the collateral. The debtor cannot use a long amortization to make a small principal payment add up to a large nominal sum unless the present-value floor is also satisfied at the cramdown interest rate prescribed by Till v. SCS Credit Corp., 541 U.S. 465 (2004), or the relevant market rate where applicable.

The election surrenders the deficiency claim. Section 1111(b)(2) provides that "such claim is a secured claim to the extent that such claim is allowed," eliminating the bifurcation that Section 506(a) would otherwise impose. The electing creditor cannot vote in the unsecured class, and cannot share in any pro-rata distribution to general unsecured creditors.

When the election makes sense, and when it does not

The election is most attractive when the collateral is expected to appreciate, when the present-value cramdown interest rate is unfavorably low, or when the unsecured class will receive a trivial distribution. The election is least attractive when the collateral is depreciating, when the cramdown rate is high, or when the unsecured class will receive a meaningful distribution.

Consider a $10 million claim secured by $6 million of real estate in a market the lender believes will recover to $9 million within five years. Without the election, the lender receives present value of $6 million on the secured piece (plus interest at the cramdown rate) and perhaps 5 cents on the dollar on its $4 million deficiency - roughly $6.2 million in present-value terms, and the lender owns no part of any future appreciation. With the election, the plan must commit to payments whose face value totals $10 million and whose present value is at least $6 million. If the debtor sells the property mid-plan at $9 million, the lender's lien attaches to the entire $9 million proceed up to the unpaid face balance. The election captures the appreciation that bifurcation would have surrendered to the equity holders.

The election is also a defensive instrument against the "negative-amortization" plan. Without the election, a debtor can propose a plan with interest-only payments for five years and a balloon payment of the principal, satisfying the secured claim at present value. With the election, every dollar of principal must eventually be paid in face terms; the balloon cannot be smaller than the original face balance, and amortization must actually retire principal.

Interaction with Section 1129(b)(2)(A) cramdown

Section 1129(b)(2)(A) sets out three sub-paths by which a plan can be "fair and equitable" with respect to a dissenting class of secured claims:

  1. Sub-path (i): The class retains its lien and receives deferred cash payments equal in present value to the allowed amount of the secured claim. The 1111(b) election modifies this sub-path by substituting "the total amount of the allowed claim" and adding the aggregate-face-amount requirement.
  2. Sub-path (ii): The property is sold free and clear of liens under Section 363(k), with the secured creditor's right to credit-bid preserved, and the lien attaches to the proceeds.
  3. Sub-path (iii): The class receives the "indubitable equivalent" of its secured claim. See the related indubitable-equivalent deep dive.

The Supreme Court held in RadLAX that these three sub-paths are not freely interchangeable when the plan involves a sale of collateral. A plan that proposes a sale must use sub-path (ii), with its credit-bid right; the debtor cannot escape the credit-bid by labeling the transaction "indubitable equivalent." The reasoning was that the specific governs the general: Congress drafted a sale-specific path with explicit credit-bid protection and a separate, catch-all indubitable-equivalent path, and reading the catch-all as an alternative route to sale would render the credit-bid protection a nullity.

The election interacts most directly with sub-path (i). When a class elects, the debtor's compliance with sub-path (i) becomes more demanding because the aggregate-face-amount floor is added. Sub-paths (ii) and (iii) remain available in principle, but the election's value depends on the debtor retaining the collateral; if the property is sold under sub-path (ii), the election is unavailable in the first place because the recourse-plus-sale carve-out of Section 1111(b)(1)(B) applies.

Strategic summary. The Section 1111(b) election is a precision instrument for undersecured creditors who want to preserve appreciation upside and prevent principal writedown, at the cost of surrendering deficiency-claim economics. It works best in real-estate cases with a single secured creditor and expected appreciation. It works poorly in operating-business cases with rapid collateral depreciation. The election must be timed with the disclosure-statement hearing and is subject to the class-vote thresholds in Rule 3014.
