11 U.S.C. Section 110 - Bankruptcy Petition Preparers

The federal statute that regulates non-attorney "typing services" preparing bankruptcy paperwork: identifying information, fee caps, prohibited acts, and remedies for misconduct.

Home › Statutes › 11 U.S.C. Section 110 - Bankruptcy Petition Preparers

What Is Section 110?

Section 110 is the only provision of the Bankruptcy Code that directly regulates non-attorney persons who prepare bankruptcy petitions and related documents for compensation. Congress added it in 1994 in response to widespread reports that non-attorneys were preparing bankruptcy paperwork for fees, providing legal advice without a license, charging inflated rates, and disappearing when something went wrong. The section creates a separate enforcement regime that operates alongside state unauthorized-practice-of-law (UPL) statutes and traditional Bankruptcy Code provisions.

A "bankruptcy petition preparer" (BPP) is defined in Section 110(a)(1) as a person, other than an attorney for the debtor or an employee of such an attorney under the attorney's direct supervision, who prepares for compensation a "document for filing." A "document for filing" includes the petition and any other document the debtor is required to submit in connection with the case.

Official citation: 11 U.S.C. § 110

Identifying Information: Section 110(b)-(c)

Every BPP must sign the documents they prepare and print their name and address. The preparer must also place an identifying number (historically a Social Security number, now generally an alternate identifier under court practice) on each document. The purpose is to create a clear chain of accountability so debtors, trustees, the United States Trustee Program, and courts can locate the responsible preparer.

A debtor's signature alone on a document does not satisfy Section 110 if a BPP in fact prepared it. The preparer must self-identify. Failure to sign or identify is a discrete violation subject to fines under Section 110(l).

Required Notice and Fee Disclosure: Section 110(b)(2) and (h)

Before preparing any document for filing, a BPP must provide the debtor with a written notice on Official Form B 119 (the BPP notice) advising the debtor that the preparer is not an attorney, may not practice law, may not give legal advice, and may not represent the debtor in court. The notice must be signed by the debtor and the preparer and filed with the petition.

Section 110(h) requires the BPP to file with the court, within ten days after the petition date, a declaration disclosing the compensation received and the source of payment. Courts may, sua sponte or on motion of the trustee or United States Trustee, disallow and order the return of fees found to be "in excess of the value of any services rendered." Several judicial districts and circuits have adopted presumptive "reasonable fee" benchmarks that a BPP must justify to exceed.

Prohibited Acts: Section 110(e)-(g)

Section 110 enumerates conduct a BPP may not engage in. The most important prohibitions are:

  • 110(e)(1): A BPP may not execute any document on behalf of a debtor.
  • 110(e)(2): A BPP may not offer a "potential bankruptcy debtor any legal advice," including advice about the appropriate chapter to file under, whether particular debts will be discharged, the tax consequences of filing, the dischargeability of tax claims, whether the debtor may keep particular property after filing, bankruptcy procedure, or the rights of creditors or the debtor.
  • 110(f): A BPP may not use the word "legal" or any similar term in advertising, or otherwise hold itself out as offering legal services.
  • 110(g): A BPP may not collect or receive any payment from the debtor for court filing fees in connection with the case.

Each of these is enforceable through fines and, in egregious cases, injunctive relief and disgorgement.

Fines, Disgorgement, and Injunctive Relief: Section 110(l) and (j)

Section 110(l)(1) authorizes the court to fine a BPP up to $500 for each violation of subsections (b), (c), (d), (e), (f), (g), or (h). Section 110(l)(2) triples the fine where the violation is part of "a clear and consistent pattern or practice" or is intentional or grossly negligent.

Section 110(j) authorizes the debtor, the trustee, a creditor, or the United States Trustee to bring an action for an injunction against a BPP who has continually engaged in unauthorized practice of law, committed fraud or unfair or deceptive acts, or violated Section 110. The court may enjoin individual conduct, or in cases of repeated misconduct, enjoin the BPP from acting as a petition preparer altogether.

Section 110(i) further authorizes statutory damages of $2,000 (or the actual damages, whichever is greater) where a BPP commits a fraudulent, unfair, or deceptive act in connection with preparation of documents and triggers dismissal or conversion of the case.

Major Doctrinal Cases

Federal courts have built a substantial body of case law applying Section 110:

  • In re Doser (D. Idaho 2002) and In re Doser (9th Cir. 2005) established that providing chapter-selection advice or selecting exemptions for a debtor crosses the line from clerical preparation into legal advice prohibited by Section 110(e)(2), even when the BPP does not appear in court.
  • In re Gomez (Bankr. N.D. Cal. 1995) was one of the earliest reported cases applying the 1994 amendments and held that "preparation" for purposes of Section 110(a) included transcription onto official forms based on debtor-supplied information.
  • In re Reynoso (9th Cir. 2007) addressed software-based "bankruptcy preparation" services and held that an interactive system that selected exemptions, suggested chapters, and prepared the petition based on user answers constituted the practice of law as well as a Section 110 violation.
  • In re Moore (Bankr. E.D. Cal. 2007) discussed presumptive reasonable-fee benchmarks under Section 110(h) and held that a BPP charging materially above the local benchmark bears the burden of justifying the excess.
  • In re Crawford (Bankr. D. Mass. 1995) and similar early cases held that a BPP cannot evade Section 110 by labeling itself a "typing service" or "paralegal."

These authorities collectively illustrate that the dividing line between permissible scrivening and prohibited legal advice is narrow, and that courts have not hesitated to impose meaningful fines and injunctions on preparers operating across that line.

Section 110 and Unauthorized Practice of Law

Section 110 operates in parallel with, not in lieu of, state UPL statutes. A BPP who engages in conduct prohibited by Section 110(e) is also generally engaged in unauthorized practice of law under the law of the state where the debtor resides or where the document was prepared. Most state attorney-general offices and state bars maintain UPL enforcement programs that accept referrals from bankruptcy courts and the United States Trustee Program.

Section 110 does not displace these state regimes; it adds a federal layer with bankruptcy-specific remedies (disgorgement, statutory damages, fines, and injunctions) that can be invoked alongside state UPL proceedings.

Related Bankruptcy Code Sections

Section 110 interacts with several other Code provisions:

  • Section 329 - Disclosure of compensation of debtor's attorney (parallel disgorgement regime for licensed attorneys)
  • Section 526 - Restrictions on debt relief agencies (statutory category that often overlaps with BPPs)
  • Section 527 - Required disclosures to assisted persons
  • Section 528 - Required contracts with assisted persons
  • Section 707 - Dismissal of Chapter 7 case, often invoked alongside Section 110 violations
  • Section 152 - Bankruptcy crimes (false statements, concealment) that may overlap with serious BPP misconduct
