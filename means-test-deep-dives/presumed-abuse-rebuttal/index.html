Rebutting the Presumption of Abuse: Section 707(b)(2)(B) Special Circumstances

The itemization and documentation framework for overcoming a Form 122A-2 presumption, and the Section 707(b)(3) totality-of-circumstances test that operates when the presumption is rebutted or never arose.

The two-track abuse inquiry

The Bankruptcy Code provides two paths to a finding of abuse under 11 U.S.C. Section 707(b). The first is the mechanical presumption at Section 707(b)(2), triggered when an above-median debtor's monthly disposable income (computed on Form 122A-2) exceeds the statutory threshold when multiplied by 60. The second is the discretionary totality-of-circumstances inquiry at Section 707(b)(3), which applies in two scenarios: when the presumption is overcome by a Section 707(b)(2)(B) special-circumstances showing, and when the presumption never arose because the debtor was below median or because above-median deductions reduced disposable income below the threshold.

This page treats both tracks. The presumption rebuttal under Section 707(b)(2)(B) is the gateway concern for above-median debtors whose Form 122A-2 produces a presumption-triggering figure. The Section 707(b)(3) totality test is the residual concern for all consumer debtors after the presumption framework concludes.

Section 707(b)(2)(B) - the statutory text

Section 707(b)(2)(B)(i) provides that the presumption "may only be rebutted by demonstrating special circumstances, such as a serious medical condition or a call or order to active duty in the Armed Forces, to the extent such special circumstances . . . justify additional expenses or adjustments of current monthly income for which there is no reasonable alternative."

Section 707(b)(2)(B)(ii) imposes four procedural requirements on the debtor invoking the rebuttal: (1) itemize each additional expense or income adjustment; (2) provide documentation for such expense or adjustment; (3) provide a detailed explanation of the special circumstances making the expenses or adjustments necessary and reasonable; and (4) attest under oath to the accuracy of the information.

Section 707(b)(2)(B)(iv) sets the arithmetic test for successful rebuttal: the special-circumstances additional expenses or income adjustments must reduce monthly disposable income by an amount sufficient to bring 60-month disposable income below the statutory threshold. A debtor whose itemized special-circumstances showing is documented and sworn but whose re-computed disposable income still exceeds the threshold has not rebutted the presumption.

The In re Pageau itemization standard

The leading reported decision on the rigor of the itemization and documentation requirement is In re Pageau, 383 B.R. 221 (Bankr. D.N.H. 2008). The court held that the special-circumstances showing must identify each additional expense with specificity, document the expense with supporting records, and articulate why the expense is necessary and why no reasonable alternative exists. A generalized claim of financial hardship is insufficient; the debtor must walk the court through each line item.

The Pageau standard requires the debtor to address four elements for each claimed special-circumstances expense or adjustment:

  1. Itemization. The expense must be identified with line-item particularity (not "medical bills" but a schedule listing each provider, date, and amount).
  2. Documentation. Supporting records (billing statements, insurance Explanation of Benefits, prescription receipts, military orders, school enrollment confirmations, etc.) must be produced.
  3. Necessity. The debtor must articulate why the expense is necessary - typically, why it is essential to the debtor's or a dependent's health, welfare, or ability to produce income.
  4. No reasonable alternative. The debtor must explain why a lower-cost option does not exist, why an existing benefit (insurance, employer subsidy, government program) does not cover the cost, and why the expense cannot be eliminated.

Several subsequent decisions have elaborated the no-reasonable-alternative requirement. A debtor cannot establish special circumstances by reference to a chosen lifestyle commitment; the absence of alternatives must be objective rather than preferential. A debtor's voluntary commitment to a particular school for a child, when public alternatives exist at no additional cost, has been held insufficient. A debtor's medically necessary specialty treatment, when no lower-cost treatment is available, has been held sufficient.

The statutory examples and their boundaries

The statute identifies two examples of special circumstances: "a serious medical condition" and "a call or order to active duty in the Armed Forces." The use of the word "such as" indicates the examples are illustrative rather than exhaustive. Courts have nonetheless treated the statutory examples as setting a tone that limits the universe of qualifying circumstances.

Serious medical condition. A condition that produces ongoing out-of-pocket medical expenses substantially beyond the National Standards healthcare allowance qualifies. Reported decisions have credited chronic-disease management costs, specialty pharmaceutical costs not covered by insurance, durable medical equipment, and home-health-aide costs. A condition that has produced only past expenses (without ongoing burden) is typically not credited because the special-circumstances rebuttal is forward-looking; the debtor must show that the additional expense will continue during the relevant period.

Active duty. Military activation that materially increases the debtor's expenses (multiple-residence costs, separation-driven childcare, equipment costs not reimbursed) or reduces income (a debtor whose civilian salary exceeds military pay) can qualify. The disabled-veteran and reservist exemptions at Section 707(b)(2)(D) provide an independent escape from the presumption framework for qualifying military debtors and are often the preferred mechanism for that population.

Beyond the statutory examples, reported decisions have credited under the special-circumstances framework: care obligations for a non-dependent family member with disability; commuting costs for an employment that has no local alternative; foreign-tax obligations for a debtor with overseas income; and education costs imposed by a custody decree.

What does not qualify as special circumstances

The reported decisions are consistent on what falls outside the special-circumstances framework. The following claimed adjustments have been rejected:

  • Lifestyle preferences. Charitable giving (beyond the 15-percent Section 1325(b)(2)(A) allowance available in Chapter 13), country-club dues, vacation expenses, and discretionary recreation do not qualify.
  • Voluntary retirement contributions in Chapter 7. Most courts hold that ongoing 401(k) contributions are not special circumstances in the Chapter 7 means test, though the question is more contested in Chapter 13.
  • Unsecured-debt service. Payments on credit cards, personal loans from family members, and student loans do not qualify; the means test treats these as the very obligations from which Chapter 7 would discharge the debtor.
  • Anticipated but uncommitted future expenses. An expectation that a child may attend private school in the future or that a medical condition may worsen does not qualify; the framework requires present and continuing expenses with documented basis.
  • Garden-variety overspending. A debtor whose monthly outflows exceed the IRS Standards in ordinary categories (food, clothing, recreation) cannot characterize the excess as special circumstances; the IRS Standards are the statutory allowance for these categories.

Income-side adjustments

The special-circumstances framework is not limited to additional-expense itemization. Section 707(b)(2)(B)(i) permits "adjustments of current monthly income." A debtor whose six-month look-back captured income that has terminated (a debtor who lost a high-paying job in the look-back's final month, for example) may seek to reduce the CMI input through a documented showing.

Reported decisions have accepted income-side adjustments where the look-back captures: a one-time bonus or severance payment unlikely to recur; rental income from property since sold; business income from operations since terminated; or wages from a job since lost through no fault of the debtor. The adjustment is mathematically equivalent to subtracting the no-longer-existing income from the CMI base; the procedural requirement (itemization, documentation, attestation, sworn explanation) is identical to the expense-side analysis.

Procedural mechanics

The debtor invoking special circumstances files an itemized statement either as a section of Form 122A-2 or as a supplemental attachment to it. The trustee may then file a motion under Section 707(b) seeking dismissal or conversion, and the special-circumstances showing becomes the centerpiece of the contested hearing. The burden of persuasion on rebuttal rests on the debtor.

The Federal Rules of Bankruptcy Procedure govern the timing of trustee motions and debtor responses. Under Rule 1017(e), a Section 707(b) motion based on the means test must be filed within 60 days after the first date set for the meeting of creditors, although the court may extend this deadline. A debtor anticipating a Section 707(b)(2) presumption motion should prepare the special-circumstances showing for filing concurrently with Form 122A-2.

The Section 707(b)(3) totality-of-circumstances backup test

Section 707(b)(3) provides that even if the presumption does not arise or has been rebutted, the court may still consider whether granting Chapter 7 relief would be an abuse "considering . . . (A) whether the debtor filed the petition in bad faith; or (B) the totality of the circumstances . . . of the debtor's financial situation demonstrates abuse."

The Section 707(b)(3) inquiry is discretionary and fact-intensive. It captures cases that satisfy the mechanical means test but that nonetheless present circumstances suggesting Chapter 7 is inappropriate. The leading pre-BAPCPA decision articulating the framework is In re Krohn, 886 F.2d 123 (6th Cir. 1989), which identified factors the court should consider: whether the debtor enjoys a stable source of future income; whether the debtor has the capacity to repay a substantial portion of debt out of future earnings; whether the petition was filed in response to sudden illness, calamity, disability, or unemployment; whether the schedules and Statement of Affairs misrepresent material facts; whether the proposed family budget is excessive or unreasonable; and whether the debtor's lifestyle is consistent with the claimed inability to pay.

Although Krohn predates BAPCPA, its factor analysis has been carried forward in post-BAPCPA Section 707(b)(3) jurisprudence. Many circuits and bankruptcy courts have cited the Krohn factors approvingly in totality-of-circumstances analyses, with adaptation to reflect the BAPCPA-era distinction between Section 707(b)(2) presumption analysis and Section 707(b)(3) totality analysis.

Ransom v. FIA Card Services in the rebuttal context

The Supreme Court's decision in Ransom v. FIA Card Services, N.A., 562 U.S. 61 (2011), is most often cited for its holding on the vehicle ownership allowance, but the decision also articulates a methodological principle relevant to the rebuttal framework. The Court emphasized that the means-test deduction schedule must be applied as Congress drafted it, even when a particular debtor's circumstances appear to call for a different result. The methodological holding is in tension with a broad reading of special circumstances: if Congress intended the IRS Standards to control, then judicially crafted exceptions for particular debtors' circumstances erode the statutory design.

The practical effect of Ransom on the rebuttal framework is to reinforce the narrow construction of "special circumstances" already established by Pageau and similar decisions. A debtor who argues that her circumstances are special because the IRS Standards produce an unfair result in her case is essentially arguing for a judicial revision of the statute, which Ransom forecloses. The rebuttal succeeds only where the debtor identifies a particularized expense or income adjustment that the standard schedule cannot accommodate and that no reasonable alternative would obviate.

In re Egebjerg and ongoing 401(k) loan repayments

A frequently cited decision on the boundary between ordinary expenses and special circumstances is In re Egebjerg, 574 F.3d 1045 (9th Cir. 2009). The Ninth Circuit held that monthly repayments of a 401(k) loan are not deductible as a Section 707(b)(2) "Other Necessary Expense" because the repayment is not paid to a third party in the ordinary sense; the debtor is essentially repaying himself. The court also rejected the debtor's special-circumstances argument, holding that the existence of a voluntary loan from one's own retirement account does not, without more, constitute special circumstances. The decision is the leading authority for the proposition that voluntary financial commitments to oneself or to favored creditors do not rebut the presumption.

The rebuttal is procedural, but the standard is substantive. Form 122A-2 invites the debtor to itemize special circumstances in a designated section. Filling out the section is the procedural step; succeeding on the substantive standard requires line-item documentation, articulated necessity, demonstrated absence of reasonable alternative, and an arithmetic showing that the additional expense or income adjustment brings disposable income below the threshold. A debtor whose Form 122A-2 output exceeds the threshold by a large margin faces a correspondingly large rebuttal burden.

Bad-faith filing under Section 707(b)(3)(A)

Section 707(b)(3)(A) captures bad-faith filings as an independent abuse category. Bad faith is established by conduct demonstrating that the debtor seeks Chapter 7 relief for a purpose inconsistent with the statute's design: pre-petition asset transfers to insulate property from creditors, concealment of income or assets, repeated filings to thwart enforcement actions, and similar conduct. Bad faith may also be inferred from the timing of the filing relative to anticipated wage increases, expected inheritance, or other near-term financial improvements that the debtor seeks to enjoy free of the discharged debt.

A bad-faith finding produces dismissal regardless of the means-test mathematics. A debtor whose Form 122A-2 output is below the presumption threshold but whose pre-petition conduct evidences bad faith may face dismissal under Section 707(b)(3)(A); the means test is not a safe harbor against bad-faith review.

Strategic implications for above-median debtors

An above-median debtor whose initial Form 122A-2 produces a presumption-triggering figure has three substantive options: rebut under Section 707(b)(2)(B) through documented special circumstances; convert to Chapter 13, where the disposable-income figure becomes the unsecured-creditor payment obligation rather than a dismissal trigger; or face dismissal of the Chapter 7 case. The choice depends on the debtor's circumstances. A debtor with substantial special-circumstances expenses can pursue rebuttal; a debtor with a stable disposable income can convert to a Chapter 13 plan funded by the disposable-income figure; a debtor with neither faces the structural conclusion that Chapter 7 relief is not the appropriate procedural vehicle.
