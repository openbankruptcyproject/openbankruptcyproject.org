11 U.S.C. Section 361 - Adequate Protection

Plain-English guide to the three statutory forms of adequate protection, how courts compute payment streams, and the relationship between equity cushions and cause for relief from the automatic stay.

Section 361 defines adequate protection. When a debtor's continued use, sale, or lease of property would diminish the value of a secured creditor's interest, the court may require the debtor to provide adequate protection of that interest as a condition of the use, sale, or lease.

What Is Section 361?

Section 361 is the operational mechanism by which the Bankruptcy Code reconciles two competing interests: the debtor's need for breathing room from creditor collection (provided by the automatic stay of Section 362) and the secured creditor's constitutional and statutory right to the value of its collateral. When a debtor proposes to use, sell, or lease property in which a creditor holds a security interest, or when a creditor seeks relief from the stay, the court must determine whether the creditor's interest is adequately protected.

The statute does not define "adequate protection" generally. Instead, it enumerates three non-exclusive methods by which adequate protection may be provided. The phrase "the indubitable equivalent" in Section 361(3) is intentionally open-ended, allowing courts to fashion case-specific protections that preserve the secured creditor's bargain.

Official citation: 11 U.S.C. § 361

The Three Statutory Forms

Section 361 enumerates three illustrative methods of providing adequate protection. The statute states that the protection "may" be provided by these means, signaling that the list is not exhaustive but rather a safe harbor.

Section 361(1): Periodic Cash Payments

A debtor may compensate the secured creditor through periodic cash payments to the extent that the stay, the use of property, or the grant of a senior lien results in a decrease in the value of the creditor's interest. This is the most common form of adequate protection for depreciating collateral such as motor vehicles, equipment, and inventory. Computation typically requires expert valuation testimony or stipulated depreciation schedules.

Section 361(2): Replacement or Additional Liens

The debtor may provide the creditor with an additional or replacement lien to the extent that any decrease in value occurs. Replacement liens are common in cash-collateral fights under Section 363, where lenders consent to use of accounts receivable proceeds in exchange for floating liens on post-petition receivables.

Section 361(3): The Indubitable Equivalent

The third method, granting "such other relief... as will result in the realization by such entity of the indubitable equivalent of such entity's interest in such property," is the catch-all. The phrase originates with Judge Learned Hand's opinion in In re Murel Holding Corp., 75 F.2d 941 (2d Cir. 1935), and was carried into the Code as a deliberate textual borrowing. Adequate protection under this prong is whatever combination of safeguards leaves the creditor in no worse position than it would occupy outside bankruptcy.

Statutory exclusion: Section 361 expressly forecloses one option: a grant of administrative priority under Section 503(b) is not adequate protection. Congress determined that an unsecured priority claim, even one with first-dollar status, cannot substitute for the security interest's lien position.

Interaction with Section 362(d)(1) Cause

Section 361 does not stand alone. It supplies the remedy for situations in which a secured creditor's interest would otherwise be diminished. The most frequent context is a motion for relief from the automatic stay under Section 362(d)(1), which authorizes the court to terminate, annul, modify, or condition the stay "for cause, including the lack of adequate protection of an interest in property."

When a secured creditor files a Section 362(d)(1) motion, the burden of proof on the issue of adequate protection rests with the debtor. The creditor bears the burden on every other issue. The debtor must come forward with a concrete proposal: a specific monthly payment, a specific replacement lien on identifiable property, or some other combination that satisfies the indubitable-equivalent standard. A vague promise of future performance does not suffice.

Valuation Timing: Briggs Transportation and Smithfield Plantation

The single most contested issue in adequate-protection litigation is the date as of which the collateral is valued. Two early decisions framed the debate. In re Briggs Transportation Co., 780 F.2d 1339 (8th Cir. 1985), held that the value of the creditor's interest must be measured as of the petition date, with adequate protection compensating for any post-petition diminution. In re Smithfield Plantation, 78 B.R. 519 (Bankr. W.D. Va. 1987), and a line of cases following it, take a forward-looking view that incorporates anticipated depreciation during the case.

The practical consequence of the valuation date is significant. Petition-date valuation favors the debtor: it locks in a baseline that is often higher than current market value for depreciating assets, and any post-petition decline becomes a quantifiable claim against the estate. A market-as-of-hearing approach favors the creditor by capturing recent declines in value as part of the protection calculation.

Equity Cushion as Default Protection

Courts have long held that an adequate equity cushion is itself a form of adequate protection. If the collateral is worth substantially more than the secured claim, the lien is over-secured and a margin of value protects the creditor from foreseeable depreciation during the case. The seminal articulation of this principle is found in In re Mellor, 734 F.2d 1396 (9th Cir. 1984), which approved a 20% cushion as sufficient.

There is no bright-line rule, but a useful rule of thumb that has emerged in the case law is the following:

  • Cushion over 20%: Generally sufficient adequate protection without additional payments or liens.
  • Cushion of 10% to 20%: Often sufficient when the collateral is appreciating, stable, or only slowly depreciating, such as real estate.
  • Cushion below 10%: Typically inadequate as the sole form of protection; courts will require periodic payments or other measures.
  • No cushion: The creditor is entitled to current payments equal to the full depreciation rate of the collateral.

Computing Depreciation-Driven Payments

Where the collateral is depreciating and no cushion exists, courts compute the periodic payment by applying a reasonable depreciation rate to the collateral value. For motor vehicles, the standard is a monthly straight-line decline derived from industry guides. For commercial equipment, expert testimony or manufacturer schedules are used. For inventory, the analysis turns on turnover rates and replacement value.

The payment is not interest on the secured claim. The Supreme Court's decision in United Savings Ass'n of Texas v. Timbers of Inwood Forest Associates, 484 U.S. 365 (1988), held that an under-secured creditor is not entitled to compensation for the delay in foreclosing on its collateral; adequate protection only compensates for actual diminution in the value of the collateral itself, not for the time value of money.

Related Bankruptcy Code Sections

This section operates in concert with several other provisions of the Bankruptcy Code:

  • Section 362 - Automatic stay (relief for lack of adequate protection)
  • Section 363 - Use, sale, or lease of property
  • Section 364 - Obtaining post-petition credit
  • Section 503 - Allowance of administrative expenses
  • Section 507 - Priorities, including super-priority for failed adequate protection

Understanding how these sections interact is essential for any practitioner litigating cash-collateral or stay-relief motions.
