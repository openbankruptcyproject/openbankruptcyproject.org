BAP vs District Court Appeals: The Section 158(c)(1) Election

The election between Bankruptcy Appellate Panel review and district court review of bankruptcy court orders, opt-out mechanics under Rule 8005, and the strategic considerations that drive forum choice.

Verbatim text of 28 U.S.C. Section 158(c)(1)

Subject to subsection (b), each appeal under subsection (a) shall be heard by a 3-judge panel of the bankruptcy appellate panel service established under subsection (b)(1) unless - (A) the appellant elects at the time of filing the appeal; or (B) any other party elects, not later than 30 days after service of notice of the appeal; to have such appeal heard by the district court. 28 U.S.C. Section 158(c)(1).

The structural choice

Congress created Bankruptcy Appellate Panels under 28 U.S.C. Section 158(b) to provide a specialized intermediate appellate forum for bankruptcy appeals. A BAP consists of three sitting bankruptcy judges drawn from districts within the circuit, none of whom is from the district where the appeal originated. BAPs operate only in circuits that have authorized them and only in districts that have not opted out. As of the most recent surveys, BAPs sit in the First, Sixth, Eighth, Ninth, and Tenth Circuits. The remaining circuits route all bankruptcy appeals to the district court.

Within a BAP circuit, the appellate route is not automatic. Section 158(c)(1) creates a default rule (BAP) and two opt-out mechanisms (appellant election; opposing party election within 30 days). The district may also opt out of the BAP system entirely under Section 158(b)(6), in which case all bankruptcy appeals from that district go to the district court regardless of party preference.

The opt-out mechanics under Rule 8005

Fed. R. Bankr. P. 8005 implements the Section 158(c)(1) election. The mechanics:

Appellant election - at the time of filing

The appellant must file a separate statement of election under Rule 8005(a) at the time the notice of appeal is filed. The statement must be in a separate writing, not embedded in the notice of appeal. The election is exclusively the appellant's first-shot privilege - it cannot be exercised after filing.

Other-party election - within 30 days

Any other party to the appeal may, within 30 days after service of the notice of appeal, file a statement of election under Rule 8005(b) to have the appeal heard by the district court. The 30-day window is set by Section 158(c)(1)(B) and is not extendable. Unlike the appellant's election, which is exercised by acting (filing the statement), the other-party election operates against the BAP default - inaction by the appellee preserves the BAP forum if the appellant did not opt out.

Effect of election

An effective election under either provision routes the appeal to the district court. If neither the appellant nor any other party has opted out, the appeal is heard by the BAP. The election decision is unilateral - any single party can force the appeal into district court. The BAP forum requires unanimous consent (or, more precisely, the absence of any contrary election).

Doctrinal weight of BAP decisions

A BAP is not Article III court; it is an Article I appellate body created under the bankruptcy judges' Article I status. BAP decisions are not binding on bankruptcy courts within the circuit in the way court of appeals decisions are. The circuits have divided on whether BAP decisions are binding on bankruptcy courts within the BAP's geographic reach. The Ninth Circuit has held that BAP decisions are binding on bankruptcy courts within the BAP circuit, at least until the circuit court speaks otherwise. The Eighth and Tenth Circuit BAPs have held that their decisions are persuasive but not binding on bankruptcy courts within their circuits.

District court decisions on bankruptcy appeals are similarly not binding on other bankruptcy courts within the district, although they are typically afforded considerable persuasive weight from the same judge or within the same district.

The forum choice is not binary. The further appeal from either the BAP or the district court runs to the court of appeals under 28 U.S.C. Section 158(d)(1). The court of appeals reviews the bankruptcy court's findings and conclusions directly, applying the same standards the intermediate court applied; the intermediate-court opinion does not insulate the underlying record from review.

Strategic considerations - factors favoring the BAP

Bankruptcy-specialized panel

BAP judges are sitting bankruptcy judges. Their familiarity with the Bankruptcy Code, the Federal Rules of Bankruptcy Procedure, and bankruptcy procedural practice is structurally deeper than that of a district court generalist. Cases turning on bankruptcy-specific doctrine - the operation of 11 U.S.C. Section 1129(b) cramdown, the elements of fraudulent transfer claims under Section 548, the mechanics of plan classification under Section 1122 - generally benefit from a panel that does not need to be educated on the basics.

Three-judge panel

The BAP is a three-judge body. A district court appeal is heard by a single Article III judge. The three-judge panel provides the structural benefit of collegial deliberation and reduces the risk of an idiosyncratic outcome.

Speed

BAPs in most circuits maintain shorter median dispositions than district courts, although the gap varies by circuit and by the district court's docket condition. The BAP's docket is exclusively bankruptcy appeals; a district judge's docket is dominated by civil and criminal trial work, with bankruptcy appeals competing for scheduling priority.

Strategic considerations - factors favoring the district court

Article III judge

The district judge is an Article III judge with life tenure. A party seeking adventurous statutory construction or a departure from circuit-court precedent may find a district judge more willing to engage with the merits than a panel of sitting bankruptcy judges, who may be more cautious about disturbing established doctrine.

Familiarity outside bankruptcy

Bankruptcy appeals frequently turn on issues outside the Code - the Federal Rules of Evidence, Federal Rules of Civil Procedure questions imported through FRBP Rule 7001 et seq., contract or property law from the state of the situs, statutory interpretation of non-bankruptcy federal statutes. A district judge sees those issues every day; a BAP panel sees them through the lens of bankruptcy practice.

Subsequent appellate posture

From the district court, the further appeal goes to the court of appeals on the same record. From the BAP, the further appeal also goes to the court of appeals, but the existence of an intermediate bankruptcy-specialized decision may either help or hurt depending on outcome. Where the appellant expects to lose at the intermediate level and wants the cleanest possible record for circuit review, some practitioners prefer the district court because a district judge's opinion may carry less circuit-level deference than a BAP's bankruptcy-specialized analysis.

Predictability of the assigned judge

The identity of the district judge is known on assignment. The BAP panel composition is not announced until later in the briefing cycle. Parties with a strong view about how a particular district judge will treat a particular issue may prefer the certainty.

Standards of review - the same on both tracks

Both the BAP and the district court apply the same standards of review to the bankruptcy court's order: clearly erroneous for findings of fact, de novo for conclusions of law, abuse of discretion for discretionary rulings. The choice of forum does not change the standard; it changes only the identity of the reviewer.

Briefing procedure - largely uniform

Part VIII of the Federal Rules of Bankruptcy Procedure governs both BAP and district court appeals. The notice-of-appeal deadline (14 days under Rule 8002), the record-on-appeal designation (14 days under Rule 8009), and the briefing schedule (Rule 8018) are identical. BAPs sometimes maintain supplemental local rules; the district court's local civil rules typically apply to bankruptcy appeals filed in that court except where Part VIII supersedes.

The district opt-out under Section 158(b)(6)

A district may, by majority vote of the active judges, opt out of the BAP designation for appeals arising in that district under 28 U.S.C. Section 158(b)(6). Where the district has opted out, the BAP option is unavailable regardless of the parties' preferences; all appeals are heard by the district court. The opt-out vote is durable; reversal requires another vote of the active judges.

Practical sequencing

Appellant's first move

Decide forum at the time of filing. The notice of appeal under Rule 8002 must be filed within 14 days of entry of the order being appealed; the statement of election under Rule 8005(a) accompanies that filing. Failure to file the election when filing the notice forfeits the appellant's opt-out and leaves the forum to be set by the BAP default or by an appellee election.

Appellee's counter-move

Within 30 days of service of the notice of appeal, decide whether to file a Rule 8005(b) election. If the appellant did not opt out, the appellee can still pull the appeal into the district court. The 30-day window is hard; tardy elections are without effect.
